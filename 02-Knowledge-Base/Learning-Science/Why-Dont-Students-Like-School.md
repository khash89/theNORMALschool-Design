## Introduction {#introduction}

### The Gap Between Research and Practice

We have learned more about how the mind works in the last 25 years than in the previous twenty-five hundred. Yet the teachers Willingham knows don't believe they've seen much benefit from what psychologists call "the cognitive revolution." The gap is understandable: when cognitive scientists study the mind, they intentionally isolate mental processes in the laboratory to make them easier to study. But mental processes don't operate in isolation in the classroom — they all operate simultaneously and interact in difficult-to-predict ways.

A simple example: laboratory studies show that repetition helps learning, but any teacher knows you can't simply have students repeat long-division problems until they've mastered the process. Repetition is good for learning but terrible for motivation. With too much repetition, motivation plummets, students stop paying attention, and no learning takes place. The classroom application would not duplicate the laboratory result.

### What This Book Offers

*Why Don't Students Like School?* began as a list of nine principles (with a tenth added in the second edition) that are so fundamental to the mind's operation that they do not change as circumstances change. They are as true in the classroom as they are in the laboratory and can therefore reliably be applied to classroom situations.

Criteria for inclusion of each principle:
1. Using versus ignoring the principle must have a big impact on student learning
2. There must be enormous amounts of data — not just a few studies — to support it
3. The principle must suggest classroom applications that teachers might not already know

The book pursues two straightforward but far from simple goals: to tell teachers how their students' minds work, and to clarify how to use that knowledge to be a better teacher.

## Chapter 1: Why Don't Students Like School? {#chapter-1}

### The Cognitive Principle

> **People are naturally curious, but we are not naturally good thinkers; unless the cognitive conditions are right, we will avoid thinking.**

### The Brain Is Not Designed for Thinking

Contrary to popular belief, the brain is not designed for thinking — it's designed to **save you from having to think**, because the brain is actually not very good at thinking. Thinking is slow, effortful, and uncertain.

**Comparison with vision and movement:** Your visual system instantly takes in a complex scene without any conscious effort. You can perform other tasks while seeing. Your thinking system, by contrast, is slow (it doesn't instantly calculate answers), effortful (it requires concentration — you can't think about something else while working on a problem), and uncertain (it may not even produce an answer). Self-driving vehicles still rely on radar and lasers to supplement visible light because computer vision is so difficult. Robots are most useful in highly predictable, repetitive environments — tasks you take for granted, like walking on a rocky shore, are far more difficult for machines than playing top-level chess.

**The candle problem:** In an empty room are a candle, some matches, and a box of tacks. The goal is to get the lit candle 5 feet off the ground. The solution: dump the tacks out of the box, tack the box to the wall, and use it as a candle platform. Most people don't solve this within 20 minutes, illustrating thinking's three properties — slowness, effort, and uncertainty.

### The Brain Relies on Memory Instead of Thinking

Most of the problems we face are ones we've solved before, so we just do what we've done in the past. Memory provides answers quickly and with little effort. Your memory system is much more reliable than your thinking system and provides answers without conscious effort.

Memory stores not only facts and personal events, but also **strategies to guide what you should do**: where to turn when driving home, how to handle a minor dispute, what to do when a pot starts to boil over. For the vast majority of decisions we make, we don't stop to reason — we rely on memory.

The brain also changes with repeated practice: if you perform the same thought-demanding task again and again, it eventually becomes automatic. Driving a car was initially demanding; with practice it became so automatic that you can drive while chatting, gesturing, and eating french fries. Mental tasks that require great thought become tasks that require little thought.

### Why People Do Enjoy Thinking (Sometimes)

Despite being bad at thinking, people actually like to think — under the right conditions. **Solving problems brings pleasure.** Neuroscientists have found overlap between brain areas important in learning and those important in perception of pleasure. When you solve a problem or satisfy your curiosity, your brain may reward itself with a small burst of naturally occurring neurochemicals.

The pleasure is in the *solving*, not the *knowing*. Being told an answer doesn't produce the same satisfaction. The pleasure evaporates if you get too many hints (you no longer feel you've truly solved it).

### The Right Level of Difficulty: Curiosity Is Fragile

Curiosity prompts people to explore new ideas and problems, but when we encounter a problem, we quickly evaluate how much mental work it will take. **If it's too much work or too little, we stop thinking.** Curiosity is maintained when we think we'll learn a lot — when the problem is neither too easy (mindless) nor too difficult (frustrating and unsolvable).

**Why some students dislike school:** Students often receive work that is either too easy (boring) or too difficult (overwhelming). They can't opt out the way adults can. If a student routinely gets work at the wrong level of difficulty, it's no wonder she doesn't like school much.

**Working memory limits:** Thinking works by combining information in working memory (the site of consciousness). Working memory is limited in capacity. Problems become harder as working memory gets crowded. The Himalayan tea-ceremony problem is actually identical to the discs-and-pegs puzzle — but it's much harder because the tea-ceremony version requires you to juggle all the rules in your head rather than having them laid out visually, overloading working memory.

### The Simple Model of the Mind

Three components interact in thinking:

- **Environment:** What's currently present to see, hear, and process
- **Working memory:** Where thinking actually happens — where information from the environment and long-term memory are combined in new ways. Limited in capacity. Can be overloaded.
- **Long-term memory:** The vast storehouse of factual knowledge and procedural knowledge (knowledge of how to do things). Information resides outside of awareness until needed.

**Thinking = combining information from the environment and long-term memory in new ways, inside working memory.**

### Implications for the Classroom

**Be sure there are problems to be solved.** Scan each lesson plan for the cognitive work students will actually do. Is it real cognitive work that can lead to the feeling of discovery? Avoid long strings of teacher explanations with little opportunity for students to solve problems.

**Respect students' cognitive limits.** Background knowledge and working memory capacity both determine whether a problem will be solvable. Chains of logic more than two or three steps long, lists of unconnected facts, and application of a just-learned concept to new material all overload working memory. Solutions: slow the pace, use visual aids (write on the board to save students from keeping too much in their heads).

**Clarify the problem to be solved.** One way to view school content: it consists of *answers*. We want students to know Boyle's law, three causes of World War I, why Poe's raven kept saying "Nevermore." But it's the *question* that piques interest, not the answer. Teachers often devote insufficient time to developing the question because it's obvious to them — but not to students. When planning a lesson, identify the key question and frame it at the right level of difficulty.

**Reconsider when to puzzle students.** Demonstrations and surprising facts are effective at building curiosity — but consider presenting them after students have enough background to appreciate the significance. A burning paper in a milk bottle is a momentary thrill before students understand air pressure; it becomes a satisfying "aha" moment after.

**Accept and act on variation in student preparation.** Giving all students the same work is self-defeating if some find it too difficult. They will mentally walk away. Where possible, assign work appropriate to each student's current level of competence.

**Change the pace.** Change grabs attention. When you shift topics, start a new activity, or otherwise signal a gear change, virtually every student's attention returns. Plan transitions and monitor whether you need them more or less often.

**Keep a diary.** The sweet spot of difficulty is not easy to find. Your classroom experience is your best guide — record what works and what doesn't. Memory of how well a lesson went fades quickly; notes don't.

## Chapter 2: How Can I Teach Students the Skills They Need When Standardized Tests Require Only Facts? {#chapter-2}

### The Cognitive Principle

> **Thinking skills depend on factual knowledge.**

### The Einstein Problem

Many people believe, following Einstein's famous quotation ("Imagination is more important than knowledge"), that critical thinking skills can be developed independently of factual knowledge. This view is false. Data from the last 40 years lead to a conclusion that is not scientifically challengeable: **thinking well requires knowing facts**, and that's true not simply because you need something to think about. The very processes that teachers care most about — critical thinking, reasoning, problem solving — are intimately intertwined with factual knowledge stored in long-term memory.

The human mind is not like a calculator, where a set of procedures can be applied to any data. When you learn to think critically about the geopolitics of Europe after World War II, it does not mean you can also think critically about a chess game or the current situation in the Middle East. Critical thinking processes are tied to background knowledge.

### How Background Knowledge Helps Reading Comprehension

**1. Vocabulary.** If you don't know what "yegg" means (it means burglar), you can't understand a sentence using it.

**2. Bridging logical gaps.** Writers routinely leave out information they assume readers have. "I shouldn't use my new barbecue when the boss comes to dinner," Mark said. Readers must supply two missing facts: people often make mistakes with new appliances, and Mark wants to impress his boss. The writer assumed the reader already knew these things. Without that background knowledge, the connection between "boss coming" and "shouldn't use new barbecue" is incomprehensible.

**3. Chunking and working memory.** Background knowledge allows you to treat multiple pieces of information as a single chunk, freeing working memory for higher-level processing. The letters X, C, N, N, P, H, D are hard to remember as individual letters — but as CNN and PhD they're easy, because they form meaningful chunks. When a reader with baseball knowledge reads "Ashburn hit a ground ball to Wirtz... Dark stepped on the bag, forcing out Cremin... threw to Anderson... Ashburn failed to beat the throw," that reader chunks the whole sequence as "double play" — one meaningful unit. A reader without baseball knowledge must try to hold six separate actions in working memory simultaneously, quickly running out of capacity.

**The baseball study (Recht and Leslie, 1988):** Junior high students were divided by reading ability (good/poor) and baseball knowledge (high/low). They read a story about half an inning of a baseball game. The dramatic finding: **baseball knowledge predicted comprehension far more powerfully than reading ability.** Students who knew a lot about baseball understood the passage much better regardless of whether they were classified as "good" or "poor" readers by standard reading tests.

**4. Disambiguation of ambiguous sentences.** Background knowledge guides interpretation when sentences are ambiguous. The "Washing Clothes" experiment: participants couldn't make sense of a vague passage about procedures (arranging things into groups, going somewhere else if facilities aren't available, etc.). When told the title was "Washing Clothes," the passage immediately became comprehensible — background knowledge told them which interpretation to apply to every sentence.

### Background Knowledge Enables Reasoning and Problem Solving

Much of what appears to be reasoning is actually memory retrieval. When faced with a problem, you first search for a solution in memory, and if you find one, you very likely use it.

**The card-sorting problem:** Given four cards (A, D, 3, 7) and the rule "if vowel on one side, even number on other," most people choose wrong cards (they flip A and 2 when they should flip A and 7). But when the same logical problem is framed as "you're a doorman enforcing the drinking age rule" — flip the "beer" card and the "17" card — most people get it right immediately. Why? Because the drinking-age version taps into familiar background knowledge (enforcing a rule about underage drinking), eliminating the need for logical reasoning.

**Chess expertise is memory, not reasoning.** Top chess players don't primarily plan moves through reasoning — they have approximately 50,000 board positions stored in long-term memory. They size up the board, immediately recognizing familiar patterns, and only then apply reasoning to select among a few candidate moves. This is why blitz chess (5 minutes total per player) barely reduces top players' skill advantage — most of their edge comes from fast memory retrieval, not slow reasoning.

### Background Knowledge Improves Memory Itself

When you have more knowledge about a topic, you remember new information about that topic more easily. In experiments, researchers gave people either a little or a lot of background about new subjects (e.g., Broadway musicals), then had them read new facts. Those with more background learned new facts more quickly and easily.

Why? Because meaning aids memory. Information that connects to things you already know gets linked to more cues, which means more ways to retrieve it later. Information floating without connections is harder to remember.

**The "rich get richer" problem:** If you know 10,000 facts and remember 10% of new ones, while someone else knows 9,000 facts and remembers 9%, the gap widens every month. The person who starts behind falls further behind — unless they are exposed to substantially more new material than those who are already ahead. This has profound equity implications: children from less enriched home environments come to school with less background knowledge and face an accelerating disadvantage.

**The fourth-grade slump:** Reading instruction through Grade 3 focuses mostly on decoding (translating printed symbols into sounds). Reading tests through Grade 3 emphasize decoding. By Grade 4, most students decode adequately, and tests shift toward comprehension — which depends on background knowledge. Children from privileged homes have a larger vocabulary and more world knowledge, giving them an edge that grows over time.

### Implications for the Classroom

**Which knowledge should students learn?** A cognitive scientist's answer: What do you want students to be able to *do*? For reading a newspaper or general publications, students need the broad but shallow knowledge that writers for those publications assume readers have. For academic subjects, students need the central unifying concepts that come up again and again in the discipline.

**When requiring critical thinking, ensure students have sufficient knowledge.** A teacher who asked fourth graders what it would be like to live in a rain forest got shallow answers ("It would be rainy") before the unit. At the end of the unit, one student immediately said she wouldn't want to live there because the poor soil and constant shade would mean she'd probably have to include meat in her diet — and she was a vegetarian. The same question, dramatically richer answers, because now there was background knowledge to support reasoning.

**Teaching metacognitive strategies is helpful but not sufficient.** Reading comprehension strategies ("before you read, use the title to predict content") are useful — but they can't tell you how the specific ideas in a text fit together, because each "fitting" is unique to the content being read. Metacognitive strategies are general; comprehension requires the specific background knowledge relevant to each specific text.

**Shallow knowledge is better than no knowledge.** You don't need deep knowledge of baseball to benefit from knowing it's a sport played with a bat and ball — that shallow definition often clarifies context enough to help.

**Do whatever you can to get kids to read.** Reading exposes children to more facts and vocabulary than virtually any other activity. Graphic novels count. Audiobooks count (comprehension processes are largely the same whether reading or listening; the main thing lost with audiobooks is decoding practice). But leisure reading exposes children to their own specific interests; it won't substitute for a rich curriculum covering the broad background knowledge students need.

**Knowledge acquisition can be incidental.** Students can learn information from math problems, through sample sentences in grammar instruction, from classroom vocabulary, and from teachers sharing their knowledge throughout the day. Every teacher knows things students don't. There are opportunities to fold that knowledge into each school day.

**Start early.** Children from less-enriched home environments arrive at school already behind. The gap grows, not shrinks, over time unless there is intervention. There are no shortcuts and no alternatives to increasing the factual knowledge children haven't picked up at home.

**Knowledge must be meaningful.** Lists of isolated facts yield little benefit. Knowledge pays off when it is conceptual and when facts are related to one another. Teaching knowledge as lists encourages the belief that school is a place of drudgery.

## Chapter 3: Why Do Students Remember Everything That's on Television and Forget Everything I Say? {#chapter-3}

### The Cognitive Principle

> **Memory is the residue of thought.**

### How Memory Works (and Fails)

Memory failure has four possible causes:

1. **Not paying attention:** Information never entered working memory, so it can never reach long-term memory.
2. **Retrieval failure:** Information is in long-term memory but can't be accessed (wrong cue, no cue).
3. **Forgetting:** The information no longer resides in long-term memory.
4. **Never stored in the first place:** Information was in working memory but never transferred to long-term memory.

**The myth of total recording:** You may think the mind records everything like a video camera, and you just can't access most of it. This is false. Hypnosis does not improve memory — controlled laboratory experiments have shown this clearly. Hypnosis makes people more confident their memories are correct, but doesn't make them more accurate.

### What Determines What Gets Stored in Long-Term Memory?

**Not emotion alone.** Emotional events tend to be better remembered, and researchers have worked out some of the biochemistry behind this. But emotion needs to be reasonably strong to have much impact, and most school content doesn't create strong emotions.

**Not repetition alone.** You've seen pennies thousands of times, yet most people can't pick the correct image of a penny from a lineup of similar images. Repetitive exposure without the right kind of engagement doesn't build accurate memory.

**Not intention alone.** A classic experiment: some subjects were warned their memory for words would be tested; others were not. Knowing about the future test did not improve memory. Wanting to remember has little or no effect on whether something is remembered.

**The key: What you think about, that is what you remember.**

**The evidence:** In the same experiment, subjects made one of two judgments about each word on a list — either whether it contained an A or a Q (a shallow, surface-level judgment) or whether it made them think of pleasant or unpleasant things (a deep, meaning-based judgment). People who made the pleasantness judgment remembered nearly *twice as many words*. Why? Because judging pleasantness requires thinking about what the word means and what other words and experiences are related to it. Thinking about meaning created richer connections and more retrieval cues.

**The aspect of meaning matters.** It's not enough to say "think about meaning." You need to think about the *right* aspect of meaning. In an experiment, subjects read sentences that led them to think of either the musical or the physical weight aspect of the word "piano" — "The moving men lugged the PIANO up the flight of stairs" versus "The professional played the PIANO with a lush, rich sound." Later, hints like "something heavy" or "something that makes music" only helped subjects recall the word if the hint matched the aspect of meaning they had focused on.

**The Underground Railroad biscuits example:** A fourth-grade teacher had students bake biscuits to understand what life was like on the Underground Railroad. The students probably thought about the relationship of biscuits to the Underground Railroad for 40 seconds and about measuring flour, mixing shortening, and similar tasks for 40 minutes. They remembered the baking, not the history.

### What Makes Things Stick: Stories

The human mind seems exquisitely tuned to understand and remember stories — psychologists call them "psychologically privileged." Stories share four structural characteristics (the "four Cs") that make them compelling:

**1. Causality.** Events are causally related to one another. "I saw Jane; I left the house" is chronological. "I saw Jane, my hopeless old love; I left the house" is causal — the two events are linked.

**2. Conflict.** A main character pursuing a goal that cannot easily be reached. In *Star Wars*, Luke's goal is to deliver the plans and destroy the Death Star; Darth Vader is the obstacle.

**3. Complications.** Subproblems that arise from the main conflict. Luke wants to deliver the plans, but first he must get off Tatooine — he has no transportation.

**4. Character.** Strong characters whose traits are revealed through action, not description. Princess Leia is shown shooting at storm troopers before anyone tells us she's brave.

**Why stories work for learning:**
- Stories are easy to comprehend because the structure helps interpretation — readers know events are causally linked and work to make the connections.
- Stories demand medium-difficulty inferences, which keep engagement alive (problems interest us when they are difficult enough to require effort but not so difficult as to be hopeless).
- Stories are memorable because they require thinking about meaning throughout, and their causal structure provides retrieval cues (remember one part, and you can reconstruct what came next).
- The same information presented in a story format is consistently rated more interesting than when presented in expository prose.

### Applying Story Structure to Lesson Plans

**The material we want students to learn is the answer to a question. On its own, the answer is almost never interesting. But if you know the question, the answer may be quite interesting.**

Structuring a lesson plan as a story means organizing the *material* using the four Cs — it doesn't dictate the instructional method (lecture, small groups, projects, etc.).

**Pearl Harbor example:** A US-centered chronological lesson presents the attack, US isolationism, and the "Germany first" decision. But from a story structure perspective, Japan is the strong character — Japan had the goal that propelled events forward (regional domination), had significant obstacles (lack of natural resources, war with China), and made the calculated decision to neutralize the American threat before sweeping up European colonies. The Japanese perspective offers a much more compelling narrative structure, even in a US history class.

**Z-score statistics example:** Rather than simply teaching the Z-score formula, Willingham spends 10-15 minutes of a 75-minute class on the fundamental problem: how do we decide whether an event is statistically unlikely by chance? He uses coin flips (easy to understand) to establish the logic, then shows how the same logic applies to advertising experiments and scientific studies. The Z-score becomes the solution to an established problem, not a formula to be memorized.

**The 20-minute Hollywood rule:** In a standard 100-minute film, the central conflict typically isn't introduced until about 20 minutes in — the first 20 minutes establish the characters and situation so you care about the conflict when it appears. Similarly, teachers should invest time establishing *why the question matters* before introducing the answer. We are so eager to get to the answers that we don't devote sufficient time to the question.

### What about Meaningless Material? Mnemonics

Sometimes material must be memorized before it has much meaning — letter-sound associations before reading, foreign-language vocabulary, anatomical names. For these cases, **mnemonics** help by imposing external structure on otherwise arbitrary material.

Common mnemonic methods:
- **Peg-word method:** Memorize rhyming pegs (one-bun, two-shoe, three-tree) and associate items to be learned with them through vivid imagery.
- **Method of loci:** Associate items with locations along a familiar mental walk or floor plan.
- **Link method:** Create a vivid image linking each item to the next.
- **Acronym method:** Create a word from first letters (ROY G. BIV for the spectrum colors).
- **First-letter method:** Create a phrase where first letters cue target words ("Dear Kate, Please Come Over For Great Spaghetti" for taxonomy: Domain, Kingdom, Phylum, Class, Order, Family, Genus, Species).
- **Songs and rhythms:** Setting material to a familiar tune or rhythm (e.g., the alphabet song). Highly effective for memorization.

**Why mnemonics work:** They provide retrieval cues — first letters, visual images, or positions along a familiar route that bridge the gap between what you know and what you're trying to recall.

**Limitations:** Mnemonics help you remember material but don't create understanding of it. They are most useful when the student already has some familiarity with the material (so the first letter cue is meaningful) and when meaning-based learning isn't yet possible.

### Implications for the Classroom

**Review each lesson plan in terms of what students will actually think about (not what you hope they will think about).** The PowerPoint lesson in the Spanish Civil War is a perfect example of a lesson that went wrong: students thought about animations, unusual fonts, and embedded videos rather than about the Spanish Civil War. The teacher's goal was to get students to think about history; what students actually thought about was presentation software.

**Think carefully about attention grabbers.** Opening demonstrations can be counterproductive if students' excitement about the demonstration prevents them from shifting attention to the lesson's actual content. Willingham's daughter remembered a dramatic chemistry demonstration but not the principle it illustrated. Consider using attention-grabbing demonstrations *after* students have enough background to appreciate what they're seeing, not before.

**Use discovery learning with care.** Discovery learning is most effective when the environment provides immediate feedback about whether a student's thinking is productive. Computer applications do this well — mistakes are immediately obvious. Letting students "mess around" with frog dissection or a historical question without feedback is less effective because students may remember incorrect "discoveries" as well as correct ones.

**Design assignments so that thinking about meaning is unavoidable.** If you want students to appreciate the experience of people on the Underground Railroad, ask them where people on the Underground Railroad obtained food, how they were able to prepare it, how they were able to pay for it. Force the right kind of thinking.

**Don't be afraid to use mnemonics.** When material is meaningless now but must be memorized as a prerequisite to deeper learning, mnemonics are legitimate tools. The multiplication table is a good example — learning it by rote before fully understanding multiplication is a reasonable pedagogical choice that makes later mathematical work much easier.

**Try organizing lesson plans around conflict.** Start with the content you want students to learn, and work backward to the question it answers. For example, models of the atom from 1900 are "the answers." The question (the conflict) is: what is the nature of matter? The obstacle: different experiments appear to conflict with one another. Each proposed model (Rutherford, cloud, Bohr) resolves one conflict but generates a new complication.

## Chapter 4: Why Is It So Hard for Students to Understand Abstract Ideas? {#chapter-4}

### The Cognitive Principle

> **We understand new things in the context of things we already know, and most of what we know is concrete.**

### Understanding Is Disguised Remembering

Students understand new ideas by relating them to ideas they already know. This is why:

**Analogies help.** When teaching Ohm's law (I = V/r), textbooks use a water pressure analogy — electrons moving through a wire are like water moving through a pipe. The pressure difference drives water flow; friction and constriction reduce it. Students already understand water flow intuitively; the analogy lets them recruit that understanding to grasp the new concept.

**Concrete examples are necessary but must be *familiar*.** It's not the concreteness that matters — it's the familiarity. Explaining ordinal versus interval measurement using the Mohs hardness scale and Rasch models (unfamiliar) fails even though both are concrete. Explaining the same concept using horse race positions (ordinal: you know who finished ahead but not by how much) and degrees on a thermometer (interval: the gaps between measurements are meaningful) works because those are familiar.

**No one can pour new ideas into a student's head directly.** Every new idea must build on ideas the student already knows. To help a student understand, a teacher must ensure that the right ideas from the student's long-term memory are pulled up and put into working memory — and that those memories are compared or combined in the right way.

**Ludwig Wittgenstein:** "The problems are solved, not by giving new information, but by arranging what we have always known."

### Shallow vs. Deep Knowledge

Even when students "understand," there are degrees of comprehension.

**Rote knowledge:** The student has simply memorized words without understanding them. "Three kinds of blood vessels are arteries, vanes, and caterpillars" — the student is just reproducing memorized text without understanding that "vanes" should be "veins" and "caterpillars" have nothing to do with blood vessels. Genuine rote knowledge (no understanding at all) is probably rarer than feared.

**Shallow knowledge:** Much more common than rote knowledge. The student has some understanding — she can understand the concept in the specific context it was presented — but the understanding is limited. She knows that a government is like a classroom because both are communities that need rules, but she can't say how a government differs from a classroom, can't apply the principle to a new example (a group of friends playing pickup basketball), and can't reason through "what-if" questions.

**Deep knowledge:** The student understands not just the parts but the whole. The pieces of knowledge are richly interconnected. She can apply the knowledge in many different contexts, talk about it in different ways, imagine how the system would change if one part changed, and answer "what-if" questions. A student with deep knowledge of Cavalier poetry could recognize elements of Cavalier ideals in ancient Chinese poetry — even though the two seem very different on the surface — because she understands the underlying principles, not just specific examples.

### Why Knowledge Stays Shallow

Students encounter new ideas by connecting them to things they already know. If understanding stops there — if the new idea is understood only in terms of the specific analogy or example used to introduce it — knowledge remains shallow. The student's understanding is tied to the concrete example provided. She can only think about the concept in the context where she first encountered it.

Understanding area calculations in terms of "tabletops" does not automatically transfer to "soccer fields" because the student has linked the procedure to a specific surface-structure (tabletops), not to the abstract concept of area. This explains why the student in the chapter's opening example — who had just solved an area problem about a tabletop — looked blank at a problem about a soccer field.

### Transfer: Why What's Learned in Class Doesn't Apply Elsewhere

**Transfer of learning** is the ability to apply knowledge learned in one situation to new and different situations. Transfer is notoriously difficult to achieve and is one of the core challenges of education.

Abstract principles don't readily transfer because the mind prefers concrete examples. The tea-ceremony problem is logically identical to the discs-and-pegs problem — but very few people notice the analogy without being told. Students who successfully apply a formula in one context often fail to apply it in what is, at a deeper level, exactly the same kind of problem.

### How to Help Students Understand and Transfer

**Use concrete examples and analogies:** Not as alternatives to abstract principles, but as pathways toward them. Ohm's law becomes accessible through the water-pressure analogy.

**Use multiple examples:** No single example or analogy is sufficient for deep understanding. Expose students to the same abstract principle through many different concrete instantiations. Calculating the area of tabletops, soccer fields, envelopes, doors, and book covers helps students see that "area" is the abstract concept, not something specifically about tabletops.

**Compare examples:** When students compare two examples of the same abstract principle presented with different surface features, they are forced to identify what the examples have in common — which is precisely the abstract principle itself. Two examples side by side are more effective than one example followed by another.

**Be explicit about the abstract principle.** After providing examples, tell students what they have in common. Don't leave students to infer the abstract principle themselves — naming it explicitly helps.

**Analogies may be the secret weapon.** Good analogies help not only by making the abstract concrete, but by making the student's existing knowledge work in the service of new learning.

### Implications for the Classroom

**Don't expect understanding from a single explanation and set of examples.** Understanding is built incrementally through exposure to multiple different versions of the abstract principle.

**Use analogies generously.** They work because they recruit the student's prior knowledge. The more concrete and familiar the analogy, the better.

**Examples must be familiar, not just concrete.** Before using an example, ask whether students actually know it well enough for it to serve as an analogy.

**Name the abstract concept after examples.** Give students the vocabulary to talk about the principle once they understand it through examples.

**Recognize that transfer is not automatic.** Just because a student can do something in one context doesn't mean she can do it in another. Design instruction to expose students to the same concept in multiple, varied contexts.

## Chapter 5: Is Drilling Worth It? {#chapter-5}

### The Cognitive Principle

> **It is virtually impossible to become proficient at a mental task without extended practice.**

### The Bottleneck of Working Memory

The fundamental bottleneck in human cognition is the limited capacity of working memory. You cannot multiply 184,930 × 34,004 in your head because you run out of room to keep track of the intermediate results. The processes are the same as for 19 × 6; the latter just requires more space than working memory can hold.

Practice addresses this bottleneck in two ways:

**1. Factual knowledge enables chunking.** Background knowledge in long-term memory allows you to group multiple pieces of information into a single chunk, freeing working memory for higher-level work. An algebra student who is uncertain about the distributive property must stop in the middle of a problem and substitute numbers to verify — consuming working memory capacity that could have been used for the overall problem. A student who knows it cold doesn't need to stop.

**2. Mental processes become automatic through practice.** Initially, a process occupies all of working memory. With sufficient practice, the process becomes automatic — it happens quickly, without conscious effort, consuming almost no working memory capacity.

**Properties of automatic processes:**
- They happen very quickly
- They occur whether you want them to or not — when you see a word, you read it (experienced readers can't avoid this)
- You are unaware of the component steps

**The Stroop task:** Look at color words printed in mismatching ink colors and name the ink color (not the word). "RED" printed in blue ink — you have to say "blue." This is hard precisely because reading is so automatic that the word reading interferes with the color naming.

**Reading:** Beginning readers must consciously sound out each letter and blend the sounds into a word. This consumes all of working memory, leaving no room to think about meaning. With practice, word recognition becomes automatic, freeing working memory for comprehension. The experienced reader doesn't think about decoding — she thinks about what the text means.

**Math facts:** Students who count to solve 5 + 4 can handle simple arithmetic but struggle with multi-digit problems, because counting consumes the working memory capacity needed for carrying digits and tracking intermediate results. Students who have memorized 5 + 4 = 9 retrieve the fact automatically, freeing working memory for the overall problem structure. Studies confirm that knowing math facts correlates with success across math tasks.

### Three Benefits of Extended Practice Beyond Initial Mastery

**1. Enables further learning.** When low-level processes are automatic, higher-level processes can operate. You cannot become good at algebra without automatic math facts; you cannot think about what you're reading if you're still thinking about how to decode the words. Practice at the foundational level is a prerequisite for learning the advanced level.

**2. Protects against forgetting.** The more you practice something, the longer you will remember it. Algebra skills learned in a single course fade, but algebra skills revisited across multiple courses (and years) are retained much longer. A study of algebra performance over twelve years found that students who took more math courses retained more algebra skills — not because they practiced the same skills but because they used those skills in new contexts.

**3. Improves transfer.** Practice also helps students recognize the abstract, deep structure of a problem even when it is dressed in unfamiliar surface features. Basketball analysts who are expert at calculating playoff probability calculations could not perform the same calculations with a different surface structure (drawing colored marbles from an urn). They had not practiced the skill in sufficiently varied contexts.

### What Doesn't Work: Increasing Working Memory Through Training

Working memory capacity is more or less fixed — you get what you get, and practice does not change it. The last decade has seen many attempts to develop training regimens to improve working memory. Reviews of this research consistently conclude that such training makes you better at the specific training task, but doesn't improve working memory more broadly. An effective working memory training regimen has not yet been developed.

### Implications for the Classroom

**What should be practiced?** The processes that need to become automatic are the building blocks — things done again and again in a subject area, prerequisites for more advanced work. Math facts, letter-sound associations for beginning readers, and key factual information central to a field are good candidates.

**Space out practice over time.** There is no reason for all practice with a particular concept to occur within a short span. Spaced practice is more effective than massed practice at building durable memory. An additional benefit: spacing requires students to think about how to apply what they know, not just perform a mechanical repetition.

**Fold practice into more advanced skills.** Rather than practicing basics in isolation until automatic, practice them also in the context of more complex work. A bridge player needs to count points automatically, but bridge instruction needn't consist only of counting drill — count during actual play. A reader needs to decode fluently, but reading instruction needn't consist only of decoding drill — read interesting texts.

**Ensure variety in practice.** Students don't just need practice with many examples of the same deep structure in order to recognize that structure — they need to practice the problem in different surface guises. Basketball probability experts couldn't transfer to colored marbles because they had only practiced in one surface structure. Vary the form of practice problems.

**Practice doesn't have to be boring.** Students who are clearly improving are motivated. The challenge is practice at the level when improvement is not obvious. To make this tolerable: vary the format, provide practice in the context of more interesting, advanced work, and help students understand why this practice pays off.

## Chapter 6: What's the Secret to Getting Students to Think Like Real Scientists, Mathematicians, and Historians? {#chapter-6}

### The Cognitive Principle

> **Cognition early in training is fundamentally different from cognition late in training.**

### Why Experts Think Differently from Novices

Experts in any field display four characteristics that novices lack:

**1. A sixth sense about what information to attend to.** Experts quickly identify what's important and what's peripheral. Novices don't know yet which details are critical. Expert teachers notice a small-group discussion on the verge of a breakthrough; novice teachers may not. Expert chess players focus their gaze on the board positions that carry the most information; novice players spread attention more diffusely.

**2. They notice subtle features that novices miss.** Because they know what to attend to, their focused attention makes subtleties more apparent. Expert radiologists reading medical images perceive patterns that trained non-experts don't.

**3. They fail gracefully.** When experts make a mistake, their course of action was, in retrospect, sensible. They had good reasons for their error. Novices may not even have a good frame for the problem.

**4. Their knowledge transfers better to new situations.** Experts can recognize the same deep structure across very different surface features. Novices tend to categorize problems by surface features (both problems involve pulleys) rather than by the underlying principle (both involve conservation of momentum).

**The key that explains all four:** Experts **see deep structure** rather than surface structure. They have learned, through extensive experience, which features of problems are meaningful and which are incidental.

### Expertise Is Built Through Extended Practice

The key ingredient to becoming an expert is not innate talent but extended practice. Ericsson's research shows that what distinguishes expert performers from ordinary ones is not native ability but the quantity and quality of practice — specifically, **deliberate practice**: goal-directed, often solitary, consisting of repeated striving to exceed your current level of performance.

The average time to reach expertise in a field is approximately 10 years (not precisely "10,000 hours" — that figure from Malcolm Gladwell's *Outliers* is a mischaracterization of Ericsson's findings). Expert performance is a product of quantity and quality of practice, not genetic predisposition, and becoming expert is not beyond the reach of normally gifted people who have the motivation, time, and discipline to pursue it.

### The Critical Mistake: Don't Try to Teach Students to Think Like Experts

The error is assuming that because experts do something a certain way, novices should be trained to do it the same way. This is wrong.

**Expert readers** make fewer eye movements because they recognize whole words or even phrases at a glance. But this does not mean novice readers should be taught to start by recognizing whole words — they need to sound out letters first. The expert's behavior is the *result* of extensive practice starting from the novice's behavior; it cannot serve as a substitute for that progression.

**Expert teachers** have automatized classroom management, so they focus their limited working memory on instruction. But first-year teachers cannot adopt expert classroom management strategies directly — they haven't yet automatized the prerequisite skills.

**Scientists, historians, and mathematicians** create new knowledge — they develop original theories, write new historical narratives, prove new theorems. Students cannot yet do this. Trying to get students to "think like scientists" treats the endpoint of expertise as the starting point for instruction, which doesn't work.

**The appropriate goal:** Not knowledge *creation* but knowledge *comprehension*. A student may not develop her own scientific theory, but she can develop a deep understanding of existing theory. She can understand how science works and progresses, follow and evaluate existing historical narratives, and understand why mathematical proofs work.

### What Students Can Appropriately Do

**First stage:** Understand and appreciate what experts achieve and why it's special.
**Second stage:** Understand the methods of experts by analyzing how they achieve what they do.
**Third stage:** Work toward using those methods (even if imperfectly), not to create new knowledge, but to deepen understanding.

Science fairs are a good example of the third stage. The projects are usually scientifically poor — questions are often unimportant, experiments poorly designed. But some students get very excited about science or engineering, and their interest is boosted even if the creative product is mediocre.

### Implications for the Classroom

**Adjust goals from knowledge creation to knowledge comprehension.** A student comprehending what scientists have found is a worthy and achievable goal. Don't abandon it for the unachievable goal of having students think like scientists.

**Don't have novices emulate experts.** When you see an expert doing something differently from how a novice does it, it may be that the expert went through the novice's behavior as a necessary step. Ralph Waldo Emerson: "Every artist was first an amateur."

**Use the language of progress, not perfection.** Replace "practice makes perfect" with "practice makes progress." Focus students on way stations — intermediate stages of success — rather than the ambitious final goal. Progress is visible; perfection is remote.

**Help students see the exciting prospect of expertise.** Expert performance is possible for ordinary people through sustained hard work. This is genuinely hopeful. Students who want to be scientists or novelists should be encouraged — the research indicates they can succeed with sustained effort, even without exceptional initial talent.

## Chapter 7: How Should I Adjust My Teaching for Different Types of Learners? {#chapter-7}

### The Cognitive Principle

> **Children do differ in intelligence, but intelligence can be changed through sustained hard work.**

*(Note: The full articulation of this principle appears in Chapter 8; Chapter 7 primarily examines what teachers can and cannot do to capitalize on differences between students.)*

### Learning Styles: The Evidence Says No

The popular notion that matching instruction to a student's preferred learning style (visual, auditory, kinesthetic) improves learning is **not supported by empirical research.** Over 70 different models of learning styles have been proposed. A major 2008 review (Pashler, McDaniel, Rohrer, and Bjork) found:

- Virtually no studies adopted the gold standard of controlled experiments to test the learning-styles hypothesis
- The few studies that did adopt this standard **consistently found that matching instruction to learning style did not improve learning**

**The critical test:** Learning-styles theory requires not just that people have preferences for how they receive information, but that they learn *better* in that format. The evidence for this simply doesn't exist.

**A perverse effect:** Learning-styles theory can cause individuals to perceive their learning abilities as limited. A student told "you're a visual learner" may conclude there's no point in reading books or listening to lectures.

**The better principle:** You learn better when you "go wide" — drawing on all your aptitudes and modes of experience — than when you limit instruction to the format you find most comfortable.

### Multiple Intelligences: Helpful Conceptually, But Not as Instruction Criteria

Howard Gardner's theory of multiple intelligences (logical-mathematical, spatial, linguistic, kinesthetic, musical, interpersonal, intrapersonal, naturalistic) has value in expanding our conception of human abilities. Unlike learning styles, it doesn't restrict — it expands the range of tools we acknowledge as part of the human cognitive toolkit.

However, the theory lacks empirical validation as a basis for differentiating instruction. Gardner himself acknowledges that determining a person's mix of intelligences is "more an art than a science."

**More useful alternative:** Robert Sternberg's triarchic theory of intelligence (analytical, creative, practical) and research showing that students tend to do better when instruction leverages their strongest ability type — not because "style matching" works, but because it enables engagement of genuine strengths.

### What Actually Differs Among Students: Background Knowledge and General Intelligence (g)

**The most consistent difference among students that matters for school success is what they already know and can do before the lesson begins.** This is why background knowledge has come up repeatedly throughout the book.

Beyond background knowledge, there is something to the idea that some students are more capable — psychologists call it "g" (general intelligence). It predicts success across a range of cognitive tasks. But as Chapter 8 elaborates, this general ability is more malleable than most people believe.

### Implications for the Classroom

**Don't evaluate students' "learning styles" and try to tailor instruction accordingly.** There is no reason to think this will help. Save time and money on books or professional development sessions on learning styles.

**Think in terms of content, not in terms of students.** Visual-auditory-kinesthetic distinctions are useful for thinking about *content* — some content should be seen (a diagram of the Petronas Twin Towers), some should be heard (the national anthem of Turkmenistan), some should be experienced physically (a textile). These distinctions apply to what makes sense for the material, not to which type of learner is receiving it.

**Use variety to promote engagement and attention.** Every teacher knows that change during a lesson invigorates students and refocuses attention. Rather than individualizing mental processes for each student, give all students practice in all types of cognitive activity. View transitions between different types of activity as opportunities for each student to start fresh.

**Don't tell children they are "smart in some way" to communicate egalitarianism.** There will always be students who are not gifted in any of the defined intelligences. Telling such students they have abilities they don't possess seldom works. More importantly, every child is unique and valuable, whether or not they possess unusual mental ability.

**Do not have high-ability students slow their thinking and learning to wait for others, and do not have lower-ability students skip steps they need.** Differentiate on the basis of your experience with each student.

## Chapter 8: How Can I Help Slow Learners? {#chapter-8}

### The Cognitive Principle

> **Children do differ in intelligence, but intelligence can be changed through sustained hard work.**

### What Intelligence Is (and Isn't)

Intelligence — the ability to understand complex ideas, reason effectively, overcome obstacles through thought, and learn from experience — predicts success in school and in the workplace. It is not "just" an ability to take tests.

**The structure of intelligence:** Most researchers favor a model in which a general factor (called "g") underlies performance across many cognitive tasks. This explains why SAT math and SAT verbal scores are more similar to each other (within a person) than SAT math and SAT verbal scores across different people. People high in g tend to perform better on all cognitive tasks.

**What g is not:** Athletic ability, musical talent, spatial ability, interpersonal skill, and similar abilities. These are important human capacities, but calling them "intelligences" creates the misleading inference that a strength in one can directly compensate for a weakness in another. It cannot.

### Intelligence Is More Malleable Than Believed

**The Flynn Effect:** Average IQs in every industrialized nation have risen since standardized sampling began in 1932. In the United States, the average IQ has risen 18 points in the last 60 years. The mean has risen, not the variation — this suggests environmental factors (nutrition, education, cultural stimulation) are responsible. IQ is not fixed by genetics alone.

**The environment is more important than previously thought.** For many years, researchers thought approximately 50% of intelligence came from genes and 50% from environment. More recent research indicates the environment is considerably more important than previously estimated.

**Schooling makes you smarter.** Studies comparing children who attended school with peers who did not (or who experienced disruptions in schooling) show that schooling itself increases IQ. Children who start school early gain IQ points; children who start late or miss school lose them.

**What this means for slow learners:** They are not dumb. They probably differ from brighter students not primarily in potential but in what they know, their motivation, their persistence in the face of academic setbacks, their self-image as students, and the resources available to them outside of school.

### Growth Mindset: The Theory and Its Nuances

**Carol Dweck's research** has demonstrated that one simple belief about intelligence makes a significant difference in learning and performance: whether you think your intellectual ability is fixed or can grow through effort.

**Fixed mindset:** Intelligence is an entity — something you are, not something you do. Failure indicates inability. Focus is on looking smart: choose challenges you're confident you can meet; avoid challenges that might reveal limitations. When faced with failure, believe you simply can't do it and give up.

**Growth mindset:** Intelligence can grow through hard work and good strategies. Failure indicates insufficient effort or a strategy that needs changing. Focus is on getting smarter: choose increasingly challenging tasks; interpret setbacks as useful information about what to try differently. Hard work, not natural talent, is what determines success.

**Dweck's experiment:** A workshop for low-performing 7th graders. Half received instruction in study techniques. The other half also received information about how the brain changes when you learn (new connections form, making you smarter). Teachers who were unaware of what each group had been taught subsequently observed that the growth-mindset group became much more aggressive learners and higher achievers.

**The praise study:** Students who solve a puzzle and are praised for being "smart" subsequently choose easier puzzles (to protect their "smart" image). Students praised for "working hard" choose harder puzzles 90% of the time. Worse: when smart-praised students then receive unsolvable puzzles, they subsequently fail to solve even puzzles they had successfully completed earlier — their identity as "naturally talented" was shaken, leading to learned helplessness.

**Dweck's key finding on failure:** Students who attribute failure to inability become helpless. Students who attribute failure to insufficient effort or ineffective strategy dig deeper and try new approaches.

**Growth mindset in practice — three elements Dweck recommends:**
1. **Praise process, not ability.** Not "you're so smart!" but praise for undertaking challenging tasks, persisting under difficulty, and taking responsibility for work.
2. **Encourage seeking feedback.** Honest, specific, informative feedback is essential for improvement.
3. **Teach resourcefulness when things go wrong.** When a strategy fails, students should look for alternative approaches rather than concluding they're unable.

**Caution:** Reviews of schools' attempts to institutionalize growth mindset strategies show "some successes but many failures." The most common misapplication is praising effort alone. "You tried so hard — that's great" when a student fails sends the message that there's no point in continuing. Genuine growth mindset praise includes effort plus direction: "you tried hard, and here's what to try differently."

**Why it's still worth doing:** Talking about intelligence is inevitable in classrooms. Growth mindset talk is very low cost — it changes how you describe things that come up anyway. "You can get smarter, but you must work hard, seek feedback, and try new things" is accurate and helpful.

### Implications for the Classroom

**Talk about intelligence using growth mindset language, but don't expect dramatic results from that alone.** The talk is cheap and helpful; dramatic transformation requires much more.

**Challenge students.** Intelligence grows by learning new things. The only way students get smarter is by doing more hard work. "Set high expectations" is familiar advice; this chapter explains why it matters: working below one's potential means treading water, not growing.

**Tell students explicitly that hard work pays off.** Praise process over ability. Make the message that intelligence is under their control not just implicit in praise, but explicit. Tell them about famous people who had to work extremely hard. If some students brag about not studying, explain that most successful students work very hard.

**Treat failure as a natural part of learning.** Create a classroom atmosphere where failure, while not desirable, is neither embarrassing nor wholly negative. Be matter-of-fact about student mistakes. Model a learning attitude toward your own mistakes. A student who fears failure will avoid the challenging work that's the only pathway to growth.

**Don't take study skills for granted.** Many students — especially those who are already behind — don't know how to study effectively. They don't know how to assess what's important, how long to study, or how to plan their time. Be explicit about these skills. Don't assume students will learn effective study techniques on their own.

**Catching up requires working harder than peers, not equally hard.** Because background knowledge helps you acquire new knowledge, a student who knows less learns new material more slowly — so to close the gap, they must do more work than students who are already ahead. Be honest about this reality and help students see it as a manageable challenge, not an impossibility.

**Set interim, achievable goals.** Rather than focusing on the ambitious long-term goal of "catching up," set concrete, short-term goals: devote a fixed time every day to homework; read a weekly news magazine; watch one documentary on science or history each week.

## Chapter 9: How Can I Know Whether New Technology Will Improve Student Learning? {#chapter-9}

### The Cognitive Principle

> **Technology affects cognition in ways that are hard to predict; evaluate technology use the same way you would evaluate any instructional technique.**

### What Technology Has Not Done

Despite widespread predictions that digital technology would fundamentally reshape how students think, the evidence shows more modest effects:

**Technology has not changed the fundamental architecture of the mind.** The brain is plastic and changes with experience, but its fundamental organization (working memory, long-term memory, the processes that operate on them) has not been remade by digital immersion.

**"Digital natives" is a misleading concept.** The claim that children who grew up with digital technology think differently from "digital immigrants" is not supported by evidence. Young people are comfortable with digital technology but don't process information differently in any fundamental way.

**Technology has not revolutionized education.** Many predicted that personalized online learning, interactive whiteboards, and ubiquitous computing would transform student learning. The evidence shows modest average effects — sometimes a small boost, sometimes no effect, occasionally negative effects. Results depend heavily on context.

**Students don't primarily use technology for self-education.** Despite the Internet providing access to virtually unlimited educational content, students use digital technology primarily for entertainment, social connection, and games — not for self-directed learning.

**Technology and mental health:** Early studies suggested a link between social media use and anxiety/depression (especially in girls). More complete analyses indicate that the relationship is not causal — other factors are responsible. The most noticeable change is that teens really love their phones.

### What Technology Has Done (More Modestly)

**Reading from screens is slightly worse than reading from paper.** Multiple meta-analyses find a small but consistent disadvantage to reading comprehension from screens vs. print. The gap may be closing as people habituate to screen reading, but students and teachers should be aware of it.

**Multitasking has a real cost.** Students who split attention between digital devices and academic tasks perform worse on the academic tasks. The popular notion that young people are effective multitaskers is not supported — no one is good at processing two streams of information simultaneously. What feels like multitasking is usually rapid switching, and each switch carries a cost.

**Sleep disruption from devices.** Having digital devices accessible in the bedroom at night is associated with significant sleep loss. This is a genuine concern: sleep is important for consolidation of learning.

**Attention and boredom:** The change teachers observe — that students seem more easily bored and distracted — may be real, but it's probably not a decreased ability to attend. It's a decreased willingness to tolerate boredom, because students now always have access to instant entertainment. The "skill" that may be most in demand in the 21st century is the ability to deploy patient, alert vigilance.

### Questions to Ask About New Technology

Before adopting a new educational technology, Willingham suggests asking:

1. **Is there good reason to be an early adopter?** By "early" he means before published data or detailed feedback from trusted educators exist. Does it make sense to wait until others have tried it?

2. **How confident am I that I can guess the impact on my students?** Technology with a single, narrow purpose (a visualizer for geometric shapes) is easier to predict than technology with broad, flexible applications (a tablet).

3. **When new technology replaces old, something is sacrificed. What is it?** Overhead projectors gained neatness and reproducibility; they lost the ability to build a lesson across the board so students could refer back to earlier parts.

4. **Make a plan for evaluation.** Be specific: What are we hoping to change? How will we know whether it has changed? By when should the change occur? What will we do if it does (or doesn't) change?

### Implications for the Classroom

**Address equity first.** The digital divide no longer means only access to hardware — it means reliable Internet access at home. When assigning work requiring digital access, be aware that students have unequal access. Provide hardware with enough internal storage to function without the Internet when possible.

**Start with the problem, not the technology.** The most common mistake in school technology adoption is being given a tool and asking "How can we use this?" instead of asking "What problems are we trying to solve?" Start with instructional challenges; then look for solutions.

**Use technology to support students with disabilities generously.** The risk of over-providing tech support is that students practice important skills less. The risk of under-providing is that students fall further behind and come to see school as a place where they are repeatedly asked to do things others find easy. Err on the side of too much support.

**Have a consistent, consistently-enforced acceptable use policy.** The policy that causes most problems leaves individual teachers to decide whether personal devices are allowed. A student who knows others can access their phones but they cannot is at a constant disadvantage (distracted by wondering what they're missing). Whatever the policy, make it consistent.

**Consider practicing sustained attention deliberately.** Jennifer Roberts at Harvard assigned students to visit a museum, select a single artwork, and observe it for three full hours taking notes. The time is striking — seemingly excessive — but students discover they continue to notice new things well beyond when they thought they would. Something similar may be valuable for students who have become expert at quickly moving on.

**Educate parents.** Many parents want help setting limits on screen time but feel uncertain or alone. Teachers can provide practical strategies, research-based information about multitasking and sleep, and encouragement to coordinate with other parents (consistency across households is valuable).

## Chapter 10: What About My Mind? {#chapter-10}

### The Cognitive Principle

> **Teaching, like any complex cognitive skill, must be practiced to be improved.**

### Teaching Is a Cognitive Skill

Everything said about students' minds applies to teachers' minds. Teaching requires:

- **Working memory** to hold the lesson's structure, monitor student understanding, track multiple students simultaneously, and adjust instruction in real time
- **Factual knowledge** about the subject matter and about pedagogy
- **Procedural knowledge** — automatized routines for classroom management, questioning techniques, and transitions

Novice teachers are overwhelmed by the cognitive demands of teaching because they have not yet automatized basic classroom routines. Everything takes working-memory capacity that should be available for monitoring and adjusting. Expert teachers have automatized those routines and can focus attention on instruction and student understanding.

**The self-observation problem:** Because teaching is so cognitively demanding, teachers often can't observe themselves while teaching. Working memory is fully occupied by instruction; there's no capacity left for observation. This makes improvement difficult: you can't easily observe the aspects of your teaching that need work.

### Deliberate Practice for Teachers

Expert performance in any domain is built through deliberate practice — goal-directed practice that specifically targets the skills to be improved, receives feedback, and adjusts based on that feedback.

For teachers, an effective program of deliberate practice involves:

1. **Identify a specific aspect of teaching to improve.** Not "be a better teacher" but something specific: better questioning techniques, more effective transitions, clearer explanations of abstract concepts.

2. **Seek feedback.** You cannot improve what you cannot observe. Video yourself teaching. Review recordings with a trusted colleague. The colleague's outside perspective reveals things invisible to you from the inside.

3. **Compare your teaching to a model.** What does skillful execution of the target skill look like?

4. **Plan your next lesson to target the specific improvement goal.** Deliberate practice means practice structured around improvement, not just doing the skill repeatedly.

5. **Repeat with new video and feedback.**

### Lower-Investment Alternatives

**Keep a teaching diary.** Record what you intended to do and how you thought it went. Did the lesson basically work? If not, why not? Periodically review past entries to look for patterns — which types of lessons work, which frustrate you, which excite you. Tips for maintaining it: write at a consistent time of day; write something every day even if brief; don't worry about quality; be honest in both praise and criticism of yourself.

**Start a discussion group with fellow teachers.** Monthly or semi-monthly, in person. Two purposes: social support (sharing successes and frustrations) and problem-solving (bringing classroom challenges to the group for ideas). Be explicit from the start about which purpose the group serves.

**Observe your class while someone else teaches.** When you're responsible for a class, you cannot truly observe your students — working memory is too occupied with instruction. Have a trusted colleague take your class for a period while you observe. Focus on student behavior: who talks differently, who shows different patterns of attention, what does the guest do differently and how do students react?

**Observe children you don't know.** Go to a park (for younger children) or a coffee shop or skatepark (for teenagers) and simply watch children in the age group you teach, acting naturally without a classroom relationship. You will notice things about their social dynamics, communication patterns, and thinking that you don't see in the formalized classroom context.

**"Sneak up on it":** Practice skills that aren't directly teaching but support teaching. If you feel inflexible, take an improv theater class. If your visual aids lack verve, study graphic design. If your lessons feel plodding, study stage magic (the creation and release of suspense). If you want to give students more autonomy, study leadership and empowerment. Every skill that strengthens a component of teaching can improve teaching overall.

## Conclusion {#conclusion}

### The Central Insight

Reynolds Price's advice to writers: proceed on the assumption that what the reader really wants to do is drop the book and turn on the television. The implication is not that writers should pander, but that they must continuously earn the reader's attention.

The same is true of teaching. The teacher tries to guide students' thoughts down a particular path. But students can't simply choose to follow — their minds have their own wishes when it comes to the focus of attention. Students may have the best intentions and still find their minds wandering.

**Teaching is an act of persuasion.** To persuade students to follow you, you must keep them interested. To keep them interested, you must anticipate their reactions. To anticipate their reactions, you must know them.

**"Know your students"** — this sounds obvious, but cognitive science gives it specific content. There are particular things you should know about your students, and particular actions you can take based on that knowledge.

## Key Concepts Glossary {#glossary}

**Automaticity:** The state in which a mental process operates so efficiently, through extensive practice, that it requires almost no working-memory capacity. Automatic processes happen very quickly, are triggered by stimuli without conscious decision, and operate below the threshold of awareness. Reading words and retrieving practiced math facts are examples in educational contexts.

**Background knowledge:** The information stored in long-term memory about a topic. Background knowledge enables: vocabulary comprehension; bridging logical gaps in prose; chunking (increasing effective working memory capacity); disambiguation of ambiguous sentences; and recognition of familiar problem patterns without needing to reason from scratch.

**Chunking:** The process of mentally grouping multiple pieces of information into a single unit based on familiarity or meaning. Three letters (C, N, N) that together form a familiar acronym function as one chunk in working memory instead of three. Chunking increases the effective capacity of working memory because capacity is limited by the number of meaningful units, not the number of raw information pieces.

**Cognitive principle:** In Willingham's framework, a fundamental truth about how the mind works that is stable across contexts (true in the laboratory and in the classroom), supported by extensive evidence, and has implications for classroom practice that teachers might not already know.

**Deliberate practice:** Goal-directed, often solitary practice specifically structured to improve performance in a targeted skill. Distinguished from mere repetition by: (1) it targets a specific aspect of the skill rather than the whole skill; (2) it requires feedback to know whether improvement is occurring; (3) it often involves activities that don't directly exercise the target skill but build underlying capabilities. The basis of expertise development (Ericsson).

**Deep knowledge:** Richly interconnected understanding of a subject. A student with deep knowledge can apply the concept in many different contexts, explain it in different ways, reason about how the system would change if one element changed, and answer "what-if" questions. Contrasted with shallow knowledge (understanding limited to the specific context of initial learning) and rote knowledge (no understanding, only surface-level reproduction of words).

**Deep structure:** The abstract principle or pattern underlying a problem, contrasted with the surface structure (the specific content, characters, or materials used in the problem). Experts see deep structure; novices see surface structure. The ability to see deep structure enables transfer of learning to new situations.

**Desirable difficulty:** (Referenced implicitly) A learning challenge that slows initial acquisition but produces stronger, more durable, and more transferable learning in the long run. Willingham discusses this through the value of practice, the role of working memory effort, and the importance of retrieving material after some forgetting.

**Elaboration:** The process of finding additional layers of meaning in new material — expressing it in your own words, connecting it to what you already know, finding metaphors, generating examples. Elaboration deepens encoding and multiplies retrieval cues. The opposite of rote memorization.

**Fixed mindset:** The belief that intellectual ability is an innate, fixed quality — you either have it or you don't. Leads to avoiding challenging tasks (to protect the appearance of natural ability), interpreting failure as evidence of inability, and reduced persistence. (Dweck)

**Flynn effect:** The sustained rise in average IQ scores in every industrialized nation since standardized sampling began in 1932. In the United States, average IQ has risen approximately 18 points in 60 years. Evidence that environmental factors can substantially influence measured intelligence across populations.

**Fourth-grade slump:** The documented phenomenon where students from underprivileged homes often read at grade level through Grade 3, then fall behind in Grade 4. Explained by the shift from decoding-focused to comprehension-focused reading instruction and assessment — and comprehension depends on background knowledge that privileged children acquire more readily through richer home environments.

**g (general intelligence):** A statistical factor that emerges from correlational analyses of cognitive tests. People who score well on one type of cognitive task tend to score well on others — g captures this shared variance. g predicts academic and workplace success. Most researchers consider g a real and important construct, while also acknowledging that specific abilities (spatial, verbal, etc.) add additional predictive power beyond g alone.

**Generation effect:** The finding that generating a response (even an incorrect one) produces better memory than passively receiving information. Struggling to recall or produce an answer, even unsuccessfully, strengthens the memory trace for the correct answer when it is later supplied.

**Growth mindset:** The belief that intelligence and ability can grow through effort, good strategies, and help from others. Leads to embracing challenges, interpreting failure as useful feedback, and persisting in the face of difficulty. Associated with higher achievement and greater long-term resilience. (Dweck)

**Learning styles:** The popular theory that people have a preferred modality (visual, auditory, kinesthetic, etc.) through which they learn best, and that matching instruction to this preference improves learning. **Not supported by empirical research.** Distinguishing students by their preferred modality for receiving information may reflect real preferences, but those preferences do not predict which instructional format will produce better learning.

**Long-term memory:** The vast storehouse of knowledge outside of conscious awareness. Contains factual knowledge (declarative memory), procedural knowledge (how to perform mental and physical tasks), and episodic memory (personal experiences). Information resides here until activated by a cue, whereupon it enters working memory.

**Memory is the residue of thought:** Willingham's central formulation for how memory works. What you remember is what you thought about. Not what you intended to remember, not what you saw or heard, not what you repeated — but what you actually thought about, and specifically which *aspect* of the material you thought about.

**Metacognition:** Thinking about your own thinking; monitoring and regulating your own cognitive processes. Includes knowing what you know and don't know, evaluating whether a strategy is working, and adjusting your approach accordingly. Teaching metacognitive strategies (e.g., "predict what you'll read before you begin") is helpful but not sufficient — applying metacognitive strategies still requires domain-specific background knowledge.

**Mnemonic devices:** Memory tricks that help remember material when it has little intrinsic meaning. Common types: peg-word method, method of loci, link method, acronyms, first-letter methods, songs and rhythms. Mnemonics don't create understanding — they create retrieval cues that help you access material you've already (minimally) learned. Most useful for memorizing arbitrary material before deeper understanding is possible.

**Multiple intelligences:** Howard Gardner's theory proposing at least eight distinct forms of intelligence (logical-mathematical, spatial, linguistic, kinesthetic, musical, interpersonal, intrapersonal, naturalistic). Valuable for expanding the concept of human ability; not validated as a basis for differentiating instructional methods. Gardner acknowledges that assessing a person's mix of intelligences is "more an art than a science."

**Procedural knowledge:** Knowledge of how to do mental tasks — the "recipes" that guide cognitive operations. Distinct from factual knowledge. Procedural knowledge for multiplication tells you the sequence of steps; factual knowledge supplies the individual products (8 × 7 = 56). Both are stored in long-term memory; both can become automatic through practice.

**Rote knowledge:** Reproduction of the surface form (exact words, phrases) of material without any underlying understanding. Distinguished from shallow knowledge (some understanding, but limited and context-specific). Willingham argues rote knowledge is less common than teachers fear; shallow knowledge is more common.

**Shallow knowledge:** Understanding that is limited to the specific context in which the material was learned. A student with shallow knowledge of government knows that "government is like a classroom because both have rules," but cannot say how they differ, cannot extend the principle to new examples, and cannot answer "what-if" questions. The understanding is tied to the specific analogy or example through which it was introduced.

**Story structure / the four Cs:** A framework for organizing information based on the natural structure of stories. The four elements: Causality (events are causally linked), Conflict (a main character pursuing a goal faces obstacles), Complications (subproblems arise in pursuit of the main goal), Character (revealed through action, not description). Organizing lesson plans around this structure helps students understand and remember by engaging the mechanisms that make stories cognitively compelling.

**Transfer of learning:** The ability to apply knowledge learned in one context to a different context. Transfer is notoriously difficult because the mind tends to store knowledge tied to the specific surface features of the situation in which it was learned, rather than to the underlying abstract principle. Exposure to the same abstract principle in many different surface contexts, combined with explicit identification of the abstract principle, improves transfer.

**Working memory:** The cognitive workspace where thinking actually occurs — where information from the environment and long-term memory are combined in new ways. Working memory is roughly synonymous with consciousness: what you're aware of is what's in working memory. Limited in capacity: you can only hold a limited number of meaningful units simultaneously. This limitation is the fundamental bottleneck of human cognition.
