# Carol Dweck — Mindset: The New Psychology of Success (Updated Edition)
### Synthesized Knowledge Base for theNORMALschool Design Team

**Source:** *Mindset: The New Psychology of Success (Updated Edition)* (2016) — Carol S. Dweck, Ph.D., Stanford University

**Purpose:** This document provides a full, mechanistic account of Dweck's mindset theory for direct application to theNORMALschool's design. Every major idea is explained from first principles — not just defined, but fully unpacked so that design decisions can be made from understanding, not surface familiarity. The updated edition's critical additions — false growth mindset, the journey to growth mindset, and organizational mindset — are given particular attention because they are the most commonly misunderstood and most directly operational.

## Part I: The Origin of the Research — What Dweck Discovered

### 1.1 The Founding Observation

Dweck began her career studying how students cope with failure. She designed a series of studies in which children were given progressively harder puzzles and observed closely. She expected to find differences in *how well* children coped — some would cope better, others worse. What she did not expect was what she found:

Some children didn't just cope with difficulty. They were *energized* by it. A ten-year-old boy rubbed his hands together, smacked his lips, and cried, "I love a challenge!" Another looked up with satisfaction and said, "You know, I was hoping this would be informative!" These children didn't think they were failing. They thought they were *learning.*

This was the founding observation of the entire research program. These children knew something that most people — including Dweck at the time — did not know: that human qualities, including intellectual skills, can be cultivated. Failure was not a verdict. It was information.

### 1.2 The Forty-Year Research Program

For over forty years, Dweck and her colleagues at Columbia University, University of Illinois, and Stanford University have studied the beliefs people hold about their own abilities — and the profound consequences of those beliefs for learning, achievement, relationships, leadership, creativity, and resilience.

The research spans:
- Thousands of children in controlled laboratory studies
- Longitudinal studies tracking students across major educational transitions
- Studies across cultures, socioeconomic groups, and demographic populations
- Intervention studies testing whether teaching the growth mindset causes measurable changes in achievement
- Adult populations in business, sports, and relationships
- Organizational-level studies of company cultures

The convergence is remarkable: across all domains, populations, and study designs, the same pattern holds. The belief a person holds about whether their abilities are fixed or developable shapes their goals, their effort, their resilience, their response to failure, and ultimately their achievement.

## Part II: The Two Mindsets — Complete Definition

### 2.1 The Fixed Mindset — Full Account

The **fixed mindset** is the belief that your basic qualities — intelligence, talent, personality, character, creativity, athleticism — are fixed traits. You have a certain amount, and that's that. These qualities are carved in stone. They can be revealed and measured, but they cannot be fundamentally developed.

This belief, even when held unconsciously, produces a specific and identifiable psychology:

**Goal orientation:** Since ability is fixed, the primary goal becomes proving you have it. Every situation becomes an evaluation: Will I succeed or fail? Will I look smart or dumb? Will I be a winner or a loser? The primary concern is how you appear, not what you learn.

**Response to challenge:** Challenges are avoided because they threaten to expose limited ability. The fixed mindset creates a preference for easy tasks where success is guaranteed over hard tasks where failure is possible. This is rational within the fixed mindset's logic: if failure reveals your permanent limits, why risk it?

**Response to effort:** Effort is seen as a bad thing. If you have ability, you shouldn't need effort. The need for effort is evidence that you don't have ability. This is why fixed-mindset people try to look effortless — appearing to try hard and still fail is the worst possible outcome.

**Response to difficulty:** When things get hard, fixed-mindset people disengage, make excuses, or find something to blame. They interpret difficulty as evidence that they lack ability. They do not push through difficulty as information about what strategies are not working.

**Response to criticism:** Criticism is received as a personal attack on fundamental worth, not as useful information about performance. It is resisted, denied, or attributed to the critic's malice. It cannot be absorbed and acted upon.

**Response to others' success:** Other people's success is a threat. If others do well, it means there's less room at the top for you — or that you are being outperformed, which in the fixed mindset means permanently outclassed.

**The deeper experience:** People with a fixed mindset in a given domain live with a persistent background anxiety: Am I smart enough? Am I talented enough? Each performance is a potential verdict. This produces a cognitive and emotional landscape dominated by self-protection rather than growth.

### 2.2 The Growth Mindset — Full Account

The **growth mindset** is the belief that your basic qualities can be developed through effort, good strategies, and help from others. You may start with a certain genetic endowment and a set of initial talents and interests, but these are just the starting point. Through dedication and hard work, your capabilities grow substantially.

This belief produces an entirely different psychology:

**Goal orientation:** Since ability can be developed, the primary goal is learning. Situations are evaluated for what can be learned from them, not for whether they will make you look smart. Growth-mindset people do not need to prove themselves — they need to develop themselves.

**Response to challenge:** Challenges are sought out because they are the mechanism of growth. Hard problems are intrinsically interesting precisely because they stretch existing capability. The ten-year-old who says "I love a challenge!" is not a curiosity — he is a fully coherent growth-mindset person.

**Response to effort:** Effort is the engine of growth. Without effort, ability does not develop. Hard work is not a sign of low ability but the path to high ability. Growth-mindset people believe that effort is what makes you smart and talented, not what reveals that you aren't.

**Response to difficulty:** Difficulty means you've encountered the edge of current capability — the exact place where development happens. Growth-mindset people push through, try new strategies, seek help, and persist. They understand that the current inability to do something means they can't do it *yet*.

**Response to criticism:** Criticism is information about what needs to improve. It can be absorbed, evaluated, and acted upon. It is not a verdict on worth but feedback on performance.

**Response to others' success:** Other people's success is inspiring and instructive. How did they do it? What can be learned from their approach? What they achieve is evidence of what's possible.

**The deeper experience:** Growth-mindset people experience challenge, difficulty, and setback differently — not as threats to identity but as the texture of genuine learning. They can be disappointed without being devastated, frustrated without giving up, confused without concluding they're incapable.

### 2.3 The Two Mindsets Are Not Personalities

A critical clarification that Dweck emphasizes: the two mindsets are not personality types that people either have or don't have. They are beliefs — beliefs that can differ by domain, change over time, be triggered by circumstances, and be deliberately cultivated.

Several important nuances:

**Domain specificity.** A person can hold a growth mindset about their artistic ability and a fixed mindset about their mathematical ability. Mindset is not a global trait; it applies to specific domains and can vary across them.

**Triggering.** Even people who generally hold a growth mindset can be triggered into fixed-mindset thinking by specific situations — high-stakes evaluations, comparisons to people perceived as much more talented, public failures. A growth mindset is not a permanent state but a set of beliefs that need to be actively maintained.

**Mixed mindsets.** Almost everyone holds a mixture of both. The goal is not to achieve a pure growth mindset (which may not exist) but to move the balance toward growth, especially in the domains that matter most, and to recognize and respond to fixed-mindset triggers.

**Dweck's self-disclosure:** "I've been studying mindset for decades, and I still sometimes fall into the fixed mindset. When I'm criticized, I feel defensive. When I fail at something, I feel bad. The difference is that I've learned to recognize my fixed-mindset triggers and respond to them."

## Part III: The Science of the Fixed Mindset — Mechanistic Evidence

### 3.1 The Founding Praise Study (Mueller & Dweck, 1998)

The most cited and most important study in the entire research program began with a deceptively simple design. Fifth graders were given a set of moderate puzzles and told their scores. They were then praised in one of two ways:

**Praise group A** was told: "You did really well on these problems. You must be smart at this."

**Praise group B** was told: "You did really well on these problems. You must have worked really hard."

One sentence. Different in only a few words. Here is what happened:

**Round 2 — Choice.** Students were then offered a choice between an easy set of problems and a hard set. Of the students praised for intelligence (Group A), 67% chose the easy problems. Of the students praised for effort (Group B), 92% chose the hard problems. A single sentence of ability praise transformed two-thirds of children into challenge-avoiders.

**Round 3 — Hard problems.** All students were then given a difficult set of problems — intentionally designed to be hard. Group A (ability praise) interpreted struggle as evidence they weren't as smart as the praise had suggested. Group B (effort praise) interpreted struggle as a signal to try harder. This difference was visible in their emotional states, their self-talk, and their measured enjoyment of the problems.

**Round 4 — Moderate problems.** All students returned to the original difficulty level. Group A (ability praise) performed significantly worse than before the hard problems. Group B (effort praise) performed significantly better. The experience of difficulty had damaged Group A's performance and improved Group B's.

**Round 5 — Disclosure.** Students were asked to write a letter to a student in another school describing their experience, including their scores. Here is what was found: 40% of students in Group A lied about their scores — always in the upward direction. The fixed mindset had made failure so intolerable that deception became the preferred response. Almost no students in Group B lied.

**The critical finding:** A single sentence of praise — specifically, praise that attributed success to a fixed ability — was sufficient to produce all the hallmarks of the fixed mindset: challenge avoidance, reduced performance after difficulty, and willingness to deceive. It required no years of conditioning. One interaction was enough.

### 3.2 The Longitudinal Study of Junior High Transition (Blackwell, Trzesniewski, & Dweck, 2007)

Dweck and colleagues tracked hundreds of students through the transition to junior high school — a universally difficult academic transition. Students who entered junior high with a growth mindset showed a steady improvement in grades over the two-year study period. Students who entered with a fixed mindset showed a steady decline.

The mindsets were assessed at the start of 7th grade — before any grades had been received. They predicted academic trajectories two years later, controlling for prior achievement. Mindset was a stronger predictor of academic growth than prior grades.

This study also revealed a critical finding about the mechanism: fixed-mindset students, when asked their goals, primarily said "to look smart and not look dumb." Growth-mindset students primarily said "to learn and improve." The goals drove the trajectories.

### 3.3 The Intervention Study — Teaching Mindset Works

Dweck's group then designed an intervention: instead of merely measuring mindset, they would teach students the growth mindset and measure the effect on achievement.

Students in the control group received eight sessions about general study skills. Students in the intervention group also received study skills content, but in eight sessions they additionally learned:
- How the brain works as a learning organ
- How neurons form new connections when you learn
- How intelligence develops rather than being fixed
- That challenging work causes neural growth

The result: The control group continued its declining trajectory. The intervention group reversed the decline and began improving. Teachers, who did not know which students were in which group, nominated students in the intervention group as having "shown the most positive changes in motivation" at a rate that was statistically significant.

One specific example: Jimmy, described as "the most hard-core, turned-off, low-effort kid in the group," looked up with tears in his eyes during the session and said, "You mean I don't have to be dumb?" From that day on he worked — staying up late on homework, submitting work he had never bothered to complete before.

## Part IV: Deep Concepts — Effort, Strategy, and the Paradox of Talent

### 4.1 The Effort Paradox

The fixed mindset creates a devastating paradox around effort:

In the fixed mindset, effort is evidence of low ability. Smart people don't need to try — they just *are* smart. Therefore, trying hard (especially if you still fail) is the worst possible signal you can send. It means you don't have what it takes.

This explains a phenomenon that baffles many educators: why do clearly capable students stop trying? Why do students who could produce excellent work choose to submit mediocre or incomplete work instead? The answer, from the fixed mindset, is rational: if you don't try, you haven't revealed your limits. You can always say, "I could have done better if I'd tried." Effort removes this protection.

This is what Dweck calls the **low-effort syndrome** — and it is not laziness or indifference. It is a psychologically coherent self-protection strategy within the logic of the fixed mindset.

For the growth mindset, the paradox is reversed: effort is not evidence of low ability but the *mechanism* of growing ability. Hard work is what makes you capable. The growth-mindset person expects to need effort, welcomes the need for it, and interprets effort as the path forward rather than a sign of deficiency.

### 4.2 The Myth of the "Natural"

One of the most consistent targets of Dweck's research is the cultural myth of the "natural" — the person who achieves great things effortlessly, whose talent is innate and simply expresses itself without struggle.

This myth is empirically false, and Dweck documents it case by case:

**Thomas Edison** is mythologized as a lone genius who invented things by sudden inspiration. In reality: he had thirty assistants, worked in a corporate-funded state-of-the-art laboratory, and the lightbulb was not one invention but a network of time-consuming inventions each requiring chemists, mathematicians, physicists, engineers, and glassblowers. The invention happened over years, not in a moment.

**Michael Jordan** was cut from his high school varsity basketball team. He became the greatest player in NBA history through obsessive practice, constant skill refinement, and a ferocious work ethic. His mother reports that practice was always first — before going out, before anything else. Former Bulls assistant coach John Bach: "If you went to the gym at 11 o'clock at night, he'd be there. If you went at three in the morning, he'd be there."

**Babe Ruth** is likewise a constructed myth. Robert Creamer, his biographer: Ruth was one of the greatest hitters ever, but not because he was a natural. He was a thinker. He studied pitchers obsessively. "He could experiment at the plate" — constantly adjusting his approach based on what he learned.

**Charles Darwin** took years of fieldwork, hundreds of discussions with colleagues and mentors, multiple preliminary drafts, and half a lifetime of dedication before publishing The Origin of Species.

**Mozart** is perhaps the most mythologized creative figure in Western culture. The reality: musicologist Robert Weisberg's analysis shows that Mozart's early works, composed before substantial training and practice, are not considered great music. His later masterworks emerged after years of deep immersion in the musical tradition, constant compositional practice, and critical engagement with peers.

The pattern is consistent: **what looks like natural genius is almost always the accumulated result of deliberate practice, strategic effort, and sustained engagement with challenge.**

The cultural myth of the natural does harm in two ways:
1. It discourages ordinary people from pursuing domains where they don't immediately excel (since "naturals" do it effortlessly)
2. It gives talented people a false framework that makes them fragile — they believe their excellence is innate and therefore cannot cope with difficulty or failure

### 4.3 Effort Alone Is Not Enough — The Role of Strategy

This is one of the most important clarifications in the updated edition, and the one most frequently missed in the application of Dweck's work:

**Effort without strategy is not sufficient for growth.** A person who repeatedly applies the same ineffective approach with great effort will not improve. The growth mindset is not merely about working harder — it is about working smarter, seeking feedback, trying new approaches, asking for help, and responding to evidence about what is and is not working.

Dweck: "Process includes more than just effort. Certainly we want children to appreciate the fruits of hard work. But we also want them to understand the importance of trying new strategies when the one they're using isn't working. And we want them to ask for help or input from others when it's needed. This is the process we want them to appreciate: hard work, trying new strategies, and seeking input from others."

The full growth-mindset process formula:
**Hard work + Good strategies + Input from others + Persistence through setbacks = Growth**

Effort is necessary but not sufficient. And critically: when a student is working hard but not making progress, the correct response is not to praise their effort and move on. It is to figure out why the effort is not working and find better strategies.

Dweck: "What keeps me up at night is the fear that the mindset concept will be used to make kids feel good when they're not learning — just like the failed self-esteem movement. The growth mindset is meant to help kids learn, not to paper over the fact that they are not learning."

## Part V: The Praise Problem — The Exact Mechanism of Damage

### 5.1 Why Praising Intelligence Backfires

The praise study (Part III) revealed something counterintuitive that has profound practical implications: praising children for being smart — even sincerely, lovingly, with good intentions — damages their learning.

The mechanism is clear: when a child is praised for being smart, they learn that looking smart is what is valued. The next time they face a challenge that might threaten this identity (a hard problem, a difficult task, any situation where they might not look smart), they have reason to avoid it. Their goal becomes maintaining the label, not developing the capability.

This creates what Dweck calls the "praise trap." The more you praise a child for intelligence, the more the child becomes oriented around proving their intelligence, which paradoxically makes them less willing to take the intellectual risks that lead to genuine development.

From a parent who wrote to Dweck:
> "My son, a fifth grader, consistently scores in the 99th percentile on standardized tests but has had very real 'self-worth' problems. My husband felt his parents never valued intellect and has overcompensated by praising our son for 'being smart.' I have suspected this was causing a problem, because my son, while he easily excels in school, is reluctant to take on more difficult work or projects — just as your studies show — because then he might not be smart. He projects an over-inflated view of his abilities but will not attempt challenging activities, because in his failure he would be shattered."

And from a Columbia student reflecting on his own history:
> "I remember often being praised for my intelligence rather than my efforts, and slowly but surely I developed an aversion to difficult challenges. This was my greatest learning disability — this tendency to see performance as a reflection of character and, if I could not accomplish something right away, to avoid that task or treat it with contempt."

### 5.2 What to Praise Instead — And How

Process praise is the evidence-based alternative to ability praise. The key principles:

**Praise the process, tied to the outcome.** Effective praise names the specific process the child used AND connects it to what was achieved. Not: "You're so smart." Not: "You worked hard." But: "You tried several different strategies on that problem until you found one that worked — and that's exactly what led to your success. That's called working smart."

Specific formulations that work:
- "You really studied for your test and your improvement shows it. You read the material over several times, you outlined it, and you tested yourself on it. It really worked!"
- "I like the way you tried all kinds of strategies on that math problem until you finally got it. You thought of a lot of different ways to do it and found the one that worked!"
- "I know school used to be easy for you and you used to feel like the smart kid all the time. But the truth is you weren't using your brain to the fullest. I'm really excited about how you're stretching yourself now and working to learn hard things."

**You can celebrate great performance.** A common misreading of Dweck's work: many people think growth mindset means you can only praise struggle, not success. This is wrong. Wonderful accomplishments absolutely merit celebration — but they should be tied to the process: "You did brilliantly on that project — and that came from the months of research you put in, the three drafts you wrote, and the way you listened to feedback. That work produced that result."

**Showing interest is as powerful as praising.** "Tell me about how you approached that problem" can be more powerful than any praise. Genuine curiosity about the child's process communicates that the process is what matters.

**Never praise a process that wasn't there.** If a child didn't actually try hard, praising their effort is dishonest and teaches them nothing useful. The praise must be accurate.

### 5.3 The Gifted and Talented Labeling Problem

"Gifted" and "talented" labels are among the most damaging applications of the fixed mindset in schools. When children are identified as gifted or talented, they receive the message: you have special, innate abilities. This message, however well-intentioned, produces the same fixed-mindset dynamics as ability praise:

- Gifted-labeled children become worried about maintaining the label
- They avoid challenges that might reveal them as less gifted than labeled
- They interpret difficulty as evidence that they don't actually belong in the gifted category
- Many "gifted" students perform worse in college, where they encounter peers of similar ability for the first time, than their measured potential would predict — because they have never developed the growth-mindset skills of coping with challenge

Ellen Winner's research (cited by Dweck): gifted children often share an "intense concentration and persistence" rather than "speed and ease of learning." What looks like natural gift is often extraordinary, sustained effort from a very young age.

The appropriate response to early signs of exceptional ability is not to label the child as gifted but to nurture the passion, the persistence, and the love of learning that will allow the ability to develop fully.

## Part VI: Mindsets in Schools — Design Implications

### 6.1 The Fixed-Mindset Classroom

In a fixed-mindset classroom, whether intentionally or not, the following messages are transmitted:

- There are smart students and not-smart students, and this is fixed
- The goal of school is to prove which category you're in
- Easy tasks are better than hard ones because they let you look smart without risk
- Effort is for those who aren't naturally capable
- Mistakes are failures to be avoided rather than information to be used
- Your grade tells you how smart you are as a person

These messages are often transmitted not through explicit statements but through:
- Seating students in ability order (as Dweck's sixth-grade teacher did)
- Giving easy tasks to "protect" struggling students rather than challenging them
- Praising students for being "smart" when they answer correctly
- Expressing surprise when a "non-smart" student does well
- Accepting "I'm just not a math person" as an explanation for difficulty rather than a fixed-mindset trigger to address

### 6.2 The Growth-Mindset Classroom — Full Model

A growth-mindset classroom communicates a different set of messages consistently:

**About intelligence and ability:** "The brain is a muscle. It grows when you use it. The more you challenge it, the stronger it gets." "Intelligence is something you develop, not something you have."

**About challenge:** "We do hard things here. They fill your brain." Marva Collins, one of the most successful teachers Dweck documents, told her students on the first day: "None of you has ever failed. School may have failed you. Well, goodbye to failure, children. Welcome to success."

**About mistakes:** Mistakes are necessary steps in the learning process. They provide information about what hasn't yet been understood and what strategy hasn't yet worked. Teachers in growth-mindset classrooms treat wrong answers as thinking in progress, not evidence of inadequacy.

**About effort and strategy:** "What strategies did you try? What worked? What didn't? What will you try next time?" Teachers focus on the process, not the product, and help students develop meta-awareness of their own learning process.

**About feedback:** Feedback is specific, process-focused, and actionable. "Your essay has a strong opening and a clear argument, but the evidence in the middle section doesn't fully support your claim. What additional evidence could you find, or how could you frame the claim more specifically?"

### 6.3 The Power of "Yet"

One of the simplest and most powerful interventions Dweck documents: changing the word "can't" to "can't yet."

"I can't do this problem" → "I can't do this problem yet."
"I don't understand this" → "I don't understand this yet."
"I'm not good at writing" → "I'm not good at writing yet."

The word "yet" is not a platitude. It is a substantive claim about the nature of ability: that the current state is a developmental stage, not a permanent condition. It opens a path forward where the fixed-mindset framing closes one.

A school in Chicago adopted a policy: instead of "failing" a course, students received the grade "Not Yet." The result was a fundamental shift in how students experienced academic difficulty — from permanent verdict to developmental stage.

### 6.4 Bright Girls and the Stereotype Problem

One of the most important findings in Dweck's work on gender: bright girls are more likely than bright boys to develop fixed mindsets about their intelligence, and the consequences for their development are severe.

The mechanism: from an early age, girls receive feedback that attributes their success to effort and goodness ("you're such a good student") while boys receive feedback that attributes success to natural ability. This sounds like it should produce growth mindset in girls (since they're receiving effort feedback) and fixed mindset in boys (ability feedback). The reality is more complex.

Girls, especially bright girls, also receive messages from culture and peers that their intelligence is not as expected or natural as boys' intelligence. As a result, many bright girls internalize the belief that their academic success is fragile — that they succeed through effort but that "real" smart people don't need effort. They become reluctant to take intellectual risks because they have more to lose.

Studies found that bright girls — specifically the very brightest girls — were the most likely to be hurt by the fixed mindset and the most likely to give up in the face of difficulty or complexity. The girls with the highest IQs were the most vulnerable to learned helplessness when problems became difficult.

This finding has direct design implications: girls, especially academically successful girls, may need explicit growth-mindset cultivation more urgently than is generally recognized.

### 6.5 The College Transition and the "Imposter Syndrome"

Dweck observed a striking phenomenon among graduate students at Columbia. Students admitted through an extraordinarily competitive process — with near-perfect grades and GREs, rave recommendations from eminent scholars — would arrive and within one day feel like "complete imposters."

The mechanism: they had been in environments where they were the obvious stars. Now they were surrounded by equally accomplished peers and a faculty with long lists of publications. They looked around and thought, "I can't do that." They forgot the word "yet."

This is the fixed mindset at work in high achievers: they had built their identity on being "smart" and "the best," and suddenly that identity was threatened. The appropriate response would be: "I'm here to learn how to do this, and learning takes time." The fixed-mindset response was: "If I don't already know how to do this, I must be a fraud."

The "imposter syndrome" — the feeling that you don't belong in the high-achieving environment you've entered — is, in large part, a fixed-mindset phenomenon. It can be resolved, not by reassurance, but by shifting to a growth framework in which not yet knowing is the expected state of someone who is there to learn.

### 6.6 The Low-Effort Syndrome in Adolescence

Adolescence is developmentally difficult even for the most growth-minded students, because it involves a genuine increase in academic challenge, a genuine expansion of social comparison, and a genuine development of self-awareness about competence.

For fixed-mindset adolescents, this can trigger what Dweck calls the "low-effort syndrome." When academic work becomes genuinely difficult for the first time, fixed-mindset students face a choice: work hard and risk exposing their limits, or not try and preserve the self-image of someone who could do it if they wanted to.

John Holt (cited by Dweck): "The worst student we had, the worst I have ever encountered, was in his life outside the classroom as mature, intelligent, and interesting a person as anyone at the school. What went wrong?... Somewhere along the line, his intelligence became disconnected from his schooling."

This disconnection is the fixed mindset's self-protection mechanism. The student is not stupid or lazy. The student has concluded — often correctly, given how schools are structured — that the game is about proving intelligence, that they might not be able to prove it, and that not playing is safer than losing.

**Design Implication:** Schools that define intelligence narrowly and treat academic performance as evidence of fixed ability will produce this disconnection. Schools that define intellectual growth broadly and treat effort and strategy as the mechanisms of development will keep students engaged.

## Part VII: Mindsets in Sports — The Champion Mindset

### 7.1 Natural Talent vs. Champion Mindset

Billy Beane (featured in Michael Lewis's *Moneyball*) was considered a natural — the next Babe Ruth. By sophomore year of high school he was the best player on three different sports teams. His physical talent was genuine.

But Beane had a fixed mindset. The moment things went wrong, he searched for something to break. "It wasn't merely that he didn't like to fail; it was as if he didn't know how to fail." As the game got harder, his performance collapsed. As one scout noted, "Billy was of the opinion that he should never make an out."

His teammate Lenny Dykstra had a fraction of Beane's physical gifts. But as Beane later observed: "He had no concept of failure.... And I was the opposite." Dykstra's growth mindset — his ability to treat failure as data, to keep adjusting, to stay curious about improvement — allowed him to maximize his genuine but smaller physical endowment.

Beane ultimately came to the conclusion that mindset was more important than talent. As general manager of the Oakland A's, this insight drove his revolutionary approach to baseball management: focus on what players do, not on the myth of natural talent.

### 7.2 Michael Jordan as the Growth Mindset Exemplar

Michael Jordan was cut from his high school varsity basketball team. By his own account, he cried that day. But then he did something distinctive: he got up the next day and worked harder than he'd worked before.

Jordan's career is a sustained story of growth mindset in action:
- When he didn't make varsity, he worked obsessively to make the team the next year
- When his shooting was criticized, he worked on it until it became a weapon
- When his defense was criticized, he worked on it until he became a defensive player of the year
- When he lost to the Detroit Pistons three years in a row, he studied what was defeating him and systematically changed his game to address it
- When his team couldn't beat the Pistons until he convinced himself and his teammates to believe they could — and developed the strategies to do so

Former Bulls assistant coach John Bach: "For Jordan, success stems from something internal, not external... it's wanting to be better every day."

**The core insight:** Jordan was not successful despite effort — he was successful *because* of effort. The myth of Jordan as a "natural" is precisely backward. He became the greatest through relentless, strategic, self-critical work.

## Part VIII: Mindsets in Organizations — Leadership and Culture

### 8.1 The Enron Story — Fixed Mindset at Organizational Scale

In 2001, Enron — the corporate poster child, the company of the future — collapsed. The mechanism, as Malcolm Gladwell analyzed in The New Yorker, was the "talent mindset."

McKinsey & Company had convinced American corporations that competitive success required identifying and recruiting "naturals" — people with innate talent — and paying them enormous sums. Enron became the apotheosis of this philosophy. It recruited big talent (mostly Ivy League degrees), paid them big salaries, and celebrated them for their brilliance.

The result: a culture that worshiped talent forced its employees to look and act extraordinarily talented at all times. The fixed mindset was institutionalized. And in the fixed mindset, admitting problems is admitting inadequacy. Enron's employees couldn't acknowledge when projects weren't working. Executives couldn't course-correct because doing so would mean admitting they'd been wrong. Almost 40% of employees lied about performance — because the institutional culture had made acknowledging failure intolerable.

A company that cannot self-correct cannot survive. Enron's collapse was the inevitable consequence of building a culture that required permanent proof of genius rather than genuine accountability for results.

### 8.2 The CEO Disease

Dweck documents what she calls "CEO Disease" — the progression of successful executives toward fixed-mindset leadership:

The pattern: a leader achieves success through genuine skill and effort. As they rise, they receive increasing deference, decreasing honest feedback, and cultural reinforcement of the idea that their success reflects innate superiority. Gradually, they develop an expectation of being right. They surround themselves with people who agree. They stop learning.

The result is leaders who are fragile to challenge, resistant to feedback, dismissive of alternatives, and ultimately unable to adapt when circumstances change. Their earlier genuine competence becomes encrusted in fixed-mindset protection.

The antidote: growth-mindset leaders explicitly cultivate honest feedback, actively seek out challenging perspectives, model vulnerability and continuous learning, and evaluate themselves on development — not just current performance.

### 8.3 Growth-Mindset Leadership in Practice

Dweck contrasts the CEO Disease with leaders who explicitly embodied growth-mindset principles. Common characteristics:

**Transparency about uncertainty.** Growth-mindset leaders are willing to say "I don't know" and "I was wrong" — because their identity is not staked on always being right.

**Genuine curiosity about feedback.** They actively seek out dissenting views, honest assessments, and bad news — because accurate information is the prerequisite for good decisions.

**Investment in others' development.** The growth mindset in leaders produces mentoring: they are genuinely invested in helping people around them grow, because they understand that capability is developed rather than discovered.

**Risk tolerance.** They can authorize experiments that may fail, because failure is information rather than verdict.

**Accountability.** They can acknowledge mistakes, analyze what went wrong, and change course — because correctability is a feature, not an admission of inadequacy.

### 8.4 Organizational Mindset — A New Concept (Updated Edition)

One of the additions in the updated edition is Dweck's research on organizational mindsets. The finding: entire organizations can hold a growth or fixed mindset, and this organizational mindset shapes the behavior of everyone within it.

**Fixed-mindset organizations** believe they have "the best people" — geniuses hired through competitive selection. This creates:
- A culture where appearing competent is paramount
- Reluctance to admit problems or mistakes
- Hoarding of information (sharing looks like exposing weakness)
- Internal competition rather than collaboration
- Employees who try to look good rather than perform well
- Innovation stagnation (you can't innovate without tolerating failure)

**Growth-mindset organizations** believe that their people can develop and that the organization can always improve. This creates:
- Psychological safety to acknowledge problems early
- Active mentoring and investment in development
- Information sharing and collaboration
- Willingness to try new approaches and honestly assess results
- High performance over time because the organization learns from mistakes

The implication for building an organization: culture is not created by mission statements. It is created by the actual behaviors of leaders — what they reward, what they punish, what they model, what they tolerate. A school that claims to embrace growth mindset but rewards only performance and punishes failure is a fixed-mindset school regardless of the language it uses.

## Part IX: Mindsets in Relationships

### 9.1 The Fixed Mindset in Relationships

The fixed mindset produces a specific relationship pathology: the belief that great relationships are ones where everything flows naturally — where partners are perfectly compatible, understand each other perfectly, and never have to work at things.

This is the romantic-comedy model of relationships: if you have to try, it's the wrong relationship. The right one should just work.

The consequence: the first sign of difficulty in a relationship — a misunderstanding, a conflict, growing apart — is interpreted as evidence that the relationship was wrong. Fixed-mindset people conclude they picked the wrong partner (just as they might conclude they chose the wrong career after early difficulty). The appropriate response, effort and problem-solving, feels like evidence of failure rather than the path to success.

Dweck's research found that fixed-mindset people are also more likely to respond to rejection with revenge fantasies rather than healing. When rejected, they feel "permanently labeled" — as if a verdict has been rendered about their worth. Their primary goal becomes not healing but retaliation.

### 9.2 The Growth Mindset in Relationships

Growth-mindset people approach relationships as something that is developed, not discovered. Compatibility is something you build, not something you have or don't have. Communication and understanding are skills that improve with practice and feedback.

This doesn't mean accepting abuse or ignoring fundamental incompatibility. It means that ordinary relationship difficulty — conflict, misunderstanding, changing circumstances — is treated as information about what needs to improve, not evidence that the relationship has failed.

Growth-mindset people also have a more productive response to rejection: they grieve, they analyze what happened, they consider what they could do differently, and they move forward. They do not treat rejection as a permanent verdict on their worth.

## Part X: False Growth Mindset — The Critical Update

### 10.1 What False Growth Mindset Is and Why It Matters

One of the most important additions to the updated edition is Dweck's extended treatment of "false growth mindset" — the widespread phenomenon of people believing they have a growth mindset when they do not, or implementing growth-mindset ideas incorrectly.

Dweck discovered this through her colleague Susan Mackie in Australia, who was observing educators and parents applying growth-mindset concepts in ways that didn't match the theory or produce the intended results. Once alerted, Dweck began identifying the most common misunderstandings:

### 10.2 The Three Common Misunderstandings

**Misunderstanding #1: Growth mindset = openness or flexibility.**

Many people hear "growth mindset" and conflate it with being open-minded, flexible, or positive in general. "I'm flexible and willing to learn new things — I have a growth mindset."

This misses the specific claim: growth mindset is about believing that *abilities and intelligence can be developed through dedication and hard work.* It is not about attitude or personality. It is a specific belief about the developability of ability. An open-minded, flexible person who nonetheless believes that talented people are born talented — and that they either are or aren't talented — has a fixed mindset about talent, despite their general openness.

**Misunderstanding #2: Growth mindset = praising effort regardless of outcome.**

Perhaps the most operationally damaging misunderstanding: many educators heard "growth mindset = praise effort" and began praising effort as a consolation prize when students weren't learning.

This produces several problems:
- If a student tries hard with the wrong strategy and makes no progress, praising their effort rewards the wrong thing and gives them no information about what to change
- It can feel patronizing — students can tell when effort praise is empty encouragement rather than genuine recognition of meaningful work
- It misses the actual point: the goal is not to feel good about effort but to develop capability. Effort is a means, not an end.

Dweck: "If a student has tried hard and made little or no progress, we can of course appreciate their effort, but we should never be content with effort that is not yielding further benefits. We need to figure out why that effort is not effective and guide kids toward other strategies and resources that can help them resume learning."

The corrected version: process praise that is tied to actual progress and learning. Not "you worked hard" in isolation, but "you worked hard AND it produced growth — here's specifically what that hard work achieved."

**Misunderstanding #3: Growth mindset = telling kids they can do anything.**

Many educators reported: "I've always had a growth mindset. I always tell my students, 'You can do anything!' " Dweck is direct: this is not growth mindset. It is empty reassurance.

The growth mindset is not a promise that all outcomes are possible regardless of circumstances. It is a framework for approaching development: that with the right effort, the right strategies, and the right help, capabilities can grow substantially beyond their current level. That growth is real and achievable.

"You can do anything" without accompanying skills, strategies, feedback, and support puts the onus entirely on the student ("if you can do anything and you're not succeeding, it must be your fault") and provides no actual path forward.

**The deepest misunderstanding: blaming children for having a fixed mindset.**

Dweck was distressed to learn that some educators were pointing to mindset charts and scolding children for "acting fixed-mindset." This inverts the entire purpose of the work. Adults are responsible for creating growth-mindset environments where children feel safe to fail, where effort is genuinely valued, and where development is what is measured. Blaming children for not having the growth mindset that the environment hasn't cultivated is precisely backward.

## Part XI: The Journey to a (True) Growth Mindset — A Four-Step Framework

### 11.1 Why a Journey Is Required

Growth mindset is not a belief you adopt by understanding it intellectually. It is a set of beliefs and habits that must be actively developed over time. Nearly everyone holds some mixture of growth and fixed mindsets. The goal is not to achieve purity but to move the balance and to develop the skill of recognizing and responding to fixed-mindset triggers.

The journey framework was added to the updated edition because Dweck realized many people were claiming to have a growth mindset without taking the actual steps required.

### 11.2 Step 1: Embrace Your Fixed Mindset

The first step is counterintuitive: accept that you have a fixed mindset in some domains and under some circumstances. Stop trying to present yourself as someone who already has a pure growth mindset.

Dweck: "We're all a mixture of growth and fixed mindsets and we need to acknowledge that. It's not a shameful admission. It's more like, welcome to the human race."

This matters because denial prevents the self-awareness needed for change. If you believe you already have a growth mindset, you cannot observe the moments when your fixed mindset is operating and cannot interrupt those moments. The foundation of change is honest self-observation.

### 11.3 Step 2: Identify Your Fixed-Mindset Triggers

What situations, circumstances, or comparisons push you into fixed-mindset thinking? Common triggers:

- Contemplating a big new challenge that might expose limitations
- Struggling with something after repeated attempts and hitting dead ends
- A decisive failure — losing a job, a relationship, making a significant mistake
- Encountering someone who is dramatically better than you in a domain you care about
- Being criticized or receiving feedback that challenges your self-image
- High-stakes evaluation situations where you feel assessed as a person, not just a performance

The triggers are not weaknesses to be ashamed of. They are predictable features of how human cognition works when beliefs about fixed ability are activated. Identifying them is the prerequisite for responding to them differently.

### 11.4 Step 3: Name Your Fixed-Mindset Persona

Dweck found, through her colleague Susan Mackie's work with executive groups, that giving the fixed-mindset persona a name creates valuable psychological distance.

In a workshop with financial executives, a senior leader said: "When we're in a crunch, Duane shows up. He makes me supercritical of everyone, and I get bossy and demanding rather than supportive." A female colleague responded: "Yes, and when your Duane shows up, my Ianni comes roaring out. Ianni is the macho guy who makes me feel incompetent. So your Duane brings out my Ianni and I become cowering and anxious."

This conversation — made possible by the naming — allowed the group to understand each other's triggers, adjust their interactions, and improve their working relationships and performance. The named persona is not shameful; it is a natural part of human psychology that becomes manageable once visible.

Dweck tested this approach with Stanford freshmen. She asked students to identify their fixed-mindset triggers and name their fixed-mindset persona. Not one student claimed to have no triggers. Every student was able to write eloquently — and painfully — about their persona and its impact.

### 11.5 Step 4: Educate the Persona — Take It on the Journey

Once named and recognized, the fixed-mindset persona is not to be suppressed or fought. It is to be educated. When the persona shows up:

- Acknowledge it: "I know you're here."
- Thank it for its protective intent: the fixed-mindset persona developed to keep you safe from the pain of failure.
- Explain why you're taking the risk anyway: "I understand this might not work out. But I want to try it. Can you come along with me?"
- When setbacks happen, talk to the persona: "Yes, this didn't go well. But here's what I learned and here's what I'm going to try next."

Over time, with repeated engagement, the persona learns that its catastrophic predictions don't materialize — that challenge can be engaged without existential threat, that failure can be survived and learned from, that effort produces results.

The growth mindset is not a destination but a direction of travel. The fixed-mindset persona never disappears entirely. But with practice, it shows up less often, is recognized more quickly when it does, and has less power to derail growth.

## Part XII: Applications to theNORMALschool — Design Principles

### 12.1 Assessment as Growth Information, Not Verdict

The fixed mindset is most powerfully triggered by assessment systems that evaluate rather than inform. Grades, test scores, and rankings all carry the implicit message: this is a measure of your intelligence. This is what you are.

A growth-mindset assessment system treats all assessment as information about the current state of learning and guidance for what comes next. Assessment communicates: "Here is where you are. Here is what needs to develop. Here is how to develop it." Not: "Here is your category."

This has implications for how feedback is delivered, how portfolios are structured, how progress is communicated, and what students are asked to do with assessment results.

### 12.2 Failure as Expected and Valued

A school that only celebrates success is a fixed-mindset school, regardless of other design features. A growth-mindset school treats failure as:
- Expected in genuine learning (if you never fail, you're not being challenged)
- Informative (failure reveals what hasn't yet been mastered and what strategies haven't worked)
- Manageable (failure can be survived, analyzed, and responded to)
- Prerequisite (meaningful success requires having failed, analyzed, and grown)

This must be embedded in culture through consistent teacher language, consistent assessment design, and consistent celebration of productive failure — not just polite tolerance of it.

### 12.3 Language in Every Interaction

Mindset is transmitted in the smallest linguistic choices. Dweck's research shows that the difference between "You're so smart!" and "You worked really hard on that!" produces measurable differences in subsequent behavior. This means every educator in the school must understand the language of growth mindset and use it consistently.

Key language principles:
- Comment on process, not ability
- When students succeed: connect the success to the specific process that produced it
- When students struggle: ask about strategies, not about capability
- Use "yet" to reframe limitations as developmental stages
- Express genuine curiosity about students' thinking and approach
- Never express surprise that a "non-smart" student did well
- Never express comfort when a "smart" student plays it safe

### 12.4 Challenge as the Default Setting

A growth-mindset school designs challenge as the default, not the exception. Easy tasks that guarantee success produce the signal (in the fixed mindset) that you're smart — briefly — but no development. Hard tasks that require real effort and involve genuine risk of failure are where growth actually occurs.

This means:
- Curriculum is designed to be at the edge of students' current capabilities, not comfortably within them
- Students are taught to seek challenge, not avoid it
- The culture treats challenge-avoidance as the problem, not as prudent self-protection
- Teachers don't "protect" struggling students from hard work — they provide the scaffolding that makes hard work productive

### 12.5 Teachers as Growth-Mindset Models

The most powerful growth-mindset curriculum is the teacher's own relationship to challenge, failure, and learning — modeled visibly in front of students. When teachers:
- Acknowledge their own uncertainty and limitations
- Try new teaching approaches and reflect honestly on what worked
- Show excitement about learning something new alongside their students
- Demonstrate that being an expert doesn't mean always knowing the answer

...they are teaching the growth mindset through the most powerful educational medium: authentic human example.

### 12.6 The Structural Danger of Gifted-and-Talented Tracking

Tracking students into "gifted" and "non-gifted" programs is one of the most powerful institutional transmitters of the fixed mindset. Both tracks receive damaging messages:

"Gifted" students learn that they have a special, innate trait to protect — producing fragility, challenge avoidance, and collapse under difficulty.

"Non-gifted" students learn that they have been assessed and found wanting — producing disengagement, low-effort syndrome, and resignation.

A growth-mindset school provides differentiated challenge (different students need different difficulty levels) without the fixed-mindset labeling that creates caste. The distinction: "You're ready for harder work in this area" vs. "You're in the smart group."
