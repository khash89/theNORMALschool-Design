## Introduction: Two Climate Crises

### The Mechanistic View of Human Systems

- Western thought has been dominated for 300+ years by mechanistic metaphors (the mind as a computer, organizations as machines with interchangeable parts).
- These metaphors are outdated and destructive. Human organizations are **organisms**, not mechanisms — made up of living people driven by feelings, relationships, and motives, not mechanical functions.
- The natural history museum displays butterflies pinned separately by type and size, but nature doesn't work this way. Creatures exist in complicated, interdependent ecosystems. Human communities are the same.

### The Environmental Crisis and the Human Resources Crisis

- Rachel Carson's *Silent Spring* (1962) revealed how industrial-age thinking about nature as an "infinite warehouse" of resources had damaged ecosystems through pollution and species extinction.
- Geologists suggest we've entered the "Anthropocene age" — a new geological era defined by human impact on Earth's geology and natural systems.
- A parallel crisis exists in **human resources**. Hundreds of millions rely on prescription drugs for depression; suicide is the third-highest cause of death for ages 15–30; addiction and self-inflicted illness are rising.
- Urban overcrowding (from 3% in 1750 to ~50% by 2000) compounds both crises. The world population doubled from 3 billion (1970) to 6 billion (1999) in 30 years; further growth to 9 billion is projected.

### Why Education Perpetuates the Mechanistic Model

- Western schools teach subjects in isolation (separate rooms for butterflies, beetles, spiders) rather than showing connections.
- The hierarchy of subjects reflects industrial-age values: math, science, language at the top; arts at the bottom.
- Standardized testing narrows what counts as intelligence and drains creativity from students.
- Schools prepare students for jobs that may not exist, using 18th/19th-century models designed for the Industrial Revolution.

### The Metaphor Shift Required

- A living organism (plant) is complex and dynamic; internal processes affect each other holistically. Farmers don't make plants grow — they provide the conditions for growth.
- Humans need the same: the right conditions in schools, workplaces, and communities for individuals to flourish.
- People flourish when they develop their unique aptitudes and passions. This is the foundation of both individual fulfillment and cultural health.

### Death Valley as Metaphor

- Death Valley lies dormant (2 inches rain/year) but contains seeds waiting for conditions. After 7 inches of rain in 2004–5, the entire valley burst into bloom with wildflowers.
- Humans are the same: dormant without the right conditions, alive when they find them. Discovery of one's Element is essential.

### Jonas Salk's Observation

- The biologist Jonas Salk noted a paradox: if all insects disappeared, other life would end in 50 years (they're essential to ecosystems). If humans disappeared, other life would flourish in 50 years (we've become the problem).
- Our capacity for imagination has produced extraordinary achievements, but our imagination is now failing us. We think too narrowly about ourselves and too little about consequences.
- **Core challenge**: Develop imagination and creativity within a framework of human purpose, aiming high rather than settling.

## Chapter 1: The Element

### The Three Epiphany Stories

#### Gillian Lynne: The Dancer

- At age 8, Gillian was a "disaster" in school: late assignments, terrible handwriting, poor test scores, constantly fidgeting and distracting others.
- School recommended she attend special education. Her mother took her to a psychologist for assessment.
- The psychologist observed her while questioning her mother, then left the room with her mother. Before stepping out, he turned on the radio.
- Watching through a window, the adults saw Gillian immediately spring up and move gracefully with the music. The psychologist said: "Gillian isn't sick. She's a dancer. Take her to a dance school."
- Her mother enrolled her. Gillian later said: "I walked into a room full of people like me — people who couldn't sit still. People who had to move to think."
- She trained, auditioned for Royal Ballet School, joined Royal Ballet Company as a soloist, later formed her own musical theater company producing hits in London and New York.
- She worked with Andrew Lloyd Webber on *Cats* and *The Phantom of the Opera*, becoming one of the world's most accomplished choreographers.
- The key: Someone recognized her true aptitude and gave her permission to be who she was, rather than medicate her into compliance.

#### Matt Groening: The Cartoonist

- Matt was a good student, but bored. He drew constantly in class (so skillfully the teacher didn't notice). Art class was his only outlet.
- In high school, he painted 30 paintings per class, stopping only when he ran out of paper. He wasn't interested in technical mastery but in storytelling and humor.
- He found inspiration in artists who couldn't draw well but had distinctive style and inventive stories (James Thurber, John Lennon, Robert Crumb).
- Teachers and even his cartoonist father discouraged him, suggesting "real" careers. He attended a non-traditional college without grades or required classes — where only one teacher inspired him.
- He refused to accept the path others prescribed. As a kid he told himself: "I knew I was going to be doing this for the rest of my life." He watched classmates "peel off" into serious careers, but he kept making comics and movies with friends who shared his interests.
- He decided to "live by my wits," expecting to work a menial job (a tire warehouse, he imagined) while drawing cartoons on breaks.
- In LA, his comic strip *Life in Hell* was published in *L.A. Weekly*. Fox asked him to create short animated segments for the *Tracey Ullman Show*. In the pitch meeting, he invented *The Simpsons* on the spot.
- The show ran 19+ years, generating movies, books, toys, and a pop culture empire.
- The lesson: **Success came because he ignored those who told him to pursue a "real" career and listened to his own inner compass.**

#### Paul Samuelson: The Economist

- Paul was a high school student when he walked into a University of Chicago economics lecture hall and experienced what he called a "born again" moment.
- The lecture on Malthus's theory of population dynamics struck him as profoundly simple and clear. He sensed (incorrectly, he later noted) that he was missing the deeper complexity.
- That lecture began a lifetime of economics. He became a MIT professor, president of the International Economic Association, author of the best-selling economics textbook of all time, winner of the Nobel Prize in Economics (1970, the first American to win).
- He describes his career as "pure fun" and emphasizes: "Never underestimate the vital importance of finding early in life the work that for you is play. This turns possible underachievers into happy warriors."

### The Common Thread

- All three found high achievement and satisfaction upon discovering what they naturally do well *and* what ignites their passion.
- These are "epiphany stories" — moments that divide life into before and after, giving direction and purpose.
- **The Element** = the meeting point where **what you love to do** and **what you're good at** intersect.
- Most people have not found their Element. Most lack understanding of their own capacities.

### Three Limitations in Self-Understanding

1. **Limited understanding of capacity range**: We use only a fraction of our powers (imagination, intelligence, feeling, intuition, physical awareness, spiritual awareness).
2. **Lack of holistic understanding**: We treat mind, body, feeling, and relationships as independent systems rather than as integrated whole (organic) systems.
3. **False assumption of linear decline**: People believe life is linear, capacities decline with age, and missed opportunities are gone forever. This is false — humans have constant potential for renewal.

### Why Education Fails to Reveal the Element

#### One Size Does Not Fit All

- Many brilliant, creative people didn't do well in school and only discovered their talents *after* leaving education.
- Examples: Paul McCartney (turned down for Liverpool Cathedral choir; teachers said he had no musical talent); Elvis Presley (barred from school glee club); John Cleese (excellent student, but teachers saw no sense of humor).
- These weren't isolated cases. Many people graduate unsure of their real talents, feeling what they're good at isn't valued by schools.
- **The problem isn't individual teachers** (many are brilliant). It's the basic culture and mindset of public education, which is worsening.

#### Three Features of Failing Education Systems

1. **Preoccupation with narrow academic ability**: Schools emphasize critical analysis and reasoning in words and numbers, ignoring other forms of intelligence.
2. **Hierarchy of subjects**: Math and science at the top; humanities in the middle; arts at the bottom. Even music and visual arts rank higher than theater and dance. Many schools are cutting arts entirely.
3. **Reliance on standardized tests**: Narrow tests create high-pressure performance culture and discourage risk-taking and creativity.

#### Why These Features Exist

- Mass education systems were designed in the 18th–19th centuries to meet the needs of Industrial Revolution economies (workers needed math, science, language skills).
- University culture pushed aside anything involving heart, body, senses, or non-rational brain.
- The result: narrow view of intelligence, overvaluation of certain talents, neglect of others, and erasure of relationships between them.
- This one-size-fits-all approach marginalizes students who don't naturally learn this way. Gillian Lynne had to move to think; most schools medicate such children instead of facilitating them.

#### The Creativity Crisis

- Young children start with sparkling imaginations and willingness to take risks. Schools systematically drain this.
- Example: At age 4, a child playing a Wise Man in a Nativity play forgot his line and improvised: "Frank sent this." Adults laughed; the child didn't worry about being wrong.
- If you're not prepared to be wrong, you'll never come up with anything original.
- The "back to basics" movement reinforces the Industrial Revolution hierarchy rather than questioning what "basics" should be.
- **Irony**: Schools are being forced to cut creativity *precisely* because they assume it's nonessential for jobs and competitiveness. But 21st-century jobs absolutely require creativity, independent thinking, and the very qualities schools are suppressing.

#### The Need to Rethink Human Resources

- Education should develop natural abilities and prepare people for the world. Instead, it stifles talents and kills motivation.
- This is replicated in corporations and institutions, creating a cycle where people get "typed" early in careers and struggle to access their other talents.

### The Pace of Change and the Unknown Future

- Children starting school now will retire in 2070. **No one knows what the world will look like in 10 years, let alone 2070.**
- Two major drivers of change: **Technology** and **demography**.

#### Technology

- Digital technology develops faster than most people can grasp.
- People over 30 learned digital technology as adults (like learning a foreign language). People under 30 are native speakers of digital.
- Young people born after the digital revolution outperform older generations. This revolution is barely begun.
- Computing power will soon match the human brain. Merging of information systems with human consciousness is forthcoming.
- Changes in the last 20 years from "simple" digital tech have transformed work and economies; future changes will be far more dramatic.

#### Population Growth

- World population doubled from 3 billion (1970) to 6 billion (1999) — in 30 years.
- Projected 9 billion by mid-21st century.
- This mass of humanity will use technologies we haven't invented yet, in ways we can't imagine, in jobs that don't exist.

#### Unknowability and Flexibility

- No one could accurately predict the world of 10 years ago. We can't predict the next 10 years.
- We *know* China, Russia, India, Brazil will play larger roles; population will grow; technology will accelerate.
- **Inescapable conclusion**: We can't know what the future will be.
- Only way to prepare: Make the most of ourselves, becoming as flexible and productive as possible.
- Many successful people didn't pursue passions for money but because they *couldn't imagine doing anything else*. They invested in mastering their crafts and can adapt them to changing environments.
- People who abandon passion for financial security face danger: that "secure" job may move offshore next decade.
- Children need multiple career tracks, not fixed paths. Some will have jobs we haven't conceived. **Isn't it our obligation to help them explore their true talents and passions?**

### What Is the Element?

#### Two Features, Two Conditions

**Features:**
- **Aptitude**: Natural facility for something; intuitive grasp of it; how it works.
- **Passion**: Deep delight and pleasure in doing it.

**Conditions:**
- **Attitude**: Our perspective on ourselves and circumstances — disposition, sense of self-worth, expectations.
- **Opportunity**: Access to environments and people where the aptitude can be developed.

**Sequence**: "I Get It; I Love It; I Want It; Where Is It?"

#### Feature 1: I Get It (Aptitude)

- An aptitude is a natural grasp of something specific (not always general). Example: Gillian with dance, not all movement. Matt with cartoon storytelling, not all art. Paul with economics and math.
- Aptitudes are highly personal. Some people are naturally good at math; others (like Robinson) are not.
- Not all people are good at everything, but everyone is good at something in particular.

#### Feature 2: I Love It (Passion)

- Being in your Element requires more than aptitude — it requires passion.
- Robinson's brother Ian's keyboard-playing friend Charles said: "No, you don't love to play keyboards. You like the idea of it. If you loved it, you'd be practicing 3–4 hours daily like I have since age 7."
- True passion involves daily discipline and commitment because the person *couldn't imagine not doing it*.

#### Condition 1: I Want It (Attitude)

- Attitude = personal perspective, disposition, emotional point of view.
- Affected by character, spirit, self-worth, perceptions and expectations of others.
- High achievers share: perseverance, self-belief, optimism, ambition, frustration.
- **Luck is partly about attitude**: People who do what they love describe themselves as lucky. People who don't often say they're unlucky. But luck is largely *created* by expectation and how we perceive and take opportunities.

#### Condition 2: Where Is It? (Opportunity)

- Without opportunity, aptitudes may never emerge. No bronco riders in Antarctica; no pearl divers in the Sahara.
- Often others help us recognize talents we can't see in ourselves. You must actively seek opportunities to explore aptitudes.
- Being in the Element often means connecting with others who share the same passion and commitment.

## Chapter 2: Think Differently

### Story: Mick Fleetwood and Learning Disability

#### The Problem

- Mick Fleetwood is one of the world's most accomplished rock drummers (Fleetwood Mac, tens of millions of albums sold).
- In school, test scores suggested he lacked intelligence. He had severe difficulty with math, couldn't recite the alphabet backward, and felt "squeezed out" at boarding school.
- He had no sense of what he was supposed to be because academics were a total failure and he had no other reference points.

#### The Family Context

- His father was an RAF fighter pilot who left the service to pursue writing, taking the family to live on a barge on the Thames for 3 years.
- His sister Sally became a sculptor; sister Susan went into theater.
- Family culture: **Brilliance comes in many forms. Poor math ability doesn't doom anyone.**
- Mick could drum. He discovered it by tapping things. It wasn't "impressive" in the way piano is, but it was his make-or-break talent.

#### The Epiphany

- As a boy visiting his sister in London, Mick attended a jazz club. He saw people playing Miles Davis, smoking, absorbed in music. He felt at home — unfettered, comfortable.
- He held onto that vision and dreamed his way out of school's "academic nightmare."
- Desperate, he left school at 16, prayed under a tree, and committed to becoming a drummer. His parents supported him (didn't insist he graduate) and put him on a train to London with a drum kit.

#### Opportunity and Chain Reaction

- While practicing drums in a garage, keyboard player Peter Bardens heard him and invited him to a gig (not to complain, but to collaborate).
- This led to London's music scene and position with John Mayall's Bluesbreakers (which included Eric Clapton, Jack Bruce, and Mick Taylor).
- He formed Fleetwood Mac with Peter Green and John McVie.
- Result: Multiplatinum recordings, sold-out stadiums, a legendary career.

#### The Paradox

- Even as a world-famous drummer, Mick admits he knows nothing about formal music theory. He doesn't know what a "four/eight" is or the difference between verse and chorus (intellectually).
- He recognizes songs by listening to lyrics and feel, not structure.
- His brain doesn't work with "structured math." He'd be "in a complete petrified mess" if asked to explain theory.
- He's a musician who can't *explain* music technically — but he plays it brilliantly because he *feels* it.

#### The Lesson

- His parents saw that his intelligence wasn't academic — it was something else entirely.
- He succeeded because he understood innately that he had talent in something tests could never measure and refused to accept that he was "useless according to the status quo."

### Taking It All for Granted: The Case of Hidden Senses

#### How Many Senses Do We Have?

- Robinson asks audiences: "How many senses do you have?" Most answer five (taste, touch, smell, sight, hearing).
- Some add a sixth (intuition).
- Rarely does anyone go beyond this.
- **Common assumption**: Five hard senses (with organs); one "spooky" sense (intuition).

#### The Anlo Ewe People

- Anthropologist Kathryn Linn Geurts studied the Anlo Ewe people of southeastern Ghana.
- When she listed the Western five senses, they asked about "the other one — the *main* one."
- They weren't describing intuition. They were describing something fundamental: **balance**.
- The fluid and bones of the inner ear mediate balance. Damage it (illness, alcohol), and daily life collapses. Yet we take it for granted.
- The Anlo Ewe never thought to *count* senses; the notion seemed beside the point. They naturally included balance.

#### The Enemy of Creativity: Common Sense

- **Bertolt Brecht**: "As soon as something seems the most obvious thing in the world, it means we have abandoned all attempts at understanding it."
- Common sense is the enemy of creativity and innovation, especially regarding our own development.

#### The Hidden Senses: 9 Senses Total

- **Five "hard" senses**: Taste, touch, smell, sight, hearing.
- **Four additional senses** (largely ignored in common thinking):
  1. **Thermoception** (temperature sense): Different from touch. You don't need to touch something to feel hot or cold. Crucial for survival (narrow temperature band for humans; why we wear clothes).
  2. **Nociception** (pain sense): Different from touch and temperature. Separate systems for internal vs. external pain.
  3. **Vestibular sense/Equilibrioception** (balance and acceleration): Essential for movement and navigation.
  4. **Kinesthetic sense/Proprioception** (bodily awareness): Understanding where your limbs and body are in space and relation to each other. Essential for walking, coordination, function.
- **Intuition**: Doesn't make the cut with most physiologists.
- All these senses contribute to feeling "in the world" and functioning.

#### Synesthesia: Overlapping Senses

- Some people experience synesthesia: senses mingle (see sounds, hear colors).
- These abnormalities illustrate how profoundly senses shape understanding of self and world.
- Yet many people don't know about senses beyond the "big five."

### Story: Bart Conner and Kinesthetic Intelligence

#### The Discovery

- Bart (from Morton Grove, Illinois) wasn't particularly active as a baby.
- Around age 6, he discovered he could walk on his hands as well as on his feet — not elegant, but amusing.
- His family encouraged him. He learned to walk up and down stairs on his hands.
- Useless skill (didn't improve grades or get jobs), but it made him popular. People who can climb stairs upside down are fun.

#### The Turning Point

- At age 10, with his mother's approval, his physical education teacher took him to a local gymnastics center.
- Bart's eyes "bulged in amazement" — ropes, parallel bars, trapezes, trampolines, ladders. A playground for his kinesthetic abilities.
- **Life-changing moment**: "It was like visiting Santa's workshop and Disneyland at the same time. It was also the ideal place for him."

#### The Achievement

- Eight years of jumping, stretching, vaulting, lifting.
- Montreal Olympics (age 18): Bart Conner represented the USA in gymnastics.
- Became America's most decorated male gymnast ever; first American to win medals at every competition level (national, NCAA, Pan-American, World, World Cup, Olympic).
- Member of three Olympic teams (1976, 1980, 1984).
- 1984 LA Olympics: Dramatic comeback from torn biceps injury to win two gold medals.
- 1991: Inducted into U.S. Olympic Hall of Fame.
- 1996: Inducted into International Gymnastics Hall of Fame.
- Now owns a gymnastics school with wife Nadia Comaneci (Olympic champion).

#### What This Reveals About Intelligence

- Elite athletes, dancers, musicians operate with extraordinary physical and intuitive intelligence.
- They engage their whole bodies in developing routines, relying on "muscle memory."
- In performance, they move too fast and complexly for conscious thinking. They draw from "deep reserves of feeling and intuition" and "physical reflex and coordination" using the whole brain, not just rational parts.
- If they relied only on conscious reasoning, their careers would never get off the ground.
- Athletes and performers challenge something taken for granted: our limited ideas about intelligence.

### Rethinking Intelligence Itself

#### The Wrong Question: "How Intelligent Are You?"

- Robinson asks audiences to rate their intelligence on a 1–10 scale.
- One or two rate themselves 10 (Robinson tells them to go home).
- Most cluster at 6–7.
- Result: A bell curve distribution.
- **Why?** We've come to take for granted certain ideas about intelligence.
- Few people challenge the question itself or ask "What do you mean by intelligence?"

#### The Commonsense View of Intelligence

- **Flawed definition**: We're born with fixed intelligence (like eye color). It shows itself in math and words. We can measure it with tests and express it as a number (IQ).
- Put bluntly, this definition is "as questionable as it is," but it runs through Western and much Eastern culture.
- It underpins education systems, the multibillion-dollar testing industry, academic ability concepts, college entrance exams, subject hierarchies, and the entire idea of IQ.

#### Historical Origins: The Enlightenment

- Dates back to Aristotle and Plato; most recent flowering in the 17th–18th century Enlightenment.
- Philosophers sought firm basis for knowledge through logic and critical reasoning (not superstition).
- **René Descartes**: "I think, therefore I am" — reason is the foundation.
- **Second pillar**: Evidence observed through human senses, not hearsay.
- These two pillars (reason and evidence) became foundations of scientific method, Industrial Revolution, and all subsequent scholarship and education.
- Logic and evidence shaped human sciences (psychology, sociology, anthropology, medicine).
- As public education grew (19th–20th centuries), it adopted these ideas about knowledge and intelligence.

#### The Rise of IQ Testing

- As mass education grew to meet Industrial Revolution labor needs, psychology provided "quick and easy" selection and assessment methods.
- Intelligence was defined as **verbal and mathematical reasoning** (quantifiable).
- **Most significant idea**: IQ (Intelligence Quotient).

#### The Original Intent (Binet) vs. Misuse (Terman and Eugenics)

- **Alfred Binet** (creator of the IQ test):
  - Designed it (commissioned by French government) to identify children with special needs for appropriate schooling — the *opposite* function of modern IQ testing.
  - Never intended it to measure degrees of intelligence or "mental worth."
  - Explicitly stated: "The scale does not permit the measure of intelligence, because intellectual qualities are not superposable, and therefore cannot be measured as linear surfaces are measured."
  - Warned against fixed-intelligence thinking: "We must protest and react against this brutal pessimism" that intelligence is fixed.

- **Lewis Terman** (1916, Stanford-Binet revision):
  - Took IQ numbers to "absurd lengths."
  - Promoted eugenics: Called poor laborers and servants "feebleminded," arguing they were born to be "hewers of wood and drawers of water."
  - Advocated segregating low-IQ children into "special classes" for "concrete and practical" instruction.
  - Promoted forced sterilization of people with low IQs based on "racial differences in mental traits."
  - Result: 30 American states passed involuntary sterilization laws (later repealed).

#### IQ Testing: Life and Death Stakes

- A criminal with IQ below 70 cannot receive the death penalty.
- But IQ scores rise ~25 points per generation, so tests are reset every 15–20 years to maintain a mean of 100.
- Therefore, someone's death penalty eligibility *depends on which part of the testing cycle their crime occurs in*.
- Study case: Death row inmate on life sentence took courses during 10 years incarceration. IQ rose 10+ points upon retesting, making him suddenly eligible for execution.

#### What IQ Really Measures

- IQ largely indicates ability to perform on tests of **certain mathematical and verbal reasoning** — only *some* types of intelligence, not the whole.
- The baseline keeps shifting as population improves.

### The Standardized Testing Industrial Complex

#### The SAT

- **Carl Brigham** (inventor of SAT) was also a eugenicist. He designed it for the military, then disowned it 5 years later, rejecting eugenics.
- But Harvard and Ivy League schools began using it as a screening tool. For 70 years, nearly all U.S. colleges used SAT (or ACT).
- The SAT measures only certain kinds of intelligence, impersonally, in one-size-fits-all fashion, driving high school juniors/seniors to spend hundreds of hours preparing at expense of other learning or passions.
- **John Katzman** (Princeton Review founder): "What makes the SAT bad is it has nothing to do with what kids learn in high school... The SAT has been sold as snake oil; it measured intelligence, verified GPA, predicted college grades. In fact, it's never done the first two at all."

#### Impact on Students

- Students who don't test well or aren't strong in SAT-style reasoning make compromises on their collegiate futures.
- All because we've accepted that **intelligence comes with a number**.
- This notion is pervasive and extends well beyond academics into corporate hiring and career trajectories.

### The Right Question: "How Are You Intelligent?"

#### Multiple Intelligences

- The question "How intelligent are you?" assumes a single scale.
- The right question: **"How are you intelligent?"** — assumes different *ways* of being intelligent.
- Survey of psychologists asked them to define intelligence from a list of 25 attributes. Only 3 were mentioned by 25%+ of respondents.
- **Analogy**: If experts describing edible vs. poisonous mushrooms responded this inconsistently, we'd prudently avoid the subject.

#### Howard Gardner's Multiple Intelligences

- Harvard psychologist Howard Gardner argues for **multiple intelligences** (not one IQ):
  - **Linguistic**: Words, language.
  - **Musical**: Sound, rhythm, music.
  - **Mathematical**: Numbers, logic.
  - **Spatial**: Visual-spatial relationships, navigation.
  - **Kinesthetic**: Body, movement, physical coordination.
  - **Interpersonal**: Relationships with others, social understanding.
  - **Intrapersonal**: Self-knowledge, understanding one's own mind.
- These intelligences are relatively independent; none is universally "more important" (though some may be "dominant" or "dormant" in individuals).
- Education should develop all of them.

#### Robert Sternberg's Three-Part Model

- **Analytic intelligence**: Solving problems using academic skills; completing conventional IQ tests.
- **Creative intelligence**: Dealing with novel situations; coming up with original solutions.
- **Practical intelligence**: Solving problems in everyday life; dealing with real-world challenges.

#### Daniel Goleman and Other Models

- **Emotional intelligence**: Understanding and managing emotions in oneself and others.
- **Social intelligence**: Understanding social dynamics and interacting effectively.
- **Robert Cooper** (*The Other 90%*): Intelligence isn't just in the brain. The gut and heart have their own neurological networks ("second brain" in the intestines). Direct experiences hit the gut/heart first (hence "gut reaction"), shaping everything.

#### The Diversity of Intelligence in Action

- Whether or not single theories perfectly capture all intelligence, **everyday experience makes clear that human intelligence is diverse and multifaceted**.
- Evidence is everywhere: the extraordinary richness and complexity of human culture and achievement.
- All humans "think" about experiences in all the ways they have them. We all have different strengths and natural aptitudes.

### Case Study: Alexis Lemaire (Mathematical Brilliance)

- Robinson notes he has no aptitude for math; Alexis Lemaire does.
- Lemaire is a French doctoral student in artificial intelligence.
- In 2007, he set a world record: calculating the 13th root of a random 200-digit number in 72.4 seconds — *in his head*.
- The number was 17+ lines long. His answer: 2,397,207,667,966,701 (correct).
- Previous best time: 77 seconds. He continues to improve.

#### How He Does It

- "The first digit is very easy, the last digit is very easy, but the inside numbers are extremely difficult."
- He uses "an artificial intelligence system on my own brain instead of on a computer. I believe most people can do it, but I also have a high-speed mind."
- He practices regularly, exercises, avoids caffeine/alcohol/high-fat/high-sugar foods to keep his brain fast.
- Math is so intense that he must take regular breaks to rest his brain. Too much could harm his health and heart.

#### The Human Element

- Robinson (unlike Lemaire) felt too much math was bad for his health for *different* reasons.
- Surprisingly, Lemaire also didn't excel in school math. He mainly taught himself through books.
- He discovered natural flair for numbers around age 11 and has refined it through constant challenge and sophisticated techniques.
- **Foundation**: "Unique, personal aptitude combined with deep passion and commitment." When he unearths roots of huge numbers, Alexis Lemaire is clearly in his Element.

### Three Features of Human Intelligence

#### Feature 1: Diversity

- Human intelligence is **extraordinarily diverse**.
- Not limited to verbal and mathematical reasoning (important, but only *one* way intelligence expresses itself).
- Gordon Parks (legendary photographer) captured the Black American experience in ways few others did. He produced Hollywood's first Black major film director. He helped found *International Photographer* magazine.
- Intelligence manifests across countless domains: spatial reasoning, kinesthetic awareness, emotional understanding, social perception, intuition, creativity, practical problem-solving.

## Chapter 3: Beyond Imagining

### Story: Faith Ringgold (Artist, Writer, Composer)

#### The Unconventional Path

- Faith Ringgold is an acclaimed artist known for painted story quilts, exhibited in major museums worldwide (Guggenheim, Metropolitan Museum, MoMA).
- She is also an award-winning writer (Caldecott Honor for *Tar Beach*) and composer.
- Her creative success came partly through illness: asthma kept her out of school starting at age 2.
- She missed kindergarten and first grade entirely. By second grade, she attended but missed 2–3 weeks yearly due to asthma.
- **Key insight**: "I was not around for some of the indoctrinations... I did not ever get hooked into the regimentation."

#### Creative Nurture During Illness

- Her mother worked with her to keep pace with school academics.
- But more importantly, they explored the arts thriving in 1930s Harlem.
- Her mother took her to see Duke Ellington, Billie Holiday, Billy Eckstine, and other great performers.
- Faith thought of these artists as the truly "highly creative" people, not her teachers.

#### The Result

- She became an accomplished, multidisciplinary artist unshackled by conventional schooling.
- Her creativity flourished because she missed the "regimentation" that constrains most children.

### Feature 2 (Continued): Imagination

#### What Is Imagination?

- Imagination is the ability to think of things that don't exist yet; to perceive things differently; to make new connections.
- It's essential to being human and to the Element.
- Children are naturally imaginative. Schools systematically suppress this ability.

#### The Constraints on Imagination in Schools

- Schools reward conformity and "right answers," not divergent thinking.
- Testing culture penalizes risk-taking and creative exploration.
- Teachers (often unintentionally) shut down imaginative thinking in favor of factual recall.
- As children age, they learn that imagination is frivolous. By adulthood, many have abandoned it entirely.

#### Why Imagination Matters Now

- In rapidly changing, uncertain futures, imagination is survival.
- Imagination drives innovation, problem-solving, and adaptability.
- People with strong imagination can envision possibilities and create paths to them.
- Jobs of the future don't exist yet; imagination is the only tool to envision them.

---

### The Barriers to Finding Your Element

#### Comparison and Self-Doubt

- People compare themselves to those perceived as "naturally talented" and conclude they lack talent.
- If someone excels without visible effort, others assume they were "born that way" and give up.
- Reality: Everyone who's excellent practices and develops their skills over years.
- Comparison undermines motivation.

#### Cultural and Family Expectations

- Families and cultures often direct children toward "practical" or "respected" paths (law, medicine, business).
- Passion is dismissed as frivolous or irresponsible.
- Children internalize these messages and suppress their own interests.

#### The "Passion Paradox"

- Many people don't discover their passion until they stop looking for it.
- Pressure to "find your passion" often creates anxiety and paralysis.
- Real passion often emerges through exploration and play, not forced searching.

## Chapter 4: Flowering

### Story: Gillian Lynne (Revisited with Depth)

#### The Long View

- Years after her dance school breakthrough, Gillian Lynne reflected on how different her life would have been without that psychologist's intervention.
- She could have been medicated, labeled as disordered, pushed into "special education."
- Instead, she was recognized and given permission to be herself.

#### The Dance Career and Beyond

- She performed in Royal Ballet Company as a soloist.
- Created her own musical theater company producing hits in London and New York.
- Collaborated with Andrew Lloyd Webber on *Cats* and *The Phantom of the Opera*.
- These productions changed musical theater and brought joy to millions.
- Her professional success was built on doing what she was meant to do.

#### The Larger Lesson

- One pivotal moment changed her trajectory entirely.
- That moment required: a wise observer, the right environment, and her own inner drive.
- Finding your Element isn't about sudden inspiration; it's about recognizing aptitude and creating conditions for it to flourish.

---

### Conditions for Growth: Opportunity and Community

#### Opportunity as Environment

- Opportunity isn't just *chance*; it's **environment that invites participation**.
- Gillian's dance school provided an environment where her natural movement needs were welcomed, not pathologized.
- Bart Conner's gymnastics center provided an environment where his physical intelligence could develop.
- Most people never encounter such environments.

#### Community and Belonging

- Finding your Element often means connecting with others on the same path.
- This connection validates the pursuit and provides feedback, collaboration, and motivation.
- People who are isolated in their interests often give up because they lack community.

#### Creating Your Own Opportunities

- If the right environment doesn't exist, some people create it.
- Others actively seek out communities aligned with their passions.
- **Active searching** is crucial: joining clubs, taking classes, finding mentors, reaching out to others.

---

### The Four Components in Action

#### Putting Aptitude, Passion, Attitude, and Opportunity Together

- **Aptitude**: Natural facility for something. It emerges through exploration and play.
- **Passion**: Deep, sustained joy in the activity. It's not always immediate; it often develops as competence grows.
- **Attitude**: Belief that you can improve, that effort matters, that you deserve to pursue what you love.
- **Opportunity**: Environment, community, permission, access to resources and mentors.

#### When One Element Is Missing

- **No aptitude but high passion**: Difficult to excel; may be satisfying as hobby.
- **High aptitude but no passion**: Can succeed professionally but feel empty; risks burnout.
- **Aptitude + passion but poor attitude**: Self-doubt, fear of judgment paralyzes action.
- **Aptitude + passion + attitude but no opportunity**: Frustration and missed potential; must actively create opportunities.

#### The Sequence Revisited

- **"I Get It"** (aptitude) comes first — you recognize you have a natural grasp.
- **"I Love It"** (passion) develops as you invest time and see progress.
- **"I Want It"** (attitude) strengthens as you commit despite obstacles.
- **"Where Is It?"** (opportunity) requires active seeking and sometimes creation.

## Chapter 5: Different Minds

### The Diversity of Intelligence: Real-World Examples

#### Visual-Spatial Intelligence

- Some people think primarily in images and spatial relationships, not words.
- An architect might see a building in 3D in her mind before drawing anything.
- A navigator reads landscapes and maps instantly.
- Traditional word-based education misses these thinkers entirely.

#### Musical Intelligence

- Musicians hear patterns, harmonies, and emotional nuances others miss.
- They may struggle with math but excel in abstract reasoning (music theory is applied mathematics).
- Schools that cut music programs eliminate an entire pathway to excellence for these minds.

#### Kinesthetic Intelligence

- Athletes, dancers, surgeons, craftspeople think through their bodies.
- They learn by doing and refining through physical feedback.
- Sitting in classrooms and reading textbooks is torture for these minds.
- Yet schools prioritize this format for all learners.

#### Intrapersonal Intelligence

- Some people are naturally introspective, self-aware, emotionally intelligent.
- They excel at understanding human motivation, psychology, relationships.
- Schools rarely assess or develop this form of intelligence.

#### The Core Point

- Each type of intelligence is valid and valuable.
- Each person has a unique profile of strengths across these intelligences.
- Educational systems that teach everyone the same way in the same pace ignore this reality.

---

### Neurodiversity and Different Brain Types

#### ADHD as a Different Operating System

- ADHD is often pathologized as a disorder, but it's better understood as a different cognitive style.
- ADHD brains are often highly creative, pattern-seeking, and hyperfocusable on areas of interest.
- Traditional schooling punishes ADHD traits (restlessness, non-linear thinking, distraction) and misses the strengths.
- Many creative and successful people have undiagnosed or diagnosed ADHD.

#### Dyslexia and Alternative Strengths

- Dyslexic individuals often have superior visual-spatial, pattern-recognition, and creative abilities.
- Many struggle with reading but excel in design, engineering, entrepreneurship.
- Label them as "learning disabled" and you've missed their actual abilities.

#### Autism Spectrum

- Autistic individuals often have intense, focused interests and exceptional attention to detail.
- They may excel in fields requiring pattern recognition, data analysis, specialized knowledge.
- Difficulty with social convention gets labeled as a deficit, missing the cognitive strengths.

#### Giftedness and Underachievement

- Highly gifted students bored by standard curriculum often appear unmotivated or lazy.
- They may be gifted in areas schools don't test (art, music, creative writing, mechanical ability).
- Labeling them "underachievers" because they don't fit school metrics is a tragedy.

#### The Education System's Failure

- Schools were designed for a mythical "average" learner that doesn't exist.
- For students outside this narrow band, school becomes a place of failure and shame.
- Many brilliant people emerge *after* leaving school because they finally had permission to be themselves.

## Chapter 6: Discovering Your Element

### The Process of Discovery

#### Phase 1: Exploration

- Finding your Element begins with **exploration** — trying different activities without pressure to excel.
- Play is crucial. When you play, you do things for their own sake, not for external reward.
- Play reveals what naturally engages you without forcing it.

#### Phase 2: Recognition

- As you explore, certain activities feel different — easier, more engaging, more "right" than others.
- You lose track of time. You're absorbed. Your natural abilities show themselves.
- This is recognition of aptitude.

#### Phase 3: Development

- Once recognized, aptitude requires **practice and commitment** to develop.
- This phase includes instruction, feedback, mentorship, and pushing through difficulty.
- Passion sustains the effort; without it, development stalls.

#### Phase 4: Integration

- Eventually, the activity becomes part of your identity.
- You've developed sufficient skill that opportunity naturally follows.
- You're "in your Element" — doing what you were meant to do.

---

### Common Obstacles During Discovery

#### Fear of Judgment

- People fear looking foolish, being mocked, or wasting time on "impractical" pursuits.
- This fear often comes from past experiences in school or family.
- It paralyzes exploration and keeps people stuck.

#### Pressure to Choose One Thing

- People feel forced to pick a single career path early and commit forever.
- This pressure creates paralysis for those with multiple interests.
- Reality: Interests change, skills transfer, and many Elements can exist in a lifetime.

#### Waiting for Permission

- Many people wait for external permission (parents, teachers, society) to pursue their interests.
- Permission rarely comes from outside. You must grant it to yourself.
- Taking action without permission is an act of self-trust.

#### The "Practical" Trap

- Pressure to pursue "practical" or "respectable" careers overrides personal interest.
- Parents want security for their children and push them toward traditional paths.
- Ironically, following passion often leads to greater success and security than forced pragmatism.

#### Comparing Your Beginning to Someone Else's Middle

- People see others' expertise and assume they lack talent because they're not immediately excellent.
- They don't see the years of practice, failure, and refinement behind the excellence.
- Comparison kills momentum. Focus on your own journey instead.

---

### Strategies for Discovery

#### Take Small Steps

- You don't need to overhaul your life immediately.
- Small experiments (one class, one club, one conversation) reveal interests without major commitment.
- Compound interest of small explorations adds up.

#### Find Your Tribe

- Look for people who share your interests, however niche.
- Online communities, clubs, classes, workshops provide belonging and feedback.
- Community sustains motivation through difficult phases of learning.

#### Seek Mentors and Teachers

- People further along the path can accelerate your learning and provide guidance.
- Mentors also validate your pursuit — they model that it's possible.
- Don't wait for formal mentorship; ask questions, observe, learn from anyone doing what interests you.

#### Give Yourself Permission

- Stop waiting for external approval.
- Recognize that pursuing your passion isn't selfish; it's actually necessary for your contribution to the world.
- Your best work comes from doing what you're meant to do.

#### Create Your Own Opportunities

- If the right environment doesn't exist, create it.
- Start a club, organize a group, build a community around shared interest.
- Action often precedes opportunity rather than the other way around.

## Chapter 7: Attitudes

### The Role of Attitude in Finding Your Element

#### Self-Belief vs. Self-Doubt

- People with strong self-belief view challenges as opportunities to improve.
- People with self-doubt view challenges as evidence of inadequacy.
- These attitudes shape whether you persist or give up when things get hard.

#### Fixed Mindset vs. Growth Mindset

- Fixed mindset: Intelligence, talent, and ability are innate and unchangeable. You either have it or you don't.
- Growth mindset: Abilities develop through effort, practice, and learning. You can always improve.
- Fixed mindset kills the Element pursuit; growth mindset enables it.

#### Blame vs. Responsibility

- When things don't work, fixed-mindset people blame external factors or themselves as incapable.
- Growth-mindset people ask, "What did I miss? How can I improve? What do I need to learn?"
- This shift from blame to responsibility is empowering.

---

### Stories of Attitude Shaping Outcomes

#### Example: Athletes and Adversity

- Champion athletes often experience major setbacks (injuries, losses, rejections).
- Their attitude toward these setbacks determines recovery.
- Some see it as permanent proof they're not good enough; others see it as temporary and fixable.
- The ones who return stronger had attitudes of resilience and learning.

#### Example: Artists and Rejection

- Successful artists face constant rejection before breakthrough.
- Many quit because rejection confirms their doubt.
- The ones who succeed reframe rejection as feedback, not judgment.
- Attitude transforms rejection from defeat to data.

---

### Cultivating Attitudes That Support the Element

#### Adopt a Growth Mindset

- Deliberately reframe failures as learning opportunities.
- Notice when you use fixed-mindset language ("I'm not good at math") and challenge it.
- Replace with growth language: "I haven't learned how to do this yet."

#### Expect Difficulty

- Anything worth doing is hard initially.
- Excellence requires years of practice, mistakes, and refinement.
- If it's easy, you're probably not growing.
- Expect difficulty and you're less demoralized when it arrives.

#### Separate Effort from Worth

- Working hard and still struggling doesn't mean you're inadequate.
- Effort is what's required; struggle is normal; growth is the result.
- Your worth as a person is separate from your current performance.

#### Cultivate Perseverance

- Perseverance is the willingness to continue despite difficulty.
- It's not about being fearless; it's about being willing to be afraid and do it anyway.
- Perseverance is learnable; you build it by persevering through small challenges.

#### Practice Gratitude and Optimism

- People who find their Element often describe themselves as lucky.
- This "luck" is often a result of optimistic framing — seeing opportunities where others see obstacles.
- Gratitude for small progress keeps motivation alive.

## Chapter 8: Collaboration

### Working With Others to Find Your Element

#### Mentorship and Apprenticeship

- Centuries of skill development happened through mentorship — novice learning directly from master.
- Modern schools abandoned this in favor of classroom instruction.
- Mentorship accelerates learning and provides emotional support.

#### Finding Mentors

- Mentors aren't necessarily formal. They're anyone further along who's willing to help.
- You find them by showing genuine interest, asking good questions, and being coachable.
- Many people are honored to mentor if asked respectfully.

#### The Role of Feedback

- People isolated in their pursuits lack objective feedback.
- Feedback from teachers, peers, or mentors reveals blind spots and accelerates improvement.
- Welcoming feedback (rather than defending) is crucial to growth.

#### Collaboration as Fuel

- Pursuing your Element with others is more sustaining than solo pursuit.
- Shared passion, mutual support, and collaborative problem-solving keep motivation alive.
- Some of the greatest achievements come from collaborating with complementary talents.

---

### Building Community Around Your Element

#### Finding Your People

- Your tribe may not exist where you are physically.
- Online communities, workshops, retreats, and conferences bring people together around shared interests.
- Being around people who "get it" is incredibly energizing and validating.

#### Creating Community

- If your tribe doesn't exist, start it.
- A blog, social media group, local meetup, or class can gather others.
- The person who creates community often becomes a hub and deepens their own commitment.

## Chapter 9: Organizations and Culture

### How Organizations Support or Block the Element

#### Organizational Culture

- Organizations have personalities shaped by leadership, history, and values.
- Some cultures celebrate creativity, risk-taking, and experimentation.
- Others punish deviation and demand conformity.
- Culture determines whether people can bring their full selves to work.

#### The Problem With Hierarchies

- Rigid hierarchies limit who gets to contribute what.
- A financial analyst who wants to innovate is blocked. A creative person in operations is stifled.
- People get "typed" and their other talents are never developed or used.

#### The Cost of Misalignment

- When people's work doesn't align with their Element, engagement plummets.
- Retention suffers. Productivity suffers. Innovation suffers.
- The organization loses the person's actual talents while enforcing compliance with a narrow role.

---

### Building Element-Friendly Organizations

#### Emphasizing Purpose Over Just Profit

- Organizations that succeed long-term connect their work to larger purpose.
- Employees who understand how their work contributes to meaningful goals are more engaged.
- Purpose attracts people driven by passion, not just paycheck.

#### Creating Space for Growth and Exploration

- Organizations that develop people's talents keep them.
- Rotation programs, learning budgets, time for projects of personal interest — these signal that growth matters.
- Google's famous 20% time (allowing engineers to work on personal projects) produced Gmail and Google News.

#### Diversity of Roles and Paths

- Not everyone is suited for the same type of work.
- Creating roles that leverage different strengths (not just credentials) opens possibilities.
- Career paths shouldn't be linear; sideways moves and explorations should be encouraged.

#### Leadership Modeling

- Leaders who are visibly in their Element and pursuing growth create cultures where this is normalized.
- Leaders who are disengaged and phoning it in create cultures of resignation.
- The tone comes from the top.

#### Flow State

- When in your Element, you experience **flow** — complete absorption in the task.
- Time disappears. Self-consciousness fades. You're fully engaged.
- This is different from happiness or pleasure; it's a state of optimal functioning.

#### Authenticity

- When in your Element, you feel like your true self.
- You're not performing a role or meeting others' expectations.
- This authenticity is deeply satisfying at a level beyond just enjoyment.

#### Energy Rather Than Depletion

- Some work drains you even if you're good at it.
- Work in your Element energizes you, even when difficult.
- The difference is alignment between your nature and the work.

- Resilience increases because you have something worth persisting for.

- People in their Element do their best work.
- This work has quality, creativity, and integrity that benefits others.
- Your excellence in your Element often serves others, even if that's not your primary motive.

#### Cultural and Organizational Impact

- Organizations full of people in their Element are more innovative, adaptive, and successful.
- Cultures that support the Element are healthier and more resilient.
- Society benefits when people are able to contribute their best.

#### It's Not a Single Destination

- Finding your Element isn't a one-time discovery after which you coast.
- As you grow, your Element evolves. New interests emerge. Skills deepen.
- The Element is a journey of continuous discovery and development, not a final arrival.

#### Multiple Elements Over a Lifetime

- You might have different Elements at different life stages.
- A musician might become a teacher. A teacher might become a writer.
- This isn't failure; it's evolution. Your skills transfer, and you find new ways to apply them.

#### The Discipline Required

- Being in your Element requires continuous practice, learning, and refinement.
- Complacency kills. Growth requires ongoing challenge.

## Chapter 11: Transformation

### Changing Systems From Within

#### Education Transformation

- Schools must shift from a one-size-fits-all model to one that recognizes and nurtures diverse intelligences.
- This requires: redefining intelligence, broadening assessment, creating flexible pathways, empowering teachers.

#### Key Educational Changes

- **Reduce standardized testing**: Replace with portfolio assessment, projects, demonstrations of learning.
- **Teach to multiple intelligences**: Offer learning through music, movement, art, hands-on making, not just reading and lecture.
- **Empower teachers**: Teachers know their students; they should have autonomy to teach to their students' strengths, not just curriculum.
- **Revalue the arts**: Arts aren't extras; they're essential. Time and resources should match academics.
- **Create interdisciplinary learning**: Show connections between subjects rather than teaching in silos.
- **Start earlier**: Elementary education should be exploration-based, not test-prep-based.

---

### Changing Organizations and Institutions

#### Rethinking Human Resources

- Organizations must stop viewing people as interchangeable resources.
- Instead, invest in discovering and developing what each person actually does well.
- This is expensive in the short term but profitable long-term in retention, innovation, and engagement.

#### Creating Cultures of Growth

- Model risk-taking and learning from failure at all levels.
- Celebrate when people discover new talents or shift directions.
- Invest in learning and development budgets.

---

### The Ripple Effect: Personal Change Enables Systemic Change

- When individuals claim permission to pursue their Element, they inspire others.
- When parents support their children's passions rather than forcing narrow paths, schools respond.
- When employees become more engaged, organizations take notice and adapt.
- Personal transformation accumulates into systemic transformation.

  - Personal fulfillment and mental health
  - Contribution to society

  - Meaning in life beyond material success

  - Education becomes humane and effective
