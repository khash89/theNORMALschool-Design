## Part I: The Core Logic — Backward Design

### 1.1 The Central Argument

Teachers are designers. Their job is to craft curriculum, instruction, and assessment to achieve specific learning purposes. The single most important shift Wiggins and McTighe propose: design backward from desired results, not forward from content or activities.

The traditional approach asks: "What should I teach? What activities will I use? What book will we read?" Backward design asks: "What should students walk out the door able to understand and do? What is evidence of such ability? Therefore, what texts, activities, and methods will best enable such a result?"

This reversal sounds like common sense but feels unnatural in practice, because most teachers have been trained to think in terms of inputs (content to cover, activities to do) rather than outputs (understandings to achieve, performances to demonstrate).

### 1.2 The Three Stages

| Stage | Core Question | What Gets Produced |
|---|---|---|
| **Stage 1: Identify Desired Results** | What should students know, understand, and be able to do? What enduring understandings are desired? | Established goals, enduring understandings, essential questions, key knowledge and skills |
| **Stage 2: Determine Acceptable Evidence** | How will we know if students have achieved the desired results? What is appropriate evidence of understanding? | Performance tasks, other evidence (quizzes, tests, observations), self-assessment |
| **Stage 3: Plan Learning Experiences** | What learning activities and teaching will promote understanding, knowledge, skill, and student interest? | Learning activities sequenced using WHERETO elements |

The critical insight: **Stage 2 comes before Stage 3.** Assessment is designed before instruction. This forces the designer to clarify what understanding actually looks like before choosing teaching methods or activities. Most teachers do the opposite: plan activities first, then figure out how to test at the end.

### 1.3 The Twin Sins of Design

Wiggins and McTighe identify two pervasive design failures, which they call the "twin sins":

**Activity-focused teaching.** Lessons are built around engaging activities (the "apples unit" — leaf-print collages, word searches, creative writing about apples) without clear intellectual goals. Students are busy but not learning anything of enduring importance. The question "So what?" cannot be answered.

**Coverage-focused teaching.** The teacher marches through the textbook, chapter by chapter, regardless of student understanding, prioritization of ideas, or connection to transfer goals. Coverage is "teaching by mentioning" — addressing a topic is confused with achieving learning. The textbook becomes the de facto curriculum, even though it is designed as a reference, not a syllabus.

Both sins share the same root: no explicit big ideas guiding the teaching, no plan for ensuring the learning.

## Part II: Understanding Understanding

### 2.1 What Understanding Is

Wiggins and McTighe make a sharp conceptual distinction between knowing and understanding:

**Knowledge** is recall of discrete facts, definitions, and procedures. It can be tested by recognition (multiple-choice) or recall (fill-in-the-blank). A student can "know" something without understanding it.

**Understanding** is the ability to make meaning from knowledge and transfer it to new situations. Understanding involves going beyond the information given (Bruner, 1957). It is demonstrated when students can use what they know "wisely, flexibly, creatively" in contexts they have not previously encountered.

The clinical test: if a student can answer a question only when it is phrased in the exact way it was taught, they know it but do not understand it. Dewey's classroom example: students who could answer "What is the state of the center of the earth?" (answer: "Igneous fusion!") but could not answer "What would you find if you dug a hole in the earth?"

### 2.2 Transfer as the Core Goal

Understanding is fundamentally about transfer — the ability to take what is learned in one context and apply it effectively to new and different situations.

Transfer is not optional. It is the primary mission of all education, because teachers can only address a small sample of any subject's content. Students must be equipped to learn far more on their own than they can ever learn from instruction. Wiggins and McTighe (drawing from Bransford, Brown, & Cocking, 2000): "Organizing information into a conceptual framework allows for greater transfer... Learning with understanding is more likely to promote transfer than simply memorizing information."

Transfer has degrees. Wiggins and McTighe propose a four-level rubric: from near transfer (applying known procedures to slightly different problems) to far transfer (confronting unfamiliar, ambiguous challenges that require creative adaptation of prior learning). Most school assessments test only near transfer or no transfer at all.

### 2.3 The Expert Blind Spot

A persistent problem: teachers who deeply understand their subject find it difficult to empathize with the novice's confusion. What is obvious to the expert was once not obvious, but the expert has forgotten the struggle. Wiggins and McTighe call this the "Expert Blind Spot."

Consequences: teachers treat understandings as facts to be transmitted rather than meanings to be constructed. They tell students the conclusion instead of designing experiences that lead students to construct the understanding themselves. They test for recall of the understanding rather than for evidence that students can actually use it.

The antidote: always remember that "what is big to the teacher or the expert is often abstract, lifeless, confusing, or irrelevant to the child." Designing for understanding requires deliberately seeing through the eyes of the novice.

### 2.4 Misconceptions Must Be Actively Addressed

Understanding is not merely additive (layering new knowledge on top of old). It often requires replacing prior beliefs with better ones. Students come to class with preconceptions — many of them wrong — that actively interfere with new learning.

Famous examples: graduating Harvard seniors who cannot explain why it is colder in winter (many say "because the earth is farther from the sun"). Well-educated adults who describe the phases of the moon as lunar eclipses. Students who compute the correct answer to a math problem but give an answer that is nonsensical in context (e.g., "31 remainder 12 buses").

Curriculum must be designed to surface, confront, and replace predictable misconceptions. This means pre-assessment of prior beliefs, not just prior knowledge.

## Part III: The Six Facets of Understanding

### 3.1 The Framework

Understanding is not one thing. Wiggins and McTighe identify six distinct but related facets, each representing a different way a person demonstrates understanding:

| Facet | Core Question | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Explanation** | Can the student provide thorough, supported, and justifiable accounts? | Accurate, coherent theories; supported generalizations; systematic reasoning |
| **2. Interpretation** | Can the student tell meaningful stories, offer apt translations, provide illuminating historical or personal dimension? | Meaningful narratives; insightful readings of texts, situations, experiences |
| **3. Application** | Can the student use knowledge effectively in new situations and diverse contexts? | Effective transfer; fluent, adaptive, graceful performance in realistic settings |
| **4. Perspective** | Can the student see from critical and insightful points of view? | Recognition of assumptions; awareness of limits; consideration of alternative viewpoints |
| **5. Empathy** | Can the student find value in what others might find odd, alien, or implausible? | Sensitivity to different perspectives; willingness to walk in another's shoes |
| **6. Self-Knowledge** | Can the student show metacognitive awareness, recognize their own prejudices and blind spots? | Reflective awareness; recognition of what one does and does not understand |

### 3.2 Design Implications of the Six Facets

The facets serve three practical functions:

**Clarifying goals (Stage 1).** Which facets are most important for this unit? A science unit might emphasize Explanation and Application. A literature unit might emphasize Interpretation and Empathy. A civics unit might emphasize Perspective and Self-Knowledge.

**Designing assessments (Stage 2).** Each facet implies different kinds of evidence. You cannot assess Application with a multiple-choice test. You cannot assess Empathy by asking students to recite facts. The facets generate assessment tasks.

**Planning instruction (Stage 3).** Each facet requires different learning activities. Explanation requires opportunities to construct and defend arguments. Application requires realistic, messy problems. Perspective requires exposure to conflicting viewpoints.

### 3.3 The Six-Facet Rubric

Wiggins and McTighe provide a comprehensive rubric that describes performance levels across all six facets, from sophisticated (nuanced, insightful, illuminating) to naïve (superficial, simplistic, unreflective). This rubric serves as both an assessment instrument and a design tool — it makes visible what genuine understanding looks like at each level of development.

## Part IV: Stage 1 — Identifying Desired Results

### 4.1 The Stage 1 Architecture

Stage 1 of the UbD Template contains four elements:

**Established Goals (G).** Formal standards, program objectives, learning outcomes that the unit addresses.

**Enduring Understandings (U).** Full-sentence generalizations that express what students should "get" by unit's end — the transferable big ideas. Always framed as "Students will understand that..." Not facts to memorize, but inferences that must be constructed through active learning.

**Essential Questions (Q).** Open, provocative questions that guide inquiry and focus instruction on uncovering important ideas. Not questions with pat answers, but questions that genuinely stimulate thinking.

**Key Knowledge and Skills (K, S).** The specific facts, concepts, and skills students need to acquire in order to explore the essential questions and build toward the enduring understandings.

### 4.2 Big Ideas

Big ideas are the conceptual linchpins of a discipline — the ideas that give meaning to facts and connect discrete topics. Examples: "rate of change" in mathematics (applicable from epidemiology to economics); "credible evidence" in research (applicable from science to law to journalism); "audience and purpose" in writing (applicable across all forms of communication).

Big ideas have four characteristics: they are transferable across contexts, they organize many smaller ideas, they are counterintuitive or non-obvious to novices, and they generate further inquiry.

Wiggins and McTighe use a three-concentric-ovals graphic to prioritize content: the outer oval is "worth being familiar with" (background knowledge); the middle oval is "important to know and do" (key facts and skills); the inner oval is "enduring understanding" (the big ideas and core transfer tasks). Most curriculum overemphasizes the outer two ovals at the expense of the innermost.

### 4.3 Essential Questions

Essential questions are the engine of a unit designed for understanding. They have four overlapping meanings:

1. **Important to living and thinking throughout life.** Timeless, overarching. "What is justice?" "Is art a matter of taste or principles?"
2. **Essential to the expert's view of the field.** "What makes a mathematical argument convincing?" "How do scientists prove things?"
3. **Essential to successful teaching.** Pointing toward specific understandings while remaining genuinely open. "How do effective writers hook and hold their readers?"
4. **Transferable across disciplines.** "What margins of error are tolerable?" (applicable in engineering, pottery, statistics, music).

Essential questions come in two scopes: **overarching** (recurring across units, courses, and sometimes subjects) and **topical** (specific to a unit but still genuinely open and inquiry-provoking).

A question is truly essential only if the whole design takes it seriously. If the question is asked but the subsequent assessments and instruction don't actually pursue it, it is merely rhetorical.

### 4.4 Crafting Understandings

Understandings are framed as full-sentence generalizations. They are not topics ("audience and purpose"), not facts ("water covers three-fourths of the earth's surface"), and not vague goals ("the student will appreciate literature"). They are specific claims that the student must come to realize through inquiry.

Examples of well-framed understandings:
- "An effective story engages the reader by setting up tensions through questions, mysteries, and dilemmas about what will happen next."
- "Healthful living requires an individual to act on available information about good nutrition even if it means breaking comfortable habits."
- "Correlation does not ensure causation."
- "A president is not above the law."

The critical pedagogical insight: understandings must not be taught as facts. Merely stating an understanding to students is the "cardinal mistake of coverage." An understanding, by definition, is something the learner must construct through designed experience. The teacher's job is to engineer the "Aha!" — not deliver it as information.

## Part V: Stage 2 — Evidence of Understanding

### 5.1 Thinking Like an Assessor

Backward design requires designers to think like assessors before thinking like activity planners. The question is not "What should I teach?" but "What would count as evidence that students understand?"

Evidence of understanding requires more than traditional tests. Tests typically assess knowledge (recall) and skill (plugging in procedures). Understanding requires evidence that students can transfer — use knowledge in new, ambiguous, realistic contexts.

### 5.2 Performance Tasks (the "Game" vs. "Drills")

The distinction between exercises and problems is crucial:

**Exercises** are straightforward applications of known procedures. There is one right approach, one right answer. Like shooting free throws in an empty gym — useful for building skill, but not evidence of game competence.

**Problems** are genuine challenges requiring judgment about which approach to use, what information is relevant, and how to navigate ambiguity. Like playing an actual basketball game — requiring strategy, adaptation, and integration of multiple skills under realistic conditions.

Most school assessments are exercises. Understanding requires evidence from genuine problems — what Wiggins and McTighe call "authentic performance tasks."

### 5.3 GRASPS Framework for Task Design

Performance tasks are designed using the GRASPS framework:

| Element | What It Specifies |
|---|---|
| **Goal** | The challenge, problem, or obstacle to overcome |
| **Role** | The role the student plays (researcher, designer, advisor, etc.) |
| **Audience** | The target audience (younger children, city council, a client, etc.) |
| **Situation** | The realistic context, constraints, and background |
| **Product/Performance** | What the student creates or does |
| **Standards/Criteria** | How the work will be judged |

### 5.4 The Assessment Continuum

Effective assessment uses a range of methods: from informal checks for understanding (oral questions, one-minute essays, observations) through traditional quizzes and tests, to complex performance tasks and projects. Understanding is assessed over time ("a scrapbook, not a snapshot") using multiple forms of evidence.

## Part VI: Stage 3 — Planning Learning (WHERETO)

### 6.1 The WHERETO Elements

Once desired results and evidence are clear, learning activities are designed using WHERETO:

| Element | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **W** | Help students know **Where** the unit is going, **Why**, and **What** is expected |
| **H** | **Hook** students early — engage, provoke, make them curious |
| **E** | **Equip** students with needed experiences, tools, knowledge, and know-how |
| **R** | Provide opportunities to **Rethink**, **Reflect**, and **Revise** |
| **E** | Allow students to **Evaluate** their work and set goals |
| **T** | **Tailor** the work to individual needs, interests, and learning styles |
| **O** | Be thoughtful about the **Organization** and sequence for maximum engagement and effectiveness |

### 6.2 Uncoverage, Not Coverage

The metaphor shift: instead of "covering" content (concealing or skimming), teachers should "uncover" content — exposing the big ideas, revealing their power, making students wrestle with them. "What any curriculum designed for understanding must do is help students realize that their job is not merely to take in what is 'covered' but to actively uncover what lies below the surface of the facts and to ponder their meaning."

### 6.3 Hooks and Entry Points

Effective unit design opens with engagement — what Wiggins and McTighe call "instant immersion" in questions, problems, challenges, or stories that require the student's wits. Four types of entry points: thought provocations (anomalies, counterintuitive facts), experiential shocks (immersive challenges), personal connections (relating to students' lives), and multiple perspectives (conflicting viewpoints on the same issue).

## Part VII: Schooling by Design — The System Level

### 7.1 The Central Thesis

*Schooling by Design* extends backward design from the unit level to the entire school system. The argument: if backward design makes individual units more effective, the same logic should govern how the whole institution operates. Mission should determine curriculum, which should determine teaching, which should determine assessment, which should determine policies, structures, and resource allocation.

The six pillars of school reform:

1. **Mission focus** — constant, explicit alignment to the long-term goal of enabling learners to transfer learning with understanding
2. **Curriculum and assessment framework** — built backward from mission, not around content coverage
3. **Principles of learning** — explicit, shared, research-based principles guiding all pedagogical decisions
4. **Aligned structures and policies** — job descriptions, schedules, and resource allocation consistent with mission
5. **Feedback and adjustment strategy** — ongoing, robust systems for identifying and closing gaps between vision and reality
6. **Tactical planning** — backward design applied to the work of reform itself

### 7.2 Mission as the Foundation

Most school mission statements are vague collections of aspirational language with no operational specificity. Wiggins and McTighe argue that a legitimate mission must be **measurable** and **operational** — it must specify what students should be able to do, not just what the school aspires to be.

The test of a mission: can you determine from the curriculum, assessments, and daily classroom practice whether the mission is being honored? If a visitor cannot distinguish your school's mission from any other school's mission by observing instruction, the mission is not operational.

The point of education captured in a single phrase: "worthy accomplishment, achieved by causing thoughtful and effective understanding that enables transfer."

### 7.3 The Curriculum Reform Argument

The most radical claim in *Schooling by Design*: **the traditional way of writing curriculum is fundamentally flawed.** A list of content, even with verbs in front of it ("know the causes of the Civil War," "identify the parts of a cell"), is not a curriculum — it is an inventory. Content mastery is not the ultimate goal of schooling; it is a by-product of a successful education.

The shift: curriculum should be written backward from **transfer goals and worthy tasks** that require students to use content wisely. The curriculum must be reframed around its key challenges — what Wiggins and McTighe call "cornerstone performances":

- In science: the design and debugging of significant experiments
- In history: the construction of a valid and insightful narrative of evidence and argument
- In mathematics: the quantifying and solving of perplexing real-world problems
- In world language: the successful translation of complex idiomatic expressions
- In writing: successful communication for specific and demanding audiences
- In the arts: the composing/performing/critiquing of sophisticated work

Like the game in sports or the play in drama, these cornerstone tasks embody key learning goals by requiring meaning-making and transfer.

### 7.4 The Spiral Curriculum

Education for understanding requires a curriculum that spirals — revisiting the same essential questions and performances at increasing levels of sophistication, year after year. This is not repetition; it is deepening. The same question ("What really happened and why?" "What's the pattern?") recurs from kindergarten through 12th grade.

Known tasks and recurring performance demands remove the harmful mystery from schoolwork, making long-term obligations clear for students and teachers. "It is no more boring for students to revisit key performances and big ideas than it is boring for good musicians or soccer players to play anew."

### 7.5 Seven Components of a Mission-Based Curriculum

Wiggins and McTighe specify seven components that a reformed curriculum document should contain:

1. **Long-term transfer goals and exit-level performance standards** — what graduates must be able to do
2. **Enduring understandings and essential questions** — the big ideas that anchor K-12 learning
3. **K-12 curriculum mapping** — showing how ideas, questions, and performances spiral through the years
4. **Cornerstone assessments and evidence collections** — authentic tasks reflective of key disciplinary challenges
5. **Analytic and longitudinal rubrics** — common criteria for evaluation and tracking growth over time
6. **Anchors** — exemplar student work (with commentary) illustrating various performance levels
7. **Enabling learning activities and resources** — strategies, tools, protocols, and troubleshooting guides

### 7.6 Formative Assessment as Design Principle

One of the most emphatic points across both books: formative assessment is not an add-on — it is a core design principle. The research is unambiguous: "some of the greatest gains in achievement require opportunities to get feedback and use it as part of the syllabus" (Black & Wiliam, 1998; Bransford, Brown, & Cocking, 2000).

The failure to build in formative assessment, in the name of content coverage, is self-defeating: it leads to less learning and lower achievement. Richard Light's Harvard Assessment Seminar found that "students say they learn significantly more in courses that are highly structured, with relatively many quizzes and short assignments. Crucial to this preference is getting quick feedback."

Curriculum must explicitly give teachers permission and guidance for deviating from the plan when results show it isn't working. Built-in "speed bumps" for checking understanding and reteaching are essential.

## Part VIII: The Teacher's Job — Redefined

### 8.1 Three Teaching Roles (Adler's Framework)

Wiggins and McTighe adopt Mortimer Adler's three-role framework for teaching:

| Role | Goal | Methods | When to Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Didactic/Direct Instruction** | Inform the learner | Lecture, demonstration, explicit teaching | When students need factual/procedural knowledge they cannot construct |
| **Facilitation** | Help students construct meaning | Questioning, probing, inquiry, Socratic dialogue | When the goal is understanding — meaning must be made by the learner |
| **Coaching** | Develop transfer and autonomous performance | Modeling, feedback, practice with increasing complexity | When the goal is fluent, flexible application in realistic contexts |

There is no one best teaching approach. The choice is determined by what the learning goals require. The problem is that most schooling defaults to direct instruction for everything, even when understanding and transfer are the goals.

### 8.2 What Effective Facilitators Do

1. Set up genuine problems, issues, and investigations for inquiry
2. Guide learners in making meaning (not tell them what the meaning is)
3. Refrain from excessive instruction — less telling, more eliciting
4. Model and encourage strategies and habits of mind
5. Work to make themselves unneeded

Harvard physicist Eric Mazur's research: replacing one-third of lecture time with concept tests and peer discussion produces dramatically greater gains in understanding than lecturing alone.

### 8.3 What Effective Coaches Do

1. Establish clear performance goals up front
2. Put students in realistic "game" situations early and often
3. Provide specific, targeted feedback on performance
4. Use just-in-time instruction (not just-in-case frontloading)
5. Adjust plans immediately in light of results

The baseball clinic analogy: Grant's son attended a five-hour pitching clinic where only one hour was formal teaching. The rest was pitching with feedback. The son said: "That's the longest I have ever listened to adults without getting bored!"

### 8.4 Three Teacher Roles When Not Teaching

*Schooling by Design* adds three non-instructional roles that are equally important:

1. **Contributor to curriculum** — not merely implementing the curriculum but actively designing, testing, and adjusting it based on results
2. **Researcher of student learning** — analyzing assessment data, examining student work, identifying patterns in understanding and misunderstanding
3. **Continuous learner** — keeping current with research, enhancing professional skills, conducting action research

## Part IX: The Job of Academic Leadership

### 9.1 Leadership by Results, Not Style

Wiggins and McTighe argue that too much leadership literature focuses on style, process, and personality rather than on what leaders must accomplish. Drucker: "The most common question asked me is: What are the qualities of a leader? The question seems to assume that leadership is something you can learn in a charm school. What matters is not the leader's charisma. What matters is the leader's mission."

### 9.2 The Core Leadership Question

"What does 'mission accomplished' require of me as a leader and us as a school?"

Academic leaders must:
- Ensure curriculum is designed backward from transfer goals
- Build and maintain feedback systems that cannot be ignored
- Create structures that institutionalize honest self-assessment
- Ensure all teachers have access to and study exemplars of best practice
- Make continuous learning an expected, job-embedded part of every teacher's role

### 9.3 The Feedback Imperative

Collins (*Good to Great*): "We found no evidence that good-to-great companies had more or better information than the comparison companies. The key lies not in better information, but in turning information into information that cannot be ignored."

Reform requires the coexistence of two paradoxical elements: unwavering commitment to core principles, and constant willingness to change direction based on feedback.

## Part X: Learning Principles (Schooling by Design)

Wiggins and McTighe propose explicit learning principles that all staff should honor:

**Principle 1:** Learning with deep understanding requires that students be at the center of their own learning — constructing meaning, connecting to prior knowledge, and engaging in authentic application.

**Principle 2:** Teachers' primary purpose is to cause learning, not to cover content. "I taught them, but they didn't learn" is a contradiction.

**Principle 3:** Transfer is the goal. Content mastery is necessary but insufficient. Students must learn to apply their learning in new situations.

**Principle 4:** Understandings cannot be given; they must be engineered so that learners see for themselves the power of an idea. More "uncoverage" than "coverage."

**Principle 5:** Learners need transparent priorities, clear goals, and a practical understanding of what excellence looks like. Rubrics, models, and exemplars should be available from the start.

**Principle 6:** Feedback and opportunities to use it are among the most powerful levers for improving learning.

**Principle 7:** Learning is most effective when students have opportunities for risk-free practice before summative evaluation.

## Part XI: Critical Gaps for theNORMALschool Design Team

### What Wiggins & McTighe Solve

They provide the most rigorous and practical framework available for designing curriculum, assessment, and instruction that produces genuine understanding and transfer. The three-stage backward design process, the six facets, the essential questions framework, the GRASPS task design tool, and the WHERETO planning framework are all directly operational.

*Schooling by Design* extends this to the institutional level — providing a blueprint for how mission, curriculum, teaching, assessment, leadership, and structures should be aligned.

### What Wiggins & McTighe Do Not Solve

1. **Personalization.** UbD provides a differentiation element (T in WHERETO) but does not offer a systematic framework for understanding individual students' aptitudes, identities, passions, or developmental needs. The student is a generic learner to be designed for, not a unique person to be discovered.

2. **Student agency and voice.** The framework is teacher-as-designer. The student's role is to engage with what the teacher has designed. There is limited attention to students co-designing their learning or pursuing self-directed inquiry.

3. **Whole-child development.** UbD is explicitly about intellectual understanding. It does not address emotional development, physical well-being, identity formation, or the social-emotional dimensions of learning — except insofar as the Empathy and Self-Knowledge facets touch on these areas.

4. **Technology and AI.** Both books predate the current AI era. The design principles are robust and technology-agnostic, but specific guidance on how AI tools might support (or undermine) understanding-focused design is absent.

5. **Non-academic competencies.** Character qualities, resilience, curiosity, courage — the domain that CCR and Robinson emphasize — are not systematically addressed. "Habits of mind" appear throughout as goals, but are not given the same rigorous treatment as intellectual understanding.

6. **Regulatory navigation.** Wiggins and McTighe work within the standards framework and show how to use standards intelligently, but do not address the specific challenge of innovating within (or around) state regulatory requirements like California Education Code.
