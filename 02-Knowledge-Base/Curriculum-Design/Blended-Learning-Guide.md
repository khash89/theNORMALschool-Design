## Introduction: Why Blended Learning? {#introduction}

### Author's Context

Catlin Tucker began teaching at Windsor High School in Sonoma County, CA (University of California–Santa Barbara graduate) and shifted to blended learning before it had wide recognition. The COVID-19 pandemic forced the entire education system to grapple with online and offline learning simultaneously — exposing a critical gap: most teachers had no framework for designing learning that works across both environments. This book provides that framework.

**The core problem blended learning solves:** Traditional instruction places the teacher at the front of the room, speaking to all students at once. This creates:
- No time for individual or small-group support
- One-size-fits-all pacing that leaves some students behind and bores others
- Little opportunity for student agency or differentiation
- Teacher burnout (no sustainable balance between whole-class instruction and individual attention)

**The core promise:** Blended learning redesigns the flow of instruction so teachers spend less time broadcasting information and more time working alongside students — while technology handles delivery of content, practice, and some forms of feedback.

## Community of Inquiry (COI) Framework {#coi-framework}

The COI framework (Garrison, Anderson & Archer, 1999; 2001) is the theoretical spine of this book. It was developed for higher education online learning but applies powerfully to K–12 blended contexts. It is grounded in **collaborative constructivism**: individuals construct meaning, and that meaning is validated through discourse, reflection, and social collaboration with others.

The framework has three overlapping presences:

### 1. Teaching Presence
**Definition:** The design, facilitation, and direction of cognitive and social processes to realize personally meaningful and educationally worthwhile outcomes. Called the "binding element" of the COI framework.

Teaching presence has **three roles**:

| Role | Description | Book Chapter |
|------|-------------|--------------|
| **Designer** | Architect of the learning experience — chooses models, structures lessons, selects technology | Chapter 3 |
| **Instructor/Connector** | Delivers instruction asynchronously and synchronously; connects students to content and each other | Chapter 4 |
| **Facilitator/Partner** | Coaches students through metacognition, discussions, and conferences; treats students as partners | Chapter 5 |

### 2. Social Presence
**Definition:** Learners' ability to project their social and emotional selves into an online or blended learning environment for the purpose of establishing trust and inviting open communication.

Social presence has **three dimensions**:
- **Affective Expression** — students feel comfortable sharing emotions, personal stories, humor
- **Open Communication** — risk-free expression and honest dialogue
- **Group Cohesion** — sense of belonging to the learning community

Social presence is addressed in Chapter 6 through the CASEL SEL framework.

### 3. Cognitive Presence
**Definition:** The extent to which learners are able to construct and confirm meaning through sustained reflection and discourse in a critical community of inquiry.

Cognitive presence is operationalized through the **Practical Inquiry Model** cycle:
1. **Triggering Event** — a question, problem, or dilemma sparks curiosity
2. **Exploration** — students investigate and gather information
3. **Integration** — students synthesize and make connections
4. **Resolution** — students apply knowledge to solve the problem

Cognitive presence is addressed in Chapter 7 through the 5E Instructional Model.

### How the Three Presences Interact

```
Teaching Presence (Design + Instruction + Facilitation)
        ↕                    ↕
Social Presence  ←→  Cognitive Presence
(Connection, trust,    (Inquiry, meaning-making,
community, SEL)        reflection, application)
```

All three presences are necessary. Strong teaching presence without social presence creates an impersonal, disengaging environment. Strong social presence without cognitive presence is enjoyable but academically shallow. Cognitive presence without social presence misses the collaborative construction of meaning.

## Chapter 1: Blended Learning Basics {#chapter-1}

### Precise Definition

> **Blended learning:** Active, engaged learning online combined with active, engaged learning offline to give students more control over the time, place, pace, and path of their learning.
> *(Adapted from Staker & Horn, 2012)*

Critical word: **active** — passive reading of a PDF is not blended learning. Both the online and offline components must involve genuine engagement.

**"All students" not "some students":** Blended learning must be designed so every student — regardless of ability, language proficiency, or learning style — can engage meaningfully. Not a gifted program. Not an enrichment track. A universal design approach.

### Why Shift to Blended Learning?

Three primary drivers of the shift:

1. **Sustainable teaching** — teachers are burning out from whole-class instruction models; blended learning creates a more manageable workload by redistributing how teacher time is spent
2. **Equity and access** — one-size-fits-all instruction fails many students; blended learning creates space for differentiation
3. **Student preparation** — 21st century skills (critical thinking, communication, collaboration, creativity) require active, inquiry-based, collaborative learning that blended models enable

### Key Benefit 1: Student Agency

**Definition:** Students' ability to make key decisions about their learning experience.

Student agency operates across three dimensions:

**Agency Over What (Content)**
Students choose which texts, media, or resources they engage with to learn a concept. Example: choosing between a video, article, podcast, or infographic to learn about the French Revolution.

**Agency Over How (Process)**
Students choose how they demonstrate understanding or practice skills. Example: writing an essay, creating a concept map, recording a video explanation, or building a model — all demonstrating the same learning objective.

**Agency Over Why (Purpose)**
Students connect learning to their own interests, goals, and questions. Example: a project-based learning proposal form where students propose a project aligned to their passions while hitting the required standards. This is the deepest form of agency.

**Why agency matters:**
- Increases intrinsic motivation (students invest when they have ownership)
- Develops self-regulation and decision-making skills
- Reduces behavior problems (students who choose are more engaged)
- Mirrors how adults actually work and learn
- Research (Patall et al., 2019; Pink, 2011) shows autonomy is a primary driver of motivation

**The zone of possibility:** Borrowed from Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development — a task is in the zone of possibility when it is within a student's ability to complete independently or with peer support. Choice boards and playlists keep students in this zone.

### Key Benefit 2: Differentiation

**Definition:** A proactive approach to designing instruction to ensure tasks are appropriate for specific learners or groups of learners, meeting students where they are in their learning journeys. *(Tomlinson, 2017)*

**Differentiation vs. Personalization — Critical Distinction:**

| Differentiation | Personalization |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| Teacher-driven | Student-driven |
| Teacher designs variations based on data | Student directs their own learning path |
| Teacher identifies who needs what | Student identifies what they need |
| Requires teacher knowledge of each student | Requires student self-awareness |
| Example: Three versions of a reading passage at different Lexile levels | Example: Student chooses their own text |

Blended learning enables both, but they are not the same thing. Effective teachers use blended structures to **differentiate** (teacher-designed) while building toward **personalization** (student-directed) over time.

**How blended learning enables differentiation:**
- Online stations or playlist items can be easily varied by readiness level
- Teacher is freed from whole-class instruction to work with small groups who need support
- Technology tools can adapt to student performance (adaptive software)
- Digital scaffolds (sentence frames, word banks, graphic organizers) can be built into online activities

### Key Benefit 3: Student Control Over Pace

In traditional instruction, the teacher sets the pace for the entire class. Some students are lost while others are bored. Blended learning allows:
- Students to move through playlists or stations at their own speed
- Faster students to extend their learning with enrichment activities
- Slower students to revisit content without public embarrassment
- Teacher to use check-in data to intervene precisely

Pace control is especially powerful in the **Playlist/Individual Rotation** and **Flex** models.

### Taxonomy of Blended Learning Models

Tucker organizes blended learning models into two tiers:

**Tier 1: Rotation Models** (teacher controls the rotation structure)
1. Station Rotation
2. Whole-Group Rotation
3. Flipped Classroom
4. Playlist / Individual Rotation

**Tier 2: Beyond-Rotation Models** (student has more control)
5. Flex
6. A La Carte
7. Enriched Virtual

These are covered in depth in Chapter 3.

## Chapter 2: Technology & the Online Classroom {#chapter-2}

### The LMS as Online Classroom

The **Learning Management System (LMS)** — Google Classroom, Canvas, Schoology, Seesaw, etc. — is the online environment where students access resources, submit work, communicate, and participate in online learning activities. Think of it as the online classroom.

**LMS Setup Checklist:**
- Welcome message with clear navigation instructions
- Organized modules or units (students should know exactly where to go)
- Embedded videos, links, and resources (not just file attachments)
- Clear instructions for each activity
- Submission portals for assignments
- Discussion boards for asynchronous interaction
- Feedback tools (comments, rubrics, audio/video feedback)

**Principles for the online classroom:**
- Reduce cognitive load — don't overwhelm students with too much at once
- Design for clarity — a confused student stops learning
- Make it visually clean and organized (use modules, not one long page)
- Test it as a student before assigning it

### The Four Cs of 21st Century Learning

From the **Partnership for 21st Century Learning (P21)**:

| C | Definition | Why It Matters |
|---|------------|----------------|
| **Critical Thinking** | Analyzing, evaluating, and constructing arguments; problem-solving | Prepares students for complex, ambiguous real-world problems |
| **Communication** | Expressing ideas clearly in written, oral, and visual forms across multiple contexts | Required in all careers and civic participation |
| **Collaboration** | Working effectively with diverse teams; contributing to shared goals | Most important work happens in teams |
| **Creativity** | Generating novel ideas, products, or approaches; taking intellectual risks | Drives innovation; can't be automated |

**Technology tools and the Four Cs:**

Paul France (educator and author of *Reclaiming Personalized Learning*) asks four key questions when evaluating any technology tool for classroom use:

1. **Does it maximize individual power and potential?** Does the tool amplify what the student can do — not just digitize what they already did on paper?
2. **Does it minimize complexity?** Does it make the learning clearer and more accessible, or does it add friction?
3. **Does it preserve or enhance human connection?** Does technology bring students closer together or further apart? Technology should never replace the human element.
4. **Does it reimagine learning?** Does it open up possibilities that were impossible without technology?

### The SAMR Framework

Developed by Ruben Puentedura (2013), SAMR describes four levels of technology integration, organized from lower to higher transformation:

```
TRANSFORMATION
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ REDEFINITION     Technology allows for creation of new tasks,   │
│                  previously inconceivable                        │
│                                                                  │
│ MODIFICATION     Technology allows for significant task          │
│                  redesign                                        │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
ENHANCEMENT
│ AUGMENTATION     Technology acts as a direct tool substitute,   │
│                  with functional improvement                     │
│                                                                  │
│ SUBSTITUTION     Technology acts as a direct tool substitute,   │
│                  with no functional change                       │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Examples across SAMR levels:**

| Level | Analog Task | Digital Version |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| Substitution | Write an essay on paper | Type an essay in Google Docs (same task, different tool) |
| Augmentation | Write and revise essay | Type essay + use spell-check, grammar tools, thesaurus |
| Modification | Write essay | Write essay + embed hyperlinks, images, audio; share with peer editors worldwide |
| Redefinition | Write essay | Create a multimedia documentary, podcast, or interactive website that was impossible on paper |

**The goal is not always redefinition.** Sometimes substitution or augmentation is appropriate (e.g., a timed assessment). The framework helps teachers think critically about whether technology is genuinely transforming learning or just digitizing the status quo.

**SAMR in practice:**
- Substitution: Using a digital version of a worksheet
- Augmentation: Using a Google Form quiz with instant feedback
- Modification: Students create video explanations using Flip (formerly Flipgrid) that classmates comment on
- Redefinition: Students collaborate with students in another country to co-create a bilingual documentary using Google Earth, video, and shared docs

## Chapter 3: Teaching Presence — Designer Role (Blended Models) {#chapter-3}

The teacher as **Designer** architects the learning experience. This means selecting the right blended model for the learning goal, grouping students strategically, and building structures that free the teacher to work alongside learners.

### Model 1: Station Rotation

**What it is:** Students rotate through a set of learning stations on a fixed schedule or teacher signal. At least one station is an online learning activity. Other stations involve offline activities (reading, writing, hands-on tasks, partner work) and typically one teacher-led small-group station.

**When to use it:**
- When you want to work intensively with small groups
- When content benefits from multiple modalities (reading + video + hands-on)
- When students have different readiness levels (differentiated stations)
- When you want to build in both peer collaboration and independent practice

**How it works:**

Typical 3-station setup (varies by class):
1. **Teacher-Led Station** — direct instruction, targeted questioning, real-time feedback; teacher sees students' thinking up close
2. **Online Station** — students engage with digital content (video, adaptive software, online discussion, interactive activity) using devices
3. **Offline Station** — students work independently or collaboratively without devices (close reading, writing, manipulatives, graphic organizers)

**Grouping strategies:**

| Group Type | Description | Best For |
|------------|-------------|----------|
| Needs-based | Students grouped by readiness/skill level | Targeted remediation or extension |
| Mixed skill-level | High + middle + low in each group | Peer teaching and support |
| Mixed speaking/listening | Quiet students with talkative ones | Building communication skills |
| Mixed personality | Introverts with extroverts | Balanced group dynamics |
| Random | Random grouping | Community-building; low stakes tasks |

**Transition tips:**
- Use a visual timer students can see (projected on screen)
- Establish a consistent signal (bell, music, verbal cue)
- Create a physical rotation map so students always know where to go
- Build in 1–2 minutes for quiet transition; don't count this against station time
- Post station instructions visibly so students don't need teacher redirection

**Template — Station Rotation (In-Class):**
```
Station 1 (Teacher-Led): [Group A, B, or C]
  Learning objective:
  Teacher actions:
  Student tasks:
  Materials:

Station 2 (Online):
  Platform/tool:
  Activity:
  Time:
  What students produce:

Station 3 (Offline):
  Activity:
  Materials:
  Individual or collaborative:
  What students produce:

Rotation schedule:
  Station time: ___ minutes
  Number of rotations: ___
  Total class time: ___ minutes
```

**Virtual Station Rotation template:** Same structure but stations are delivered via LMS links; students remain at their seats but move between digital "stations" (e.g., a Jamboard, a Google Form quiz, a discussion board, a video with reflection questions).

**Differentiation in Station Rotation:**
- Use needs-based grouping at the teacher-led station to provide differentiated direct instruction
- Offer differentiated online activities (different Lexile levels, different complexity)
- Build scaffold tools into offline stations (word banks, sentence frames, graphic organizers) for students who need support

**Maximizing benefits:**
- Use data from the online station to inform teacher-led station instruction
- Record notes during teacher-led station interactions to inform future grouping
- Use the offline station for collaborative tasks that build community

### Model 2: Whole-Group Rotation

**What it is:** The entire class rotates together between an online activity and an offline activity (typically in two rotations). Instead of splitting into small groups, the whole class does online work, then offline work, then optional additional rotations.

**When to use it:**
- When you want all students on devices simultaneously for a specific task
- When the learning benefits from full-class discussion after individual online work
- When technology infrastructure makes small-group device sharing difficult
- When you want a simpler structure than multi-station rotation

**How it works:**
- Rotation 1: Whole class engages with an online activity (video + reflection questions, interactive simulation, reading + digital annotation)
- Rotation 2: Whole class engages in an offline activity (Socratic seminar, written response, hands-on task, group discussion)
- Optional Rotation 3: Second offline activity, peer feedback, or synthesis task

**"Would-You-Rather" choice variation:** Instead of one path, give students two offline choices after the online rotation — e.g., "Would you rather discuss in small groups OR write a reflection alone?" This adds agency within the whole-group structure.

**Template — Whole-Group Rotation:**
```
Online Rotation (__ minutes):
  Platform/tool:
  Task:
  What students submit or produce:

Offline Rotation 1 (__ minutes):
  Activity:
  Individual or collaborative:
  What students produce:

Optional Offline Rotation 2 (__ minutes):
  Activity:
```

**Maximizing benefits:**
- Use the online rotation for individual work that feeds into group discussion
- Use whole-group discussions immediately after online work while content is fresh
- Debrief what students noticed during online work to build shared understanding

### Model 3: Flipped Classroom

**What it is:** Students watch instructional video content asynchronously (at home or during a free period) so that class time is freed for active practice, discussion, application, and teacher-student interaction. The "instruction" is flipped from class to home; "homework" is flipped from home to class.

**When to use it:**
- When direct instruction is necessary but you want to maximize class time for practice
- When students need to revisit content multiple times (video allows rewinding)
- When students have different schedules and can't all receive instruction at the same time
- When you want a differentiated version of instruction (different videos for different readiness levels)

**The Three-Part Lesson:**

**Part 1 — Pre-Video (Activate Prior Knowledge):**
- Students engage in a brief activity before watching the video to activate what they already know
- Examples: KWL chart, vocabulary preview, a provocative question to consider
- Done independently, often assigned as the "before" step

**Part 2 — Video (Instruction):**
- Teacher-created or curated video delivers the direct instruction
- Students take notes, pause and reflect, answer embedded questions
- Best practice: use a platform like Edpuzzle to embed comprehension questions directly into the video
- Video length rule: **1 minute per grade level** (5th grade = 5 minutes; 10th grade = 10 minutes)

**Part 3 — Post-Video (Apply/Practice/Discuss):**
- Class time is spent on what students couldn't do alone: discussion, practice, application, feedback
- Teacher circulates, conferences, and works with students who are struggling
- Students who mastered the content extend their learning

**Four video creation principles:**

| Principle | Description | How to Apply |
|-----------|-------------|--------------|
| **Segmenting** | Break content into small, digestible chunks | Create multiple short videos rather than one long video; use chapters |
| **Signaling/Cueing** | Direct attention to key information | Use verbal cues ("This is important..."), visual highlights, on-screen text |
| **Weeding** | Remove irrelevant information | Cut tangents, excessive details, and off-topic examples that distract from the core concept |
| **Matching Modality** | Pair spoken explanation with relevant visuals | If explaining a process, show the process visually; avoid mismatched text and audio |

**Three-step video creation process:**
1. **Script or outline** — write out exactly what you'll say; this prevents rambling and ensures clarity
2. **Record** — use Screencastify, Loom, Educreations, or any screen recording tool; Google Slides or PowerPoint as visual backdrop
3. **Review and refine** — watch the video as a student; cut anything unnecessary; add chapter markers or embedded questions

**Tools:** Screencastify, Loom, Educreations, Google Slides, Keynote, PowerPoint (for slides); Edpuzzle (to embed questions in any video); Crash Course (curated video library).

**Making it work:**
- Provide a note-taking guide so students know what to capture
- Build accountability into the pre-video and video steps (embedded questions, note submission)
- Use the class time gained wisely — if you just lecture again, you've lost the benefit
- Create differentiated versions for students who need more scaffolding or more challenge
- For students without home internet access, provide in-school time to watch the video

**Flipped Classroom Template:**
```
Pre-Video Activity (homework/async):
  Task:
  Time estimate:

Video Instruction:
  Title/URL:
  Length:
  Key concepts covered:
  Note-taking guide provided? Y/N
  Embedded questions? Y/N

Post-Video Class Activity:
  Activity type (discussion / practice / application / project):
  Individual or collaborative:
  Teacher role during this phase:
  What students produce:
```

### Model 4: Playlist / Individual Rotation

**What it is:** Each student has a personalized sequence of learning tasks (a "playlist") they move through at their own pace. The playlist is typically housed in the LMS and includes a mix of online and offline activities. Students self-direct their learning; the teacher circulates, holds check-ins, and provides targeted support.

**When to use it:**
- When students have significantly different readiness levels
- When you want to give students control over pace
- When you have content that works well as a sequence of tasks
- When you want to build student self-regulation and metacognitive skills
- When you want to free yourself to work with individuals or small groups

**How a playlist is structured:**

```
Unit Playlist: [Topic]
Must-Do Items (all students complete):
  [ ] Item 1: Watch video — [link] — Reflection questions
  [ ] Item 2: Read article — [link] — Annotate and summarize
  [ ] Item 3: Complete practice activity — [link or offline task]
  [ ] Item 4: Submit synthesis — [Google Form or doc]

Choice Items (students choose at least 2):
  [ ] Option A: Create a concept map
  [ ] Option B: Write a reflection paragraph
  [ ] Option C: Record a video explanation
  [ ] Option D: Complete extension problem set

Teacher Check-In Required Before Moving to Choice Items: Y/N
```

**Teacher check-ins:** The playlist model requires scheduled check-ins so students don't get lost or fall behind without intervention. Options:
- Set a "checkpoint" in the playlist that requires teacher sign-off before advancing
- Circulate continuously and check in with every student at least once per class period
- Use a sign-up board or Google Form where students request a conference
- Hold brief (2–3 minute) check-ins to ask: What are you working on? Where are you stuck? What's your plan?

**Differentiated playlists:**
- Create tiered playlists with the same learning objectives but different texts, videos, or tasks at different complexity levels
- Allow all students to see all tiers to reduce stigma; frame as "choose your challenge"
- Build in optional scaffolds (vocabulary support, graphic organizers) within items

**Mixed media in playlists:** Include a variety of modalities — video, audio, text, interactive, hands-on, collaborative — so all learning preferences are served within the same playlist.

**Maximizing benefits:**
- Build metacognitive reflection into the playlist (students track their own progress)
- Use playlist completion data to inform small-group instruction for students who are behind
- Have students who finish early become peer tutors or move to extension playlists

### Model 5: Flex

**What it is:** Students have significant control over the time, pace, path, and sometimes place of their learning. The teacher provides on-site support as needed (primarily in fluid small groups or one-on-one) while students self-direct. Most instruction is delivered via online platforms.

**When to use it:**
- Ideal for independent study, project-based learning, or competency-based progression
- Works well when students are working on different projects or at different stages
- Requires strong student self-regulation skills

**Key feature:** The schedule is highly flexible; students don't all do the same thing at the same time.

### Model 6: A La Carte

**What it is:** Students take one or more courses entirely online while enrolled in a brick-and-mortar school. These online courses supplement — not replace — the regular schedule.

**When to use it:**
- When a student needs a course not offered at their school (advanced, remedial, or elective)
- When scheduling conflicts prevent a student from taking a desired course
- When a student is recovering credits
- Summer school or credit recovery programs

**Key feature:** The online course is taken independently, often with a remote teacher; the physical school is not directly involved in delivering the online course.

### Model 7: Enriched Virtual

**What it is:** The primary mode of learning is online (mostly asynchronous), but students have scheduled face-to-face sessions with the teacher for specific activities — labs, discussions, assessments, or check-ins.

**When to use it:**
- Hybrid or hyflex course structures
- Students who cannot be present daily (medical, travel, etc.)
- Schools that operate on alternating in-person schedules

**Key feature:** Online is the default; face-to-face is purposeful and selective.

## Chapter 4: Teaching Presence — Instructor/Connector Role (Async & Sync) {#chapter-4}

### Asynchronous vs. Synchronous — Core Comparison

| Dimension | Asynchronous | Synchronous |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Definition** | Learning at different times and places | Learning in real time in a shared space |
| **Student control** | High (time, place, pace) | Low |
| **Social interaction** | Limited (threaded, delayed) | High (real-time conversation) |
| **Best for** | Content delivery, practice, reflection, writing | Discussion, peer learning, real-time feedback, teacher coaching |
| **Risk** | Isolation, procrastination, disengagement | Not all students ready at the same time |
| **Examples** | Pre-recorded video, online quiz, discussion board post | Small-group lesson, fishbowl discussion, live Q&A |

**Key insight:** Neither is superior — they serve different learning purposes. Blended learning requires intentional design of **when** each mode is appropriate.

### Asynchronous Video Instruction

The most common form of asynchronous instruction is teacher-created video. Video is powerful because it:
- Can be paused, rewound, and replayed
- Allows students to learn at their own time and pace
- Frees class time for active, collaborative, or high-order thinking tasks
- Can be differentiated (multiple videos for different levels)
- Is accessible for students who are absent

**How long should videos be?** The research (Guo, Kim & Rubin, 2014) suggests engagement drops sharply after the first 6 minutes for college students. For K–12: use the **1 minute per grade level** rule as a guideline.
- Grade 1 = ~1 minute
- Grade 5 = ~5 minutes
- Grade 10 = ~10 minutes

**Four principles of effective educational video** (Brame, 2015):

**1. Segmenting**
- Break a complex topic into multiple short videos rather than one long one
- Use a clear structure: intro → main concept → summary
- Chapter markers allow students to navigate directly to the part they need to revisit

**2. Signaling and Cueing**
- Explicitly direct student attention ("Here is the most important idea...")
- Use on-screen text, arrows, or highlights to emphasize key information
- Repeat critical points using verbal and visual channels simultaneously

**3. Weeding**
- Remove everything that does not directly support the learning objective
- Avoid tangents, excessive humor, or complex background visuals
- Less is more — cognitive load is real; every extra element competes for attention

**4. Matching Modality**
- Pair narration with relevant visuals (not redundant on-screen text of what you're saying)
- If explaining a diagram, show the diagram and narrate it; don't read a script
- Avoid putting full sentences on screen while also saying them aloud — forces split attention

**Three-step video creation process:**

**Step 1 — Plan**
- Define the single learning objective the video will address
- Decide on format: talking head, screencast, whiteboard, or narrated slides
- Write a script or detailed outline
- Prepare visuals: slides, diagrams, worked examples

**Step 2 — Record**
- Use Screencastify (browser extension), Loom, or Educreations
- Google Slides, PowerPoint, or Keynote for slide decks
- Record in a quiet space; use a microphone if possible
- Keep recording focused and concise

**Step 3 — Share and Embed**
- Upload to YouTube (unlisted), Google Drive, or directly to your LMS
- Embed using Edpuzzle to add comprehension questions inside the video
- Provide a note-taking guide alongside the video
- Track completion data if your platform allows

### Synchronous Small-Group Instruction

Once students are engaged in asynchronous work (online stations, playlists, offline tasks), the teacher uses that freed time for **synchronous small-group instruction**. This is the heart of the blended model — the teacher stops broadcasting to the whole class and instead has focused, high-quality interactions with small groups of 4–8 students.

**Four synchronous small-group strategies:**

#### Strategy 1: Hook the Group

**Purpose:** Activate prior knowledge, generate curiosity, establish shared context at the start of a small-group session.

**How it works:**
1. Teacher presents a provocative question, image, video clip, data set, or scenario
2. Students react individually (written or verbal)
3. Group discusses their reactions
4. Teacher uses the discussion to surface misconceptions, assess prior knowledge, and frame the lesson

**Example hooks:**
- "What do you notice about this data? What surprises you?"
- Show a 60-second video clip: "What questions does this raise for you?"
- Present a real-world problem: "How would you solve this? What do you need to know?"

**Template — Hook the Group:**
```
Hook prompt:
What prior knowledge am I activating:
What misconceptions I anticipate:
How I'll transition from hook to instruction:
Time: ___ minutes
```

#### Strategy 2: I Do / We Do / Groups Do / You Do (Gradual Release)

**Purpose:** Scaffold new skills and concepts by gradually transferring responsibility from teacher to students.

Based on Pearson & Gallagher's (1983) Gradual Release of Responsibility framework.

| Phase | Teacher Role | Student Role |
|-------|-------------|--------------|
| **I Do** | Models the skill explicitly; thinks aloud | Observes; takes notes |
| **We Do** | Guides practice; students contribute with teacher support | Attempt the task with heavy scaffolding |
| **Groups Do** | Monitors; provides feedback | Small groups attempt the task together |
| **You Do** | Available for questions | Independent practice |

**In a blended context:**
- "I Do" can be asynchronous (the instructional video) so class time begins at "We Do"
- "Groups Do" happens during online or offline station work
- "You Do" happens during the playlist or individual practice phase
- Teacher-led small group focuses on "We Do" with students who need more scaffolding

**Planning template:**
```
Learning objective:
I Do: What I'll model and think aloud
We Do: Guided practice activity
Groups Do: Collaborative task (station or playlist item)
You Do: Independent practice task
Assessment: How I'll know students can do it independently
```

#### Strategy 3: Real-Time Feedback

**Purpose:** Give immediate, targeted feedback while students are working — not after the work is submitted.

**How it works:**
- Students work on a task at the table (problem-solving, writing, analysis)
- Teacher circulates or sits with students
- As students work, teacher asks questions: "Walk me through your thinking here." "What made you choose that approach?"
- Teacher provides specific, actionable feedback in the moment: "Your claim is strong. Your evidence doesn't directly support it yet — what would make the connection clearer?"
- Students revise immediately based on feedback

**Why real-time feedback is more powerful than written feedback on completed work:**
- Students are still in the thinking process — feedback changes their thinking, not just their product
- Students can ask clarifying questions immediately
- Teacher can see the work in progress and catch errors before they solidify
- Less time overall (no homework pile to mark; revision happens in class)

**Planning document:**
```
Task students are working on:
What I'm looking for (success criteria):
Feedback questions I'll use:
Common errors I anticipate:
How I'll record observations for later grouping decisions:
```

#### Strategy 4: Present-Pause-Discuss (Interactive Mini-Lecture)

**Purpose:** Maintain student engagement during brief direct instruction by building in structured processing time.

**How it works:**
1. **Present** — teacher delivers 3–5 minutes of direct instruction
2. **Pause** — teacher pauses and poses a question or prompt
3. **Discuss** — students turn-and-talk (pairs), small group discussion, or quick write
4. Repeat cycle (typically 2–3 cycles per session)

**Why this works:** The pause-discuss cycles activate retrieval practice, allow students to consolidate new information with prior knowledge, surface confusion in real time, and keep attention engaged.

**Variations of the discuss step:**
- Turn-and-talk (partner)
- Think-pair-share (think silently → pair → share with group)
- Quick write (60-second written response before discussing)
- Concept attainment model: show examples and non-examples; students deduce the rule

**Planning template:**
```
Topic:
Segment 1 (__ min): [Content]
  Pause prompt: [Question]
  Discussion format: [Turn-and-talk / Think-pair-share / Quick write]
Segment 2 (__ min): [Content]
  Pause prompt: [Question]
  Discussion format:
Segment 3 (__ min): [Content]
  Pause prompt: [Question]
  Discussion format:
Total time: ___ minutes
```

## Chapter 5: Teaching Presence — Facilitator/Partner Role {#chapter-5}

### Teachers as Partners

Blended learning shifts the teacher-student relationship from **hierarchical** to **collaborative**. The teacher is not a dispenser of knowledge but a partner in learning. This requires intentional relationship-building and a restructuring of classroom power dynamics.

**Six principles of genuine partnership:**

| Principle | What It Looks Like in Practice |
|-----------|-------------------------------|
| **Mutual Trust** | Teacher is honest about uncertainty; admits mistakes; follows through on commitments |
| **Shared Purpose** | Class co-creates norms and learning goals; students understand the "why" behind assignments |
| **Open Communication** | Students can share concerns, confusion, or feedback without fear of judgment |
| **Timely, Focused, Actionable Feedback** | Teacher feedback is specific, timely, and tells students exactly what to do next |
| **Balance of Power** | Students have meaningful choices; teacher doesn't control every aspect of learning |
| **Shared Commitment to Learning** | Teacher models learning alongside students; admits what they don't know; asks students for input |

**Grading in a partnership:** Traditional grading can undermine partnership (students work for the grade, not the learning). Consider:
- Standards-based grading (grades reflect mastery, not compliance)
- Self-assessment as part of grades
- Revision opportunities (learning isn't one-and-done)
- Transparency about how grades are calculated

### Metacognitive Skill Building

**Metacognition** is thinking about one's own thinking — monitoring understanding, regulating effort, and adjusting strategy. It is one of the most powerful predictors of academic success. Blended learning provides natural opportunities to build it.

The teacher as facilitator builds metacognition across three phases:

**Before Learning — Goal Setting**

Students set explicit goals before beginning a task or unit. This activates purpose and primes attention.

Three "where/how/when" questions for goal setting:
1. **Where am I going?** What is my learning goal? What does success look like?
2. **How will I get there?** What strategies will I use? What might get in the way?
3. **When will I know I've arrived?** What evidence will tell me I've met my goal?

**Welcome routine for goal setting:**
- Students log into LMS and update a shared goal-tracking document
- Teacher reviews goals and uses them to inform who needs a check-in
- Goals are visible to both teacher and student throughout the task

**During Learning — Self-Assessment**

Students monitor their own progress during a task or class period:
- "Am I making progress toward my goal?"
- "Am I using the strategies I planned?"
- "Where am I stuck? What do I need?"
- "Have I checked for understanding?"

**Tools:** Progress trackers embedded in playlists; teacher check-in protocols; self-rating scales (1–4 on each success criterion).

**After Learning — Reflection**

Students reflect on what they learned, how they learned it, and what they would do differently:
- Exit tickets with a reflection component
- "I used to think... Now I think..." reflection (Project Zero)
- Glow-and-grow documents (what went well; what to improve)
- Portfolio entries with written reflection

### Academic Discussion Strategies

Discussion is central to both social and cognitive presence. The teacher as facilitator designs discussion structures that include all voices, push thinking deeper, and build community.

#### Strategy 1: Teacher-Facilitated Fishbowl

**What it is:** A small group of students (4–6) discusses a question or text in the center of the room while the rest of the class observes and takes notes. After the inner circle finishes, the outer circle responds.

**Why it works:**
- Models high-quality academic discussion for the whole class
- Every student is accountable (inner circle talks; outer circle listens and annotates)
- Surfaces diverse perspectives
- Can be rotated so all students experience both roles

**How to run it:**
1. All students prepare by reading or viewing assigned content
2. Teacher selects or students volunteer for the inner circle (4–6 students)
3. Teacher poses the discussion question
4. Inner circle discusses for 8–12 minutes; outer circle takes notes (what arguments are made? what questions arise?)
5. Outer circle responds: adds to the discussion, asks questions, offers counterpoints
6. Full class debrief

**Online fishbowl (for virtual or blended classes):**
- Inner circle participates via video; outer circle observes via muted video
- Use breakout rooms to separate inner/outer circles
- Outer circle uses a shared Google Doc to record observations in real time
- Tips for online fishbowl:
  - Assign note-taking roles to outer circle (record key claims, evidence, questions, moments of agreement/disagreement)
  - Use Flip (Flipgrid) for asynchronous fishbowl (inner circle records responses; class responds via video comments)

#### Strategy 2: Four-Corner Conversations

**What it is:** Four positions or perspectives are posted in the four corners of the room (or as four discussion boards online). Students move to the corner that reflects their view, then discuss with those who share their position, then engage with opposing corners.

**How to run it:**
1. Pose a debatable question or present a scenario
2. Label corners: Strongly Agree / Agree / Disagree / Strongly Disagree (or four positions on an issue)
3. Students move to their chosen corner and discuss with groupmates (3–5 minutes)
4. Groups share their reasoning with the full class
5. Students may move corners if their view changed
6. Debrief: What arguments were most compelling? What changed your thinking?

**Why it works:** Physical movement activates engagement; taking a position commits students to an argument; seeing others' positions models intellectual humility.

#### Strategy 3: Asynchronous Online Discussions

**What they are:** Text or video discussions that happen in an LMS discussion board or tool like Flip, where students post responses and reply to peers at different times.

**Five tips for effective online discussions:**

1. **Ask the right question.** Factual recall questions ("What happened in chapter 3?") generate low-quality discussion. Use questions that require interpretation, evaluation, or synthesis: "To what extent does the author's argument hold up in today's context?"

2. **Require evidence.** Students must cite specific evidence from the text, data, or their experience to support their claim. Post-without-evidence norms lead to opinion-trading, not learning.

3. **Require genuine engagement with peers.** "I agree with Samira" is not engagement. Require students to quote a peer's argument, explain why they agree or disagree, and add a new idea.

4. **Model what good looks like.** Post an example discussion thread yourself that demonstrates the quality of thinking and writing you expect.

5. **Use discussion as assessment data.** Read posts before the next class session; identify misconceptions, strong arguments, or patterns to address in synchronous time.

### Timely, Focused, Actionable Feedback

**Feedback is one of the highest-impact instructional strategies** (Hattie & Timperley, 2007; Wisniewski, Zierer & Hattie, 2020). But most school feedback is ineffective because it is:
- Too late (returned days after submission, when students have moved on)
- Too vague ("Good work!" or "Needs improvement")
- Not actionable (tells students what was wrong but not how to fix it)

**Effective feedback is:**

| Quality | Definition | Example |
|---------|------------|---------|
| **Timely** | Delivered while students can still use it — during or immediately after the task | Real-time feedback during a small-group session, not 5 days later |
| **Focused** | Addresses 1–2 specific aspects of the work, not everything at once | "Focus on your thesis statement and your use of evidence in paragraph 2" |
| **Actionable** | Tells the student exactly what to do next | "Rewrite your thesis to include your claim AND the main reason it's true" |

**Feedback modes in blended learning:**
- **Written comments** in LMS (Google Docs, Canvas comment boxes)
- **Audio feedback** (Loom video, voice memo) — more personal, nuanced; students can replay
- **Video feedback** — teacher records screen with student work visible, annotating and talking through it
- **Real-time conferencing** — the most powerful; see Strategy 3 in Chapter 4
- **Peer feedback protocols** — structured peer review with clear criteria

### The 4Ps Conferencing Framework

One-on-one conferences between teacher and student are the most individualized form of feedback. The 4Ps framework makes them efficient and consistent.

**The Four Ps:**

| P | Definition | What It Involves |
|---|-----------|-----------------|
| **Purpose** | Why are we having this conference? | Identify the specific focus: a piece of writing, a skill gap, a goal check-in, a behavior concern |
| **Preparation** | What does the student bring to the conference? | Student reviews their own work, identifies questions, completes a self-assessment form before arriving |
| **Post-Conference Plan** | What will the student do after? | Teacher and student co-create a specific next step or revision plan; student writes it down |
| **Parent Communication** | When should parents be informed? | If the conference reveals a significant concern or significant progress, share with parents promptly |

**Running a 4Ps conference:**
1. Student arrives with work in hand and self-assessment completed
2. Student shares what they think is strong and where they are stuck
3. Teacher asks probing questions to surface understanding
4. Teacher provides focused, actionable feedback
5. Together, teacher and student write a next-step plan
6. Conference ends with the student knowing exactly what to do next

**Glow-and-grow documents:** A running document where teacher and student each contribute observations — what's going well (glow) and what to work on (grow). Updated at each conference and used to track progress over time.

## Chapter 6: Social Presence — Dynamic Learning Communities (SEL) {#chapter-6}

### SEL and Social Presence

Social presence — students' ability to project their authentic selves in the learning environment — cannot be assumed or mandated. It must be intentionally cultivated. The vehicle is **Social-Emotional Learning (SEL)**.

Tucker uses the **CASEL Framework** (Collaborative for Academic, Social, and Emotional Learning) to structure social presence work. CASEL identifies five core SEL competencies:

1. Relationship Skills
2. Self-Awareness
3. Social Awareness
4. Self-Management
5. Responsible Decision-Making

These are not separate curriculum add-ons — they are embedded in how class time is structured, how students interact with each other online and offline, and how the teacher designs the environment.

**Connection to blended learning:** Many teachers initially worry that technology will reduce human connection. When designed intentionally, blended learning actually **increases** high-quality human interaction because:
- The teacher is no longer tied to the front of the room
- Students have more opportunities for peer collaboration
- The teacher has data about student thinking and can respond more personally
- Asynchronous tools give introverted students equal voice

**SEL Activity Template:**
```
SEL Competency:
Activity name:
Setup:
Facilitation:
Debrief questions:
Online or offline (or both):
Time:
```

### SEL Competency 1: Relationship Skills

**Definition:** The ability to establish and maintain healthy and supportive relationships, and effectively navigate settings with diverse individuals and groups.

**Why it comes first:** Without trust and genuine connection, learning cannot happen. Students cannot take intellectual risks, ask for help, or engage authentically unless they feel known and safe.

#### Activity: Daily Informal Conversations

**What:** Teacher greets students personally at the start of class — by name, with a genuine observation or question.
**Why:** Research consistently shows that students who feel known by their teacher learn more. This costs 30 seconds per student but signals: you matter here.
**How:** Vary the entry routine; sometimes use a question prompt displayed as students enter ("If you could learn anything this week, what would it be?").

#### Activity: Class Scavenger Hunt

**What:** A structured "find someone who..." activity, done at the start of a unit or school year, where students move around the room (or through an online form) to find classmates who share specific experiences, interests, or opinions.
**Why:** Breaks down social barriers, surfaces common ground, creates conversation starters across social groups.
**How:** Design questions that reveal genuine information ("Find someone who has lived in more than one state," "Find someone who is learning a musical instrument"). Debrief: What surprised you? What do you want to learn more about?

**Sentence starters** can scaffold participation: "I noticed that..." / "I was surprised that..." / "I wonder why..."

#### Activity: Random Autobiography

**What:** Each week or month, a different student (randomly selected) shares 3–5 minutes about themselves — their background, interests, family, passions — using any format they choose (slides, photos, video, spoken).
**Why:** Builds deep knowledge of each person in the community; humanizes students beyond their academic performance; honors diverse backgrounds.
**How:** Give students a sentence-starter bank to structure their autobiography. Post autobiographies in the LMS so students can revisit them. Reference students' stories in future lessons.

**Sentence starters for autobiography:**
- "I was born in... and grew up..."
- "Something most people don't know about me is..."
- "I care deeply about..."
- "My family's culture/tradition includes..."
- "I'm curious about..."

### SEL Competency 2: Self-Awareness

**Definition:** The ability to understand one's own emotions, thoughts, and values and how they influence behavior across contexts.

**Why it matters in blended learning:** Students must monitor their own attention, motivation, and emotional state — especially during asynchronous self-directed tasks. Without self-awareness, they cannot self-regulate.

#### Activity: Feeling Check-Ins

**What:** Brief opening routine (2–3 minutes) where students share or record how they are feeling before the learning begins.
**Why:** Validates that emotions affect learning; helps teacher identify students in distress; normalizes emotional experience as part of being human.
**How:**
- Verbal check-in: "Rate your energy from 1–5 and share one word about how you're feeling"
- Digital check-in: Google Form with an emotion scale (can be anonymous)
- Visual check-in: "Find a color that represents your mood and explain it"

**Teacher response:** Don't analyze or fix; acknowledge. "I hear that a lot of you are tired today — let's design some movement into our work." Noticing and naming creates safety.

#### Activity: Identity Exploration — "Who Am I?" Thinking Routine

**What:** Students explore aspects of their identity using Project Zero's "Who Am I?" thinking routine, which prompts reflection on multiple dimensions of identity (family, culture, values, interests, roles).
**Why:** Students who have a strong, complex sense of identity are more resilient and more engaged. Exploring identity also builds empathy — students recognize their own complexity and can extend that recognition to others.
**How:**
- Use the "Who Am I?" slide template (Project Zero)
- Students complete as a visual map or written reflection
- Optional: share with a partner before sharing with the class
- Display anonymized responses in the LMS as a class identity gallery

#### Activity: Mindful Breathing

**What:** A 1–3 minute guided breathing or mindfulness practice at the start or middle of class.
**Why:** Research (Bazzano et al., 2018; Van de Weijer-Bergsma et al., 2012) shows mindfulness reduces stress and improves attention. In a blended environment where students move between online and offline tasks, brief mindfulness resets help maintain focus.
**How:**
- 4-7-8 breathing: inhale for 4 counts, hold for 7, exhale for 8
- Box breathing: inhale 4, hold 4, exhale 4, hold 4
- Body scan: notice 5 things you can see, 4 you can touch, 3 you can hear, 2 you can smell, 1 you can taste

### SEL Competency 3: Social Awareness

**Definition:** The ability to understand the perspectives of and empathize with others, including those from diverse backgrounds and cultures.

#### Activity: Co-Creating Class Agreements

**What:** At the start of the year, students co-create (rather than receive) the class norms — what behaviors make learning possible for everyone.
**Why:** Ownership of norms dramatically increases compliance. Students who helped write the rules understand the reasoning behind them and feel invested in upholding them. It's also a lesson in democratic participation.
**How:**
1. Ask: "What do you need from this community to learn and grow?"
2. Collect responses (anonymous Google Form or sticky notes)
3. Cluster responses into themes
4. Draft norm statements collaboratively
5. Class votes/approves the final list
6. Display norms prominently (online and offline)
7. Reference norms when addressing behavior — "We agreed that..." not "The rules say..."

#### Activity: Perspective Taking

**What:** Structured activities that require students to understand an experience, argument, or situation from a perspective different from their own.
**Why:** Empathy is a learnable skill. Practice in perspective-taking reduces bullying, increases cooperative behavior, and deepens cognitive understanding of complex topics.
**How:**
- Text-based: After reading a historical account, ask "How would this event have felt from the perspective of [a different character]?"
- Role play: Students receive a role card and must argue from that perspective in a debate
- Empathy mapping: Students fill in what a character/person sees, hears, thinks, feels, and fears

#### Activity: Appropriate Consequences

**What:** When class norms are violated, teacher and students co-determine appropriate and proportional consequences — rather than teacher unilaterally imposing punishment.
**Why:** When students participate in determining consequences, they develop moral reasoning skills and are more likely to accept the consequence as fair.
**How:**
- Present a scenario: "If someone is rude to a classmate during a group activity, what should happen?"
- Small groups brainstorm possible responses
- Class discusses: What's proportional? What would actually help the person learn?
- Create a shared document of agreed consequences for common infractions

### SEL Competency 4: Self-Management

**Definition:** The ability to regulate one's emotions, thoughts, and behaviors effectively in different situations; managing stress, controlling impulses, and setting and working toward goals.

**Why this is critical in blended learning:** Self-directed tasks require self-management. Students who can't regulate attention, manage frustration, or persist through challenges will struggle in blended models. This competency must be explicitly taught.

#### Activity: Creating a Vision Board

**What:** Students create a visual representation of their goals, values, and aspirations — displayed in the LMS or posted in the classroom.
**Why:** Research shows that visualizing goals increases commitment and follow-through. Vision boards also make aspirations visible to the teacher, enabling more personalized encouragement and connections between learning and student goals.
**How:**
- Canva, Google Slides, or physical collage
- Include: academic goals, personal goals, values, images/words that represent them
- Optional: "This year I will..." / "I am becoming someone who..." framing

#### Activity: Why-How-What Goal Setting (Start With Why)

Based on Simon Sinek's (2009) framework: Start with **Why** (purpose/values) → How (strategies/approach) → What (specific goals/actions).

**Why it matters:** Most goal-setting focuses on "what" (outcomes) without connecting to "why" (meaning). Purpose-driven goals are more motivating and durable.

**The exercise:**
1. **Why:** "Why does learning this subject matter to me? How does it connect to what I care about?"
2. **How:** "How will I approach this work? What strategies will I use? What habits will I build?"
3. **What:** "What specific, measurable goal will I set for this unit/semester?"

Students record this in a goal-tracking document updated at each check-in.

#### Activity: Brain Breaks and Mood Management

**What:** Brief physical or mental breaks built intentionally into class time.
**Why:** Sustained cognitive work depletes attention and increases stress. Regular breaks improve focus, memory consolidation, and emotional regulation.

**Mood-management choice board:**
Give students 3–5 minutes to choose a mood reset activity that works for them:
- 5 minutes of free drawing or doodling
- Breathing exercise
- Walk around the room
- 5-minute free write about anything
- Listen to music with eyes closed
- Gentle stretching

The key: offering **choice** validates that different students need different resets. Mandating one activity for all (e.g., "everyone must do jumping jacks") can backfire.

### SEL Competency 5: Responsible Decision-Making

**Definition:** The ability to make caring and constructive choices about personal behavior and social interactions across diverse situations.

#### Activity: "If I Could Do It Differently" Reflection

**What:** After completing a task, students reflect on what they would change about their approach — not the outcome, but the process.
**Why:** Develops growth mindset and metacognitive awareness. Separates the person from the performance: "I made choices; I can make different choices next time."
**How:** Structured prompt:
- "If I could approach this task again, I would..."
- "The decision that most affected my outcome was..."
- "Next time, I will try..."

#### Activity: Engaging Students in Self-Assessment

**What:** Students formally evaluate their own work against explicit criteria before submitting or after receiving feedback.
**Why:** Self-assessment builds the self-monitoring skills needed for lifelong learning. It also creates dialogue: teacher and student compare their assessments, which surfaces misconceptions about quality.
**How:**
- Provide a rubric or checklist students complete before submitting
- Compare teacher and student ratings; discuss discrepancies
- Use self-assessment to identify revision priorities

#### Activity: Celebrating Each Other — Kindness Carousel and Celebration Circle

**Kindness Carousel:** Students write one genuine compliment or appreciation for each classmate on a rotating sheet. Collected and distributed at the end of a unit or semester.

**Celebration Circle:** End-of-class or end-of-unit routine where students share one thing they're proud of (their own work or a classmate's contribution). Explicitly names and amplifies good work.

**Why:** Gratitude and recognition have measurable positive effects on wellbeing (Cregg & Cheavens, 2021). These rituals also strengthen group cohesion — a core dimension of social presence.

## Chapter 7: Cognitive Presence — Student-Centered Inquiry (5E Model) {#chapter-7}

### Cognitive Presence and Inquiry

**Cognitive presence** is the learner's ability to construct and confirm meaning through sustained reflection and discourse. It is the intellectual heart of learning.

Cognitive presence is built through inquiry — the process of grappling with a genuine question or problem, exploring information, making connections, and arriving at new understanding. Tucker's book argues that blended learning, when designed well, is the ideal infrastructure for inquiry because it:
- Creates time for sustained individual exploration (async tasks)
- Creates time for collaborative sense-making (sync small groups and discussions)
- Gives students agency over how they explore and demonstrate understanding
- Frees the teacher to ask probing questions, not deliver answers

**Curiosity as the engine:** All cognitive presence begins with curiosity. Tucker quotes Matthew Lipman (2003): "Good thinking begins with wonder." The teacher's job is to design experiences that trigger genuine wonder — not just engagement in a shallow sense, but intellectual disruption that makes students want to know more.

**Inquiry cycle (from the COI framework):**

```
Triggering Event → Exploration → Integration → Resolution
(What sparks        (Gathering,     (Connecting,    (Applying,
 the question)       researching)    synthesizing)   resolving)
```

### The 5E Instructional Model

Developed by the Biological Science Curriculum Study (Bybee, 2014), the 5E model maps directly onto the COI inquiry cycle. Each "E" represents a phase of learning:

```
ENGAGE (Trigger curiosity, activate prior knowledge)
  ↓
EXPLORE (Investigate, gather information, observe)
  ↓
EXPLAIN (Articulate and communicate understanding)
  ↓
ELABORATE (Deepen, extend, apply to new contexts)
  ↓
EVALUATE (Assess understanding, reflect, demonstrate mastery)
```

The 5E model works across subjects and grade levels. It is not linear — students may cycle back to explore before they explain, or elaborate before they fully evaluate. The teacher designs experiences at each stage and uses blended tools to support both individual and collaborative work.

**5E Template:**
```
Unit topic:
Essential question:

ENGAGE:
  Trigger activity:
  Prior knowledge activation:
  Time:

EXPLORE:
  Resources provided (curated links, materials):
  Thinking routine or observation task:
  Time:

EXPLAIN:
  How students articulate understanding:
  Peer discussion structure:
  Time:

ELABORATE:
  Extension/application task:
  Complexity challenge:
  Time:

EVALUATE:
  Performance task or exit ticket:
  Self-assessment component:
  Time:
```

### Stage 1: ENGAGE

**Purpose:** Create a triggering event that sparks curiosity, activates prior knowledge, and poses the essential question.

**Why it matters:** Without engagement, exploration is reluctant and shallow. The engage phase should create cognitive dissonance — something unexpected, puzzling, or personally relevant that makes students want to know more.

#### Activity: Media + "See/Think/Wonder" Thinking Routine (Project Zero)

**What:** Students observe an image, video clip, or data set using a structured protocol:
- **See:** What do you literally observe?
- **Think:** What does it make you think?
- **Wonder:** What questions does it raise?

**Why:** Separates observation from interpretation; surfaces diverse reactions; generates genuine questions that can drive the Explore stage. Works across all subjects.

**Examples:**
- Science: Show a striking photograph of a natural phenomenon
- History: Show a primary source image with no caption
- Math: Show a surprising or counterintuitive data set
- Literature: Show an ambiguous opening scene from a film adaptation

**How:** Post the image/video in the LMS or display it in class. Students complete "See/Think/Wonder" individually (written) before sharing in pairs or small groups. The class compiles a list of "wonders" that become the inquiry questions for the unit.

#### Activity: Carousel Brainstorm

**What:** Multiple prompts or questions are posted around the room (or in a shared digital workspace like Jamboard). Students rotate through stations in small groups, adding their responses to each prompt.

**Why:** Generates many ideas quickly; every student contributes; visible to the whole class for shared understanding.

**How:**
- 4–6 prompts posted around the room or on digital sticky notes
- Groups rotate every 2–3 minutes, adding new ideas or building on existing ones
- Debrief: What patterns do you notice across the responses? What surprises you?

#### Activity: Sharing Prior Knowledge

**What:** Before exploring new content, students share what they already know — either verbally or in a digital tool.

**Why:** Activates existing schema, which improves new learning (Ausubel, 2012). Also helps teacher gauge starting points and identify misconceptions to address.

**How:** Google Form with "What do you know about...?" / "What do you wonder about...?"; pair-share; class mind map; "sort-it-out" activity (students sort statements as true/false/uncertain based on prior knowledge).

### Stage 2: EXPLORE

**Purpose:** Students investigate the essential question — gathering information, making observations, testing ideas. This is the heart of the inquiry cycle (Exploration phase in COI).

**Key design principle:** Provide structure without over-scaffolding. Students need enough guidance to stay on track but enough autonomy to make discoveries. Over-directing the explore stage eliminates the discovery experience.

#### Activity: Teacher-Curated Online Resources + "Connect, Extend, Challenge" (Project Zero)

**What:** Teacher provides a curated set of resources (not a single textbook chapter) — varied in format and complexity. Students engage with resources using the "Connect, Extend, Challenge" thinking routine:
- **Connect:** How does this connect to what I already know?
- **Extend:** What new learning does this add to my understanding?
- **Challenge:** What questions, complexities, or confusions remain?

**Why:** Curation gives students choice and agency. The thinking routine ensures active processing — not passive reading or watching.

**Resource types to include:**
- Video (explanatory, documentary, interview)
- Article (varied reading levels)
- Data set or infographic
- Primary source
- Podcast or audio clip
- Interactive simulation

**How to curate:** Build a resource library in the LMS, organized by topic. Include a brief description of each resource so students can choose strategically. Indicate approximate time to engage with each resource.

#### Activity: Making and Documenting Observations

**What:** Students observe a phenomenon, object, process, or artifact — in real life or through media — and record structured notes.
**Why:** Observation is a foundational scientific and analytical skill. Slowing down to observe carefully before interpreting is a cognitive discipline.
**How:** Field notebooks (physical or digital); photo documentation; annotated screenshots; graphic organizers with prompts ("I observe... I wonder... This reminds me of...").

Tools: Google Drawings, Jamboard, Whiteboard, sketchbooks.

#### Activity: Interviewing Family Members or Community Members

**What:** Students interview a person in their life (family member, community member) about the topic being studied.
**Why:** Connects school learning to the real world; validates students' home cultures as sources of knowledge; develops communication skills; generates primary source data.
**How:**
- Provide an interview guide with open-ended questions
- Students conduct the interview via FaceTime, Zoom, or in person
- Students complete a post-interview reflection: 3-2-1 (3 things I learned, 2 things that surprised me, 1 question I still have)
- Share findings with the class as data points

### Stage 3: EXPLAIN

**Purpose:** Students articulate and communicate what they have understood — consolidating learning through explanation.

**Research basis:** Students learn more deeply when they explain their understanding (Sparks, 2013). Explanation forces students to identify what they actually know (vs. what they think they know) and exposes gaps.

#### Activity: Recording Video Explanations

**What:** Students record short (2–5 minute) video explanations of a concept, process, or argument using tools like Flip, Screencastify, or phone cameras.
**Why:** Speaking aloud forces students to organize their thinking and commit to an explanation. Viewing peers' videos exposes students to multiple approaches and perspectives.
**How:**
- Give a clear prompt: "Explain [concept] as if teaching it to a younger student"
- Students plan their explanation before recording (outline or script)
- Post to Flip or LMS for class to view and respond
- Peers comment: "What I understood clearly...", "A question I had..."

#### Activity: Discussing in Small Groups

**What:** Teacher-facilitated small-group discussions where students explain their understanding to each other, build on each other's ideas, and identify areas of confusion.
**Why:** Peer explanation is powerful — students often explain in terms that make sense to their peers more intuitively than adult explanations.
**How:**
- Assign roles: facilitator, note-taker, connector (someone who makes connections between ideas), challenger (someone who pushes back)
- Use sentence starters: "I think this means...", "Building on what [name] said...", "I'm confused about..."
- Teacher circulates and listens — probes with questions but does not provide answers

#### Activity: Writing or Visually Depicting Explanations

**What:** Students write explanations (paragraphs, essays, reports) or create visual explanations (diagrams, sketchnotes, concept maps, infographics).
**Why:** Different students process and communicate understanding in different ways. Offering both verbal and visual options (choice!) removes barriers and honors different strengths.

**Visual explanation tools:**
- Concept maps (show relationships between ideas)
- Sketchnotes (combination of text and simple drawings)
- Frayer model (concept, definition, examples, non-examples)
- Venn diagrams (compare and contrast)
- Flowcharts (show sequence or process)

### Stage 4: ELABORATE

**Purpose:** Students extend, deepen, and apply their understanding to new contexts or more complex problems.

**Why it matters:** Understanding is not fully consolidated until students can transfer it — apply the concept or skill in a new situation. The elaborate stage is where "knowing" becomes "being able to use."

#### Activity: Making Connections with a Concept Map

**What:** Students create a concept map linking the main concept to related ideas, examples, and applications. Unlike a simple list, a concept map shows relationships between ideas.
**Why:** Concept mapping is one of the most powerful strategies for deep learning (Roessger, Daley & Hafez, 2018). It forces students to articulate how ideas connect — the hardest cognitive work.
**How:**
- Provide a central concept node
- Students add related concepts, examples, and connections with labeled relationship lines
- Discuss: "What connections did you make that surprised you?"

**Concept map vs. sketchnotes:** Concept maps emphasize relationships; sketchnotes emphasize memorable representation. Both are valuable; they serve different purposes.

#### Activity: "Tell Me How" Challenge

**What:** Teacher presents a real-world problem, scenario, or design challenge. Students must apply what they've learned to explain how they would address it.
**Why:** Application reveals whether understanding is surface-level or transferable. Also motivating because students see the real-world relevance of what they're learning.
**Examples:**
- Science: "Tell me how you would design a water filtration system for a community without clean water access using the materials available in this room."
- Math: "Tell me how you would use what you know about compound interest to advise someone on whether to take a car loan or save up to pay cash."
- History: "Tell me how the events we studied connect to a current news story."

**YummyMath** — a resource specifically for applying math to real-world contexts.

#### Activity: Review and Practice Choice Board

**What:** A menu of review and practice activities students choose from — all targeting the same learning objective but in different formats or at different complexity levels.
**Why:** Student agency increases engagement and allows students to identify their preferred mode of practice.

**Example choice board (3x3 grid):**
```
┌─────────────────┬─────────────────┬─────────────────┐
│ Write a summary │ Create a visual │ Record a video  │
│ of the main     │ timeline of     │ quiz for a      │
│ ideas           │ events          │ classmate       │
├─────────────────┼─────────────────┼─────────────────┤
│ Complete        │ FREE CHOICE:    │ Design a        │
│ practice        │ Any format that │ concept map     │
│ problems        │ demonstrates    │ connecting unit │
│                 │ mastery         │ concepts        │
├─────────────────┼─────────────────┼─────────────────┤
│ Write 5 exam    │ Teach a concept │ Identify 3      │
│ questions +     │ to a partner    │ real-world      │
│ answer key      │ (record it)     │ applications    │
└─────────────────┴─────────────────┴─────────────────┘
```

### Stage 5: EVALUATE

**Purpose:** Students and teachers assess the depth of understanding, reflect on the learning journey, and determine next steps.

**Critical shift:** Evaluation is not just about the teacher grading a product. Authentic evaluation includes self-assessment, peer feedback, and reflection on growth over time.

**Authentic assessment** (McTighe, Doubet & Carbaugh, 2020): Tasks that mirror the real-world application of knowledge and skills. Not "write an essay about photosynthesis" but "design a plant-based food system for your school using what you know about photosynthesis and ecosystems."

#### Activity: Project Choice Board (Performance Tasks)

**What:** Students choose a project format from a menu to demonstrate their mastery of the unit's learning objectives.
**Why:** Choice in demonstration of learning is the deepest form of student agency. Students are more invested in high-quality work when they chose the format.

**Example project options:**
- Podcast episode
- Documentary film (3–5 minutes)
- Op-ed article for a real publication
- Infographic for a public audience
- Design proposal with annotated blueprint
- Interactive website or digital presentation
- Community presentation or town hall

**All options address the same learning objectives — they differ in format and product, not in intellectual rigor.**

#### Activity: "Would You Rather?" Performance Tasks

**What:** Present two performance task options that are equally rigorous but differ in context, format, or medium. Students choose which one to complete.
**Examples:**
- "Would you rather write a letter from the perspective of a historical figure OR create a museum exhibit label that interprets an artifact from the period?"
- "Would you rather solve a complex word problem using algebraic methods OR design a real-world problem that has the same mathematical structure?"

**Why:** Forces students to make a genuine choice based on their strengths and interests; both options are equally valid demonstrations of mastery.

#### Activity: Three-Part Exit Ticket

**What:** A three-question reflection students complete at the end of a class period or unit.
**Why:** Combines metacognitive reflection with assessment data. Gives the teacher immediate insight into student learning and readiness for next steps.

**The three parts:**
1. **I learned:** [Summarize the most important idea you understood today]
2. **I'm still wondering:** [What question do you still have? What remains unclear?]
3. **I will:** [What will you do next — practice, research, ask for help, extend your learning?]

**Digital version:** Google Form with these three prompts. Teacher reviews responses before next class to plan small-group instruction (group students by common "still wondering" questions).

**Exit ticket sample:**
```
Three-Part Exit Ticket — [Topic]

1. I learned:
   _______________________________________

2. I'm still wondering about:
   _______________________________________

3. My next step is:
   _______________________________________
```

## Chapter 8: Taking Blended Learning to the Next Level {#chapter-8}

### Formative Assessment as the Engine of Differentiation

Blended learning without formative assessment is navigation without a map. Formative assessment tells the teacher where each student is so instruction can be precisely targeted.

**Definition:** Formative assessment is assessment *for* learning — not assessment *of* learning. Its purpose is to inform teaching decisions and student learning decisions, not to generate grades.

**When to assess:**

| Timing | Purpose | Tools |
|--------|---------|-------|
| **Before** (prior knowledge) | Identify starting points; group students; plan instruction | Entrance tickets, four-minute talks, sort-it-out, pre-assessment forms |
| **During** (checking for understanding) | Adjust pacing; redirect misconceptions; target feedback | Thumbs up/middle/down, Google Forms, real-time feedback, whiteboard checks |
| **After** (reflection and evidence) | Measure growth; plan next unit; celebrate learning | Exit tickets, sketchnotes, then-and-now charts, portfolios |

**Building formative assessment into the learning cycle:**
- Design assessments before writing lessons (backward design)
- Every lesson should have at least one during-learning check
- Use data from formative assessments to form flexible small groups the same day or the next

**Assessing prior knowledge — specific strategies:**

**Entrance Ticket:**
- 2–3 question Google Form students complete as they enter or log in
- Questions probe: What do you already know? What do you think about...? Rate your confidence on a 1–4 scale.
- Teacher reviews in real time during the opening activity to adjust instruction

**Example entrance ticket template:**
```
Topic: _______
1. What do you already know about [topic]? (1 sentence)
2. On a scale of 1-4, how confident are you with [skill]? 
   (1=never heard of it, 4=could teach it)
3. What question do you most hope we answer today?
```

**Four-Minute Talks:**
- Students pair up; one person explains everything they know about a topic for exactly 4 minutes (other person listens); switch
- Teacher listens and takes notes on misconceptions and gaps
- Follows with whole-class debrief: "What was challenging to explain? What seemed solid?"

**Sort-It-Out Activity:**
- Provide a set of statements about the topic — some true, some false, some debatable
- Students categorize: True / False / Unsure
- Reveals prior knowledge and misconceptions

**Checking for understanding during learning:**

**Creating an analogy:**
- "Compare [concept] to something in everyday life. What is it most like? Why?"
- Reveals depth of understanding — surface-level learners struggle to generate meaningful analogies

**Engaging in error analysis:**
- Provide a worked example with a mistake
- Students identify and explain the error
- Reveals whether students understand the concept well enough to recognize when it's applied incorrectly

**Rating your understanding:**
- Quick 1–4 scale at multiple points during a lesson: "Rate your understanding of [concept] right now"
- Teacher groups students by rating for targeted support

**Encouraging reflection — tools:**

**Sketchnotes:**
- Students create a visual note combining text and drawings to summarize learning
- More flexible and personal than standard notes; reveals how students are organizing information

**Then-and-Now Charts:**
- Two-column table: "What I thought/knew BEFORE" / "What I think/know NOW"
- Makes growth visible; reinforces that learning involves changing one's thinking

**"I Used to Think... Now I Think..."** (Project Zero thinking routine):
- Written or verbal reflection: "I used to think [concept] meant... Now I think it means..."
- Captures conceptual change over time

### Differentiation in Blended Learning

Blended learning is uniquely suited for differentiation because the teacher has more flexibility in how and when they work with different students.

**Three dimensions of differentiation** (Tomlinson, 2017):

#### Differentiating Content

**What students learn or how they access content:**
- Provide the same essential concept through different texts at different reading levels
- Offer content in multiple formats (video, text, audio, interactive) — some formats are more accessible to some learners
- Use adaptive software that adjusts difficulty based on performance
- Build scaffold resources into online stations (vocabulary support, graphic organizers, worked examples)

**Digital scaffolding tools:**
- Embedded vocabulary glossaries (linked definitions in digital text)
- Text-to-speech features
- Captioned videos
- Sentence frames for written responses
- Partially completed graphic organizers

#### Differentiating Process

**How students make sense of content:**
- Provide tiered tasks — same objective, different complexity or degree of scaffolding
- Offer varied practice formats (traditional problems, real-world applications, games, peer teaching)
- Use needs-based grouping in teacher-led station to provide targeted instruction

**Concept attainment model** (Bruner, Goodnow & Austin, 1956): Present examples and non-examples of a concept; students deduce the rule. This structure works across levels because all students grapple with the same puzzle, but teacher questions can scaffold or extend thinking in the moment.

#### Differentiating Product

**How students demonstrate mastery:**
- Offer choice boards with multiple formats for demonstrating the same learning objective
- Allow students to choose their audience (write for a classmate, a parent, a younger student)
- Permit varied levels of complexity in final products (basic demonstration vs. extended application)
- Peer assessment and self-assessment as integral parts of the product

### Designing for Balance

Tucker's concept of **balance** addresses a common concern: in blended learning, are students spending too much time on screens? Is there enough human interaction?

**The balance framework** — designers should consciously balance across these dimensions:

| Dimension | Balance Point |
|-----------|---------------|
| Teacher voice ↔ Student voice | Reduce whole-class teacher talk; increase student discussion and self-direction |
| Online ↔ Offline | Not all online; not all offline — intentional mix based on learning goal |
| Individual ↔ Collaborative | Both are necessary; don't always do one or the other |
| Synchronous ↔ Asynchronous | Match to the learning purpose |
| Structured ↔ Open | Some highly structured tasks; some open exploration |
| Teacher-paced ↔ Student-paced | Vary by task and student readiness |

### Synchronous vs. Asynchronous Decision Framework

Not every learning experience benefits from being synchronous or asynchronous. Use this framework to decide:

**Use asynchronous when:**
- Delivering foundational content that students can access independently
- Allowing students to move at their own pace
- Building in reflection time before a group discussion
- Enabling students in different time zones or schedules to participate equally
- Providing choice in how students engage with content

**Use synchronous when:**
- Building community and human connection
- Navigating complex or emotionally charged discussions
- Providing real-time feedback and coaching
- Needing to clarify misconceptions immediately
- Collaborative problem-solving that benefits from real-time interaction
- Celebrating learning milestones

### Thinking Outside the Box: Model Variations

Once teachers are comfortable with the standard models, Tucker introduces variations that give students even more agency:

#### Student-Paced, Free-Flow Station Rotation

**What:** Instead of rotating on a fixed schedule, students move between stations at their own pace — spending more time at stations where they need support and less time at stations where they are already proficient.
**Why:** Honoring individual pace is more respectful of where each student actually is. Not all students need the same amount of time at each station.
**How to manage it:**
- Clear instructions at each station so students can work independently
- Use a checklist or tracking sheet students manage themselves
- Teacher stays at one station (usually teacher-led) and students come to the teacher when ready
- Cap the number of students at each station to prevent overcrowding

#### Your-Choice Station Rotation

**What:** Students choose which stations to visit based on their own assessment of what they need — not all students visit all stations.
**Why:** Deepest form of station agency. Students must know themselves as learners and make strategic decisions.
**How to make it work:**
- Frame stations clearly: "Station A is for students who want to review the basics; Station B is for students ready to apply; Station C is for students who want to extend with a challenge"
- Require students to explain their station choices (builds metacognitive self-awareness)
- Teacher monitors choices — a student always choosing the lowest-level station needs a conversation, not a mandate

**Peer feedback** as a station option: One station is a structured peer review where students exchange work and provide feedback using a rubric or protocol. Builds community, develops analytical skills, and reduces teacher feedback burden.

## Conclusion: Five Reminders for the Journey {#conclusion}

Tucker closes with five reminders for teachers beginning or continuing the blended learning journey:

### Reminder 1: Think Big, Start Small

Don't try to transform everything at once. Choose one model, one unit, one class period. Get comfortable. Then expand.

**What "starting small" looks like:**
- Try a station rotation model with just one unit before committing to it all year
- Flip one lecture per week; gradually increase
- Create one playlist for a topic you already know well
- Pilot one asynchronous discussion before building a full discussion-based unit

**The risk of going too big too fast:** Overwhelm leads to abandonment. A small, well-executed experiment teaches more than a large, chaotic transformation.

### Reminder 2: Cultivate a Growth Mindset (And Use the Word "Yet")

You will have lessons that don't work. Technology will fail. Students will be confused. The "yet" mindset (Dweck, 2015) transforms these moments:
- "This isn't working" → "This isn't working *yet*"
- "I don't know how to do this" → "I don't know how to do this *yet*"
- "My students aren't ready for this" → "My students aren't ready for this *yet*"

Model this language with students. When students say "I can't do this," respond: "You can't do this *yet*. What do you need?"

**Building on small wins:** Document what worked. Share successes with colleagues. Each small win builds confidence for the next experiment.

### Reminder 3: Design to Free Yourself from the Front of the Room

The entire purpose of blended learning design is to reduce the amount of time you spend delivering whole-class instruction so you have more time to be present with individual students and small groups.

Technology is not a replacement for human connection — it **creates space** for it. When a video delivers the instruction students could receive from a lecture, the teacher is free to sit next to a struggling student, ask a probing question, notice who is disengaged, and build the relationship that makes learning possible.

**The paradox:** More technology, done right, means more human connection — not less.

### Reminder 4: Prioritize Student Agency to Remove Barriers and Provide Flexible Pathways

Our classrooms comprise learners with different skills, abilities, needs, interests, learning preferences, and language proficiencies. A single pathway rarely moves all students toward a firm learning goal.

**Intentional barrier analysis:** Before designing a lesson, ask:
- Who might struggle to access this content? (text complexity, language, format)
- Who might struggle to engage with this activity? (introversion, anxiety, pace)
- Who might struggle to demonstrate understanding in this format? (writing challenges, language, disability)

For each identified barrier, design an alternative pathway. This is not lowering the standard — it's removing the obstacle between the student and the standard.

**Student agency is the solution:** When students can choose the path that works for them, they self-select around their barriers. Agency is both equitable and empowering.

### Reminder 5: Embrace Your Role as the Lead Learner

You are not the expert who has mastered blended learning. You are the lead learner who is figuring it out alongside your students.

**What this looks like in practice:**
- When a lesson doesn't work, ask students: "What would have made this better?"
- Regularly collect feedback from students about their learning experience
- Share your own learning journey with students: "I tried something new today and it was hard — here's what I learned"
- Remain curious — there is always more to learn, always space to stretch and grow

**The most exciting part:** There is no endpoint. Every year, every class, every unit is a new experiment. The teachers who thrive in blended learning are those who never stop learning themselves.

## Glossary {#glossary}

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Adaptive software** | Software designed to adjust the level of rigor based on the user's performance to provide a personalized experience |
| **Asynchronous learning** | Learning that takes place at different times and from different places; ideal for learning that benefits from a high degree of student control over the time, place, and pace of learning |
| **Blended learning** | Active, engaged learning online combined with active, engaged learning offline to give students more control over the time, place, pace, and path of their learning |
| **Cognitive presence** | The learner's ability to construct and confirm meaning through sustained reflection and discourse in a critical community of inquiry (Garrison et al., 2001, p. 11) |
| **Community of inquiry (COI) framework** | A framework composed of social, cognitive, and teaching presences, grounded in the construct of collaborative constructivism — the belief that individuals construct meaning and that meaning is validated in collaboration with others via discourse, reflection, and social collaboration |
| **Crowdsource** | To allow members of the class, or the crowd, to generate and share information and ideas |
| **Differentiation** | A proactive approach to design to ensure the task is appropriate for the specific learner or group of learners; strives to meet students where they are in their learning journeys so everyone can be successful in making progress toward mastering the learning objectives (Tomlinson, 2017) |
| **Social presence** | Learners' ability to project their social and emotional selves in an online or blended learning environment for the purpose of establishing trust and inviting open communication (Garrison & Vaughan, 2008; Swan, 2019) |
| **Student agency** | Students' ability to make key decisions about their learning experience |
| **Student engagement** | Students' affective or emotional reaction, behavioral or academic and social response, and cognitive response or investment of mental energy and focus in learning activities and peer interactions (Borup et al., 2014) |
| **Synchronous learning** | Learning that takes place in real time in a shared physical or virtual space; ideal for learning that benefits from social interaction, peer support, or interactions with a learning community |
| **Teacher engagement** | A motivational construct reflecting teachers' choices to dedicate energy resources to their work; composed of cognitive, emotional, social (with students), and social (with colleagues) engagement (Klassen, Yerdelen & Durksen, 2013) |
| **Teaching presence** | The design, facilitation, and direction of cognitive and social presences in a course to yield high-quality learning; the "binding element" of the community of inquiry framework (Garrison et al., 1999, p. 96; Swan, 2019) |
| **Zone of possibility** | A spin on Vygotsky's zone of proximal development; describes a task within students' ability to complete on their own or with peer support |
