# The Power of Making Thinking Visible
**Authors:** Ron Ritchhart and Mark Church  
**Publisher:** John Wiley & Sons, 2020  
**Series:** Companion to *Making Thinking Visible* (Ritchhart, Church & Morrison, 2011)

---

## Overview

This book introduces 18 new thinking routines organized around three types of engagement — with **Others**, with **Ideas**, and in **Action**. It builds on the original MTV (2011) framework, which established the foundational six powers and four practices. The goal is not simply to "do" routines but to make thinking *routine* — a culture, not an activity. The book is grounded in Harvard Project Zero research and contains rich "Pictures of Practice" from classrooms worldwide.

**Core premise:** Learning is a consequence of thinking. Visible thinking = visible learning.

**Key insight:** The book is not a toolkit add-on; it is a framework for transforming how teachers perceive their role and how students experience learning. Making thinking visible is *the plate* on which all other classroom agendas sit.

---

## Part One: The Power of Making Thinking Visible

### Chapter 1: Six Powers of Making Thinking Visible

The six powers describe *why* making thinking visible matters — the outcomes it produces.

---

#### Power 1: Foster Deep Learning

Deep learning has three components:
- **Mastery** — command of content and discipline-specific ways of thinking
- **Identity** — seeing oneself as a learner and thinker in the discipline
- **Creativity** — ability to generate, apply, and transfer ideas

Hewlett Foundation's deeper learning competencies reinforce this model. Making thinking visible keeps understanding — not just content delivery — at the center. When students engage in the *process* of building understanding, not just arriving at the product, deep learning results.

**Key tension:** Content coverage vs. depth. Effective MTV teachers choose depth; they "buy more time in the long run" because students make connections across content.

---

#### Power 2: Cultivate Engaged Students

Three types of engagement:
1. **Behavioral** — on-task, following directions
2. **Emotional** — interest, belonging, relevance
3. **Cognitive** — genuine intellectual investment

**Research:** David Shernoff (2010, 2013) found that 75% of the variation in student engagement is explained by *classroom context*, not by student characteristics. Teachers have enormous leverage.

- Gallup found most US teens associate school with boredom and fatigue
- Goodlad (1983): Most classroom time is passive — listening, answering low-level questions
- Making thinking visible shifts the ratio: students become active creators of meaning

**Practical signal:** Students who groan when the bell rings (don't want to stop) are cognitively engaged.

---

#### Power 3: Change the Role of Student and Teacher

Traditional model:
- Teacher = knowledge transmitter
- Student = passive recipient

MTV model:
- **Teacher** = orchestrator of thinking, student *of students*, listener, documenter, questioner
- **Student** = active creator of understanding, resource for peers, contributor to the collective

**Vygotsky:** "Children grow into the intellectual life of those around them." If teachers model intellectual curiosity, students adopt it.

**Collins et al. (1991), cognitive apprenticeship:** Learning happens through immersion in expert practice — not just instruction. Making thinking visible = cognitive apprenticeship in action.

---

#### Power 4: Enhance Formative Assessment

**Critical distinction:** Formative assessment (FA) is a *practice*, not a task.

FA that is truly formative:
- Lives in ongoing listening, observing, and reflecting — not in quizzes or exit tickets alone
- Shapes instruction in real time
- Requires the teacher to suspend judgment and seek to *understand* student thinking, not evaluate it

**Wiliam (2014, 2016):** The feedback loop must be closed — teachers must act on what they learn about student thinking.

**MTV practices that enable FA:**
- Listening (empathetic, educative, generative)
- Documentation (capturing visible thinking for analysis)
- Questioning (facilitative questions surface reasoning, not just answers)
- Thinking routines (generate analyzable thinking artifacts)

**Quality check for routines:** "What have I learned about my students' thinking as a result of this routine?" If you can't answer — look for: focus on correctness, treating the routine as a task, weak content, absence of thinking models.

---

#### Power 5: Improve Performance on Standardized Tests

MTV improves test scores — not despite deep learning, but *because* of it.

**Evidence:**
- **WIS IB English SL (Washington International School):** Mean scores rose from 5.2 → 6.07 → 6.23 over three years
- **St. Leonard's College (Australia) ATAR:** Median score rose from 81.55 → 90.5 after four years of MTV
- **MIMS and PARCC** scores improved at multiple Michigan schools
- **Way Elementary and Bemis Elementary** showed gains on state assessments
- **INACAP (Chile, Sepulveda & Venegas-Muggli 2019):** Students in thinking-routine classrooms scored 1.3 points higher on exams vs. control group

**Mechanism:** Deep understanding is transferable. Students who build understanding through thinking don't just recall — they apply, analyze, and synthesize, which is what tests increasingly demand.

---

#### Power 6: Develop Thinking Dispositions

A **thinking disposition** is not just a skill — it has four components:
1. **Ability** — the cognitive capacity to think in a particular way
2. **Inclination** — the tendency to *want* to think that way
3. **Awareness** — knowing *when* a thinking move is appropriate
4. **Motivation** — caring enough about the situation to invest thinking

**Critical finding:** The biggest impediment to thinking dispositions is not lack of ability — it is **failure to spot the occasions** when a thinking move is called for. Students may know how to reason with evidence but not recognize when a situation calls for it.

**Implication:** Naming, noticing, and making explicit the thinking moves happening in the classroom builds dispositional awareness. Teachers who regularly say "I notice you're considering a different perspective here" are building dispositional radar.

## Part Two: Making Thinking Visible as a Goal and Practice

### Chapter 2: MTV as Goal and Set of Practices

#### The Core Goal

Learning is a *consequence of thinking*. Teaching for thinking means:
- Making the *process* of understanding visible, not just the product
- Treating the classroom as a community of inquiry where thinking is valued, visible, and actively promoted
- Engaging students as apprentices in disciplinary ways of thinking (cognitive apprenticeship model)

**Vygotsky:** "Children grow into the intellectual life of those around them." The intellectual environment teachers create is the most powerful variable.

---

#### The Understanding Map (8 Thinking Moves)

The Understanding Map is an orienting framework for both planning and instruction. It identifies eight moves that build understanding:

1. **Observe and Describe** — Notice details; attend closely before interpreting
2. **Build Explanations and Interpretations** — Make sense of what is observed; theorize
3. **Reason with Evidence** — Support claims with specific, grounded evidence
4. **Make Connections** — Link ideas to prior knowledge, other subjects, personal experience
5. **Consider Viewpoints and Perspectives** — Step inside other points of view
6. **Capture the Heart / Form Conclusions** — Distill essence; identify what matters most
7. **Wonder and Ask Questions** — Generate inquiry questions; identify what's unknown or puzzling
8. **Uncover Complexity** — Recognize nuance, tension, and multiple layers

**Use in planning:** Ask which thinking moves a unit requires → select routines that develop those moves. The Understanding Map prevents "routine for routine's sake" — it keeps the thinking goal in view.

---

#### The Four Practices of MTV

**1. Questioning**

The goal of questioning is to *facilitate* thinking, not to test knowledge.

- **Facilitative questions** — open-ended, follow up on student responses, promote elaboration: "What makes you say that?" "Can you say more?" "What's the question behind the question?"
- **"What makes you say that?" (WMYST?)** — described as the "magic question." It works in any context, requires students to provide reasoning, and shows the teacher values the thinking, not just the answer
- **Reflective toss (Minstrell):** Three-step move: (1) catch the student's meaning carefully, (2) toss back a probing or elaborating question, (3) repeat. Keeps the intellectual work with students.

**Three barriers to good questioning:**
- Asking questions to which you already know the answer
- Evaluating rather than following up on responses
- Moving on too quickly after a "correct" answer

**2. Listening**

Three types of listening:
- **Empathetic listening** — Attending to the learner's perspective, emotions, and frame of reference
- **Educative listening** — Listening specifically for misconceptions, confusions, emerging understanding
- **Generative listening** — Listening for new questions, unexpected insights, and teaching opportunities

**Three barriers to teacher listening:**
- Rush toward quick judgment ("that's right/wrong")
- Predicting what students will say and listening for confirmation
- Desire for closure — finishing students' sentences

Good listening requires: comfort with silence, genuine curiosity about student thinking, willingness to be surprised.

**3. Documentation**

Documentation involves four overlapping actions:
1. **Observing** — Watching students think, not just watching them work
2. **Recording** — Capturing the thinking in some form (notes, photos, student work, charts)
3. **Interpreting** — Making sense of what the documentation reveals about understanding
4. **Sharing** — Making documented thinking visible to others to deepen learning

Documentation serves multiple purposes:
- Makes thinking visible to students (metacognitive mirror)
- Provides material for formative assessment
- Creates a record of the community's intellectual journey
- Enables professional learning through protocols (LAST, CAD)

**4. Thinking Routines**

Thinking routines are simultaneously:
- **Tools** — chosen because they are right for a specific thinking goal (like choosing the right wrench)
- **Structures** — provide a scaffold for sequencing thinking and organizing discussion
- **Patterns of Behavior** — build classroom culture over time: "This is how we think here"

**Quality test:** "What have I learned about my students' thinking from this routine?" If you can't answer, check whether the routine was used as a task (to complete) rather than as a tool (to think with).

## Part Three: The Thinking Routines

### Chapter 3: Routines for Engaging with Others

These routines develop the capacity to learn from, with, and alongside others.

---

#### 1. Give One Get One (GOGO)

**Purpose:** Listen closely, engage in divergent thinking, seek perspectives from peers, build collective knowledge.

**Steps:**
1. Setup — introduce the topic
2. Individual brainstorm (timed or numbered — e.g., generate 5-10 ideas on your own)
3. Stand up and find a partner
4. Give one idea + explain its significance → Get one idea → Record
5. Repeat with 3+ partners (or for a set time)
6. Return to seat; process expanded list

**Key feature:** Students must *explain the significance* of the idea they give — not just share facts. This deepens engagement.

**Debrief:** What ideas did you hear that were new to you? What ideas resonated most?

**Variations:** Can be done with sticky notes; can be used at the end of a lesson as a synthesis tool.

---

#### 2. Ladder of Feedback (David Perkins, 2003)

**Purpose:** Give communicative feedback — positive, improvement-oriented, and supportive of mutual learning. Works with any work-in-progress.

**Steps (up the ladder):**
1. **Clarify** — Ask questions about anything you don't understand before evaluating ("Could you explain what you mean by...?")
2. **Value** — Identify what is strong, what you appreciate ("I value..." or "I notice...")
3. **Questions and Concerns** — Share concerns as questions, not criticisms ("I wonder if..." or "I'm not sure about...")
4. **Suggest** — Offer concrete improvement suggestions framed as possibilities ("What if...?")
5. **Thank** — Acknowledge the person's work and openness to feedback

**What makes it "communicative" feedback (Hattie & Timperley 2007):**
- Both positive and improvement-oriented
- Supports learning for both giver and receiver
- Does not reduce to evaluation or correction alone

**Applications:** Peer feedback on writing, projects, plans, presentations, prototypes. Works for teacher-to-student and student-to-student feedback.

---

#### 3. Leaderless Discussion (Heather Woodcock)

**Purpose:** Develop student ownership of learning, equity of voice, and the ability to formulate meaningful discussion questions.

**Steps:**
1. Students pre-read a text (done in advance)
2. Each student independently creates 2 discussion questions and reflects on their personal response
3. Student 1 reads their first question aloud + explains *why* the question is interesting/important to them
4. Group discusses (max 5 minutes; timekeeper holds the boundary)
5. Student 1 summarizes the key ideas that emerged
6. Next student repeats the process with their question
7. After all rounds, whole group reflects: What themes emerged? What questions remain?

**Teacher role:** Absent from the discussion itself. This is the "leaderless" aspect — students own the conversation.

**What it develops:** Students learn that good questions generate discussion. They learn to listen, build, and synthesize.

---

#### 4. SAIL (Share–Ask–Ideas–Learned)

**Purpose:** Build a community of learners who see peers as intellectual resources; provide early feedback on developing projects.

**Best used:** Early in project development — not as a final critique.

**Steps:**
1. **Share** (3-4 minutes): Presenter shares plan/draft covering: Why this? How? What specifically? Where am I stuck?
2. **Ask**: Group asks clarifying questions first, then probing questions ("What would happen if...?")
3. **Ideas**: Group offers ideas and suggestions — presenter records *without evaluating* or defending
4. **Learned**: Presenter states what they learned or will take away from the conversation

**Key norms:**
- The presenter listens and records during the Ideas phase — no debate or defense
- The group's job is to be generative, not critical

**Distinction from peer critique:** SAIL focuses on expanding possibilities, not evaluating quality.

---

#### 5. Making Meaning

**Purpose:** Collaboratively construct a shared definition or understanding of a complex concept. Groups of 5-8.

**Steps:**
1. Write the central word/concept on a large chart
2. Each person writes one-word associations (one per sticky note, unique — not repeating others)
3. Add-ons: Students build on others' words by extending, qualifying, or contrasting
4. Draw connection lines between words, labeling the relationship
5. Each student writes an individual question about the concept on a sticky note
6. Students write their own definition on a sticky note, read it aloud, and post it on the chart
7. Gallery walk to read all definitions

**Color-coding:** Use different colors for each round (associations, add-ons, connections, questions, definitions) to make the thinking process visible.

**Debrief:** What commonalities appear across definitions? What surprised you? What tensions exist?

---

#### 6. +1 Routine

**Purpose:** Use retrieval practice to lock learning into long-term memory; build collective notes collaboratively; replace passive note-taking.

**Research basis:** Karpicke (2012) — retrieval practice (recalling from memory) promotes more durable learning than re-reading. Brown, Roediger & McDaniel (2014), *Make It Stick*.

**Steps:**
1. After a learning episode (lecture, video, reading), push notes aside
2. Individually recall key ideas from memory (2-3 minutes, write without looking)
3. Pass paper to the right; each person adds "+1" — one new idea the original author didn't include
4. Repeat 2-3 passes
5. Paper returns to original owner; they add final additions
6. Whole group discussion of what emerged

**Key principle:** The struggle to recall is beneficial — it strengthens memory consolidation. Don't shortcut the retrieval effort.

**Advantage over traditional notes:** The collective note-building produces richer, more complete records than any individual would generate alone.

### Chapter 4: Routines for Engaging with Ideas

These routines develop deep thinking about content — interpretation, analysis, synthesis, and exploration of complexity.

---

#### 7. Question Sorts

**Purpose:** Help students distinguish between productive and trivial questions; develop inquiry skills and prioritize lines of investigation.

**Steps:**
1. Brainstorm questions — one per sticky note (individual or group)
2. **Sort by Generativity (horizontal axis):** How much will this question generate learning? (low → high)
3. **Sort by Genuineness (vertical axis):** How much do we actually care about this? (low → high)

**Four quadrants:**
- **Q1 (high generativity / high genuine):** Best bets — pursue these
- **Q2 (low generativity / high genuine):** Investigate briefly; can be answered but limited depth
- **Q3 (low/low):** Not worth the effort right now
- **Q4 (high generativity / low genuine):** Worth exploring to build interest, but not urgent

**Variation — Action Sorts:** Replace the axes with Power (how much impact) vs. Manageability (how feasible). Sort proposed *actions* rather than questions to identify high-leverage, doable steps.

**Connection:** Links to Rothstein & Santana (2011), *Make Just One Change* — teaching students to ask their own questions.

---

#### 8. Peeling the Fruit (David Perkins)

**Purpose:** Build layered, comprehensive understanding of a concept, text, object, or topic by moving from surface observation to core meaning.

**Graphic organizer:** Concentric circles

**Layers (outside to inside):**
1. **Skin (outer ring):** What do you notice? Prior knowledge, surface features, first observations
2. **Membrane (next ring):** Questions and wonderings — what puzzles or intrigues you?
3. **Substance (large middle ring):** Connections (to other ideas, texts, experiences), Explanations (theories, interpretations), Perspectives (whose viewpoint? what do others see?)
4. **Core (center):** Capture the essence — what is the heart of this? Your core understanding
5. **Step Back:** What new complexities have you uncovered? What layers did you discover?

**The substance layer:** Can be broken into three sub-phases for beginners:
- Connections first
- Explanations second
- Perspectives third
(Experienced learners often move fluidly between these — no fixed order required)

**Assessment note:** Don't formally grade routine work — but students who engage fully are positioned to perform better on graded assessments. The purpose is to build, not to test.

**Uses:**
- Single-session deep analysis (poem, primary source, image, artifact)
- Evolving unit document — layers added over multiple lessons
- End-of-unit synthesis — return to the core after extensive study

**Tips:**
- First time: do whole class with teacher as documenter
- Encourage questions in the membrane ring — students who skip to examples are avoiding inquiry
- Core statements don't need to match "textbook definitions" — personal understanding is the goal

**Picture of Practice:** Tom Heilman (Washington International School) uses it for poetry analysis — groups produce chart-paper organizers, present their core understanding, and write analytic essays as follow-up homework.

---

#### 9. The Story Routine: Main–Side–Hidden

**Purpose:** Analyze documents, events, images, or situations at increasing depth by constructing three distinct narratives.

**Steps:**
1. **Setup:** Examine the source material carefully — slow looking, attending to details and nuance
2. **Main:** Identify the central story — the core narrative, most apparent and prominent
3. **Side:** What else is happening around the edges? Who are the other actors? What peripheral issues, influences, or factors are at play?
4. **Hidden:** What is obscured, neglected, or happening below the surface — the story we aren't readily aware of initially?
5. **Share:** Class shares main (often consensus), side (wide variety), and hidden stories; use WMYST? for elaboration

**Key move for hidden stories:** "Why do you think that story has been hidden?" Pushes into motive, power, and systemic analysis.

**Assessment:**
- Main story = ability to grasp central narrative
- Side stories = ability to identify peripheral actors/factors and connect them to the main
- Hidden story = speculative but evidence-grounded; requires flexible thinking and perspective-taking

**Uses:**
- History and primary sources (developed with Densho for Japanese American incarceration documents)
- Social-emotional learning (counselor uses drawings to uncover hidden dynamics in student behavior)
- Accounting/data (Steve Davis and Darrel Cruse, Penleigh and Essendon Grammar): adapted as Main=what data says, Side=accounting assumptions that apply, Hidden=effect on decision-making. Students' written responses improved significantly — lower-middle students rose from 40-55% to 60-75%; 2-3 students per class reached 100% responses (vs. 1-2 out of 300 externally marked papers previously).

**Tip:** Watch Tracy Chevalier's TED Talk on finding the story in a painting — demonstrates how hidden stories are built on evidence, not imagination.

---

#### 10. Beauty & Truth

**Purpose:** Explore the complex relationship between aesthetic/appealing elements and factual/real elements in journalism, art, and complex global issues. Developed by Project Zero colleagues Veronica Boix Mansilla and Flossie Chua.

**Steps:**
1. **Setup:** Present source material; allow silent, close looking/reading
2. **Beauty:** Where can you find beauty in this? (broad definition: aesthetically appealing, inspiring, powerful, valuable)
3. **Truth:** Where can you find truth? (facts, realities, universals — broadly defined)
4. **Reveal:** How might beauty *reveal* truth? Where does beauty illuminate or bring truth to light?
5. **Conceal:** How might beauty *conceal* truth? Where does beauty obscure or make it harder to see truth?
6. **Sharing:** Surface what wasn't initially apparent; close with "How has your understanding changed?"

**Facilitative question throughout:** "What makes you say that?"

**Don't over-define beauty or truth at the outset** — students bring richer interpretations than teachers expect. Let the concepts expand through use.

**Applications:**
- Journalism (photographic and print) — original context
- Works of art — aesthetic and ethical analysis
- Complex issues (energy, climate, identity)
- Literature and poetry
- Geography and social studies

**Tips:**
- Split into two parts: steps 2-3 as whole class (expanding inventory), then steps 4-5 as discussion
- Can invert: "How might truth reveal beauty?" or "How might truth conceal beauty?"
- Pairs well with Give One Get One and Headlines (MTV 2011)

---

#### 11. NDA: Name–Describe–Act

**Purpose:** Develop close observation, working memory, visual memory, and expressive vocabulary (especially useful for second-language learners). Creates a "motivated brain state" through curiosity activation.

**Steps:**
1. **Setup:** Present image for exactly one minute. Students observe silently. Remove image from sight.
2. **Name:** From memory, list everything you can (nouns — specific things you could point to)
3. **Describe:** For each named object, add adjectives — how would you describe it?
4. **Act:** For each named object, how is it acting? What verbs apply? What is its function, relationship to other parts, contribution to the whole?
5. **Reveal:** Put the image back up; students confirm memories, discuss surprises
6. **Share:** Compare lists — who noticed things others didn't? Which words were most vivid?

**Memory science connection (Briggs 2014, 2017; Schwartz 2015):**
- Retrieval from memory (step 2) builds long-term memory consolidation
- Visual memory (step 3) activates imagery as a learning strategy
- Chunking (grouping related objects) reduces memory load — why we remember phone numbers in groups

**Adcock "motivated brain state":** When information follows NDA on an intriguing image, students are more motivated and more likely to retain it.

**Variation for text:** Instead of an image, use a passage. Name = key characters/objects. Describe = characterize them. Act = what role do they play? (Tara Surloff, AP Spanish, Carmen Laforet story)

**Variation for unit review:** Name = everything learned; organize into categories; Describe = key characteristics; Act = significance, relationship to other ideas.

**Tips:**
- Resist showing the image again during Name phase — the struggle to recall is the learning
- Works as a game; leverage fun to lower anxiety
- Young students: do verbally (turn-and-talk naming; teacher records; act it out)

---

#### 12. Take Note

**Purpose:** Engage students actively with content during or after a learning episode; provide teachers with real-time formative assessment; replace passive note-copying with reflective distillation.

**Inspired by:** Harvard physics professor Eric Mazur's "just-in-time teaching" technique for flipped classrooms.

**The four prompts (students choose ONE):**
1. What is the most important point?
2. What are you finding challenging, puzzling, or difficult to understand?
3. What question would you most like to discuss?
4. What is something you found interesting?

**The variety of prompts ensures every student has an entry point** — the confused student, the engaged student, and the bored student all have a prompt that works for them.

**Steps:**
1. Encourage active listening without note-taking during the episode
2. At regular intervals (every 10-15 minutes for lectures) or at end: distribute index cards; students respond anonymously to one prompt
3. Sharing options: small groups discuss; cards passed to another group and randomly redistributed for response; teacher collects and synthesizes; exit ticket reviewed before next class

**Brookfield and Preskill's "structured silence" (2005):** Brief pauses for reflection are essential in learning conversations — students need time to process before responding.

**Formative power:** Anonymous collection reveals what students won't say aloud. "The great thing about this routine is it gave me feedback about areas we needed to work on as a class." (Lee Crossley)

**Tips:**
- Show students you actually *use* their responses — shape next-day instruction around patterns you find
- Don't require all four prompts — the choice of one is the scaffold. Requiring all four turns it into a worksheet.
- Content must be rich and complex enough to generate varied, substantive responses

### Chapter 5: Routines for Engaging in Action

These routines develop the capacity to *do something* with learning — to reason through investigations, reflect on experience, analyze work, and connect ideas to action in the world.

---

#### 13. PG&E: Predict–Gather–Explain

**Purpose:** Guide inquiry and investigations so students do more than follow steps — they theorize, design, collect data, and make sense of results. Suitable for science, math, and any inquiry-based learning.

**Steps:**
1. **Setup:** Clarify the question, investigation, or problem; make sure students understand what is being asked
2. **Predict:** What do you think will happen? Why? (Write down prediction + reasoning before gathering any data)
3. **Gather:** How will we collect the data? Students *design* the investigation — not just execute it. What do we need to measure? How will we record?
4. **Explain:** How do we make sense of the data? Why did we get these results? How does it relate to our prediction? What would we need to investigate further?
5. **Share:** Groups report on data collection methods and explanations

**Distinction from Predict-Observe-Explain (White & Gunstone 1992):** The "Gather" phase explicitly addresses *design* of the investigation — appropriate for larger, extended inquiries, not just single experiments.

**Key pedagogical move:** Let students design data collection (even imperfectly). The messiness of real data organization is learning. Standardization issues will emerge naturally when groups try to combine data.

**Examples:**
- PE: "How does adding or removing forces affect our high jump?" (Matt Magown, UNIS Hanoi)
- Geography: Water sample acidity testing with red cabbage pH indicator (Alice Vigors, Australia)
- Math: Rolling three dice 100 times — what sum comes up most often? (Michael Upton, Holy Trinity Primary — detailed example of students discovering probability through data organization and the "Why?" of their results)

---

#### 14. ESP+I: Experience–Struggles–Puzzles–Insights

**Purpose:** Make reflection substantive by going beyond "report what you did" to identify key actions, productive struggles, lingering questions, and genuine insights. Used after extended projects or investigations.

**Research finding:** In Sweden's Visible Thinking project, students reported that reflection was both the most common thinking they did in school AND the least useful. The discrepancy: most "reflection" was just reporting, not real thinking.

**Components:**
- **Experience:** What key actions or activities *moved your thinking and learning forward*? (Not a list of what you did — a selection of what mattered)
- **Struggles:** What challenges did you face and how did you work through them? (Productive struggle — overcoming, not just encountering)
- **Puzzles:** What new questions came up along the way that you still have? What remains unresolved?
- **+ Insights:** What new understanding did you gain? About the topic? About the process? About yourself as a learner?

**Distinction (tip):** Struggle = something worked through and resolved. Puzzle = still lingering, representing future inquiry.

**Steps:**
1. Individuals (or collaborative teams) write responses — template with four sections or paper divided into fourths
2. Table groups discuss 2 minutes per section
3. Groups share highlights; teacher documents class patterns
4. Final individual: fill in Insights (+I) based on group discussion

**Timing:** Can be used mid-project (not just at the end) as a "re-energizer" to restore self-direction and momentum (Jess Alfaro, Summit School, 3rd-grade math design project).

**What to look for:** Students who have no puzzles probably completed the project *for the teacher* rather than as a learner. No struggles may mean the task wasn't challenging enough.

#### 15. Be-Sure-To

**Purpose:** Develop self-directed learners who can identify, internalize, and articulate the specific actions needed to produce high-quality work — rather than following teacher dictates.

**Origin:** Julie Manley (Bellevue School District, WA) noticed students were executing skill instruction without *ownership* — they repeated what they were told without understanding why it mattered.

**Steps:**
1. **Setup:** Review exemplars of high-quality work OR brainstorm key elements of quality with the class. Produce a collective documentation of features, skills, and actions.
2. **Be-Sure-To statements:** Each student asks: "What must *I* be sure to do to achieve this goal?" Records 2-3 personal, specific, action-oriented commitments.
3. **Be-Sure-To-Avoid statements:** What problematic features, likely errors, or mistakes do I want to avoid?
4. **Share:** Go-around, think-pair-share, or table groups — sharing expands repertoire of possibilities

**Key principle:** Students write their own Be-Sure-To statements based on their *own needs and gaps* — not the teacher's priorities. The teacher's role is to facilitate the analysis of exemplars, then step back.

**Caution about rubrics:** Rubrics used as checklists produce compliance, not ownership. Focus on the *actions* behind each criterion — what kind of thinking does this require? What would it look like in practice?

**Do not collect Be-Sure-To statements as exit tickets** — this turns it into compliance for the teacher's benefit. Keep them in students' hands.

**What it enables:** FA from student-generated goals. Teachers identify who needs support and what mini-lessons to run based on what students identify as challenges. "It helped me listen to them first, rather than directing them."

#### 16. What? So What? Now What?

**Purpose:** Develop self-awareness and responsible action by systematically identifying events/observations, making meaning of them, and planning concrete next steps.

**Origin:** Gene Thompson-Grove (National School Reform Faculty / School Reform Initiative) — originally designed for teacher professional learning groups.

**Steps:**
1. **What?** Describe what happened, was observed, or was done. What key events, ideas, or actions stand out? (Ground in specifics, not generalities)
2. **So What?** Why does it matter? What is its significance, purpose, or effect? What connections emerge? Why did those events happen?
3. **Now What?** Given the above, what actions are worth taking? Next steps, goals, plans, implications.
4. **Share:** Pair or small group; whole-class documentation of patterns

**Multiple uses:**
- Reflection on a learning unit (wrap-up or mid-unit)
- Analysis of a text (fiction = predict "Now What?"; nonfiction = identify actions to take)
- Professional development (teachers reflecting on practice)
- Coaching and counseling (probing with "So what?" and "Now what?" to help someone make sense of an experience)

**Example:** Amy Richardson (music teacher, Sydney) used it with 11th graders studying jazz history after sensing student disengagement. The "So what?" phase gave space for disgruntlement while pushing toward relevance. Students shifted from "American history doesn't matter to me" to articulating the moral imperative of understanding jazz as resistance, resilience, and identity. Follow-up email responses were rich and personal.

**Assessment focus:** Do students' "So what?" responses move beyond personal preference to consider multiple perspectives, causal relationships, and broader significance? Does the "Now what?" connect specifically to the "So what?" — or is it generic?

---

#### 17. The 3 Y's

**Purpose:** Help students make personal, community, and global connections to issues and topics; develop significance-finding as a thinking disposition; prepare students for global competence.

**Origin:** Project Zero colleagues Veronica Boix Mansilla and Flossie Chua (Interdisciplinary and Global Studies initiative, Independent Schools Victoria, Australia).

**Steps:**
1. **Setup:** Introduce the topic with a compelling provocation (short video, image, data, quote, story)
2. **Why might this matter to ME?** Connect personally — how does this issue touch my life, directly or indirectly?
3. **Why might this matter to my COMMUNITY?** (Define community as fits the context: class, school, town, nation)
4. **Why might this matter to the WORLD?** Both now and in the future — global reach and implications

**Recording options:** Three columns, Y-chart, or concentric circles (self → community → world)

**Variations:**
- Can reverse the order for historical events (start global, move to personal)
- Can be used at the beginning of a unit to build investment or at the end as synthesis
- Naturally leads into The 4 If's

**Assessment:** Are students able to consider indirect influences? Can they take on communal perspectives? Do responses reflect long-range, not just immediate, implications?

**Examples:**
- Delta Kelly Elementary: Whole-school assembly on literacy — 570 students used 3 Y's in media classes, then shared at assembly with GOGO
- Connie Weber: Growth mindset unit — students connected mindset to self, others, and world; responses showed genuine transfer
- Trisha Matelski (IB Spanish): Malaria infographic — students made deeply personal and moral connections: "With privilege comes responsibility"

---

#### 18. The 4 If's

**Purpose:** Move students from understanding an issue to *taking action*. Generates possible courses of action at personal, community, national/world, and inaction-consequence levels.

**Companion to:** The 3 Y's (which uncovers why an issue matters; the 4 If's identifies what to do about it).

**Structure:**
1. **If I take this ideal/principle seriously…** — What are the day-to-day implications for how I live? What would I do differently? When would I speak out?
2. **If my community takes this ideal/principle seriously…** — What collective actions? What behaviors change?
3. **If our nation/world takes this ideal/principle seriously…** — What policies? What wrongs need righting?
4. **If I/we don't do anything…** — What will happen? (Inaction as a choice with consequences)

**Key addition:** The fourth If (inaction) distinguishes this from other action-planning tools. Inaction is not neutral — it has consequences.

**Steps:**
1. After 3 Y's or similar exploration, frame an overarching principle: "Everyone deserves access to clean water," "An active lifestyle contributes to well-being"
2. Individual pre-thinking before group work
3. Groups record on large paper/whiteboards (vertical surfaces encourage discussion)
4. Gallery walk + identify highest-leverage, most doable actions
5. Optional: Tug of War routine to explore benefits/problems of specific actions; sort into direct vs. indirect actions

**Assessment:** Can students think beyond indirect action (fundraisers, posters) to direct personal action? Do they consider cause-and-effect relationships — not just "that's a good idea" but "why would this work?"

**Examples:**
- Alexandra Sánchez (3rd grade, Novi): Tree of Life synagogue shooting — students found agency through personal actions (spread love, speak out against hate) and understood violence as preventable
- Sharonne Blum (12th grade, Bialik College): Australian same-sex marriage plebiscite — students identified beyond marching to *engaging in dialogue* with those who hold different views
- Caitlin Williams (6th grade, Del Mar): Eco-friendly school design — 3 Y's + 4 If's opened creative thinking beyond "add solar panels"; students proposed chewing gum recycling stations, policy changes, garden-based school lunches

## Part Four: Realizing the Power

### Chapter 6: Using Thinking Routines for Maximum Effect

The four key areas teachers must attend to in order to move from superficial use to maximum effect: **Planning, Priming, Pressing, Positioning.**

---

#### Planning for Thinking

Common limiting approaches to planning:
- **"Activity dump"** — accumulate activities from past years, whittle down
- **"Efficiency approach"** — divide curriculum by days in term; cover content

Both approaches don't rely on student thinking for direction. The cure:

**Use the Understanding Map**
- Plan by asking: "What thinking move do I want students to develop?" Then ask: "What routine supports that move?"
- Use it to make interdisciplinary units cohesive (Kendra Daly, International School Beijing): "What kind of thinking am I going for today?"

**Plan for "To-Be-Continued" Moments**
- Design so today's thinking feeds tomorrow's learning
- Walter Basnight (AIS Chennai): "I scaffold thinking over the course of a semester's experiences"
- Avoid isolated lessons; build connected sequences

**Plan for Interactions, Environment, and Time**
- Plan who will talk to whom, where, and when
- Plan the physical environment: What thinking will go on the walls?
- Plan *time* — if students aren't given time to think, they don't

**Plan Where You Will Listen**
- Schedule listening moments deliberately — if you don't plan to listen, you'll fill the space with your own voice
- Wayne Cox (Newington College): "The hardest thing is not being overengaged in my own learning. I try to stay very aware — I plan to avoid talking too much."

**Don't Overplan**
- Over-scripted lessons reduce students to passengers
- When rough edges are scrubbed from content, the thinking is already done — but by the teacher, not the students
- Leave space for student thinking to drive direction

---

#### Being Primed for Thinking

**Definition:** Being on the ready for thinking to emerge — noticing it everywhere, all the time.

**The frequency illusion analogy:** Once you buy a MINI Cooper, you see them everywhere. Once you're sensitized to a thinking move, you notice it constantly. Priming for thinking means reaching that sensitized state.

**Tools for priming:**

**Display the Understanding Map**
- Post it large enough to be in your line of vision; point to it during instruction
- Gene Quezada (IS Beijing): "The key change in me is that I now see the importance of naming and noticing the thinking my students are doing. Students are always thinking, but am I primed to notice it?"

**Let Students Know Their Thinking Matters**
- Three actions: Name and notice student thinking publicly; Use their thinking to shape teaching direction; Identify and address misconceptions
- Tahireh Thampi (Magic Years IS Bangkok): "I let students influence the direction of our learning. I take their thinking seriously as a researcher of my own students."

**Play Detective in Your Own (and Others') Classrooms**
- Observe other classes to see thinking moves in contexts different from your own
- Spot thinking as a matter of consciousness until it becomes habitual

**Honor the Unplanned Moments**
- Unplanned thinking often produces the most authentic learning
- Louise-Anne Geddes: "I naturally find myself asking 'What makes you say that?' when a student shows insight. Digging deeper often takes the lesson in directions I hadn't planned."
- Pursuing unplanned moments sends students a message: their learning matters more than the teacher's agenda

---

#### Pressing for Thinking

**Definition:** Engaging with and extending student thinking in the moment — knowing when and how to move it forward.

**Metaphor (Blythe & Allen 2004, The Facilitator's Book of Questions):** Like a jazz musician — sometimes the improvisation soars, sometimes it meanders. Pressing is an art, not a formula. But it is rarely catastrophic.

**Techniques:**

**Pause and Then Decide Whether to Speak**
- 15 seconds of pause is enough to shift from reactive to reflective
- Chris Fazenbaker (AES New Delhi): "When a moment for thinking comes up, I pause, stay in that moment. Not structuring class too much — letting thinking guide learning."

**Be on the Ready with Facilitative Questions**
- "Can you say a little more about that?"
- "What's the question behind the question, really?"
- "What makes you say that?"
- These questions *in a situated learning moment* can be transformational

**Step Out Before Stepping In**
- Kendra Daly: "If the learning falls apart when I, the teacher, step out of the center, then I am doing too much of the legwork."
- Stepping out = getting off the microphone, opening eyes and ears
- Jeff Watson: "Knowing when to step in and out is the true art of great teaching. I reflect each day — was that move best for the kids?"

**Don't Turn Watering Holes into Mini Campfires (Thornburg 2004)**
- Campfire = teacher-led whole group (storyteller + audience)
- Watering hole = peer-to-peer, less hierarchical, empowered
- Cave = individual reflection
- When a teacher enters a watering-hole conversation and dominates, they turn it into a mini-campfire
- Effect: students become passive audience; teacher becomes storyteller; student-to-student thinking stops
- Better question: Not "should I say something?" but "When and *how* can I engage without commandeering?"

---

#### Positioning for Thinking

**Definition:** Making thinking visible is a *stance*, not an activity. Positioning is the set of beliefs and attitudes that make everything else possible.

**Mehta and Fine (2019), *In Search of Deeper Learning*:** Teacher stance — the position they take toward the enterprise — is what insulates effective teachers from distracting practices and energizes their forward movement.

**Four stances:**

**"This Is the Right Place and Right Time"**
- Every subject area and grade level is *perfect* for thinking routines — because every subject involves understanding
- The difference between teachers who see opportunities and those who see obstacles is belief, not circumstance

**"The Curriculum or Program Isn't My Foe, Nor Is It My Master"**
- MTV is compatible with IB, project-based learning, inquiry, workshop models, standards — it enhances all of them
- Reframe: "How does advancing students as thinkers work *in service of* the curriculum, rather than in opposition to it?"

**"I Can Learn from My Students"**
- Regular debriefs with students: What connections did you make? What extended your thinking? What challenges remain? (Use Connect-Extend-Challenge, MTV 2011)
- Communicates that student thinking is a priority

**"Making Thinking Visible Is the Plate"**
- Not one more thing on the plate — it *is* the plate
- All classroom agendas — projects, assignments, exhibitions — sit on the plate of rich thinking
- Reframe: "How do class projects *feel* when placed on the plate of engaging in thinking vs. work completion?"

---

### Chapter 7: Learning to Support One Another as We Make Thinking Visible

Professional learning for MTV must go beyond "training" in tools. It must develop the **skill set** and **mindsets** needed to use those tools effectively.

---

#### Beyond the Toolset: Essential Skills

Six skills essential for effective MTV use:

1. **Listening to students** — Suspending judgment, genuine curiosity, battling the rush to evaluate, the tendency to predict, and the desire for closure
2. **Identifying key understandings** — Finding the 2-3 big ideas in a unit that are worth grappling with (not just facts to convey); requires collaborative conversation with colleagues
3. **Connecting student learning to thinking** — Asking: "What thinking do students need to do with this content?" Use the Understanding Map to identify and name the thinking
4. **Maintaining a formative assessment lens** — Suspending evaluative judgment; asking: What am I learning about my students' thinking? What surprises me? How will I use this?
5. **Being instructionally responsive and flexible** — Using FA to advance learning, not just detect deficits; being willing to rework plans
6. **Analyzing students' learning responses** — Recognizing evidence of thinking in work; distinguishing understanding from performance; this takes time and requires community support

---

#### Beyond the Skill Set: The Mindsets That Motivate Action

Three belief areas shape whether teachers use MTV tools effectively:

**Perception of Students**
- Rosenthal & Jacobson (1968), Pygmalion: Teacher expectations shape student performance
- Onosko (1992): Successful teachers of thinking were *optimistic* about students' capacity; unsuccessful ones wrote off whole groups as incapable
- Erika Lusky (Rochester High): Neurodiverse learners surprise teachers when routines tap into their thinking
- Jennifer LaTarte (Bemis): "A roadblock is teachers unknowingly putting limits on students. The power is in our acceptance and eagerness — knowing that they can think."

**Understanding of the Goals of Teaching**
- If teaching = transmission + test prep, then MTV feels like a distraction
- Ryan Gill (senior history): "I used to think MTV would take more time. Now I think it *buys* more time — students make connections and make more sense of what they're learning."
- MTV requires: depth over breadth, true learning over work completion, complexity over simplification, originality over reproduction

**Conceptions of Thinking and Learning**
- Onosko: Effective teachers could articulate *precisely* which thinking moves they were nurturing and how they related to disciplinary learning
- Learning happens when learners *do something cognitively* with information — not when they receive it
- When teachers view learning as active cognitive exploration, thinking routines feel central, not additive

---

#### Supports: Tools and Structures for Professional Learning

**Learning Labs**

A three-phase collaborative professional learning structure:

1. **Planning (1 class period):** A host teacher shares a goal and possible content. The group collectively designs a prototype lesson — selecting routine, planning logistics, constructing the task. The lesson is "owned" by everyone.
2. **Teaching (in host classroom):** Host + co-facilitator teach the lesson. Observers focus on students — their responses, questions, problem-solving, thinking. Not a demonstration; explicitly a prototype.
3. **Post-observation Discussion:** Focus on students and instructional decisions, not teacher performance. Examine student work. Raise new questions. Each teacher considers how to adapt this in their own class.

**Key value:** The planning and analysis are where the learning happens, not the teaching itself. ("If there is any magic to routines, it is in the planning before and analysis afterward.")

**Common failure mode in planning:** Teachers want to over-scaffold, reduce complexity, guide toward predetermined outcomes. Facilitators must express confidence in students and push against this.

---

**The LAST Protocol (Looking At Student Thinking)**

A 45-60 minute structured protocol for collaborative analysis of student work.

**Structure:**
- **Presentation & Preparation** (≤5 min): Presenter provides context, goals, task requirements. Group asks clarifying questions. Silent individual reading of work (≤7 min).
- **Discussion & Analysis** (Presenter is SILENT throughout):
  1. **Describing the Work** (5-7 min): What do you actually see? Avoid interpretation — just describe what is present
  2. **Speculating about Students' Thinking** (5-7 min): Where in the work do you see thinking? Use the Understanding Map as a reference
  3. **Asking Questions** (5-7 min): What questions does this work raise? Not about the lesson — about the thinking. "Ask the question behind the question."
  4. **Implications for Teaching and Learning** (5-7 min): Where might this work go next? What practical possibilities exist?
- **Debriefing:**
  - Presenting teacher responds (≤5 min): What was gained from listening? Where will you go next?
  - Reflect on the protocol itself (5 min): How did it feel? What would you change?
  - Thank presenting teacher and documenter

**Why the presenting teacher is silent:** To prevent the conversation from defaulting to a monologue about the teacher's decisions. Focus stays on student thinking, not teacher performance.

**Value beyond the individual:** Teachers develop skills of analyzing student work, recognizing thinking, raising questions, and planning responsive instruction — not just feedback for one teacher.

---

**Protocol for Collaborative Analysis of Documentation (CAD)**
*(Krechevsky, Mardell, Rivard & Wilson, 2013)*

A shorter (25 min) protocol focused on analyzing documentation rather than a collection of student work.

**Structure:**
- Presenter provides brief context; group looks in silence
- Presenter silent during discussion
- Group discusses (free-flowing or sequential):
  - What do you see/hear? What strikes you as important or meaningful?
  - What questions does the documentation raise? (Presenter doesn't answer)
  - What are the implications for teaching, learning, and next steps?
- **Debrief:** Presenter take-aways; group take-aways (each member jots one idea for their classroom)

**Advantage:** Two rounds possible in a single class period. Grounds interpretations in observable evidence — promotes debate about what is actually visible vs. assumed.

---

**Teacher Reflection Prompts**

Used at the start of professional learning group meetings. Teachers select 1-2 and spend 3-5 minutes writing before sharing with a partner or small group:

1. What moment/s this week did you feel your students were most connected and engaged to their learning? Why?
2. What moment/s did students seem disconnected or going through the motions? What might have led to that?
3. What example of student thinking most took you by surprise and opened new avenues for exploration?
4. Of everything you did to make thinking visible this week, what would you do differently?
5. What questions did you hear yourself asking that were most effective in revealing, pushing, or prompting students' thinking?
6. Where were there opportunities (taken or not) for documenting student thinking?
7. Where did you listen with vigorous intent? Where do you wish you had listened more intently?

**Use as ongoing reflection log:** Teachers keep these prompts and reflect daily or weekly, bringing entries to group meetings.

---

## Conclusion

**Core message:** Teachers are not charged with *producing* learning — they are charged with *producing the conditions* for learning. Thinking routines, questioning, listening, and documentation are the means by which those conditions are created.

But tools alone are not enough. Teachers must develop:
- The **skill set** to use them effectively (listening, identifying key understandings, formative assessment, responsiveness, analysis of student work)
- The **mindsets** that motivate their use (optimism about students, commitment to depth over coverage, understanding learning as active cognitive construction)

**Three communities are required:**
1. The **classroom community** — learning with and from students; becoming students of your students
2. The **school community** — colleagues in inquiry, experimenting and analyzing together
3. The **broader professional community** — learning from new perspectives worldwide

**Final note:** Every teacher featured in this book once encountered these tools as new processes that needed to be tried, reflected upon, and retried. Mistakes are part of the process. Use each instance of visible thinking as the natural springboard for your next teaching move — responsive teaching that meets learners where they are.
