## 1. What Is Project Based Learning?

Project Based Learning (PBL) is a **teaching method** in which students gain knowledge and skills by working for an extended period of time to investigate and respond to an authentic, engaging, and complex question, problem, or challenge. It is a **main course**, not a dessert — standards and skills are *learned through* the work of the project, not applied after instruction ends.

### PBL vs. "Doing a Project"
| Traditional "Dessert" Project | Gold Standard PBL |
|---|---|
| Added on after content is taught | Content is learned *through* the project |
| Teacher-directed from start to finish | Student voice & choice throughout |
| Single correct answer | Open-ended driving question |
| Product assessed by teacher only | Public product shared with real audience |
| Reflection is rare | Structured reflection is built in |
| Feedback given once at end | Ongoing critique & revision cycles |

### Why PBL Works
- Students retain knowledge longer when it is learned in meaningful context.
- Projects develop 21st-century success skills: critical thinking, collaboration, communication, creativity, and self-management.
- Authentic work increases motivation, ownership, and engagement.
- Making learning public raises expectations and quality.
- PBL bridges school and real-world experience, including students' own cultures, interests, and communities.

## 3. The 7 Essential Project Design Elements

These are the characteristics that make a project Gold Standard. Use the **Essential Project Design Elements Checklist** to evaluate any project before launching.

---

### 3.1 Key Knowledge, Understanding & Success Skills

**What it is:** The project is focused on teaching students key knowledge and understanding derived from standards, plus explicit success skills (critical thinking/problem solving, collaboration, self-management).

**Why it matters:** Success skills cannot be taught in the abstract — students gain them by thinking critically *about* math, science, history, etc. Each project should explicitly teach and assess **one or two** specific success skills.

**Checklist questions:**
- Are learning goals specific and aligned to standards?
- Are success skills explicitly targeted, taught, and assessed?
- Is the project focused rather than covering too much?

---

### 3.2 Challenging Problem or Question

**What it is:** The heart of a project — a meaningful problem to solve or a question to explore — operationalized through an open-ended, student-friendly **Driving Question**.

**Characteristics of a strong Driving Question:**
- Open-ended (more than one possible answer)
- Understandable and inspiring to students
- Aligned with learning goals — answering it requires gaining the targeted knowledge
- Student-friendly language, not academic jargon
- Captures the central challenge (not just a theme)

**Examples:**
- *"How can we improve our school's recycling system so we reduce waste?"*
- *"Should the U.S. have fought the Vietnam War?"*
- *"How can we help new students feel welcome in our school?"*

**Avoid driving questions that:**
- Have a single or simple answer
- Are difficult for students to understand or connect with
- Sound more like a topic than a challenge

**Why it matters:** A well-designed problem/question makes learning meaningful — students aren't just gaining knowledge to remember it; they're learning because they have a real need to know something.

---

### 3.3 Sustained Inquiry

**What it is:** An active, in-depth process over time in which students generate questions, find and use resources, ask further questions, and develop their own answers. PBL inquiry is **iterative** — it repeats and deepens.

**Key features:**
- Lasts more than a few days (it's a process, not an activity)
- Driven by student-generated questions throughout
- Mixes research modes: reading, labs, field trips, expert interviews, surveys
- Students pose questions, gather and interpret data, develop and evaluate solutions, and ask further questions

**Why it matters:** Students develop the capacity to ask meaningful questions and the agency to seek answers. Content "sticks" because students are actively seeking and co-constructing knowledge, not passively receiving it.

---

### 3.4 Authenticity

**What it is:** Connection to the real world through any combination of:
- **Authentic context** — problems like those faced by real professionals (entrepreneurs, engineers, civic advisors)
- **Real-world processes, tasks, tools, and quality standards** — designing experiments, using professional software
- **Real impact on others** — addressing a school or community need, creating a product others will actually use
- **Personal authenticity** — speaking to students' own concerns, interests, cultures, identities

**Why it matters:** When projects are authentic, students are engaged for a purpose — they see why what they're learning matters. Authenticity bridges the gap between school and home and creates opportunities for students to use the knowledge and skills they bring to the classroom.

---

### 3.5 Student Voice & Choice

**What it is:** Opportunities for students to make their voices heard and exercise meaningful choice over:
- Topics to investigate
- Questions to ask
- Resources to use
- People to work with
- Products to create
- Use of time
- Organization of tasks

**Levels of choice:**
- **Low-stakes:** choosing which website to use for research, how to divide tasks within a team
- **Medium:** selecting topics of interest within content standards, choosing research angles
- **High:** writing their own driving question, deciding how to investigate and demonstrate learning

**Why it matters:** Choice creates ownership. Students care more about and bring more of themselves to a project when they have a say. Both voice and choice contribute to a culture of **shared power**.

---

### 3.6 Reflection

**What it is:** Structured, regular opportunities for students and teachers to reflect on three areas throughout the project:

| Focus | Sample Prompts |
|---|---|
| **Content** (WHAT we are learning) | How does new information change our understanding? How does this concept connect to a related one? |
| **Process** (HOW we are learning) | How does our collaboration compare to our working agreements? What resources are we using? What do we need to change? |
| **Purpose** (WHY we are doing this) | How does what we're learning help answer the driving question? Why does this matter to the world? |

**Reflection strategies:**
- Exit tickets
- Journals / learning logs
- Triangle-Square-Circle protocol
- Think-Pair-Share
- Peer interviews
- In-2-Out protocol (personal → team → whole group)
- "I Used to Think… Now I Think" thinking routine

**Why it matters:** As John Dewey wrote: *"We do not learn from experience. We learn from reflecting on experience."* Reflection helps students solidify knowledge, transfer learning, and take ownership of their growth.

---

### 3.7 Critique & Revision

**What it is:** Structured processes through which students give and receive constructive feedback in order to revise their ideas and products or conduct further inquiry.

**Types of critique protocols:**
- **High-level feedback on multiple artifacts:** Gallery Walk, Feedback Carousel — appropriate early in the project when work is still developing; critics are often anonymous
- **Deep feedback on a single artifact:** Tuning Protocol, Praise-Question-Suggestion — more useful in later stages for final revisions; interactive, done in small groups

**Keys to effective critique:**
- Use rubrics to guide feedback
- Establish clear norms: be kind, specific, and helpful
- Use Ron Berger's "Austin's Butterfly" to model the critique process
- Provide sentence starters to scaffold quality feedback
- Build feedback-revision cycles into the project calendar
- Include outside adults and experts, not just teacher and peers

**Why it matters:** Critique and revision builds a **growth mindset** — shifting students from "it's right or wrong" to "work is always improving." It distributes responsibility for formative assessment across the whole class, not just the teacher.

---

### 3.8 Public Product

**What it is:** Students share their work with an audience beyond the classroom. Forms can include:
- Presentation to an authentic audience
- Exhibition of project work
- Published written or visual work
- Design or product used by the community
- Video conference with real stakeholders
- Podcast, website, or social media campaign

**Why it matters:**
1. **Motivating power** — when students present to real audiences, the stakes and quality bar are raised.
2. **Social dimension of learning** — product becomes discussible; creates a "learning community."
3. **Communication with families and community** — effectively communicates what PBL is and what it does for students.

## 4. The 7 Project Based Teaching Practices

These are the instructional moves teachers make to bring a project to life. Many are familiar practices, reframed in the context of a project.

### 4.1 Design & Plan
Create or adapt a project for your context and students, and plan its implementation from launch to culmination while allowing for some degree of student voice and choice.

**Gold Standard looks like:**
- Project includes all 7 Essential Design Elements
- Detailed plans include scaffolding, assessment, and a flexible project calendar
- Resources anticipated and arranged well in advance

### 4.2 Align to Standards
Use standards to plan the project and make sure it addresses key knowledge and understanding from subject areas to be included.

**Gold Standard looks like:**
- Criteria for products are clearly and specifically derived from standards
- Scaffolding, critique/revision protocols, assessments, and rubrics consistently refer to and support achievement of specific standards

### 4.3 Build the Culture
Explicitly and implicitly promote student independence and growth, open-ended inquiry, team spirit, and attention to quality.

**Gold Standard looks like:**
- Norms are co-crafted *with* and self-monitored *by* students
- Student voice and choice is regularly leveraged and ongoing
- Students usually know what they need to do with minimal direction from the teacher
- Students work collaboratively in healthy, high-functioning teams
- Students understand there is no single "right answer" and it is OK to take risks
- Values of critique, revision, persistence, and pride in high-quality work are shared and student-owned

### 4.4 Manage Activities
Work with students to organize tasks and schedules, set checkpoints and deadlines, find and use resources, create products, and make them public.

**Gold Standard looks like:**
- Appropriate mixture of individual/team work, whole-group/small-group instruction
- Classroom routines and norms consistently followed
- Project management tools (group calendar, contract, learning log) used to support student self-management
- Realistic, flexible schedules and checkpoints; no bottlenecks
- Well-balanced teams formed according to project nature and student needs

### 4.5 Scaffold Student Learning
Employ a variety of lessons, tools, and instructional strategies to support all students in reaching project goals.

**Gold Standard looks like:**
- Each student receives necessary instructional supports
- Scaffolding is guided by students' questions and "need to knows" — teacher does not over-front-load
- Key success skills are taught using a variety of tools; students practice, apply, and reflect on them
- Student inquiry is facilitated while allowing students to act and think as independently as possible

### 4.6 Assess Student Learning
Use formative and summative assessments of knowledge, understanding, and success skills, including self and peer assessment.

**Gold Standard looks like:**
- Products and other sources of evidence thoroughly assess both standards and success skills
- Individual student learning is adequately assessed, not just team products
- Formative assessment used regularly with a variety of tools and processes
- Structured critique/revision protocols used at checkpoints
- Regular structured opportunities for students to self-assess and peer-assess
- Standards-aligned rubrics used by students and teacher throughout the project

### 4.7 Engage & Coach
Engage students in their learning and work alongside them to identify when they need skill-building, redirection, encouragement, and celebration.

**Gold Standard looks like:**
- Teacher's knowledge of individual student strengths, interests, backgrounds, and lives is used to engage and inform decisions
- Students and teacher co-define goals and benchmarks (e.g., co-constructing rubrics)
- Student questions play the central role in driving inquiry
- Appropriately high expectations for all students, clearly established and reinforced
- Individual student needs identified through close relationships
- Students and teacher reflect regularly and formally throughout the project

## 5. The Project Path: 4 Phases

The Project Path is the structure that guides student inquiry from launch to culmination. All four phases are present in every Gold Standard project, though time in each phase varies.

```
Phase 1: LAUNCH
├── Entry Event (hook that sparks curiosity)
├── Driving Question presented or co-constructed
└── Need to Know questions generated

Phase 2: BUILD KNOWLEDGE, UNDERSTANDING & SKILLS
├── Students research and investigate
├── Need to Know questions guide inquiry
├── Teacher provides lessons, scaffolds, and guidance
├── Learning Logs track progress
└── Thinking Routines support sense-making

Phase 3: DEVELOP & CRITIQUE
├── Students develop products/answers to Driving Question
├── Critique protocols provide structured feedback
├── Revision cycles improve work quality
├── Teacher provides additional experiences for new knowledge
└── Rubrics used for self- and peer-assessment

Phase 4: PRESENT PRODUCTS
├── Students share work with authentic public audience
├── Reflection on process and learning
└── Post-project feedback gathered from students
```

### What Students Think About at Each Phase

**Phase 1 (Launch):** What is the project asking me to do? What do I need to know? Why is this important? Who will I share my work with?

**Phase 2 (Build Knowledge):** What resources can I use? Can I trust this information? How can I apply what I've learned? What new questions do I have?

**Phase 3 (Develop & Critique):** Is my work on the right track? Do I need more information? How can I improve based on feedback?

**Phase 4 (Present):** What should I explain about my work? How can I best share this with others? What have I learned? What would I do differently next time?

### How Teachers Support at Each Phase

| Phase | Teacher Moves |
|---|---|
| Launch | Conduct entry event; present/co-construct driving question; facilitate generation of student questions |
| Build Knowledge | Facilitate use and evaluation of resources; provide lessons, scaffolds, and guidance in response to student needs |
| Develop & Critique | Help students apply learning to project tasks; provide additional experiences to generate new knowledge; facilitate feedback processes |
| Present | Help students evaluate their work; facilitate student reflection on process and learning |

## 6. Project Design: Step-by-Step Process

### Step 1: Start With Learning Goals
Before designing the project, identify:
- **Key knowledge and understanding** — what content standards will students learn?
- **Success skills** — which 1–2 skills will be explicitly taught and assessed? (collaboration, critical thinking, creativity, complex communication, self-directed learning)

### Step 2: Define the Three Foundational Elements
These three must be tightly aligned with each other:

**A. Learning Goals** — what content knowledge, skills, and success skills should students master?

**B. Driving Question** — what open-ended, student-facing question or problem will guide inquiry? Check that it:
- Is open-ended (multiple possible answers)
- Is understandable and inspiring to students
- Requires gaining the targeted knowledge to answer it
- Captures the central problem/challenge

**C. Public Products** — what will students create and share publicly to demonstrate their learning?
- Specify both individual products and team products
- Identify how work will be made public (exhibition, presentation, publication, etc.)

### Step 3: Determine Authenticity
- What is the real-world context for this project?
- What authentic tasks, tools, and quality standards will students use?
- What real impact could students' work have?
- How does this connect to students' own interests, identities, and communities?

### Step 4: Map the Project Path
For each phase (Launch → Build Knowledge → Develop & Critique → Present), identify:
- Major milestones
- Anticipated student Need to Know questions
- Learning experiences, lessons, activities, and scaffolds

### Step 5: Plan the Day-by-Day Calendar
Create a flexible calendar that includes:
- Scaffolds for all learners
- Intentional literacy supports
- Structured reflection opportunities
- Formative assessment checkpoints
- Critique and revision processes
- Flexible time to adjust the plan based on student needs

### Step 6: Gather Resources in Advance
- Identify outside experts to bring in
- Arrange field experiences
- Prepare project wall materials
- Develop rubrics and formative assessment tools
- Draft the project information sheet and family letter

## 7. Core Classroom Strategies

### 7.1 Entry Event (Project Launch)
An engaging stimulus that hooks students and initiates inquiry. Can be:
- A video, article, or news story
- A visit from a guest speaker
- A field trip or site visit
- A challenging scenario or problem statement
- A piece of provocative artwork or data

Design the entry event so students will naturally ask the questions you want them to answer during the project. Share the driving question and project information sheet to focus inquiry.

---

### 7.2 Need to Know Questions
**Purpose:** Student-generated questions that initiate and sustain inquiry throughout the project.

**The NTK Table:**
| KNOWS | NEED TO KNOWS | NEXT STEPS |
|---|---|---|
| What students already know | Questions they need to answer | How they'll start finding answers |

**Facilitation Steps:**
1. **Launch with entry event** — design it so students naturally ask the key questions
2. **Collect KNOWS** (5–10 min) — activate prior knowledge; capture students' exact words
3. **Collect NEED TO KNOWS** (10–20 min) — spend more time here; probe for specific research plans
4. **Collect NEXT STEPS** (5–10 min) — encourage specificity beyond "do research"
5. **Post and revisit frequently** — update as questions are answered; add new questions

**Revisiting NTK throughout the project:**
- Before each lesson, identify which NTK questions the lesson will address
- After lessons, mark answered questions; celebrate learning
- Encourage students to add new questions as learning deepens
- Use NTK questions to prioritize inquiry and sequence lessons

---

### 7.3 Project Wall
A visual hub for the classroom — bulletin board or digital space — that tracks:
- The driving question (prominently displayed)
- Need to Know questions list (updated regularly)
- Project calendar / key dates and milestones
- Rubrics and quality criteria
- Work in progress samples
- Resources and reference materials
- Team progress indicators (Kanban board, checkmarks)

**Digital Project Wall options:** Padlet, Wakelet, Google Classroom, Canvas, Schoology, SeeSaw, class webpage

**Why it reduces teacher dependence:** Students can answer their own questions and check their own progress without interrupting the teacher. Visitors instantly understand what the class is working on.

---

### 7.4 Learning Log
A research-tracking tool students use during the project to document their inquiry.

**Basic Learning Log structure:**
| Question(s) I Have | Resource I Am Using | What I Learned (& Date) | Further Questions |
|---|---|---|---|

**Process:**
1. Students identify one NTK question that interests them most
2. Research for 15 minutes
3. Pause: share question, what was learned, new questions
4. Research 15 more minutes
5. Repeat — building individual knowledge before bringing to team

**For primary grades:** Simplify to "What do I want to know? / What do I think I know? / How did I learn this?"

---

### 7.5 Workshop Model
A consistent daily structure for managing inquiry, collaboration, and productivity.

**Workshop cycle:**
```
MINI-LESSON (10–15 min)
│ Targeted instruction on a specific skill/concept needed for project work
│ Connect explicitly to current project tasks
│ Be brief and focused — one specific topic
↓
WORK TIME (20–40 min)
│ Individual or team work on project tasks
│ Teacher circulates, confers, coaches
│ Optional mid-point reconnection if needed
↓
REFLECTION / SHARE (5–10 min)
│ Students share progress, ask questions, give feedback
│ Build culture of sharing and accountability
```

**Why it works:** Mirrors real-world cycles of learning new skills, applying them in meaningful contexts, and revising work based on feedback. Allows differentiation and supports student autonomy.

---

### 7.6 Critique Protocols

**High-Level Feedback on Multiple Artifacts (early stages):**
- **Gallery Walk** — students rotate through displayed work, leave written feedback
- **Feedback Carousel** — structured rounds of silent written feedback
- Owner is not present; critics can be anonymous

**Deep Feedback on a Single Artifact (later stages):**
- **Tuning Protocol** — structured rounds of warm/cool feedback
- **Praise-Question-Suggestion** — balanced feedback framework
- **Charrette Protocol** — rapid design feedback
- Owner is present and responds; done in small groups

**Keys to effective critique:**
1. Establish norms first (kind, specific, helpful)
2. Use rubrics to anchor feedback
3. Provide sentence starters for students
4. Start with a whole-class critique of an anonymous sample
5. Watch "Austin's Butterfly" (Ron Berger) as a model for quality feedback
6. Build in time for reflection after critique, before revision
7. Revisions happen *after* reflection — build both into the calendar

---

### 7.7 Reflection
*(See Section 3.6 for the three focus areas)*

**Formal reflection structures:**
- **Exit tickets** — brief written responses at end of class
- **Journals** — ongoing documentation with prompted or open reflection
- **Triangle-Square-Circle** — three key points / things that square with thinking / circling questions
- **Think-Pair-Share** — individual think → partner discuss → whole group share
- **Peer interviews** — structured partner conversations
- **In-2-Out** — individual → small group → whole class protocol
- **"I Used to Think… Now I Think"** — track shifts in understanding (also great for post-project)

**Assessment note:** Avoid grading or correcting reflections — it is counterproductive. Instead affirm new realizations and ask probing questions to deepen thinking.

---

### 7.8 Models of Quality Work
**Purpose:** Students analyze exemplars of high-quality work to develop a clear, concrete understanding of expectations.

**Steps:**
1. **Identify exemplars** — professional work, published examples, or strong past student work. Use 2–4 to avoid restricting thinking.
2. **Students explore** exemplars (gallery walk, stations activity)
3. **Analyze as "mentor texts"** — identify key features that make each one excellent
4. **Collaboratively generate quality criteria** — students create the list, post on Project Wall
5. **Use criteria to co-construct or refine rubrics**
6. **Return to exemplars** during product development for self-assessment

---

### 7.9 Rubrics
**When to use them:**
- **Project Launch:** Share rubrics *before* creating products; analyze with exemplars; optionally co-construct with students
- **Build Knowledge phase:** Use rubrics routinely to check progress; set weekly rubric-check days
- **Develop & Critique phase:** Implement critique protocols using rubrics; self- and peer-assess progress; assess success skills too
- **After Presentation:** Students use rubric to self-reflect on final products before receiving teacher's final assessment

**Types of rubrics in PBL:**
- Product rubrics (aligned to content standards)
- Success skills rubrics (collaboration, critical thinking, creativity, complex communication, self-directed learning)
- Project design rubric (for teacher self-evaluation)

---

### 7.10 Student Choice
**Three categories of student choice:**

**Content choices:**
- Topics to investigate within content standards
- Sub-content or themes to focus on
- Research questions and themes
- Real-world issues to address

**Process choices:**
- Partners and teams
- Research methods (interviews, surveys, field work, reading)
- Workflow and organization of tasks
- How to use work time

**Product choices:**
- Type of product to create (when multiple options are viable)
- Presentation format
- Audience to address
- Tools and technologies to use

**Important:** Choices should be authentic and meaningful — the kinds of decisions adults and professionals actually make. Be strategic: calibrate choice to students' age, PBL experience, subject area, and time of year.

---

### 7.11 Thinking Routines
Structured protocols from Harvard's Project Zero that make thinking visible and support inquiry.

**At LAUNCH (introducing/exploring ideas):**
| Routine | Purpose | Example Use |
|---|---|---|
| See-Think-Wonder | Inquiry-based close observation of stimuli | Analyze artwork or photograph for entry event |
| Zoom In | Close examination of a portion of an image | Use historic photo to generate questions |
| Think-Puzzle-Explore | Identify current understanding and curiosity | Reflect on driving question topic before teams form |
| Generate-Sort-Connect-Elaborate | Nonlinear understanding of a topic | Connect key concepts after entry event |

**During BUILD KNOWLEDGE:**
| Routine | Purpose | Example Use |
|---|---|---|
| +1 Routine | Identify key ideas and commit to memory | After guest speaker presentation |
| Sentence-Phrase-Word | Text-based: identify what was most important | With informational texts for comprehension |
| Micro-lab Protocol | Equal-participation structured conversation | Facilitate equitable discussion; also for critique |
| The 4Cs | Text-based discussion of key concepts | Colonial history texts, differentiated for grade |
| What Makes You Say That? | Push students to give evidence for assertions | Build culture of evidence-based feedback |

**During DEVELOP & CRITIQUE:**
| Routine | Purpose | Example Use |
|---|---|---|
| Chalk Talk | Silent discussion on paper — all students contribute | Teams critique each other's work |

**At PRESENT:**
| Routine | Purpose | Example Use |
|---|---|---|
| I Used to Think… Now I Think | Self-reflection on how thinking has shifted | Final reflection after presentations |

**How to implement:**
1. Analyze learning goals to identify what kinds of thinking are needed
2. Align routines to the Project Path phase
3. Explicitly teach each routine; give repeated practice
4. Gradually release ownership to students to select and apply independently

---

### 7.12 Outside Experts
**When to bring experts in:**

| Project Phase | How Experts Help |
|---|---|
| Launch | Answer NTK questions, correct misconceptions, support sustained inquiry, give feedback on proposed lines of inquiry |
| Build Knowledge | Provide specific insight into content and process; deeper feedback on drafts |
| Develop & Critique | Provide specific feedback on products; students produce higher quality when they know an expert will review it |
| Present | Serve as part of the public audience; ask probing questions; provide authentic feedback |

**How to prepare experts:**
- Connect via phone/video/email before the visit
- Brief them on what students are learning and what NTK questions they've been asking
- Share rubrics if you want them to assess work
- Be clear about time commitment (how long, when, where, in-person or virtual)

**Where to find experts:** Local businesses, university faculty, nonprofit leaders, family/community members, students' families. Don't be afraid to reach out — most people are willing to support student learning.

---

### 7.13 Academic Vocabulary
**Purpose:** Ensure all students — including English Learners — become fluent in the language of the project's content.

**Key approaches:**

| Approach | Description |
|---|---|
| Frontload Key Terms | Preview and practice key terms before students encounter them; use photos, analogies, drawings; tap prior knowledge |
| Concept Before Vocabulary | Do an activity that exemplifies the term, then introduce the word (*e.g.,* explore viscosity of liquids before introducing the term) |
| Model Word Solving | Think aloud using context clues and sentence structure to decode unfamiliar words |
| Read Aloud Regularly | Call attention to content-specific terms as they appear; debrief with students |
| Teach Morphology | Teach roots, prefixes, suffixes to decode unfamiliar academic vocabulary |

**Planning tip:** Document language objectives in your project plan alongside content objectives. Examples: "Students will use context clues to decipher unfamiliar words" or "Students will correctly use the terms *ecosystem*, *biome*, *organism*, *consumer*, and *producer*."

---

### 7.14 Interviews
**When to use:** To identify needs of a "client" or audience (design thinking), or to interview experts/stakeholders who have direct experience in the content area.

**Key interview skills to teach:**
- Ask open-ended questions
- Ask follow-up questions for clarification and elaboration
- Engage in active listening; be mindful of body language
- Provide wait time / leave space for silence
- Take notes and/or record (with permission)

**Preparation process:**
1. Students clarify goals for the interview
2. Conduct background research
3. Generate questions (use graphic organizers and stems)
4. Role-play to practice, using fishbowl for feedback

**Post-interview reflection questions:**
- What were your goals? How well did the interview address them?
- What did you learn? What surprised you?
- What questions remain unanswered?
- What would you do differently?

---

### 7.15 Preparing Students for Presentations
**Start early:** Tell students from Day 1 when and to whom they will present. This creates purpose and sustained motivation.

**Steps:**
1. Define audience and format before launch; consider authenticity
2. Share authentic models of the presentation format (TED talks, business pitches, panel presentations)
3. Scaffold the planning process: outlines, scripts, storyboards, presentation plans
4. Build rehearsal, critique, and revision cycles into the project calendar
5. Use early feedback sessions for *content*; later sessions for *delivery* (volume, eye contact, pacing)
6. Use the **Presentation Day Checklist** to prepare logistics

**Presentation Day Checklist:**
- Schedule set; guests know when/where to attend
- Guest/audience materials duplicated
- Room arranged for presenters and audience
- Equipment tested (tech support on standby)
- Teacher materials in place
- Audience role explained
- Timekeeping device ready

---

### 7.16 Audience Feedback Form
**Purpose:** Convert exhibitions from passive events into lively, meaningful exchanges. Gives audience a valued evaluator role.

**Design principles:**
- Keep forms short and simple
- Include prompts that require deep content engagement
- Consider digital format (online survey, voice recording) to increase completion
- Have students critique and revise the form before the exhibition (it is itself a public product)
- Tailor form to whether it's a work-in-progress or culminating exhibition

**Standard audience feedback questions:**
- What did you learn from this presentation, or what did it make you think about?
- What did you like about this presentation?
- Do you have any questions about the topic or how the project was done?
- Any other comments?

---

### 7.17 Post-Project Reflection (for Teachers)
**Purpose:** Gather student feedback on the project to guide future project design.

**Plus/Delta protocol:**
| PLUS (what worked) | DELTA (what to change) |
|---|---|
| Strengths of the project | What would you do differently next time? |
| What surprised you? | What was most challenging? |

**After collecting feedback:**
- Reflect on whether the project met intended goals
- Consult the Project Design Rubric and Teaching Rubric to diagnose gaps
- Make notes while fresh
- Celebrate a job well done

## 8. Success Skills: Definitions & Rubrics

Each rubric has four performance levels: **Beginning → Emerging → Developing → Demonstrating**

### 8.1 Collaboration (Grades 6–12)
*"Students work together to complete a project and must combine thinking, skills, and efforts to do so."*

**Key dimensions:**

**Planning & Group Decision-Making:**
- Beginning: Inflexible; assigns work without equity considerations
- Demonstrating: Suggests roles based on group members' unique knowledge/abilities/interests; identifies tradeoffs in different approaches; suggests decision-making strategies for disagreements

**Building on Each Other's Thinking:**
- Beginning: Restates what others say
- Demonstrating: Rephrases the group's thinking, connects it to new information, identifies gaps

**Productive Disagreement:**
- Beginning: Withdraws or causes conflict
- Demonstrating: Frames disagreements productively; helps group find common ground

**Research basis:** Focus is on the *quality of collaborative interactions*, not the academic output.

---

### 8.2 Critical Thinking (Grades 6–12)
*"Students make judgments demonstrating explanation & analysis, inferences & interpretation, and evaluation."*

**Key dimensions:**

**Explanation/Analysis:**
- Beginning: Summarizes, notes important details, uses evidence as support
- Demonstrating: Analyzes using chains of reasoning to uncover interrelationships, patterns, inconsistencies, and counterexamples

**Interpretation/Evidence (Inference):**
- Beginning: Draws conclusions using evidence-based inferences
- Demonstrating: Draws logical conclusions and generalizes to other concepts using evidence from multiple sources, accounting for quality and limitations of evidence and claims

**Evaluation:**
- Beginning: Identifies strengths/weaknesses in argument
- Demonstrating: Evaluates information/arguments using multiple criteria; identifies bias and alternative perspectives

**Research definition:** *"Purposeful, self-regulatory judgment resulting in interpretation, analysis, evaluation, and inference… The ideal critical thinker is habitually inquisitive, well-informed, trustful of reason, open-minded, flexible, fair-minded in evaluation, honest in facing personal biases, prudent in making judgments, willing to reconsider."* (Facione, 1990)

---

### 8.3 Self-Directed Learning (Grades 6–12)
*"Students demonstrate self-regulation, autonomy, motivation, and personal responsibility."*

**Key dimensions:**

**Self-Regulation:**
- Beginning: Goals set by teacher; difficulty initiating tasks; frustration stalls learning
- Demonstrating: Independently sets ambitious yet attainable goals; completes on time using self-feedback; uses strategies to overcome frustration; reflects on quality of work and obstacles overcome

**Motivation:**
- Beginning: Engages minimally; relies on external motivation
- Demonstrating: Intrinsically motivated; demonstrates growth mindset; persists through challenges

**Personal Responsibility:**
- Beginning: Blames others for setbacks
- Demonstrating: Takes responsibility for own actions; demonstrates integrity

**Autonomy:**
- Beginning: Depends on teacher for direction
- Demonstrating: Recognizes available choices; takes charge of own learning; engages in ongoing reflection

---

### 8.4 Creativity (Grades 6–12)
*"A process through which an individual or group produces something that is both novel and useful."*

**Key dimensions:**

**Creative Product (Novelty & Usefulness):**
- Beginning: Product copies an example or conventional approach
- Demonstrating: Product reflects both a *novel* and *useful* idea/solution to address the problem/task objectives

**Generating & Experimenting:**
- Beginning: Generates ideas disconnected from the problem/task
- Demonstrating: Generates a range of ideas directly connected to objectives; manipulates, synthesizes, and adapts ideas to develop new potential solutions

**Explaining Creative Choices:**
- Beginning: Cannot explain choices
- Demonstrating: Clearly articulates the reasoning behind creative decisions and how they serve the purpose

**Research definition:** *"Creativity is the interaction among aptitude, process, and environment by which an individual or group produces a perceptible product that is both novel and useful as defined within a social context."* (Plucker et al.)

---

### 8.5 Complex Communication (Grades 6–12)
*"Students listen and read carefully to understand the message; explain thoughts and ideas through words, writing, and body language; use technology to enhance a message; and communicate effectively with people in diverse cultures and backgrounds."*

**Key dimensions:**

**Integration & Sharing:**
- Beginning: Shares central idea and basic details; limited vocabulary
- Demonstrating: Shares analysis that interprets, synthesizes, and evaluates what was read/heard; uses precise vocabulary; draws logical conclusions; communicates in organized, clear way

**Non-Verbal Communication:**
- Beginning: Body language sometimes contradicts message
- Demonstrating: Non-verbal communication consistently aligns with and enhances the message

**Technology Use:**
- Beginning: Uses technology but it distracts from the message
- Demonstrating: Uses technology strategically to enhance the message for the specific audience

**Audience Awareness:**
- Beginning: Little consideration for audience
- Demonstrating: Adapts communication style, vocabulary, and approach effectively for diverse audiences and cultures

## 9. Assessment in PBL

### Formative Assessment Tools
- Need to Know questions (gauge understanding at launch)
- Learning logs (track inquiry progress)
- Exit tickets (end-of-class check-ins)
- Reflection journals (ongoing metacognition)
- Self-assessment against rubrics
- Peer critique protocols
- Teacher observation and conferring during work time
- Project work reports (individual and team)

### Summative Assessment
- Final products assessed against standards-aligned rubrics
- Public presentation (audience feedback informs teacher assessment)
- Individual written work or product (to ensure individual accountability when team products are primary)

### Self & Peer Assessment
- Students should regularly self-assess progress on both content rubrics and success skill rubrics
- Peer assessment protocols structured to be kind, specific, and helpful
- Annotated rubrics returned to students with final grade, showing where and why scores landed

### Individual Accountability in Team Projects
This is critical. Strategies:
- Individual products alongside team products
- Individual written reflections
- Project work reports (individual version)
- Teacher conferences with individuals during work time
- Individual components assessed separately from team components

## 10. Product Types Toolkit

Each product type has specific planning steps, resources, and quality criteria. Choose products that are authentic to the driving question and accessible to students.

### Business Plan
A document outlining goals and strategies for an existing or new business/organization. Gives students authentic context for applying learning. Audiences: investors, community members, city council.

### Podcast
An audio recording or series focused on a particular topic. Helps students make meaning of content, articulate new knowledge, and make learning public. Multiple genres: interview, documentary, narrative, debate.

### Policy Proposal
A formal document or presentation recommending a course of action to address a specific problem. Fosters critical thinking, persuasive communication, and evidence-based reasoning. Audiences: government officials, school boards, organizational leaders.

### Infographic
A visual representation of data or ideas using graphics and minimal text. Effectively summarizes information, presents research findings, or raises awareness. Tools: Canva, Adobe Express, Google Slides.

### Public Service Announcement (PSA)
A short, impactful message to raise awareness or influence behavior about a specific issue. Formats: video, poster, audio recording. Channels: school announcements, social media, local TV/radio.

### Social Media Campaign
A coordinated effort using social media to promote a message, product, or cause. Fosters creativity, strategic thinking, and digital literacy. Includes content creation, audience analysis, and platform strategy.

### Museum Exhibit
A curated collection of artifacts organized around a theme to educate viewers. Can include interactive elements, audio statements, interpretive signage. Formats: physical display, digital exhibit, school hallway installation.

### Physical Model/Prototype
A tangible 3D representation of a design or concept. Used for engineering, design thinking, and science projects. Includes iterative design process with critique and revision cycles.

### Picture Book
A written and illustrated book for a specific audience. Combines ELA skills with content knowledge. Audience: younger students, families, community libraries.

### Idea Talk
A short, well-prepared presentation of a single idea (similar to a TED talk format). Students must synthesize learning into a clear, compelling, concise message. Great for all subjects and grade levels.

### Event Planning and Hosting
Students plan, organize, and host a real event. Applies math (budgeting), communication (invitations, promotion), and content knowledge in a highly authentic context.

### Interactive Map/Tour
A digital or physical map or tour that communicates information about a place, community, or topic. Combines geography, research, and communication skills.

## 11. Team Management

### Forming Teams
**Key questions to consider:**
- How big will teams be? (Recommend 2–4 students; adjust for age and project complexity)
- How will you balance teams? (Skills, work styles, interests, relationships)
- Will teams be heterogeneous or homogeneous? (Usually heterogeneous is more beneficial)
- How much student voice will students have in team formation?
- How will you handle conflict?

**SING Learner Profile:** A tool to gather information about students' learning preferences — Social, Independent, Nonlinear, and Goal-oriented tendencies — to inform team formation and role assignment.

**Gold Standard:** Well-balanced teams formed according to the nature of the project and student needs, with appropriate student voice and choice.

### Team Contracts
A co-created document in which team members commit to shared working agreements.

**Standard commitments:**
- Listen to each other's ideas with respect
- Do your work as best you can
- Complete work on time
- Ask for help when needed
- [Student-generated additional commitment]

**Process for conflict:** If a team member breaks an agreement, the team holds a meeting. If unresolved, ask teacher for help.

### Team Roles
**Key principles:**
- Design roles as **delegator roles** (project manager, creative director) rather than **doer roles** (artist, materials manager)
- Roles should be equitable in responsibility
- Roles should enhance collaboration, not isolate work
- Roles should reflect real-world work environments
- Rotate or adjust roles across projects so all students develop leadership skills

### Project Management Log (Team)
Track all tasks with: Task name | Who is responsible | Due date | Status | Done (checkbox)

## 13. Teaching Rubric: Beginning → Gold Standard

Use the **Project Based Teaching Rubric** for teacher self-reflection and professional growth. It describes three levels — Beginning, Developing, and Gold Standard — across all 7 teaching practices.

### Key Distinctions by Practice

**Design & Plan:**
- Beginning: Some design elements present but not at highest level; calendar is rough; resources not fully prepared
- Gold Standard: All 7 design elements at highest level; detailed, flexible plan; all resources arranged in advance

**Align to Standards:**
- Beginning: Criteria given but not derived from standards; scaffolds don't refer to standards
- Gold Standard: All criteria specifically derived from standards; all scaffolds, protocols, assessments, and rubrics consistently support specific standards

**Build the Culture:**
- Beginning: Norms feel like teacher-imposed rules; rare student choice; teams need frequent intervention
- Gold Standard: Norms co-crafted and self-monitored by students; student voice and choice ongoing; healthy, high-functioning teams with rare teacher intervention; students take risks and learn from mistakes

**Manage Activities:**
- Beginning: Too much whole-group instruction; routines not established; schedules loosely followed
- Gold Standard: Appropriate balance of instruction formats; routines consistently followed; project management tools used for student self-management; flexible yet realistic schedules

**Scaffold Student Learning:**
- Beginning: Some supports provided but many needs unmet; content front-loaded before launch
- Gold Standard: Every student receives necessary support; scaffolding guided by student NTK questions; success skills explicitly taught with practice opportunities; inquiry facilitated with maximum student independence

**Assess Student Learning:**
- Beginning: Mostly traditional tests; team products only; occasional formative assessment; superficial feedback not used to revise
- Gold Standard: Products and multiple sources of evidence assess both standards and success skills; individual accountability ensured; regular formative assessment with varied tools; structured critique protocols; regular self- and peer-assessment; rubrics used formatively throughout

**Engage & Coach:**
- Beginning: Limited knowledge of students affects instructional decisions; students willing but not engaged with ownership; reflection mainly at end
- Gold Standard: Deep knowledge of students drives engagement and decisions; students co-define goals; student questions drive inquiry; high expectations shared and reinforced; needs met through close relationships; regular formal reflection throughout

## 14. Equity-Centered PBL

Gold Standard PBL is designed to meet the needs of **all** learners. Four equity levers:

### Lever 1: Knowledge of Students
- Know students' strengths, interests, backgrounds, and lives
- Use that knowledge to engage them and inform instructional decisions
- Connect projects to students' cultures, identities, and communities

### Lever 2: Cognitive Demand
- Maintain appropriately high expectations for *all* students
- Provide differentiated scaffolding — supports that are *removed* when no longer needed
- Use rigorous, open-ended inquiry that allows multiple entry points

### Lever 3: Literacy
- Intentional literacy supports are built into every project
- Academic vocabulary instruction scaffolded for English Learners and all students
- Read-alouds, text-based discussion protocols, and writing supports embedded throughout

### Lever 4: Shared Power
- Student voice and choice is not optional; it is an equity issue
- Co-create norms, co-construct rubrics, co-define goals
- Students learn to recognize their own power and use it responsibly
- Culture shifts from teacher authority to student ownership

### Additional Equity Practices:
- Use norms and team contracts to establish safe, respectful culture
- Design critique protocols that ensure all voices are heard
- Leverage thinking routines like Micro-lab Protocol for equitable participation
- Use culturally relevant contexts and authentic issues students care about
- Provide multiple ways to demonstrate learning (not just written tests)

## 15. Using AI to Support PBL Planning

AI tools (Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, etc.) can support and enhance project planning when used with intentional prompting.

### What AI Can Help With
- Checking alignment between driving question, learning goals, and public products
- Planning scaffolded learning experiences at each milestone
- Crafting formative assessments aligned with milestone goals
- Generating multiple project ideas to compare
- Refining driving question language
- Identifying potential outside experts or authentic contexts
- Drafting project information sheets and family letters

### How to Use AI Effectively

**Step 1: Orient the chatbot to your context**
Include: your role and grade level, teaching context (location, student population, needs and interests, community context), and your intention for using AI.

*Example:* "I am an 11th-grade Modern Hawaiian History teacher working with a wide range of readers in Hawaii. I need 3–4 project ideas that align with the PBLWorks Gold Standard Framework."

**Step 2: Upload or reference the framework**
If possible, upload the Project Design Rubric so the AI can reference the Essential Project Design Elements.

**Step 3: Focus on the three foundational elements first**
1. Learning Goals
2. Driving Question
3. Public Products

**Step 4: Evaluate AI output critically**
Ask yourself:
- Is the driving question open-ended?
- Does it require gaining the intended knowledge, understanding, and skills to answer it?
- Is it understandable and inspiring to students?
- Are the public products authentic and aligned to learning goals?
- Does the project idea have potential for authenticity, student voice, and sustained inquiry?

**Step 5: Iterate**
Refine and revise your inputs until you are confident the project is Gold Standard. AI is most powerful as a thought partner for ideation and planning — your expertise in your students and context is essential.

### Important Cautions
- AI generates ideas; *you* ensure they are appropriate for your students and community
- Always verify factual content AI provides
- Student work should remain original — AI planning tools assist *teachers*, not replace student inquiry
- Final judgment on all project decisions belongs to the teacher

## 16. Grade-Level Considerations

### Pre-K through Grade 2
- Norm creation and NTK process best done as whole-group discussions
- Break NTK process across several days with read-alouds to ignite questions
- Simplify learning logs: "What do I want to know? / What do I think I know? / How did I learn this?"
- Use picture clues on rubrics; add visuals to support language
- Provide simplified rubrics reviewed in small groups or individually
- Focus on just one row of one success skill rubric per project
- Pre-K to Grade 1: SING profile completed one-on-one with teacher
- Team roles: even kindergartners can understand "project manager" with picture clues
- Projects can include whole-class products alongside individual products

### Grades 3–5
- Students can handle more independence in NTK generation and research
- Introduce critique protocols with strong scaffolding; pair with Micro-lab Protocol
- Begin using success skill rubrics for self-assessment with teacher guidance
- Team contracts work well; students can co-create norms
- Choice boards helpful for offering content and product options

### Grades 6–12
- Students can take on higher levels of voice and choice, including writing their own driving questions
- Full success skill rubrics (6–12 versions) appropriate
- More sophisticated critique protocols (Tuning Protocol, Charrette)
- Students can meaningfully conduct outside expert interviews
- Social media campaigns, policy proposals, and business plans well-suited to older students
- Can handle more complex project management tools and longer timelines

## 17. Quick-Reference Checklists

### Essential Project Design Elements Checklist
Use this before launching any project:

- [ ] **Key Knowledge, Understanding & Success Skills** — Project is focused on important standards-derived knowledge; 1–2 success skills explicitly targeted to teach and assess
- [ ] **Challenging Problem or Question** — Meaningful, appropriately challenging; framed by an open-ended, understandable, inspiring, standards-aligned driving question
- [ ] **Sustained Inquiry** — Active, in-depth process over time; student-generated questions drive inquiry throughout
- [ ] **Authenticity** — Real-world context; real-world processes/tools/standards; real impact and/or connection to students' own concerns, interests, identities
- [ ] **Student Voice & Choice** — Students make meaningful choices about topics, resources, tasks, products, and/or use of time
- [ ] **Reflection** — Structured opportunities for students to reflect on content, process, and purpose throughout and at project end
- [ ] **Critique & Revision** — Regular structured opportunities for students to give and receive feedback; students use feedback to revise work
- [ ] **Public Product** — Work presented, displayed, or offered to people beyond the classroom; students explain their reasoning, process, and learning

---

### Project Planning Checklist (Teacher)

**Before Launch:**
- [ ] Learning goals identified (content standards + 1–2 success skills)
- [ ] Driving question written and checked against quality criteria
- [ ] Public products defined (individual and team)
- [ ] Project path mapped with milestones
- [ ] Rubrics prepared or co-construction plan ready
- [ ] Outside experts identified and contacted
- [ ] Entry event designed
- [ ] Family letter drafted
- [ ] Project wall set up
- [ ] Day-by-day calendar drafted (flexibly)
- [ ] Resources anticipated and arranged

**During the Project:**
- [ ] Entry event delivered; NTK questions generated and posted
- [ ] NTK questions revisited regularly
- [ ] Formative assessment happening routinely (exit tickets, learning logs, rubric checks)
- [ ] Critique protocols scheduled and implemented
- [ ] Student voice and choice embedded in multiple places
- [ ] Reflection structured at regular checkpoints
- [ ] Scaffolds provided and removed as needed
- [ ] Project wall updated regularly
- [ ] Individual accountability maintained

**At Project End:**
- [ ] Presentations prepared and rehearsed
- [ ] Audience invited and briefed
- [ ] Audience feedback form designed
- [ ] Final products assessed using rubrics
- [ ] Post-project student reflection structured
- [ ] Teacher reflection completed

---

### Driving Question Quality Check
- [ ] Open-ended (more than one possible answer)
- [ ] Understandable by students (no unexplained jargon)
- [ ] Inspiring and personally relevant to students
- [ ] Aligned with learning goals (answering it requires gaining targeted knowledge)
- [ ] Captures the central challenge (not just a topic or theme)
- [ ] At appropriate level of challenge (not too simple to justify a project)
