# Prologue: Why Rethinking the What of Education Matters So Much
*By Andreas Schleicher, Director for Education and Skills, OECD*

> **TL;DR**: Education has shifted from transmitting knowledge to building navigation skills for an uncertain world. The skills easiest to teach are also the easiest to automate. What education must now develop is the ability to apply knowledge in novel situations — and to synthesize across disciplines.

- In the past, education was about **teaching people something**. Now it's about ensuring individuals develop a reliable compass and the navigation skills to find their own way through an increasingly uncertain, volatile, and ambiguous world.
- A generation ago, teachers could expect what they taught would last a lifetime. Today, schools must prepare students for **jobs not yet created, technologies not yet invented, and problems not yet known**.
- The core dilemma: **the skills easiest to teach and test are also the easiest to digitize, automate, and outsource**.
- Deep knowledge in a discipline remains important — innovative people typically have specialized skills. But educational success is no longer mainly about reproducing content knowledge; it's about **extrapolating from what we know and applying it in novel situations**.

> 💡 The world no longer rewards people just for what they know — search engines know everything — but for **what they can do with what they know, how they behave, and how they adapt**.

- Conventional problem-solving broke challenges into manageable bits and taught techniques to solve them. Today, we also create value by **synthesizing the disparate bits** — connecting ideas that previously seemed unrelated. This requires curiosity, open-mindedness, and familiarity with knowledge across fields.
- The world is no longer divided into **specialists** (deep skills, narrow scope) and **generalists** (broad scope, shallow skills). What counts increasingly are **versatilists** — people who apply depth of skill to a progressively widening scope of situations, gaining new competencies, building relationships, and assuming new roles.
- Schools currently certify individual achievement. But the more interdependent the world becomes, the more we rely on **great collaborators** who can join others in life, work, and citizenship. Innovation is now rarely the product of individuals working in isolation.
- The persistent obstacle: the status quo has many protectors. Attempts to make room in crowded curricula produce "mile-wide but inch-deep" syllabi that constrain development of deep competencies.

> 💡 The foundational problem: we lack an **organizing framework** that can help prioritize educational competencies and systematically structure the conversation around what individuals should learn at various stages of their development.

- *Four-Dimensional Education* provides that framework — not a one-size-fits-all list, but a structure within which educators, curriculum planners, policymakers, and learners can **establish what should be learned, in their context and for their future**. The OECD's Education 2030 project builds directly on this CCR work.

# Introduction

> **TL;DR**: CCR's Four-Dimensional Education framework addresses whether we are teaching the right things — not just how to teach better. The framework organizes education around Knowledge, Skills, Character, and Meta-Learning, providing a structure for curriculum redesign relevant to the 21st century.

- Education — formal schooling — is meant to prepare students to thrive in the world. Designed well, it can lead to more empowered individuals, more peaceful and sustainable societies, and more economic fairness.
- The world continues to **transform dramatically** while education is not adapting quickly enough. The challenges of today are starkly different from those of the Industrial Revolution, when the first blueprint for modern education was crafted — and even from those of just a few decades ago, before the Internet.
- This book does not focus on *how* education is implemented — a worthy goal addressed elsewhere — but asks a prior question: **Are we teaching and testing the right things?**

> 💡 The question CCR is built to answer: **What should students learn for the twenty-first century?**

- The CCR framework focuses on four dimensions:
  - **Knowledge** — what students know and understand
  - **Skills** — how they use that knowledge
  - **Character** — how they behave and engage with the world
  - **Meta-Learning** — how they reflect on themselves and adapt by continuing to learn and grow
- The audience for this book: teachers, department heads, heads of schools, administrators, policymakers, standard setters, curriculum and assessment developers, and other thought leaders who seek to understand the needs and challenges we all face — and to help devise innovative solutions.

# Chapter 1: Redesigning Education for a Transforming World

> *"The future ain't what it used to be."* — Yogi Berra

> **TL;DR**: The world is transforming exponentially while education lags. Global challenges — sustainability, VUCA, automation — demand a fundamentally different kind of learner. The jobs of the future are non-routine and personal; education must build versatile, adaptable people rather than rote knowledge.

### Global Trends and Challenges

- The broadly agreed goals for a better future: **more peaceful, sustainable societies**, comprised of more personally fulfilled people, making full use of their potential.
- Growing evidence — from scientific studies, employer surveys, public opinion, and educators themselves — shows that education systems globally are **not delivering on this promise**. Students are often not adequately prepared to succeed in today's, let alone tomorrow's, world.
- The root cause: the world continues to transform dramatically while education is **not adapting quickly enough**. Today's challenges are starkly different from those of the Industrial Revolution, when the blueprint for modern education was first crafted.
- Global hyper-connectedness creates new fragility: the 2008 financial crisis showed how failures in one part of a system now propagate throughout interwoven global networks. *(N.N. Taleb, Antifragile, 2012)*

> 📊 [Figure 1.1 — Global Trends and Risks: WEF diagram mapping interconnections between world risks, including links between rising income disparity and social instability]

### Sustainability

- The global human population has only recently exploded to an **unsustainable rate**.
- We now use in one year the resources the Earth takes **1.5 years to produce** *(Global Footprint Network)*.
- If everyone consumed resources at the rate of the highest-consuming countries, multiple additional Earths would be needed to sustain humanity.

> 📊 [Figure 1.2 — World Population Growth (Population Reference Bureau)]

> 📊 [Figure 1.3 — Populations and Land: ecological footprint by country (Global Footprint Network)]

- Evolutionary biologist Jared Diamond (*Collapse*, 2005) draws a direct parallel between the collapse of Easter Island's civilization — through resource competition and overconsumption — and today's global civilization: *"The world's environmental problems will get resolved, in one way or another, within the lifetimes of the children and young adults alive today. The only question is whether they will become resolved in pleasant ways of our own choice, or in unpleasant ways not of our choice."*
- **Education can be a powerful tool for survival** — but the competencies to meet these challenges are currently not being taught consistently or effectively.

### VUCA and Values

- **VUCA** — Volatility, Uncertainty, Complexity, Ambiguity — emerged from late-1990s military strategy. It warns that our world is becoming increasingly difficult to predict and manage.
- Our future also depends on our **values**. Consumerism and materialism, while unsustainable long-term, are socially and culturally determined — and therefore can change as a culture's values shift.
- Societal values determine where a culture lies on key continua: antagonism ↔ tolerance; individualism ↔ social cohesion; materialism ↔ search for deeper meaning.

> 📊 [Table 1.1 — Pushes and Pulls: values driving societal change (Raskin et al., *The Great Transition*, 2002)]

> 💡 Awareness of powerful forces shaping modern life allows us to **act as agents with a design mindset**, not merely react. This agency needs to be reflected in an effective 21st-century education.

### Exponential Progress and Future Predictions

> *"It's difficult to make predictions, especially about the future."* — Mark Twain

- For a human mind accustomed to linear thinking, **exponential change is genuinely difficult to grasp**. The chess-board rice legend illustrates: by the 20th square, 1 million grains; by the 64th, more than 100 quadrillion grains — more than 1,000× current world rice production.
- **Moore's Law**: the density of transistors doubles every 1.5–2 years, with corresponding increases in computational speed and storage. Computing and communication technologies grow this way.
- Adoption speed of transformative technologies: radio took **38 years** to reach 50 million people; television **13 years**; the Internet **4 years**; Facebook **2 years**.
- Predictions based on past trends regularly fail. In 2004, the best-selling phone was the Nokia 2600; three years later, the iPhone appeared and made those predictions obsolete. Change is often **discontinuous with existing trends**.

### Technology's Impact on Society

> *"Technology gives us power, but it does not, and cannot, tell us how to use that power."* — Jonathan Sacks

- Apprehension about technology is ancient: Socrates believed writing would "create forgetfulness in the learners' souls" — and in terms of memorization, he was right. Entire books were once held in human memory; that skill is now obsolete.
- Yet writing created a **collective history** that can be viewed and added to at any time, enabling people to build on and critique each other's work. Technology is simultaneously a very old worry and a great hope.
- Current critiques: rising childhood obesity, face-to-face socialization replaced by screens, addictive media behaviors, lower comprehension from electronic reading. But many of these are being addressed by new technology adaptations.
- Core principle: **technology is an amoral amplifier**. Nuclear energy can be a positive energy source or a destructive weapon. Commoditized Internet knowledge can spread access widely — or spread dangerous knowledge.

> 💡 We may not be able to stop accelerating progress, but we can carefully manage how it is used. Education must build the **competencies, expertise, and wisdom** to use technology intentionally — not merely as novelty or crutch.

### Technology, Automation, Outsourcing and Jobs

> *"We are currently preparing students for jobs that don't yet exist, using technologies that haven't been invented, in order to solve problems we don't even know are problems yet."* — Richard Riley

- Technology has progressively removed: physical dirt and danger → dull mental tasks → now threatens expert decision-making. (Example: computers trained to diagnose breast cancer, potentially factoring in more variables than human doctors in real time.)
- Automation is not new: horses → cars; medieval scribes → Gutenberg press; launderers → washing machines; cashiers → barcode scanners. Even as jobs are automated, **new job types emerge** (social media manager, cloud services engineer).
- Key questions automation raises:
  - What types of occupations are subject to automation, and to what degree?
  - What new jobs get created, and what competencies will they require?
  - How do we prepare students for jobs that will actually exist when they graduate?

> 📊 [Figure 1.6 — Job Types over Time (% share, 1850–present): IPUMS-USA, University of Minnesota]

> 📊 [Figure 1.7 — Job Types over Time (raw numbers, 1850–present): IPUMS-USA, University of Minnesota]

> 📊 [Figure 1.8 — Mannequins replacing human models at H&M]

> 📊 [Figure 1.9 — Programming Difficulties: spectrum of tasks from easy to difficult to automate (Third Way, *Dancing with Robots*)]

> 📊 [Figure 1.10 — Worker Tasks: routine vs. non-routine job growth/decline since 1960 (Autor, MIT 2013)]

- **Two forces** determine which jobs survive automation:
  1. **Personal delivery**: tasks requiring in-person interaction resist offshoring
  2. **Non-routine nature**: tasks requiring flexibility and synthesis resist automation

- **Routine tasks** (manual and cognitive alike) are increasingly automated — demand is dropping.
- **Non-routine manual tasks** (e.g., plumbing) are declining more slowly but remain necessary.
- **Non-routine interpersonal skills** (consulting, teaching, caregiving) and **non-routine analytical skills** (engineering design, medical surgery) are the skills most needed in the future. *(Autor & Price, 2013)*

> 📊 [Figure 1.11 — Routine and Non-Routine Jobs: two-axis map of automation risk vs. offshorability (CCR, using Blinder and Autor/Levy/Murnane)]

- As a general rule: education for employment must **refocus away from routinized, impersonal tasks** and toward more complex, personal, creative tasks that only humans can do well.
- As computers take over routine tasks, humans are left with the jobs they do best — often using computers as assistive tools to extend their work to new heights rather than being replaced.

### The Race Between Technology and Education

> *"Civilization is a race between education and catastrophe."* — H. G. Wells

> 📊 [Figure 1.12 — Technology and Education: diagram of the race between technological progress and educational attainment (CCR, inspired by Goldin & Katz)]

- As technology progresses, the education required to utilize it effectively also grows. **Education must keep pace or society suffers**.
- When education lags: people are unqualified for jobs; productivity suffers; economic inequality grows as those with access to exceptional education secure more opportunities.

> 📊 [Figure 1.13 — Percentage of Respondents Who Agree that Graduates/New Hires Are Adequately Prepared: a factor-of-2 disconnect between satisfied education providers and dissatisfied employers + youth (McKinsey & Company, 2014)]

> 💡 McKinsey found a **factor-of-2 disconnect**: education providers mostly satisfied with graduate preparation; youth and employers mostly dissatisfied. This gap is the central problem *Four-Dimensional Education* is designed to address.

- The answer is not simply teaching *more* knowledge — but learning **more relevant knowledge**, knowing how to apply it in new and different ways, and developing skills, character, and meta-learning strategies.

# Chapter 2: Education Goals for the Twenty-First Century

> *"If we teach today's students as we taught yesterday's, we rob them of tomorrow."* — John Dewey

> **TL;DR**: Individual development follows Maslow's Pyramid; societal goals parallel it at scale. Education serves four functions, but only one — goals, standards, and curricula — is CCR's focus. A 21st-century curriculum must be adaptable and balanced across ten tensions. CCR's 4D Framework is the result of a rigorous process designed to be comprehensive, compact, uncorrelated, appropriate, and globally relevant.

### The Nature and Evolution of Education Goals

- Individual development is captured by **Maslow's Pyramid of Needs** — physiological → safety → love and belonging → esteem → self-actualization → self-transcendence.
- The pyramid is not sequential: all levels are always present and can be fulfilled simultaneously. Students in poverty face a harder path because unmet lower-level needs constrain focus on higher ones.
- **Self-actualization**: realizing one's full potential — looks different for every individual.
- **Self-transcendence**: aligning with a higher goal outside oneself — service to others, spiritual practice, contribution to society.

> 📊 [Figure 2.1 — Maslow's Pyramid of Needs (CCR)]

### Societal Goals

- Societal goals mirror Maslow's Pyramid at scale: ensuring the human species thrives and has security at lower levels; fulfilling collective potential, overcoming prejudice, acting on best scientific information at higher levels.
- Traditional societal progress was measured by **GDP** — increasingly recognized as limited (ignores health, environment, well-being).
- Emerging measurement frameworks:
  - **OECD Better Life Initiative**: 11 dimensions — community, education, environment, civic engagement, health, housing, income, jobs, life satisfaction, safety, work–life balance
  - **UN Sustainable Development Goals**: 17 areas for growth by 2030 with measurable outcomes
  - **Social Progress Index**: basic human needs, foundations of wellbeing, opportunity
  - **Good Country Index**: contribution across 7 global areas
  - **Gross National Happiness** (Bhutan) and **Happy Planet Index**

> 📊 [Figure 2.2 — Sustainable Development Goals (@theglobalgoals)]

> 📊 [Figure 2.3 — Social Progress Index (Social Progressive Imperative)]

> 💡 Individual and societal goals are not a false dichotomy — they are deeply interdependent. When education lags, individuals can't meet workforce needs, and society suffers through income inequality, productivity losses, and social instability.

### Education Goals

Formal K–12 education serves four functions:

1. **Child Care** — aggregates the task of child supervision across families
2. **Socialization** — students learn basic and complex social skills through self-managed interactions
3. **Accreditation and Evaluation** — provides a standardized signal of completed learning; brand names (e.g., Harvard) serve as quick proxies for quality
4. **Education Goals, Standards, and Curricula** — the common canonical set of knowledge, skills, and competencies designed to prepare students for success and bind societies together

- CCR focuses entirely on item 4. For individuals, it must nurture development across Maslow's Pyramid; for society, it must prepare students for rapidly shifting demands.
- **Tension in accreditation**: standardized testing reinforces *extrinsic* motivations (external performance, sorting) which can undercut *intrinsic* motivations for learning. The "marketplace function" of universities further shifts dynamics away from personal mastery.

### Is Education Evolving?

- The core set of knowledge disciplines taught in schools has remained **remarkably consistent** across centuries — new subjects (higher math, sciences) have been added; rhetoric was dropped; but the fundamental canon is largely the same.

> 📊 [Figure 2.4 — School Subjects Over Time: music, art, philosophy, history, languages, mathematics, sciences (CCR)]

- Main obstacle: **historical inertia**. New goals and content are tacked onto an already overburdened curriculum. Under pressure of standardized testing, few educators can consistently integrate new learning goals. Changing education requires effort from teachers, administrators, parents, policymakers — in addition to reform-minded academics.

### Key Qualities of a Twenty-First Century Curriculum

#### Adaptability

- In nature, **versatility is the key to survival** across environmental change — not just fitness for a single environment. The Great Tit bird (*Parus major*) survives by laying eggs at optimal moments based on current conditions, and evolving rapidly as a species. *(Vedder, Bouwhuis & Sheldon, 2013)*
- Humans have flourished through adaptability — tools, agriculture, mass production, governance, global communication.
- A curriculum that is not adaptive becomes **rigid**. There is no perfect curriculum that doesn't need updating; relevant programming languages change every two years, while ancient philosophy remains constant.
- A truly adaptable curriculum is **never finished** for two reasons:
  1. Humanity's knowledge base continuously grows and changes
  2. Portions must be tailored to each individual student's needs, interests, and growth goals
- Personal control over one's learning is crucial to motivation and positive outcomes — and is itself an important lifelong learning strategy. *(Barker et al., 2014)*

> 📊 [Figure 2.5 — Developmental Shifting of Control of Instructional Time: learner autonomy increases progressively from early schooling through adulthood (CCR)]

#### Balance

A 21st-century curriculum must incorporate and balance ten tensions. CCR takes a firm stance against false dichotomies:

| Tension | Both Sides Matter Because… |
|---------|---------------------------|
| **1. Modern Knowledge & Traditional Subjects** | Modern topics (robotics, coding, entrepreneurship) must enter, but traditional foundations (reading, math, language) remain essential |
| **2. Depth & Breadth** | Students need both expertise in chosen domains AND high-level understanding of the knowledge landscape |
| **3. STEM & Humanities** | STEM meets job demand; well-designed humanities teach critical thinking and creativity. Steve Jobs: *"Technology married with liberal arts… yields the results that make our hearts sing."* |
| **4. Mind & Body** | *"Mens sana in corpore sano"* — physical health, exercise, sleep, mindfulness all show positive benefits for learning and motivation |
| **5. Knowledge, Skills, Character & Meta-Learning** | Knowledge alone is insufficient; skills apply it; character builds motivation and social intelligence; meta-learning enables self-directed growth |
| **6. Outcome & Process** | Rewarding only outcomes undermines intrinsic motivation; focusing only on process can leave students unprepared for broader expectations |
| **7. Personal & Societal Goals** | Individual fulfillment and societal contribution reinforce each other; people ideally find jobs that use their talents, match their passions, and improve the world |
| **8. Global & Local Perspectives** | CCR's framework leaves room for local communities to determine what is important from their perspective — global structure, local customization |
| **9. Deeply Internalized & Flexible** | The framework must be internalized to drive redesign, yet remain adaptable as the world changes |
| **10. Social Progress Ideals & Local Norms** | Universal ideals (food, water, peace, sustainability) apply globally; finer-grained social ideals must respect cultural context |

### A Unifying Framework of Educational Goals

> *"If you have no idea where you want to go, it makes little difference how fast you travel."* — Italian Proverb

#### Why a New Education Framework?

- Great confusion exists globally about what wording and constructs to use as common language. Countries vary widely: Canada/Québec uses "key/cross-curricular competencies"; Guatemala uses "framework competencies" and "grade competencies"; Indonesia uses "cross-cutting" and "subject-bound" competencies. *(UNESCO)*
- UNESCO: *"Quality education systems have to enable learners to continuously adapt their competencies while continuously acquiring and even developing new ones… Their typologies and approaches are as diverse as the entities — countries, organizations and individuals — that define them."*
- Growing consensus on the *types* of competencies needed, but wide variety in formulation.

> 📊 [Table 2.1 — Core Competencies by country/region: UK/Ireland, Norway, Scotland, Australia, New Zealand, Indonesia, Singapore, Namibia, South Africa (UNESCO)]

Key themes across frameworks: literacy, numeracy, communication, thinking/critical thinking, self-management, social/collaborative skills, character/values, digital/ICT literacy, creativity.

- The CCR framework aims to **synthesize across all existing frameworks** while maximizing accuracy, clarity, and usefulness — like the Food Pyramid laying out a healthy learning diet without prescribing specific meals.

> 📊 [Figure 2.6 — Food Pyramid analogy: framework as healthy learning diet (The Washington Post Company)]

#### Our Theory of Change

- CCR's focus is on the **first two rooms of renovation**: standards and assessment — because "what gets counted ends up counting."
- Sequence of change: **Goals → Standards → Assessments → Curricula → Professional Development** (with feedback loops at every level)

> 📊 [Figure 2.7 — Sequence of Change Management Focus: Goals → Standards → Assessments → Curricula → Professional Development (CCR)]

- Individual countries and jurisdictions determine how to progress on curriculum and professional development, in ways aligned with updated goals and suited to their specific values.
- **College entrance requirements** are a largely unchallenged silent influence — constructed to assess traditional knowledge ability, rarely reflecting skills, character, or meta-learning. Not a predictor of life success outside academia. Often biases school systems (e.g., requiring algebra as a proxy for tenacity rather than for its utility). British Columbia is among jurisdictions now challenging this.

#### The CCR Process

Three pillars: **Synthesize → Analyze → Organize**

> 📊 [Figure 2.8 — The CCR Process (CCR)]

**Synthesize**: Meta-syntheses of existing frameworks from ministries of education, professional bodies, organizations (P21.org). Employer needs analyses (IBM study of 15,000 CEOs from 60 countries, 33 industries). Constant monitoring of learning science research and global trend analyses.

**Analyze**: Feedback from 600+ teachers worldwide. International conferences and colloquia on mathematics, character, metacognition, employability. Input from a global network of thought leaders and partner organizations (OECD, Harvard, etc.).

**Organize**: Five design goals for the final framework:

| Design Goal | What It Means |
|-------------|---------------|
| **1. Comprehensive** | Covers all important educational goals — no silver bullets, no single-aspect fixes. Resilience includes grit, perseverance, etc. |
| **2. Compact** | Follows Miller's Law (7 ± 2 items in working memory): four categories, each with fewer than seven components — memorable and actionable |
| **3. Uncorrelated** | Correlated items are grouped; least-correlated items kept separate. Separating meta-learning from critical thinking, for example, allows decision-making to draw on all dimensions simultaneously |
| **4. Appropriate** | Concepts placed at sensible levels of abstraction — multiplication is a subcategory of math knowledge; ethics is a character quality at a higher level |
| **5. Globally Relevant** | Broad enough to transcend culture; provides common understanding for cross-cultural communication; all countries can customize it for their own values and needs |

> 💡 Is CCR's framework radical or incremental? CCR calls it **"incrementally ambitious"** — like the transformation from caterpillar to butterfly: same DNA, but unrecognizable as the original, reflecting foundational tenets.

> 📊 [Figure 2.9 — Caterpillar to Butterfly: same DNA, transformed form]

### Beyond Knowledge — A Twenty-First Century Competencies Framework

- Curriculum as traditionally conceived consists mostly of content knowledge. Progress keeps adding more knowledge at faster rates, creating overload.
- E.O. Wilson: *"We are drowning in information, while starving for wisdom. The world henceforth will be run by synthesizers, people able to put together the right information at the right time, think critically about it, and make important choices wisely."*

> 💡 Growing employer consensus: knowledge-focused curriculum does not adequately prepare students for today's workforce, much less tomorrow's. Students must **practice applying knowledge using skills**.

- Character qualities are now recognized by policymakers — countries track social progress indicators (poverty, violence, corruption, sustainability) that require positive character to address.
- The **fourth dimension** — meta-learning — must be made explicit, not just implicit. It is the internal process by which we reflect on and adapt our learning across all other dimensions. Without explicitly foregrounding it, we lose the engine of continuous improvement.

The four dimensions together form the complete framework:

| Dimension | Core Question |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Knowledge** | What do I know and understand? |
| **Skills** | How do I use what I know? |
| **Character** | How do I behave and engage with the world? |
| **Meta-Learning** | How do I reflect on myself and continue to learn and grow? |

## Knowledge — Traditional and Modern

### Evolving Maps of Traditional Knowledge Disciplines

Attempts to synthesize the disciplines important for an educated person date back to the sixth century. The most influential early Western formulation was the **Trivium and Quadrivium** — a medieval revival of classical Greek education defining seven liberal arts for university study: grammar, logic, rhetoric, astronomy, geometry, arithmetic, and music. Completing these qualified one for the professions of the time: philosophy, theology, law, and medicine.

Knowledge discipline standards in U.S. secondary education were first standardized in **1893 by the Committee of Ten**, convened by Harvard president Charles Eliot under the National Education Association. Their subject requirements — mathematics, sciences, history, English, foreign languages — remain substantially reflected in secondary graduation requirements worldwide today.

> 📊 [Figure 3.1 — Columbia University's Core Graduation Requirements: contemporary version of liberal arts core]

> 📊 [Figure 3.2 — Committee of Ten Required Courses (1893): showing subjects and number of class periods required]

The organization of knowledge also evolved through encyclopedias and library science, which produced high-level classification schemes such as those shown in Table 3.1.

**Table 3.1 — Knowledge Classification Schemes**

| System | Organizing Principle |
|--------|---------------------|
| Encyclopédie (Diderot/d'Alembert) | Memory, Reason, Imagination |

With the Information Age, both the amount of knowledge produced and the ease of accessing it expanded exponentially. New and more innovative **knowledge maps** are now needed to navigate the complexities of our expanding landscape.

New technologies — Big Data, cloud computing, AI, and visualization — are enabling dynamic knowledge representations that show relationships among scientific fields in real time *(Rafols, Porter, & Leydesdorff, 2010)*.

This aligns with cognitive research on expertise development: networks of connections between concepts are foundational to genuine understanding.

### Curating Traditional Disciplines for Relevance

The traditional disciplines taught in most education systems are:

- Mathematics
- Science
- Languages — domestic and foreign
- Social Studies (history, geography, civics, economics)
- Arts (including music)
- Wellness (physical education)

These disciplines occupy most available curriculum time, leaving little space for newer subjects, modern disciplines, skills, character, or meta-learning. The result: **overburdened curricula** that favor quantity of testable knowledge over depth of understanding and application.

> 💡 The traditional system also tends to encourage coverage of content rather than depth of understanding and the ability to use knowledge with competencies. We have continued to add rather than curate.

The instant availability of factual and procedural knowledge on the internet changes the calculus of what is worth teaching. To make room for modern knowledge and competencies, **four lenses** help identify what is essential within any traditional discipline (using mathematics as the example throughout):

#### 1. Concepts and Meta-Concepts

**Concepts** are the ideas students carry throughout their lives — either for direct practical use or for enriching their worldview. The key question: *What is essential to this discipline? What sticks long after graduation?*

- *Example:* **Rate of change** — taught first as slope (rise over run), deepened through physics (position → velocity → acceleration), applied in epidemiology, economics, biology. Non-STEM students still need it to reason about how a disease spreads or how a financial bubble forms.
- The American Association for the Advancement of Science's **Project 2061** exemplifies a well-curated set of essential science concepts, organized by age group.

**Meta-concepts** are overarching concepts that span the discipline and often beyond it.

- *Example:* **Proof** in mathematics — applicable to philosophical argumentation, political rhetoric analysis, and everyday critical reasoning. The same rigor that checks a geometric proof can dissect a marketing claim.

#### 2. Processes, Methods, and Tools

**Processes** are the big-picture elements of a discipline.

- *Mathematics:* formulating questions mathematically; employing mathematical concepts and reasoning; interpreting outcomes and conclusions *(OECD PISA 2015 Mathematics Framework)*.

**Methods** are reasoning skills within a discipline.

- *Example:* **Divide and conquer** — break a complex problem into manageable pieces, solve separately, then integrate. Applicable far beyond mathematics: outlining a book, managing a project, debugging software.

**Tools** are the most granular methods — multiplication tables, specific algorithms — whose value lies in enabling the methods above.

#### 3. Branches, Subjects, and Topics

Within any discipline, some branches are more relevant to the changing world than others. The question: *Which are increasingly important?*

- *Example:* **Discrete mathematics** — game theory, combinatorics, probability. Game theory's *Prisoner's Dilemma* illuminates doping in sports, advertising economics, and political negotiation — topics every citizen encounters.

The critique of over-specificity is direct: most domain-specific vocabulary (biotic, abiotic, quaternary consumer) is irrelevant unless a student becomes a specialist — who will relearn it on the job anyway. What sticks for the non-biologist is the deeper pattern: organisms self-organize into hierarchies and networks of competition and cooperation, all channeling the sun's energy. That pattern transfers; the taxonomy does not.

#### 4. Interdisciplinary Connections

Because knowledge transfers across disciplines, making these connections explicit is a powerful pedagogical move.

- *Example:* **Exponentials** — taught alongside compound interest (finance), bacterial growth (biology), resource exhaustion (environmental literacy), and financial bubbles (history/sociology). The concept becomes immediately relevant and multiply reinforced.

> 💡 Why not abandon disciplines entirely and reorganize around big ideas? The answer: **feasibility**. Teaching worldwide is structured along disciplinary lines. Wholesale abandonment is implausibly complex at this stage. CCR advocates crisp rethinking *within* disciplines while preserving the structure — incrementally ambitious change rather than wholesale disruption.

Every subject area has three layers of value that should each be honored in curriculum design:

| Aspect | What It Means | Key Consideration |
|--------|--------------|-------------------|
| **Practical** | Concepts and skills students need in daily life and projected careers | Most subject to change as the world changes; must be updated deliberately |
| **Cognitive** | Studying the subject enhances higher-order thinking (critical thinking, creativity) that transfers to other contexts | An empirical question — assumptions about transfer must be tested rigorously, not assumed |
| **Emotional** | The inherent beauty and power of a discipline to illuminate the world | A source of intrinsic motivation; should not wait until practical and cognitive aspects are covered |

## Themes

Themes are cross-cutting strands of learning that run through both traditional and modern disciplines. They are not courses — they are **lenses** that educators can apply to any content area or competency. CCR has identified six:

### Global Literacy

Our world is irreversibly interconnected. Every subject must now be taught from multiple cultural perspectives:

- World history includes histories from countries around the world — not only Western narratives
- Mathematics classes discuss Eastern mathematicians (Arab, Indian, Chinese), not only Western ones
- Students are prompted to critically examine their own cultural biases and develop understanding of other viewpoints

Throughout the curriculum, students should learn to see individual issues within their global socio-cultural significance, develop international awareness, and cultivate deep appreciation for cultural diversity.

### Information Literacy

Eric Schmidt (Google): every two days we create as much information as existed from the dawn of civilization to 2003. Scientific paper output doubles roughly every ten years. Knowing how to *search* is not the same as knowing how to *evaluate and synthesize*.

**Twenty-First Century Information Literacy Tools (TILT)** *(The People's Science, Sasse & Bialik)* identifies five essential capacities:

| TILT Capacity | What It Means |
|---------------|---------------|
| **Dynamic disposition** | Accept the progressive nature of information; stay open to new evidence |
| **Socio-cultural lens awareness** | Recognize how cultural context shapes information interpretation and dissemination |
| **Comfort with competing evidence** | Treat informed debate as a critical step toward eventual consensus — not as failure |
| **Source credibility evaluation** | Assess credibility at common access points in the information cycle |
| **Informed orientation** | Understand how specific evidence fits in the broader landscape of relevant knowledge |

> 📊 [Figure 3.5 — TILT Information Literacy Framework: five capacities (The People's Science)]

### Systems Thinking

Scientific and social systems are converging on the ideas of **complex systems** *(Bar-Yam, 1997)*. This requires a paradigm shift from reductionist 20th-century thinking toward an integrated approach — where analysis isolates parameters for deep treatment while synthesis considers each part as a whole and each whole as part of a larger system.

Educational theorist Derek Cabrera's **DSRP framework** gives students four lenses for any idea:

| DSRP | Prompt |
|------|--------|
| **D**istinctions | What is this thing, and what is it not? |
| **S**ystems | What are the parts and wholes involved? |
| **R**elationships | What are the connections between things? |
| **P**erspectives | How does this look from different points of view? |

> 📊 [Figure 3.6 — Systems Thinking diagram: Bar-Yam's complex systems properties]

Systems thinking is not the same as holistic thinking — it *combines* reductionist and holistic thinking.

### Design Thinking

Every institution, product, and service needs redesign in light of connectivity, sustainability, longer lifespans, and ICT integration. Design thinking is a mindset for approaching challenges, not just products.

Four core principles *(Plattner, Meinel & Leifer, 2011)*:

| Principle | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| **Human rule** | All design is ultimately social in nature |
| **Ambiguity rule** | Design thinkers must preserve ambiguity — do not over-specify too early |
| **Redesign rule** | All design is redesign — iteration and mistakes are natural and necessary |
| **Tangibility rule** | Making ideas tangible (prototypes, models) facilitates communication and refinement |

> 📊 [Figure 3.7 — Design Thinking Process Model: Stanford d:School framework adapted for curriculum]

### Environmental Literacy

Humankind is fast approaching or may have already surpassed several of the planet's ecological limits. Every citizen needs foundational understanding of environmental science.

**P21's definition of environmental literacy** includes the abilities to:

- Demonstrate knowledge of the environment and conditions affecting it — air, climate, land, food, energy, water, ecosystems
- Demonstrate knowledge of society's impact on the natural world — population growth, resource consumption, development
- Investigate and analyze environmental issues; make accurate conclusions about effective solutions
- Take individual and collective action on environmental challenges — designing solutions, participating in global efforts

### Digital Literacy

Technological savvy is becoming a baseline requirement. The majority of jobs will require **upskilling** as technological innovations integrate across careers. Students must be comfortable with existing technologies — internet search, word processing, spreadsheets, social media — and equally comfortable *learning* new ones.

> 💡 The goal is not to teach specific tools, which will change. The goal is to cultivate **comfort with the process of learning new technologies** — the meta-skill of rapid tool adoption.

> *"Education must enable one to sift and weigh evidence, to discern the true from the false, the real from the unreal, and the facts from the fiction. The function of education, therefore, is to teach one to think intensively and to think critically."*

## Skills and Learning Transfer

Psychological research consistently shows that **active engagement** in learning leads to better outcomes *(Perkins, 2006)*. Instead of only listening and routine exercises, learners exercise higher-order thinking by investigating, debating, and taking differing viewpoints. A constructive approach further emphasizes the social and creative dimensions of learning — knowledge is often socially constructed and deepened by creating or re-creating it.

The elusive goal of **transfer** — applying what is learned in one setting to a different context — is best understood as preparation for future learning *(Bransford & Schwartz, 1999)*. Educational environments that emphasize students' active roles, self-regulation, communication, and reflection have been shown to successfully enhance learning transfer to new situations *(De Corte, 2003)*.

### Knowledge and Skills Together

A longstanding debate assumes that teaching skills detracts from teaching content. This is a false dichotomy. When knowledge is learned passively — without engaging skills — it tends to be memorized but not understood, not easily reusable, and short-lived. Deep understanding and real-world application emerge only when skills are applied to content knowledge, so that **each enhances the other**.

P21 has created skills maps for mathematics, science, social studies, geography, English, world languages, and arts — showing at different grade levels how knowledge and skills can be learned in a mutually reinforcing way.

## Creativity

> *"Imagination is more important than knowledge. For knowledge is limited to all we now know and understand, while imagination embraces the entire world, and all there ever will be to know and understand."*
> — Albert Einstein

Creativity is often falsely equated with art alone — what the research literature calls **art bias** *(Runco & Richards, 1997)*. In reality, creativity is integral to scientific thinking, entrepreneurship, design thinking, and mathematics.

A **2010 IBM study** of 15,000 CEOs from 60 countries and 33 industries named creativity the most important leadership quality for meeting the challenges of complexity and uncertainty in the world. Csikszentmihalyi: *"Most of the things that are interesting, important, and human are the results of creativity... When we are involved in it, we feel that we are living more fully than during the rest of life."*

Countries are responding: the UK revamped secondary curricula around idea generation; the EU designated 2009 the European Year of Creativity and Innovation; China is replacing rote-teaching with project-based learning; Japan is implementing creativity-oriented educational reforms.

### What Creativity Looks Like

The dominant research model defines creative individuals as possessing **divergent thinking** abilities: idea production, fluency, flexibility, and originality *(Guilford, 1968)*. A large meta-analysis found divergent thinking scores predict creative achievement better than IQ *(Kim, 2008)*.

> 📊 [Figure 4.2 — Creative Qualities: divergent thinking dimensions illustrated through test examples]

### Levels of Creativity in the Classroom

Creative thinking exists on a spectrum from **perfect imitation** (no novelty) to **complete originality** (high novelty in both form and content). Both ends and everything between represent genuine creative engagement.

> 📊 [Figure 4.3 — Creativity Taxonomy: from imitation to originality (Nilsson)]

> 📊 [Table 4.2 — Classroom Examples of Creativity: activities across the novelty spectrum]

### Teaching for Creativity

- Open-ended, problem-based learning is more effective than single-right-answer exercises
- Humor primes the brain to think outside reality-bound patterns *(Ziv, 1983)*
- Play is uniquely suited to enhance creative thinking *(Russ, 1998)*
- **Helpful constraints** can *increase* creative demands — boundaries within which to innovate are often more effective than fully open assignments
- If assignments are too open-ended before students have the necessary skills, they overwhelm rather than invite creativity

> 💡 Creativity may be the most important skill for the 21st century — it is necessary for devising innovative solutions to the many challenges we face.

## Critical Thinking

The National Council for Excellence in Critical Thinking defines it as: *"the intellectually disciplined process of actively and skillfully conceptualizing, applying, analyzing, synthesizing, and/or evaluating information gathered from observation, experience, reflection, reasoning, or communication, as a guide to belief and action."*

Historian William Graham Sumner: critical thinking is *"the examination and test of propositions of any kind which are offered for acceptance, in order to find out whether they correspond to reality or not... It is our only guarantee against delusion, deception, superstition, and misapprehension."*

The core meaning: **questioning claims rather than accepting them at face value**.

Critical thinking traces back to Socrates — using questions to make students clarify assumptions and expose gaps in reasoning. Over 2,400 years later, it remains a top educational priority. Yet because critical thinking is harder to assess, it has been too often absent from knowledge-overloaded curricula. Students learn to take tests — a skill rarely transferable beyond the education system.

### Taxonomies of Critical Thinking

Bloom's Taxonomy organizes educational objectives from lower levels (remembering, retrieval) to higher levels (analysis, synthesis, evaluation). Multiple subsequent frameworks have organized similar components differently.

> 📊 [Figure 4.4 — Taxonomies of Educational Objectives: comparison of Bloom's and successor frameworks (Greenstein)]

> 💡 Current learning research shows these levels can be effectively *mixed together* in learning activities — they are not nearly as sequential as Bloom originally proposed *(Anderson & Krathwohl, 2001)*.

### Teaching Critical Thinking

Approaches range from explicit curricula devoted to component critical skills to projects involving:
- Interpreting information
- Analyzing parts and wholes; analysis and synthesis
- Evaluating evidence
- Taking multiple perspectives
- Discerning patterns; grasping abstract ideas

Teaching critical thinking is closely tied to **metacognitive habits of mind** — each can support and strengthen the other *(Kuhn, 1999)*. The main challenge: successful *transfer* of critical thinking skills to contexts outside the one in which they were originally learned.

---

## Communication

All professions require communication in various forms — negotiating, giving instructions, advising, building relationships, resolving conflicts. Research on communication skill development spans from preschool to medical school.

Traditional schoolwork (papers, presentations) is often one-sided and inconsequential — whether the audience actually understood the message rarely matters in the grade. This fails to develop truly interactive communication.

### More Effective Approaches

**Collaborative tasks** provide authentic communication contexts — real audiences, real feedback on whether the message was understood.

**Peer tutoring** is particularly powerful: teaching others demands clear communication, provides immediate feedback (did the other student understand?), increases the tutor's effort, and elevates their self-concept.

In the digital age, **media literacy** added to traditional reading and writing goals has been shown to:
- Increase learning by connecting literacy to students' home cultures
- Accommodate diverse learning styles and multicultural learners
- Develop creativity, self-expression, teamwork, and workplace skills *(Hobbs & Frost, 2015)*

---

## Collaboration

> In a world of increasing complexity, the best approaches to multifaceted problems involve collaboration among people with different skills, backgrounds, and perspectives.

When executed well, collaboration enables groups to make better decisions than any individual — by incorporating multiple viewpoints *(Surowiecki, 2005)*. When executed poorly, it collapses into **groupthink** *(Janis, 1971)*. Studies of scientific fields confirm that while specialization is real, interdisciplinary collaboration is often integral to major advances.

**Four proven classroom tactics for collaboration:**

| Tactic | Purpose |
|--------|---------|
| **Group agreements + accountability** | Sets stage for division of labor and synergy of efforts |
| **Listening skills** | Creates space where ideas can be shared, received, and applied |
| **Art of asking good questions** | Open-ended, thought-provoking questions expand knowledge and drive better solutions |
| **Negotiation skills** | Patient listening, flexibility, articulating points of agreement, clear thinking under pressure |

### Evidence for Collaborative Learning

- Increases learning outcomes, enjoyment of subject matter, self-esteem, and inclusiveness *(Johnson & Johnson, 1986)*
- Meta-analysis: collaborative learning more effective than individual or competitive learning for academic achievement *(Johnson, Johnson & Stanne, 2000)*
- Students more positive about school, subjects, teachers, and each other *(Johnson & Johnson, 1990)*
- **Synergistic** with the other 4Cs: authentic goal of communication, enhancer of critical thinking *(Gokhale, 1995)* and creativity *(Uzzi, 2005)*

---

## Applied Learning

Skills represent how we *use* what we know. The 4Cs are:

- In high demand by employers
- Key to deep understanding of knowledge
- Essential to facilitate transfer to new environments
- Inseparably connected to content knowledge — it is impossible to think critically about *nothing*

CCR's core position: **knowledge and skills developed together in a virtuous cycle** — the knowledge we engage in class becomes the source of creativity, the subject of critical thought and communication, and the impetus for collaboration. In this way students are better equipped for today's global challenges, tomorrow's workforce demands, and the timeless challenges of personal and societal fulfillment in a swiftly changing world.

## Why Develop Character Qualities?

Since ancient times, education has aimed to cultivate confident, compassionate students who become successful learners, contribute to their communities, and serve society as ethical citizens. **Character education** is about the acquisition and strengthening of virtues, values, and the capacity to make wise choices — for a well-rounded life and a thriving society.

The Millennium Project tracks 30 global variables and identifies "where we are winning, losing, and unclear." The highly worrisome areas where humanity is *losing* — environmental issues, corruption, terrorism, income inequality — all have significant ethical and character implications.

> 📊 [Figure 5.1 — Millennium Project Analysis of Areas Where We Are Losing: global tracking of 30 variables by status]

Advances in science and technology are a double-edged sword: they create new opportunities for collaboration *and* new ethical challenges — nuclear energy, genetic modification, material-progress-oriented value systems.

An 80-country global survey by the Business and Industry Advisory Council (BIAC) to the OECD found that **80% of employer organizations declared character education is becoming a more important issue** — with 100% saying their education system should do more to promote it.

### Three Broad Purposes

1. Build a foundation for lifelong learning
2. Support successful relationships at home, in the community, and in the workplace
3. Develop the personal values and virtues for sustainable participation in a globalized world

UNESCO (1996): *"There is every reason to place renewed emphasis on the moral and cultural dimensions of education… this process must begin with self-understanding through knowledge, meditation and the practice of self-criticism."*

**Important note:** character education does not require a religious framework. Religious perspectives may support it, but are neither necessary nor universal. The values in this dimension are relevant to all humans in the modern world regardless of faith tradition. And character qualities are not fixed traits — neuropsychology shows the brain is highly plastic, and many character qualities can be learned and developed through practice *(Lickona, 2004)*.

---

## The Six Character Qualities

**Character** is a concise, cross-culturally recognizable term that encompasses: agency, attitudes, behaviors, dispositions, mindsets, personality, temperament, values, beliefs, social and emotional skills. (CCR does not endorse the misleading labels "non-cognitive skills" or "soft skills.")

Character qualities — *how we behave and engage in the world* — are distinct from skills — *the ability to effectively use what one knows*. Knowledge and skills together are not sufficient; character qualities may be the better predictors of student success in further learning, productive work, and active civic engagement *(Poropat, 2014; Tough, 2013)*.

### Mindfulness

*Associated terms: self-awareness, self-esteem, self-actualization, growth, insight, consciousness, compassion, empathy, patience, acceptance, gratitude, interconnectedness, balance*

> *"If every 8-year-old in the world is taught meditation, we will eliminate violence from the world within one generation."* — Dalai Lama

Mindfulness is defined as *"the awareness that emerges through paying attention on purpose, in the present moment and non-judgmentally, to the unfolding of experiences moment by moment"* *(Kabat-Zinn, 1990)*. While often practiced through meditation, the two are not the same — mindfulness can be practiced through any daily experience: eating, walking, driving.

Ellen Langer: traditional "no pain, no gain" education — repetitive drills, unwavering focus — is designed for a static, predictable environment. For our constantly changing environment, mindfulness education is far more relevant and effective *(Langer, 1993)*.

**Research findings:**
- Enhances attention and focus; improves memory, self-acceptance, self-management, and self-understanding
- Associated with higher positive emotional affect, vitality, life satisfaction, self-esteem, and optimism
- Associated with higher autonomy, competence, and relatedness *(Brown & Ryan, 2003)*
- Has been used clinically for: stress, chronic pain, anxiety, depression, eating disorders, addiction
- Even brief training reduces fatigue and anxiety, and improves visual-spatial processing and executive functioning *(Zeidan et al., 2010)*

---

### Curiosity

*Associated terms: open-mindedness, exploration, passion, self-direction, motivation, initiative, innovation, enthusiasm, spontaneity*

> *"I have no special talents. I am only passionately curious."* — Albert Einstein

Curiosity traces to Cicero (*"an innate love of learning without the lure of any profit"*) and Aristotle (*an intrinsic desire for information*). Modern research reveals it is both:
- A **trait** (general capacity) and a **state** (sensitive to context, malleable with experience)
- An **internal drive** (homeostatic) and a **response to external cues** (stimulus-evoked)

The **information-gap theory** *(Loewenstein, 1994)*: curiosity is the feeling generated by a gap between what one knows and what one wants to know. The **optimal arousal model** — arrived at independently by Hebb (neuroscience), Piaget (developmental psychology), and Hunt (motivation) — shows curiosity follows an inverted U-curve: greatest when we know *enough to be interested*, are *surprised* by what we experience, but are still *uncertain* of how to make sense of it.

An fMRI study found: the greater the curiosity, the more resources participants would spend to get an answer — and the more likely they were to *remember* that information later. Higher curiosity also correlated with stronger activation of reward, prediction error, and memory regions of the brain *(Jeong et al., 2009)*.

---

### Courage

*Associated terms: bravery, determination, fortitude, confidence, risk-taking, persistence, optimism, zest, energy, stability*

> *"Nothing in the world is worth having or worth doing unless it means effort, pain, difficulty… I have never envied a human being who led an easy life."* — Theodore Roosevelt

Courage is the ability to act despite fear or uncertainty — in risky situations, or when feeling vulnerable *(Brown, 2012)*. A healthy dose of courage is highly valuable in professional, social, and personal life.

Entrepreneurship research illustrates the distinction well: entrepreneurs are not simply higher risk-takers on self-rated measures — they are **courageous** in that they perceive equivocal situations more positively than others do, seeing more strengths than weaknesses, more opportunities than threats *(Palich & Bagby, 1995)*. One paper characterizes organizational failures as "failures of courage" — no one responsible acted to prevent them *(Rate & Sternberg, 2007)*.

The capacity for courage is not fixed — it can be developed through appropriate learning experiences. The **courageous mindset** requires three positive intrapersonal traits that "loosen the hold a negative emotion has on the mind and body": openness to experience, conscientiousness, and self-efficacy strategies *(Hannah, Sweeney & Lester, 2007)*.

*Note:* risk-taking is higher in adolescents than in children or adults, and higher in males than in females — relevant context for how courage is developed and expressed at different ages.

---

### Resilience

*Associated terms: perseverance, resourcefulness, tenacity, grit, spunk, adaptability, flexibility, self-discipline, self-control, diligence, dealing with ambiguity*

> *"The greatest glory in living lies not in never falling, but in rising every time we fall."* — Nelson Mandela

Resilience is *"a dynamic process encompassing positive adaptation within the context of significant adversity"* *(Luthar, Cicchetti & Becker, 2000)*. The "dynamic process" framing is important — resilience is a *constellation of factors*, not a single trait.

**Grit** — Angela Duckworth's seminal work defines it as "perseverance and passion for long-term goals" and found it accounts for an average of 4% of variance in success outcomes *(Duckworth et al., 2007)*.

Three factors consistently identified in schools, communities, and social support systems as positively influencing resilience in youth *(Benard, 1995)*:

1. **Caring relationships**
2. **Communication of high expectations**
3. **Opportunities for meaningful involvement and participation**

Early resilience research focused on high-risk communities; now the field studies resilience as relevant to *all* students — and emphasizes building positive resilience factors rather than merely mitigating risk factors.

---

### Ethics

*Associated terms: humaneness, kindness, respect, justice, equity, fairness, compassion, tolerance, inclusiveness, integrity, honesty, authenticity, forgiveness, generosity, care*

> *"To educate a person in mind and not in morals is to educate a menace to society."* — Theodore Roosevelt

Ethics as a teachable quality is grounded in moral development theory — pioneered by Piaget and Dewey, expanded by Kohlberg and Gilligan. Children naturally progress through stages of moral reasoning:

| Stage | Orientation |
|-------|-------------|
| **Pre-conventional** | Obedience/punishment; self-interest |
| **Conventional** | Interpersonal accord; authority and social order |
| **Post-conventional** | Social contract; universal ethical principles |

Environments that encourage moral development provide: group participation, shared decision-making, and accountability for consequences of actions.

**Kohlberg's three classroom conditions for moral growth:**
1. Exposure to the next higher stage of reasoning
2. Situations that pose problems and contradictions for the student's current moral structure
3. An atmosphere of interchange and dialogue that combines the first two

> 💡 **Critical insight:** Knowledge of ethics does not automatically lead to ethical action. Moral behavior is highly context-dependent and involves motivation, emotion, courage, and the presence of strong ethical role models.

Empirical support: in one study, only 15% of post-conventional students cheated, vs. 55% of conventional and 70% of pre-conventional students. Within the conventional stage, 26% of "strong-willed" students cheated vs. 74% of "weak-willed" students *(Krebs & Kohlberg, 1973)*. Ethics is appropriately treated as a character quality rather than a knowledge domain — though studying ethics embedded in subject areas (bioethics, business ethics) may have some influence on ethical behavior.

---

### Leadership

*Associated terms: responsibility, accountability, integrity, humbleness, inspiration, teamwork, mentorship, social awareness, cross-cultural awareness, negotiation, diversity*

> *"As for the best leaders, the people do not notice their existence… When the best leader's work is done the people say, 'We did it ourselves!'"* — Lao-Tsu

The traditional view of leadership — the charismatic, extraordinary individual who inspires followers to act for a fixed organization — is giving way to a fundamentally different framework.

**Traditional (systems-control) view:**
- Leaders as heroes: exceptional, largely innate, unteachable
- Subordinates as followers; leaders as control maximizers
- At odds with research on quiet leadership *(Badaracco, 2001)* and studies showing successful leaders are often "shy, unpretentious, awkward and modest but with enormous ambition — not for themselves but for the organization" *(Collins, 2001)*

**Emerging (process-relational) view:**
- Organizations are social constructs of "ongoing patterns of meaning-making and activity brought about as people are in relationships"
- Leadership is a **set of processes, practices, and interactions** — not one individual's vision
- Complete control is neither possible nor desirable
- Group processes matter more than individual vision
- The skills needed (negotiation, asking insightful questions) are more learnable and more practical

This aligns with complex systems science: the individual leader **facilitates group processes and relationships** rather than imposing a top-down vision — which would limit the organization's capacity to a single individual *(Bar-Yam, 2002)*.

A widely accepted model defines leadership as *"a relational and ethical process of people attempting to accomplish positive change together"* *(Komives, Lucas & McMahon, 2006)*. Dimensions: **inclusive, empowering, purposeful, ethical, and process-oriented**.

# Chapter 6: The Meta-Learning Dimension

> *"The illiterate of the twenty-first century will not be those who cannot read and write, but those who cannot learn, unlearn, and relearn."*
> — Herbert Gerjuoy, as quoted by Alvin Toffler in *Future Shock*

---

The surest way to prepare students for a changing world is to give them the tools to be **versatile, reflective, self-directed, and self-reliant**.

The OECD calls this dimension *reflectiveness*. The EU Reference Framework, Hewlett Foundation Deeper Learning, and ATC21S all call it *learning how to learn*. CCR calls it **meta-learning** — and treats it as a distinct fourth dimension, not a background assumption implicit in the other three.

---

## Metacognition — Reflecting on Learning Goals, Strategies, and Results

Metacognition is, simply put, **thinking about thinking**. It is present across all aspects of school and life — self-reflection on one's current position, future goals, potential strategies, and results. It is a basic survival strategy; evidence of it has been found even in rats.

The most important function of metacognition: **improving the transfer of competencies** — applying what was learned in one context to a different context *(Schraw & Moshman, 1995)*. Transfer is the ultimate goal of all education. It is also frequently the missing link.

> 📊 [Figure 6.1 — Metacognition: the reflective loop connecting goals, strategies, monitoring, and results (CCR)]

**A vivid example from mathematics:** when novice students were compared to seasoned mathematicians, novices selected a strategy and continued applying it without checking whether it was working — wasting significant time in fruitless pursuits. Experienced mathematicians continuously monitored their approach to see if it was leading to a solution or a dead end *(Gourgey, 1998)*. Metacognition optimizes the problem-solving process at a high level and is applicable across all disciplines.

### Levels of Metacognitive Process

Research has identified three levels of metacognitive reporting — and they are not equally valuable:

| Level | Description | Effect on Learning |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **1** | Verbalization of already-verbal knowledge (recalling what happened in a story) | Limited |
| **2** | Verbalization of non-verbal knowledge (recalling how one solved a Rubik's Cube) | Limited |
| **3** | Verbalization of *explanations* of knowledge (explaining how one uses a story's rhetorical structures while reading) | **Linked to improved problem-solving** |

Only Level 3 metacognitive processes have been linked to improved results in problem solving *(Hacker & Dunlosky, 2003)*.

> 💡 Traditional learning strategy methods — note-taking, self-testing, scheduling — often produce initial motivation and short-term improvement, then revert. More strategic methods that develop metacognition for *deeper* learning — growth mindset, goal-setting, monitoring, persistence — produce more permanent learning gains *(Biggs, 1988)*.

**An important equity note:** metacognition can be developed regardless of starting achievement level. It may be *most useful for lower-achieving students*, as higher achievers are already applying effective strategies. For learning-disabled and low-achieving students, metacognitive training improves behavior more effectively than traditional attention-control training *(Larson & Gerber, 1987)*.

Students with higher self-efficacy are more likely to engage in metacognition, and in turn are more likely to perform at higher levels — creating a **positive feedback loop** for high-achieving students that is also amenable to instruction.

---

## Internalizing a Growth Mindset

Students absorb from society a subconscious model of how talent and hard work contribute to success. These models surface in familiar ways: bragging about not having studied for an exam; claiming to be "just not good at" a subject; giving up when challenged.

Carol Dweck's research identifies two broad categories of mental model:

> 📊 [Figure 6.2 — Two Mindsets: Fixed vs. Growth (Dweck/Holmes)]

| Mindset | Core Belief | Behavioral Pattern |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Fixed** | Qualities like intelligence and talent are fixed; talent alone creates success | Documents performance rather than developing ability; sees mistakes as failures; reduces effort when challenged |
| **Growth** | Abilities can be developed through hard work; talent is a starting point | Loves learning for its own sake; sees mistakes as opportunities; increases effort when challenged |

Learning-oriented students (growth mindset) tend to:
- Employ more metacognitive strategies
- Attain higher levels of academic achievement *(Coutinho, 2007)*
- Expend more effort when confronted with challenges

These internal models of learning capacity influence metacognitive processes **as early as third grade** *(Miele, Son & Metcalfe, 2013)*.

Alfred Binet, inventor of the IQ test, was himself a growth mindset advocate: *"We must protest and react against this brutal pessimism [that intelligence is fixed]… With practice, training, and above all, method, we manage to increase our attention, our memory, our judgment and literally to become more intelligent than we were before."* We now know the brain changes with experience in every single moment — neuroplasticity is established science.

---

## Why Meta-Learning Is the Fourth Dimension

As adults, external goal-setting and deadlines are removed. The majority of most people's lives will be spent out of school, requiring **internal motivation to keep growing**.

Two sobering realities:

1. In clinical research, the **half-life of truth is 45 years** *(Poynard et al., 2002)*. Half of what doctors learned in school — if they don't update their knowledge themselves — is wrong by the time they retire.

2. On average, only **7% of people** who sign up for an online class follow it through to completion. Even the motivated few struggle to self-direct.

More sharply: deep knowledge of ethics does not make people more ethical in their lives. One study found that academic ethicists were no more likely than others to donate to charity, vote in elections, stay in contact with their mothers, or return library books *(Schwitzgebel, 2013)*. Understanding something is not the same as applying it.

**Meta-learning bridges this gap.** It is:

- **Metacognition** to recognize opportunities for improvement
- **Growth mindset** to believe improvement is possible
- **Metacognition again** to plan, monitor, and evaluate learning strategies

Meta-learning is the internalized voice that asks: *"How do I know this is the right approach?"* and says: *"I can do this, if I keep trying."* It supports and rounds out every other dimension — creating the goal-setting and feedback loops through which students continue to improve **without needing teachers or parents to prod them at every step**.

It sets students up for: lifelong self-directed learning, productive careers in a shifting world, and continued growth as the world continues to change what it means to be an effective twenty-first century person.

## A Feedback Loop Between What and How

This book focuses on the *what* of education — but the what and the how are inseparably linked. Jurisdictions decide what standards and assessments get implemented; schools provide feedback from the practices of curriculum and instruction, and what works as measured by assessments, evaluations, and R&D.

> 📊 [Figure 7.1 — What and How Feedback Loop: jurisdiction standards ↔ school curriculum and instruction ↔ assessments and R&D (CCR)]

The same topic, covered one way, will be uninteresting and ineffective. Covered another way, it can have practical, cognitive, and emotional value that lasts a lifetime. From the sections on skills, character, and meta-learning, it is clear that courage cannot be developed strictly through classroom time, or communication through passive lecture. **Practices must match content and educational goals.**

Twenty-first century learning activities go beyond didactic lecturing, into:
- Project-based learning
- Inquiry learning
- Debate and dialogue
- Design challenges
- Performance and exhibition
- Expedition and fieldwork
- Sport and play
- Contemplation and mindfulness practices

## The Interplay with Technology

> *"How can technology enhance what we aim to do in the classroom?"* — a better question than "How can we use technology in the classroom?"

Teaching is the priority; **technology ought to be invisible**. If we showcase technology in our schools, we are missing the point — we ought to showcase the learning that the technology enables. Content and competence must be king and queen.

Technology is neither a silver bullet nor an education-ruiner. It is a set of tools to enhance students' educational experiences.

**The risk:** if a student is not in the habit of sense-making while doing math problems, a computer will only *hide and exacerbate* this missing skill. Technology can produce superficial understanding.

**The opportunity:** the same technology — used differently — can *develop* deeper understanding. Example: the QAMA calculator requires students to enter an appropriate estimate before seeing the correct answer, making the tool demand rather than bypass mathematical thinking.

**Four key capabilities of technology in education:**

| Capability | What It Enables |
|-----------|----------------|
| **Processing power** | Frees students from computational drudgery to focus on higher-order thinking — software handles complex algorithms and data so students can analyze and interpret |
| **Global resources** | Exposes students to diverse ideas and perspectives; develops critical information consumption in a world of infinite opinion |
| **Human connection** | Connects students to people worldwide — instantly and richly — enabling genuine practice of communication, collaboration, and cross-cultural understanding |
| **Personalization** | Potential (still being realized) to adapt to each student's needs, present optimal difficulty and feedback, signal to teachers where guidance is most needed, and integrate assessment with learning in real time |

> 💡 **Computer-assisted vs. computer-based learning:** computer-*assisted* education simply transposes traditional learning to a computer interface. Computer-*based* learning uses computers as tools that students use to train their higher-level thinking. The distinction matters enormously.

The future learning environment — immersive, game-like, personalized, formatively assessed in real time — is possible. Student autonomy, mastery orientation, and larger purpose as motivations for learning are the design goals. We are not there yet, but the direction is clear.

## Education, Evidence, and Action

Some may argue that not enough scientific evidence yet exists to conclude that all dimensions and elements of the CCR framework would truly benefit humanity if taught well. The authors reject this framing directly.

There is a danger in believing a false dichotomy: either science has 100% proven something true, or we can say nothing about it. Even well-established concepts like evolution suffer from this false critique. We must not let perfect be the enemy of necessary.

> 💡 **The streetlight effect** — observational bias: only looking for answers where we expect to find them. CCR has deliberately come from a practical notion of world needs and appropriate educational goals, synthesizing the education we *need*, not merely an incremental improvement on the education we *have*.

**Not acting is itself an action** — and one for which we have strong evidence of harmful consequences. The current system is not succeeding in preparing all students for the twenty-first century. The gaps between what students need and what schools provide have not been closing fast enough. A false standard of absolute scientific proof is not a viable option for inaction.

The existing system also should not receive a free pass: there is substantial research documenting its shortcomings. Even for students who *succeeded* within the current system, we cannot know whether a different system might have enabled them to flourish even more.

Conrad Wolfram distinguishes:
- **Innovation-led evidence**: build something new, then test its worthiness
- **Evidence-led innovation**: use formal evidence from past products to design new ones

Wolfram argues the former is more productive for fundamental innovation — the latter, by definition, restricts new products to the space of what already existed. Significant innovation requires a *leap*: new insights from new perspectives, observation, iteration, and flashes of understanding that aren't entirely evidence-led.

### The Evidence We Do Have

The Hewlett Foundation's **Deeper Learning initiative** — research on over 500 schools — shows strong evidence that all students can be more successful when given opportunities to learn an expanded set of competencies aligned with the CCR framework. An American Institutes for Research report found that students in Deeper Learning network schools:

- Performed better than similar students in comparison schools on test scores
- Showed higher measures of interpersonal and intrapersonal skills
- Had higher on-time high school graduation rates
- Had higher college enrollment rates

We can do better. We must synthesize all of the expertise we have gained, and then make a well-considered leap to innovate.

## Societal Meta-Learning

Seen from a wider angle: what CCR is attempting is itself a meta-learning process — at the scale of society. We are examining our collective learning goals and strategies, monitoring and reflecting on our progress and setbacks, learning from experience, and trying new innovations — all to redesign education for our times.

This book is one step in that direction: a summary of the challenges facing our twenty-first century world, and a framework of educational goals to best meet those challenges.

The question that started it all — and remains the animating question of CCR's work:

> **What should students learn for the twenty-first century?**

We can think of no greater challenge, or more exciting journey.

## About CCR

**The Center for Curriculum Redesign (CCR)** is an international convening body and research center seeking to expand humanity's potential and improve collective prosperity by redesigning K–12 education standards for the twenty-first century.

CCR brings together constituencies with diverse points of view — international organizations, jurisdictions, academic institutions, corporations, and nonprofit organizations — to respond to the central question:

> *"What should students learn for the twenty-first century?"*

**Guiding principle:** a relevant education, based on meaningful curriculum, is critical to creating sustainability, balance, and wellbeing. CCR argues that the *what* of K–12 education is at least as important as the *how* — and brings a singular focus to the what.

CCR avoids dogma and emphasizes innovation and synthesis — multiple inputs applied and organized for optimum clarity and impact.

**CCR's Assessments Research Consortium** aims to harmonize disparate global assessment efforts and establish a collective field for redesigned systems of measuring student progress aligned to 21st-century goals — across all four dimensions of education: Knowledge, Skills, Character, and Meta-Learning. The consortium targets completion of assessment recommendations within a three-to-five-year period.
