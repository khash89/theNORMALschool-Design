# Prologue
*by Olli-Pekka Heinonen, Director General, International Baccalaureate Organization; Former Minister of Education of Finland*

---

We are living in decisive times for education. PISA 2022 showed an unprecedented drop in performance across the OECD — only partly attributed to the pandemic. Globally, 44 million teachers are short of what is needed to reach every child. Many existing teachers are leaving the profession due to stress and working conditions. An increasing number of parents and students are questioning the value of time and money spent on schooling.

The multiple crises we face are global and adaptive by nature. They cannot be solved only by leaders, innovators, or scientists — they require changes in how we perceive the world, treat one another, and relate to the environment. **Current global challenges are also educational challenges.**

AI magnifies these pressures sharply. Existing curricula are biased toward *just knowing* — the exact area where AI most outperforms human beings. Discussion around 21st-century skills has been underway for 30 years, yet the education community is still struggling to embed these skills into everyday classroom practice. It is becoming painfully evident that "21st century skills" may ironically refer to the fact that at the present rate, it will take a century to integrate them systematically.

> 💡 We do not have a shortage of well-formulated educational strategies. What we lack is the capability to embed them into the reality of *every* school and classroom — not just top-ranked ones.

Education systems respond well to acute crises, but poorly to slow and deep societal fluctuations. Non-urgent but vital changes get buried under day-to-day routines. The best antidote to AI-related uncertainty among educators: learn more about AI and the human/AI relationship, and discern how to use it as a tool for better teaching and learning.

The core question throughout this text: *what does it mean to be human in today's world?* AI is positioned as a mirror, reflecting back who we are. We cannot keep everything taught previously and simultaneously add every new demand. In curriculum, **less can be more**. Prioritizing what is essential — and having the courage to trade away what is not — is more important today than ever.

*— 12 January 2024*

# Introduction: Education During Rapidly-Changing Times

---

## The Increased Need for Change

In CCR's 2015 book *Four-Dimensional Education*, we forewarned about converging global trends putting significant pressure on *what* should be learned, while worldwide efforts had focused almost entirely on the *how*. In 2018, we sounded the alarm about the AI wave — then Covid became the dominant global focus.

Both waves have now combined. The AI wave may force action on the 4D agenda that Covid deferred — because **there is no hiding from AI**. Its ramp-up took a century; its impact has been sudden. That is the hallmark of exponential systems: deceiving, then explosive.

The traditional functions of schooling remain necessary: child care, socialization, education goals/curricula, and accreditation. This book addresses the third — *what* students should learn.

## Education's Four Ideologies

Boston College's Michael Schiro identified four ideologies that have driven education debates *(Schiro, 2007)*:

| Ideology | Core Goal |
|----------|-----------|
| **Scholar Academic** | Help children learn the academic knowledge of a culture |
| **Learner Centered** | Growth of the individual in harmony with personal attributes |
| **Social Reconstruction** | Social transformation through education |
| **Social Efficiency** | Train youth to become contributing members of society |

These are routinely framed as "OR" debates — but all four goals are needed simultaneously. CCR's "education engineering" position: it is perfectly possible to accommodate all goals at once through careful, solid design. Chapter Eight gives a worked example.

## Why Updating the 4D Framework Matters

This book is a needed update to *Four-Dimensional Education* — published a mere nine years ago, which is an eternity in an age of rapid technological progress. The good news: the fundamental 4D framework (now available in 23 languages) has not only held strong, it has become *more imperative* given AI and societal pressures. The bad news: education systems had not yet begun seriously addressing the 4D needs described nine years ago, due to deep systemic inertia.

This book documents the added pressures from AI and frames a **"Rev 1.2"** of the framework — subtle but significant adaptations for learning *for* and *with* AI. It revisits CCR's central question: *"Why, What, and How should students learn for the age of AI?"*

> 💡 What CCR will **not** cover: the philosophical debates about human vs. artificial intelligence, AI consciousness, General Intelligence, or Superintelligence. What happens by the 2040s is speculation. The focus here is on the **Artificial Capable Intelligence (ACI)** phase at hand — the capabilities that are real, present, and already reshaping education.

## The Need for Stability: Schools as Islands of Stability

This may seem contradictory to the call for change — but it addresses a different dimension: **emotional grounding**. Students are stressed. The more technology pushes digitalization and virtuality, the more grounding becomes necessary.

Toffler's "future shock" concept and Harvey's "time-space compression" both predicted that accelerating change would produce disorientation and alienation — and that people would need "islands of stability": familiar spaces, rituals, values *(Azhar, 2023)*.

Schools can provide these islands — for familiarity, rituals, values — while also educating students on how to *handle* ephemerality (adaptability, resilience, learning how to learn). The latter is what most of this book addresses.

**Deliberate exclusions from this book** (to be published as subsequent CCR papers):
- Emotional support / mental well-being
- Psychomotor skills
- Psychophysiological aspects (sensation, perception, attention, memory)
- Personality traits and developmental phases
- Formative assessments and accreditation

## Move 37: An "Unhuman" Strategy

When DeepMind beat Go world champion Lee Sedol, it played a move humans couldn't comprehend — and won. What seemed like genius was simply a strategy humans don't use: because DeepMind can compute 50–60 moves ahead and calculate the probability of winning even by the slightest margin, it can take risks no human would dare. Human Go strategy maximizes gain territory out of fear of running short — not playing at the edge of losing.

The same happened when AI faced a fighter pilot in a DARPA trial: it dove toward him, calculating it could "draw and shoot faster." No human would risk that maneuver. In both cases, AI deploys a **non-anthropocentric strategy** — devoid of the anti-loss biases that come from fear of death.

> 💡 AI is not "courageous" in human terms. It is simply devoid of fear — "fear-less" not "fearless." It computed its best chances of success and acted accordingly.

## The Limitations of Language

English has restricted vocabulary for nuanced attributes like creativity, curiosity, and resilience — unlike Saami reindeer herders who have up to 1,000 lexemes for snow and ice. This limitation generates persistent confusion in public and academic discourse about AI:

- "Hallucinations" → more accurately: **confabulations**
- "Emergence" → more accurately: **thresholding**
- "Transfer" → more accurately: **generalization**
- Invoking the Turing Test when far more precise benchmarks exist

Precision matters. Imprecise language about AI capabilities fuels both unjustified fear and unjustified complacency.

## Four Levels of AI

| Level | Name | What It Is |
|-------|------|------------|
| 1 | **ANI** — Artificial Narrow Intelligence | Excels at one specific task; cannot generalize beyond it (Go, protein folding, facial recognition) |
| 2 | **ACI** — Artificial Capable Intelligence | Broad, highly potent range of impactful capabilities. **The phase we are in.** Most of this book addresses ACI. |
| 3 | **AGI** — Artificial General Intelligence | Mimics full human cognitive abilities across all domains. Gary Marcus: "a few paradigm shifts away." |
| 4 | **ASI** — Artificial Superintelligence | Exceeds all human capabilities in every dimension. Raises existential questions. Speculative. |

The jump from ANI to ACI is enormous and underappreciated. Many debates skip this critical middle stage entirely. Several leading voices push back on AGI hype:

- **Mustafa Suleyman** (DeepMind/Inception.ai) coined "ACI" for the present phase
- **Yann LeCun** (Meta): "Society is more likely to get 'cat-level' or 'dog-level' AI years before human-level AI"
- **Gary Marcus**: "No fantasy about AGI survives contact with the real world"
- **Fei-Fei Li** (Stanford): "In terms of urgency, I'm more concerned about ameliorating the risks that are here and now"
- **Sam Altman**: "There are more breakthroughs required in order to get to AGI"

**Three reasons for the overhype:**
1. "Proud Dad syndrome" — LLM researchers fervently hope to see their creation do it all after decades of toiling
2. The intense human tendency to anthropomorphize makes even experts "over-patternize"
3. The need to raise enormous capital biases thinking

## The Major Change Brought on by Machine Learning

Good innovations take decades to combine and explode. The lineage of modern AI:

- **1895** — Markov process: probability of each event depends only on the prior state
- **1921/1958** — Transistor, then integrated circuit; Moore's Law begins
- **1950s–70s** — Artificial neural networks, backpropagation algorithm
- **1989** — Still could only compute 3 layers of artificial neurons
- **2000s** — Ten-million-fold increase in semiconductor performance; GPUs enable Deep Learning
- Deep Learning re-emerged and could solve: gaming (Go, Stratego), protein folding, gene mutations, artistic style transfer, facial/vocal recognition, translation, recommendation systems

Brian Cantwell Smith's framing: **Reckoning vs. Judgment**. AI excels at reckoning — calculative prediction and formulaic decision-making. Judgment — deliberative thought, grounded in ethical commitment, appropriate to context — remains distinctly human.

Despite its prowess, early ML could not *transfer* across domains ("bounded problems"). It was also:
- **Brittle** — a pixel change breaks recognition
- **Opaque** — neural nets don't show their work
- **Greedy** — requires petabytes of data
- **Prone to over-tuning** — finds a trained factor in the wrong places

---

## The Sudden Improvement Due to LLMs

LLMs exploded the bounded-problem constraint by training on enormous, highly varied datasets. ChatGPT (OpenAI), Bard/Gemini (Google), Claude (Anthropic) are the main proprietary instances; LLaMA, Falcon, Mistral are open alternatives.

Claude Shannon's 1948 *Theory of Communication* laid the conceptual foundation: computing the probabilities of letters, then digrams, then words would eventually create meaningful sentences. It took until ~2020 — when high computing power, low storage costs, and novel algorithms combined — to realize it at scale.

> 📊 [Figure: CCR's "punctuated equilibria" diagram — technology progresses in steps, not smoothly]

**Software accelerants now in play:**
- Continued LLM token and context window growth (Moore's Law)
- Algorithmic refinements 3–5× faster than Moore's Law
- LLMs with persistent memory (OpenAI Project Sunshine)
- Multimodality (GPT/Whisper, Gemini) — text, image, audio, video
- Multi-Agent Models working in ensemble (like brain areas)
- Co-processors at the edge enabling local LLM on laptop/smartphone
- Verticalization to specialist corpora (healthcare, finance, education)

**UC Berkeley's Steinhardt on "LLM2030":**
- Superhuman at coding, hacking, math, protein design
- 5× faster than humans in words processed/minute; parallelizable to 1.8 million years of work
- 2,500 human-equivalent years of learning per day (from shared weights)
- Trained on additional modalities: molecular structures, brain scans, astronomical images, low-level machine code

> 💡 CCR agrees with Roy Amara (Institute for the Future): *"We tend to overestimate the effect of technology in the short run, and underestimate the effect in the long run."*

## What AI Can Do Today

- **Automation**: repetitive and tedious tasks — data entry, QC, testing; frees humans for strategic/creative work
- **Prediction**: identifies patterns across vast data; weather, markets, diagnostics
- **Personalization**: tailors products/services to individual preferences and behavior
- **Image and speech recognition**: medical imaging, security, accessibility
- **Language processing**: chatbots, virtual assistants, translation, summarization

## LLM Limitations

- **Severe anterograde amnesia**: cannot form new memories or learn from past interactions beyond the training window. Answers are limited to what was trained, plus what's in the current prompt.
- **Confabulations ("hallucinations")**: generates incorrect, nonsensical, or unrelated output despite coherent input — due to data gaps, context misinterpretation, overgeneralization, training bias, or edge cases.
- **No world model**: no real understanding of content; cannot update knowledge autonomously; no experience-based learning; no common-sense reasoning. LLMs must "speak in order to think."
- **Recency**: freshness depends on last training run — improving in newer models.

Yann LeCun: *"If future AI systems are built on the same blueprint as current LLMs, they may become highly knowledgeable but will still be dumb. They will still hallucinate, still be difficult to control, and still merely regurgitate what they've been trained on. They will still be unable to reason, invent new things, or plan actions."*

## What AI Can't Do — and CCR's Counterarguments

| Common Claim | CCR's Counterargument |
|---|---|
| **Creativity**: AI lacks true originality | By far the largest proportion of creativity is *incremental* and can be guided by patterns. Radical creativity (imagination, originality) is more human, but "temperature" settings and harvesting confabulations can approximate it. |
| **Emotional intelligence**: AI can't understand feelings | It cannot understand, but it can *represent* and *read* human emotions. It can be taught empathy and is naturally patient and tireless. |
| **Judgment**: AI can't make ethical/moral decisions | Safeguards are being put in place. Its answers can be pre-filtered. But trained on human data, it reflects human biases back at us. |
| **Contextual understanding**: AI misreads ambiguous situations | Training on multimodal real-world sources via APIs and LangChains is improving this. Sensory inputs from IoT, cameras, and instruments give AI far broader dynamic range than human senses. |
| **Common sense**: AI makes mistakes in unusual situations | Improving via better real-world understanding and embrace of neuro-symbolic algorithms. |

**Additional limitations (ChatGPT's own assessment, with CCR responses):**

| Limitation | Possibly Overcome By |
|---|---|
| Dependence on training data | APIs to specialized tools + LangChained vector databases |
| Bias (reflects training data biases) | Pre-filtering; but bias mirrors human bias — by design |
| Overgeneralization | Algorithmic work ongoing; industry-specific datasets help |
| Verifiability (outputs may be wrong) | Retrieval Augmented Generation with source citations |
| Ethical misuse (deepfakes, disinfo) | Safeguards exist but are not yet very effective; state-level bad actors remain a concern |
| Resource intensity | Cloud services reducing upfront cost; environmental policy needed |
| No goal-directed behavior | CCR: *"That is a good thing, and eminently fillable by humans."* |

## Human Weaknesses to Watch

- **Anthropomorphization**: the natural tendency to read human traits into AI behavior. Products are often *designed* to activate it (the "Like" button's micro-dopamine hit is a mild precursor). *Narayanan & Kapoor*: developers should avoid behaviors that make anthropomorphization easy; journalists should avoid clickbait.
- **Double standards on error**: One autonomous vehicle kills a person and everyone screams "bad robot" — yet humans kill thousands yearly while driving. Why do we demand perfection from machines but extend grace to humans?
- **Fear of the new apex predator**: Yann LeCun: *"They won't want to dominate us because they won't have any objective that drives them to dominate — unlike social species like humans."*
- **Anthropocentricity**: We expect AI to have human-like intelligence. Feynman: *"Airplanes don't fly like birds. They fly, but they don't fly like a bird."*

## Potential Saturation: The Limits on Exponential Growth

Exponential growth cannot remain exponential forever:

- **Diminishing returns** from scaling model size — developers conceding that multimodality and better datasets matter more than bulk
- **Running out of high-quality data** (forecast for 2026): humanity generates high-quality data far more slowly than AI trains on it; "synthetic data" is being explored with unclear results
- **Moore's Law plateauing**: transistors at 1nm; migration to mixed CPU+GPU architectures, neuromorphic processors, less data-hungry algorithms
- **Reasoning breakthrough needed**: LLMs operate on pattern recognition. LeCun: *"Train a system on 20,000 years of reading material, and they still don't understand that if A = B, then B = A."* Neuro-symbolic AI is the next frontier — but breakthroughs are not available on demand (witness the $100B meltdown in autonomous vehicles).
- Legal, geopolitical, environmental, and management challenges all act as brakes.

> 💡 CCR remains concerned that AI's progress will remain significantly faster than humans' ability to absorb it — despite all limitations.

## Unintended Consequences

The "unknown unknowns" are the greatest risk. SMS was deployed as a simple way to fill unused bandwidth. No one anticipated it would contribute, two decades later via Twitter, to the erosion of democratic discourse. AI's unintended consequences will be similarly unanticipated.

**How AI may alter human cognition:**
- **Memory outsourcing**: As we offloaded phone numbers to smartphones, we'll offload more cognitive tasks to AI — potentially reducing internal memory formation
- **Decision-making scaffolding**: Professionals increasingly use AI as a cognitive tool, potentially altering how they evaluate options
- **Extended mind**: tools and technologies become integrated into cognitive processes, extending the mind beyond the brain *(Clark & Chalmers, 1998)*

AI could enhance cognitive abilities in data processing, pattern recognition, and prompt crafting — while potentially diminishing skills that get outsourced: analogizing, rote memory, certain forms of judgment.

## Agents and the Birth of "Kybernetology"

Machine-to-machine interaction without human intervention is already active: autonomous vehicles coordinating traffic, smart grids balancing load, factory robots collaborating, healthcare devices sharing patient data in real time.

As increasingly sophisticated agents interact at the "cognitive" level, a new field of study will emerge. CCR predicts: **Kybernetology** — the study of interactions between reasoned AI agents. (From the ancient Greek *kybernḗtēs*: helmsperson. "Cybernetics" was already taken.)

## LLM Business Advantages

- **Verticalization**: Industry-specific LLMs tailored to domain needs — e.g., Google and Stanford medical LLMs, Bloomberg and Columbia finance LLMs, OpenEvidence.
- **Rapid Prototyping & Ease of Use**: Fine-tuning without new model designs; faster time-to-market.
- **Dynamic Adaptation**: Task-centric LLMs adapted on-the-fly to changing business needs.
- **Scalability and Generalization**: LLMs reusable across business applications via fine-tuning.
- **High Performance**: State-of-the-art results in NLP, image recognition, data analysis.
- **Knowledge Transfer**: Learning applied from one domain to another (limited but improving).
- **Open-source Ecosystem**: Community support around foundation models (LLaMA, Falcon, Mistral).

## LLM Business Concerns

- **Rapid Evolution**: Monthly paradigm shifts can require engineers to restart implementation — a double-edged sword of perpetual improvement and perpetual catch-up.
- **Misuse & Safety**: Easy generation of harmful/misleading content; safety measures lag.
  - Ethical/societal impact: models perpetuate training data biases and stereotypes
  - Data leakage: sensitive information inadvertently encoded in model weights
  - Lack of interpretability: opaque decision-making complicates regulatory compliance
  - Overfitting/brittleness: unreliable outputs on out-of-distribution data
  - Legal ambiguity: unresolved attribution, liability, and IP questions

## Reasons for Skepticism

Oxford's Frey & Osborne (2013) alarmed policymakers with a claim that "up to 47% of jobs might be automatable" — a headline that used two conditionals ("up to," "might") but landed as fact. A more sober OECD analysis (2018) concluded 14%. Frey & Osborne's 2023 follow-up quietly dropped quantitative forecasts. Morgan Stanley and McKinsey estimates (300M jobs; 30% of occupations) also remain high-level and unverifiable. The WEF's framing — "23% of global jobs will change in the next five years" — is more grounded, focusing on language-intensity of tasks.

**The test → task → occupation problem:** Passing an exam (law, medicine) is not the same as performing a task (background research); a task is not an occupation (which combines multiple tasks plus underappreciated "glue" between them). The complexity jump may be a factor of ten at each level — and nobody has measured it rigorously.

Context-dependency further complicates automation projections. Driving in rural Manitoba is not driving at the Place de l'Étoile in Paris. UN interpreting is not hotel check-in translation. Routine versions of language-heavy tasks are more automatable; high-stakes, context-dependent versions are much less so.

The US O*NET database — the best available occupational dataset — is self-reported and thus biased. A proper **Cognitive Task Analysis** (following professionals to document what they actually do) has not been conducted at scale. Bureau of Labor Statistics data shows jobs change *far* more slowly than feared: cashier jobs are only beginning to decline, and mainly due to online shopping, not self-checkout scanners (which are actually in retreat).

> 💡 Futurists and labor economists have a consistently poor track record at forecasting *new* jobs that will emerge.

**Jobs NOT predicted a decade ago** *(World Economic Forum, 2016)*:

| Job | Pay Level |
|-----|-----------|
| App developer | High |
| Driverless car engineer | High |
| Cloud computing specialist | High |
| Big data analyst / data scientist | High |
| Social media manager | Medium |
| Sustainability manager | Medium |
| YouTube content creator | Medium |
| Millennial generational expert | Medium |
| Drone operator | Medium |
| Uber driver | Low |

Speculative future jobs (hit-or-miss): 3D Printed Clothing Designer, Augmented Reality Architect, Machine Psychologist, Data Hostage Negotiator, Smart Dust Programmer, Urban Agriculturist, Global System Architect, Personality Manager — and many more.

**Often-overlooked factor**: unemployment fears completely ignore the present difficulty of filling existing vacancies in both developing and developed countries — a shortage further accelerated by declining demographics (e.g., Germany turning to robots as baby boomers retire).

## The Sober View: Substitution vs. Complementarity

Two effects are unfolding simultaneously — they are not mutually exclusive.

**Substitution**: Technology replaces workers or jobs. The widely cited statement — *"AI won't replace you, but someone with AI will"* — has been substantiated by Harvard Business School. Those who harness AI will outperform and outcompete those who don't. Better to get in the car than run behind it.

**Complementarity**: Technology enhances the value of human labor rather than replacing it. A landmark HBS field study *(Dell'Acqua et al., 2023)* on knowledge workers described the **"jagged technological frontier"** — some tasks are easily done by AI, others seemingly similar in difficulty are entirely outside AI's reach. Two patterns of successful human-AI integration emerged:

- **Centaurs** (half-horse/half-human): dividing tasks between self and AI — delegating what AI handles well, retaining what humans do better.
- **Cyborgs**: fully integrating task flow with AI, continuously interacting with the technology throughout.

**Risk: skill atrophy.** Over-reliance on technology pushes workers into "auto-pilot" mode — they stop actively engaging, fail to refine skills, and lose the capacity to adapt when technology fails or circumstances change *(Autor, Levy & Murnane, 2003)*.

**Real-world sector examples:**

- **Journalism**: LLMs assist with preliminary research and standard reports. Critical thinking, fact-checking, analysis, storytelling, and ethical judgment remain harder to replicate.
- **Customer Service**: LLMs handle initial queries efficiently. Complex issues requiring emotional intelligence, empathy, and relationship-building still require humans.
- **Medicine**: LLMs parse medical literature and suggest differential diagnoses. Actual practice involves physical examination, non-verbal cue interpretation, and decisions combining objective data with subjective judgment.

## Recap

1. Economists do not fully understand the complexity gap between tests, tasks, and jobs.
2. Technology diffusion in businesses historically takes decades.
3. Forecasters have consistently missed the emergence of new occupations.
4. Employment demographics are routinely underappreciated.
5. Substitution and complementarity will both be at play — and remain hard to forecast.

With all these caveats: the fear that "all jobs will disappear" any time soon can be dismissed. Since jobs will still exist for the foreseeable future, the goal of education remains *dual* — psychosocial and economic. How to pursue both is the subject of the rest of this book. It starts with the ultimate goal of education: **Wisdom**.

## Why Wisdom? Societal Advancement — and Survival

Wisdom has been central to human group survival since hunter-gatherer societies: wise elders enabled decision-making, conflict resolution, and navigation of environmental threats. As societies grew more complex, wisdom became more important — navigating hierarchies, understanding norms, foreseeing consequences, reconciling differing perspectives.

Yet globally, beliefs are winning against knowledge, amplified by social media's appeal to emotion. André Malraux: *"Le 21ème siècle sera religieux ou ne sera pas"* — civilization will either find guiding principles or self-destruct. MIT's Danny Hillis: *"Welcome to the Age of Unreason."* Fourteen potential evolutionary dead-ends for humanity *(Søgaard Jørgensen et al., 2023)* make the stakes for collective wisdom higher than ever. University entrance and employment requirements are necessary — but not sufficient. Education must produce the next generation of change agents.

## What Exactly Is Wisdom? Insightful Discernment in Context

Wisdom is loosely and variously defined across cultures:

| Tradition | Core Definition |
|-----------|----------------|
| **Oxford (UK)** | Ability to make sensible decisions and give good advice from experience and knowledge |
| **Merriam-Webster (USA)** | Ability to discern inner qualities and relationships; insight; good sense; judgment |
| **Sagesse (French)** | Higher ideal of life; sound, informed judgment; prudence, moderation, strength of character |
| **Sophía (Greek)** | "Clever, skillful, wise"; Socratic epistemic humility — "I know that I know nothing" |
| **Prajñā / Sherab (Tibetan)** | Sixth of the six paramitas; precise discernment of all things; "pristine" knowledge |
| **Hinduism** | Profound self-awareness; recognition of one's connection to all creation and the Supreme Soul |
| **Prajñā (Buddhist)** | "Seeing things as they are"; penetrative understanding of all phenomena |
| **Ḥikma (Arabic)** | Knowledge, understanding, and means of nourishing the spirit or intellect |
| **Taoism** | The three treasures: charity, simplicity, humility; knowing others vs. knowing oneself |
| **Zhì huì (Chinese)** | Ability to make good judgments; knowledge gained from many life experiences |

The progression from **Data → Information → Knowledge → Insight → Wisdom**: wisdom is not merely knowing, but applying knowledge thoughtfully and with empathy toward others, ensuring choices that serve both personal and societal interests.

## CCR's Synthesis: Wisdom as the Golden Mean

**Preferred definition:** The Golden Mean — *"nothing in excess,"* applied self-referentially. Balance, moderation, maturity, gravitas, common sense.

Wisdom as balance is the never-ending evolutionary tug-of-war between selfishness and altruism, constantly adjusting via dynamic equilibrium. It requires strong metacognitive self-control (executive function) to avoid extremes, plus metacognition and metaemotion to discern *when* to bypass the rule.

**Key associated concepts:**

- **Maturity**: Responding to situations in resolved, resolute, and secure ways — not automatic with age, but an ongoing process. Allport's (1967) markers: self-extension, warm relationships, emotional security, skills and tasks, self-objectification, a unifying philosophy of life.
- **Gravitas**: Seriousness, dignity, and credibility in presence. Related to but distinct from maturity — one can be mature without gravitas, or display gravitas without full maturity.
- **Humility**: Recognition of limitations; genuine openness to others' ideas. The cornerstone of wisdom and developable through education. Socratic method and exposure to diverse perspectives are key instruments.
- **Common sense**: Practical everyday judgment — a component or stepping stone to wisdom, not equivalent to it. Dr. Ernest Davis (NYU): common sense supports reasoning, is integrated with other cognitive abilities, extends across tasks and modalities, is largely learned early in life, and concerns generalities more than individuals.
- **Proverbs**: Mnemonic distillates for common sense and wisdom across cultures — *"It takes a village to raise a child"* (African); *"Give a man a fish..."* (Chinese/Confucian).

---

## Why Wisdom Should Be the Ultimate Goal of Education

Wisdom facilitates complex, interdisciplinary understanding of multifaceted ethical questions — safety, long-term consequences, global implications. It integrates insights from diverse domains (technology, ethics, law, sociology) while promoting compassion, risk management, and moral leadership.

**Global citizenship**: Navigating today's multicultural, ethically complex world requires cultural dexterity, appreciation for diversity, and sensitivity to different perspectives. The speed at which novel ethical quandaries appear is unprecedented. Wisdom lays the foundation for cohesive, cooperative interactions.

**Dunning-Kruger antidote**: Wisdom recognizes the evolving nature of knowledge and the humility of admitting ignorance — the commitment to lifelong learning and open-mindedness.

**Emotional intelligence**: Closely intertwined with wisdom; its development facilitates self-reflection, empathy, and perspective-taking — the bedrock of successful interpersonal relationships in all life domains.

## Wisdom and Evolution

Evolution has wired life to propagate itself above all else. Christian de Duve (Nobel Medicine, 1974): *"We have evolved traits that will lead to humanity's extinction — so we must learn how to overcome them…Original sin is none other than the fault written into human genes by natural selection."*

Natural selection has:
- Indiscriminately privileged personal traits contributing to immediate individual success (selfishness, competitiveness — "All for Me")
- Privileged group cohesion *within* groups, and hostility *toward* other groups ("Us vs. Them")
- **Not** privileged the foresight needed to sacrifice immediate benefit for the future ("Now, not Later")

CCR adds two more evolutionary "faults":
- **"Follow the Power Leader"**: In VUCA times, a strong desire to follow powerful, charismatic, self-serving leaders
- **Linear thinking only**: Humanity's challenges (climate change, pandemics) are exponential in nature — our minds are not wired to grasp them

These tendencies are actively contributing to potential extinction — but they can be counteracted. Social customs like the **Golden Rule** (*"treat others as you wish to be treated"*) are present across all major traditions (Confucianism 5th c. BCE, Mahabharata, Islam), demonstrating that empathetic reciprocity is deeply embedded across humanity. These instincts can be mitigated via education, community norms, and benevolent cultural structures.

## Key Challenge: Compressing Experience

Wisdom is inherently acquired over time through varied life experiences — errors, lessons, contextual understanding built over 50+ years. K-12 education cannot replicate this directly. Instead, it provides **experience compression**: gathering and condensing centuries of others' experiences into digestible form.

**The What — disciplines that excel at experience compression:**

**History**: Primary sources, analysis of past decisions, drawing parallels to contemporary issues fosters empathy, ethical reasoning, and intellectual curiosity. *"The true use of history is not to make man clever for the next time, it is to make him wise forever."* — Sir Michael Howard.

**Literature**: Connects learners with broader personal, historical, and cultural contexts; develops critical thinking through ambiguity and moral dilemmas; fosters emotional intelligence and self-reflection. *"Employ your time in improving yourself by other men's writings."* — Socrates.

**Anthropology & Sociology**: Explores the myths, narratives, and societal structures shaping cultures; uses ethnographic accounts to connect students with lived experiences of diverse communities. *"Science gathers knowledge faster than society gathers wisdom."* — Asimov.

**Interdisciplinary themes:**
- **Systems thinking**: Recognizes intricate relationships between components; anticipates unintended consequences; aligns decisions with ethical, societal, and environmental contexts
- **Sustainability/environmental literacy**: Embeds long-term thinking, ethical consideration, and holistic well-being — COP23 as an example of systems thinking at global scale

## The How — Pedagogical Strategies

**Experiential learning:**
- Service-learning (volunteering, community projects) — exposes learners to diverse social contexts; reflective journaling deepens impact
- **Project-Based Learning (PBL)** — learners as active agents; interdisciplinary problem-solving; teachers as facilitators
- Outdoor/adventure-based learning — cultivates resilience, critical thinking, appreciation of nature
- Simulations and role-playing — immersion in complex ethical scenarios; perspective-taking; anticipating far-reaching consequences

**Exposure to diverse perspectives:**
- Protagonist empathy: reflecting from the perspective of characters in stories, films, or historical situations
- Focal point shifting: exploring events from multiple positions to demonstrate that "right" and "wrong" viewpoints are often positional
- Direct engagement with diverse communities (fieldwork, dialogue with elders, multimedia/VR)

**Promoting emotional intelligence:**
- Activities: reflective journaling, group discussions on emotional experiences, role-playing emotional scenarios
- Curriculum integration: embedding emotional intelligence principles in classroom dynamics; modeling emotionally intelligent behavior
- Connecting to content: analyzing characters' emotions in literature, emotional impact of historical events

## The Role of Technology

**Leveraging technology for wisdom:**
Could technology help teach wisdom? LLMs encompass a significant portion of humanity's accumulated knowledge and attitudes. Digital repositories, online courses, interactive simulations, and AI-driven tools enable:
- Highly accessible knowledge and continuous learning
- Interactive simulations as decision-making laboratories
- Virtual Reality for cultivating empathy (e.g., immersive experiences of refugee camps)
- Enhanced cognitive flexibility through exposure to diverse scenarios

**Mitigating technology's counter-productive effects:**
- **Over-reliance**: Internet and digital devices reshape neural pathways, making deep reflective thinking harder; readily available information promotes superficiality *(Carr, 2010)*
- **Filter bubbles/echo chambers**: Social media algorithms prioritize content aligning with pre-existing beliefs, continuously reinforcing preconceived notions — antithetical to wisdom's requirement for diverse perspectives and recognition of one's own limitations
- Wisdom requires considering multiple perspectives, understanding nuance, and deep reflection — all threatened by dopamine-driven algorithmic homogeneity

## The Importance of Precise Terminology

CCR uses learning-science-extracted definitions. For example, Creativity is not a vague word — it is synthesized into subcompetencies (Developing personal tastes; Generating new ideas; Being comfortable with risk and failure; Connecting and refining ideas; Realizing ideas within constraints) and associated constructs (Inspiration, Originality, Ingenuity, Vision, Inventiveness, Idea Generation, Cleverness, Resourcefulness).

Precision also matters when assessing AI's educational impact. Roger Schank (2004) listed attributes of an educated mind — determining connections, spotting analogies, predicting outcomes, learning from failure, recovering from failure, seeking explanations, absorbing newness, handling exceptions, dealing with abstraction, generalizing reasonably, being self-aware. When polled worldwide, educators agree: except for self-awareness, all others are within current AI reach — *with major caveats*. **"It depends"** is always the right answer. AI taxonomies are moving UP the cognitive stack over time: from passive capabilities toward analysis, synthesis, creativity, and valuation (while remaining unable to internalize emotions).

Schooling remains necessary. CCR's only fundamental axiom: *"We cannot imagine a pedagogically sound argument that would leave a 10-year-old at home, all the time and by themselves, learning via AI on a VR headset."*

---

## "If AI Can Do Everything, Why Learn Anything?"

This is the wrong question. AI cannot "do everything" as earlier chapters showed. This recurring worry — previously raised about calculators and search engines — misunderstands the tool. "Google knows everything" was debunked: it indexes ~5% of the internet's digital output and "knows" nothing.

**Six reasons humans still need to learn:**

**1. Learning requires scaffolding.** Casual passive interaction with technology does not equip students to use it effectively. Scaffolding — progressive support toward higher comprehension — is essential. Students must learn to engage with AI deliberately, not just encounter it.

**2. Learning happens in context.** Isolated online information cannot capture context. Daniel Willingham's example: a student who looks up "meticulous" and then writes "he meticulously balanced on the edge" applies the definition without understanding usage. The ability to look something up is not the ability to apply it correctly.

**3. Avoiding unconscious incompetence (Dunning-Kruger effect).** Without a minimum understanding of a domain, one doesn't know what one doesn't know. In a 2014 Gallup survey, 63% of Americans thought crime rates were rising despite a 20-year low — affecting their policy positions. Studies show people allowed to Google trivia questions rated themselves as smarter than those who weren't — even when correct-answer rates were equalized. Internet access may *exacerbate* D-K, not cure it.

**4. Speed, fluency, and automaticity.** Having to look up everything is impractical. Fluency — the combination of accuracy and speed — is necessary for real-time contexts (lectures, group work). Lack of automaticity in lower-level components creates bottlenecks to higher-level learning. Research shows fluency "increases retention, endurance under distraction, and transfer of training."

**5. Shared social background.** Media and discourse assume background knowledge. E.D. Hirsch's work on Cultural Literacy identifies what shared information citizens require. Without it, basic communication breaks down.

**6. Building toward complex concepts.** Complex understanding cannot be assembled by simply accumulating raw facts — it requires structured learning progressions that ground students in experiences before introducing abstract layers.

**The right question:** *"Given AI's powerful capabilities, how do we adapt education to remain relevant?"*

---

## Expertise and Transfer

**Expertise** is the expected result of education. Contrary to popular opinion, it is made, not born. Anders Ericsson's Theory of Deliberate Practice: what distinguished elite surgeons, chess players, writers, athletes, and pianists was the habit of engaging in tasks they *couldn't yet do*, continually analyzing errors and adjusting. (The "10,000 hours" concept was Gladwell's popularization — Ericsson's actual finding was about *deliberate* practice, not merely time spent.)

**Transfer** — applying learning from one context to another — is education's more fundamental but less heeded goal. Harvard's David Perkins: a student can learn to transfer without first becoming an expert, as an *"expert amateur"* engaging with concepts in natural language (e.g., *"History does not repeat itself, but it rhymes"*; *"Exponentials are deceiving then explosive"*). Transfer and Expertise reinforce each other.

**Can AI do these?**
- AI as Expert: Yes, demonstrably — chess, Go, protein folding, disease modeling, and across most occupations.
- AI as Transfer/Generalizer: Yes, increasingly. LLMs detect analogies across far-apart fields and will eventually surpass humans in near- and medium-transfer. *Far* transfer remains hard — for humans and AI alike — putting special pressure on **Imagination** (addressed in Chapter Six).

Ethan Mollick (Wharton): *"On some tasks, AI is immensely powerful, and on others, it fails completely or subtly. And, unless you use AI a lot, you won't know which is which."*

## Consequences for Education: Wider and Wiser Curricula

**Why Wider? For Versatility.**

The world is changing profoundly. In an age of uncertainty, the wise strategy is to hedge by embracing versatility. Swiss army knife analogy: equip learners with a broad set of tools that can be sharpened as circumstances require — poet, physician, painter, and physicist.

Education ≠ Training. Education is broad and life-related; Training is narrow and job-related. Both are eventually needed, but must not be conflated — they serve different goals (psychosocial vs. economic).

Knowledge variance matters:
- **Slowly-variant**: Philosophy doesn't change rapidly
- **Step-variant (medium)**: Digital Literacy evolving into Prompt Design
- **Step-variant (large)**: Teaching concepts/competencies and reforming assessment — big structural lifts
- **Rapidly-variant**: Which LLM to use — best addressed via communities of users

David Epstein (*Range*, 2019): "wicked" real-world environments reward wide ranges of experience. A sampling period before specialization leads to better match quality (fit between interests, abilities, and career). BUT: this is an AND position, not OR. IBM Research's T-shaped model — depth AND breadth, Expertise AND Transfer. That is CCR's position.

**How Wiser? By redesigning What and How.**

Harvard's Chris Dede: *"The current curriculum and high-stakes tests often prioritize fostering skills at which AI excels — reckoning skills involving calculative prediction and formulaic decision-making. However, AI cannot easily replicate human judgment, which is flexible and contextual based on experiential knowledge, ethics, values, relationships, and culture."*

All four dimensions matter — Knowledge, Skills, Character, Meta-Learning:

- **Knowledge**: Declarative knowledge more challenged than ever by LLMs; need to be far more discriminant about what is essential. Counterintuitively, this also requires *broader* declarative knowledge for versatility. LLMs increase pressure toward more conceptual and procedural knowledge (core concepts, projects).
- **Skills**: Both challenged and augmented by AI
- **Character**: Some aspects remain significantly human (Ethics); others are helped (Curiosity)
- **Meta-Learning**: Metacognition and Metaemotion more critical than ever — *learning how to learn*

**Four Drivers of Personalization** (Chapter Seven):
Motivation, Identity, Agency, Purpose — of which Motivation and Purpose remain quintessentially human.

## The How: AI in Education

Two major application areas:

- **Design**: AI used by teachers and curriculum developers to create curricula, lesson plans, and assessments
- **Delivery**: Teacher- and student-led experimentation with AI in instruction (rapidly shifting; better covered by timely blogs)

Over time: AI assistant offering personalized learning suggestions → eventually, Intelligent Tutoring Systems (ITS) used directly by students.

- AI as What (two levels): Digital literacy for everyone + Computer Science for specialists
- AI as How: AI as an education tool

## What Is Knowledge?

Before the internet, education's primary function was filling learners' brains with facts they'd carry for life. Near-universal internet access — and now fast-improving AI — requires a significant refinement of this model.

Historical knowledge traditions (Hellenistic seven liberal arts; Chinese literature, calligraphy, philosophy, mathematics, martial arts, medicine) persist as the core of most education systems today. But taxonomizing knowledge has always been contested — CCR's synthesis of 10 sources (academic papers, search engines, LLMs) generated **58 different "types" of knowledge**, with only four appearing in more than half of sources.

---

## CCR's Three Axes of Knowledge

### Axis 1: Knowledge Classification

Three types of knowledge appeared in over half of synthesized sources:

1. **Declarative knowledge**: Understanding of facts, information, and data that can be explicitly stated. Historically received the most curricular attention — vocabulary, formulas, historical events, scientific laws.
2. **Procedural knowledge**: Understanding of *how* to perform specific tasks, processes, or actions. Often mistakenly taught the same way as declarative knowledge (memorizing steps rather than executing them).
3. **Conceptual knowledge**: Understanding of how knowledge compounds into patterns and organizing principles. Requires internalization — not just recall.

**Epistemic expertise** is the fourth, culminating level: experts who can "think like a mathematician/historian/engineer" have internalized concepts as a flexible toolkit they deploy across novel situations to create new knowledge.

> ⚠️ The surprising success of AI in passing medical, legal, and other exams highlights how much these tests measure *memorization* rather than deep understanding. AI passing the bar ≠ AI practicing law.

**The exponentials example**: Students can memorize "exponents are deceiving, then explosive" (declarative). They can calculate exponential growth in numerical exercises (procedural). But they only *internalize* the concept when exposed to it across multiple contexts — viral spread, population growth, social media — allowing Transfer. COVID-19 response illustrated the global cost of conceptual failure here.

**Each classification requires different pedagogy:**
- Declarative: traditional memorization, repeated use in context
- Procedural: authentic execution, gradual release of responsibility
- Conceptual: repeated exposure across varied contexts, resonant examples for near and far transfer

### Axis 2: Knowledge Acquisition Methods

CCR's synthesis identifies two acquisition methods:

1. **Experiential knowledge** (a posteriori / tacit): gained via personal experiences, observations, and reflections. Not acquirable from a classroom alone — smell of air, perceived size of a tree, reading a room's cultural norms.
2. **Theoretical knowledge** (a priori / explicit): gained without personal experience, through deduction and logic. Can be transmitted via text, handbook, or instruction.

Experiential learning (Kolb's learning cycle; Project-Based Learning) recognizes that direct experiences are sometimes the *only* way to acquire certain knowledge. They are also more resource-intensive — field trips cost money, require proximity, and demand teacher customization. Schools should optimize resources specifically to generate high-value experiential knowledge that cannot be replicated otherwise.

### Axis 3: Knowledge Domain

The area of content within which knowledge is relevant — typically grouped by Discipline.

---

## Knowledge as Substrate for Competencies

Knowledge cannot be discussed in a vacuum. Competencies are best taught through fitting content. Of 58 knowledge types synthesized, 19 overlap with Skills, Character, and Meta-Learning — an intentional feature of the CCR Venn diagram, not a bug. A complex real-world project (e.g., launching software) requires declarative, procedural, and conceptual knowledge simultaneously with collaboration, curiosity, courage, and metacognition.

## AI's Impact: Redesigning Disciplines

### Modernizing Traditional Subjects

Using Mathematics as an example, CCR works with Australia's ACARA at three levels:

- **Minor changes**: Rearranging content items — historical and painstaking, not CCR's focus
- **Significant rework**: CCR's process:
  1. Analyze topic needs (OECD): reaffirm underserved traditional areas (number sense, estimation, irregular shapes, proportionality, basic probability, exponentials); add modern relevance topics (Bayesian probability, Discrete/Computational mathematics — algorithms, complex systems, game theory)
  2. Develop Core Concepts at discipline- and branch-level (e.g., Engineering: "Fragility — consider how a product may behave under stress"; CS: "Garbage In/Garbage Out")
  3. Identify top Competencies for the discipline (Mathematics: Critical Thinking, Resilience, Metacognition, Growth Mindset)
- **Deepest redesign — Personalization**: Three levels for each discipline:
  - **Produce** (mathematicians, specialists)
  - **Interpret** (policymakers, professionals in related fields)
  - **Appreciate** (citizens who can ask informed questions — e.g., a firefighter questioning whether firebreaks are placed fractally)
  
  Traditional "Honors/Regular/Vocational" tracks fail because they adjust *speed*, not *depth*.

---

### Three Modern Disciplines Missing from K-12

Based on occupational growth data, three disciplines are crucial yet systematically absent:

**1. Technology and Engineering (T&E)**
K-12 teaches "St_M" — Science and Mathematics — while T and E are largely ignored. Justifications:
- Drives innovation and economic growth more than any other discipline
- Bridges the gap between the natural world (science) and the human-made world
- Develops core concepts (prototyping before production; feedforward vs. feedback) applicable across fields
- Naturally project-based; integrates with STEM
- Introduces inventive methodologies like TRIZ (Theory of Inventive Problem Solving)
- CCR has developed standards for six branches: civil, design, electrical, graphic, manufacturing, mechanical

**2. Social Sciences**
Psychology, sociology, anthropology, and political science are rarely required, yet:
- Psychology: cognitive biases, heuristics, developmental milestones — understanding human behavior
- Sociology: social forces shaping individuals (inequality, discrimination, socialization)
- Anthropology: cultural differences and common humanity across time and place
- Political science: government functions, citizenship, democracy, justice, liberty

**3. Entrepreneurship / Business**
Job creation happens chiefly in small-to-medium businesses. Yet entrepreneurship is rarely mandatory:
- Prepares students for real-world economic life (economics, accounting, marketing, sales, law)
- Fosters financial literacy — crucial for personal well-being
- Develops entrepreneurial mindset — opportunity-seeking, problem-solving
- Teaches competencies difficult to develop otherwise, particularly **Courage**

# Chapter Six: Competencies for the Age of AI

## Rev 1.2: Why an Update Was Needed

The CCR Competencies Framework 1.0 was published after a 3-year synthesis of 100+ frameworks and 861 learning sciences papers — the most rigorously researched framework of its kind. Five design parameters: Comprehensive, Compact, Uncorrelated, Appropriate abstraction, Globally relevant. An additional implicit goal: **stability**.

No changes were made in five years. But CCR's "Education Engineering" mindset recognizes that blind adherence in the face of new evidence fails the mission. The rapid proliferation of AI is the trigger for **Version 1.2**.

---

## The Four Questions

For each subcompetency, CCR asks:

1. Does AI (or automation) **outperform** humans at this subcompetency?
2. If so, does AI have a possible **complementarity** with humans?
3. If not, can AI **catch up** with human performance within the next 5 years?
4. Could AI have a **detrimental** effect on human performance?

---

## Skills

### Creativity

> *"Although novelty, surprise and value are three key components to measure if AI is being creative, I think a fourth element must also be introduced if we are going to herald real creativity in AI: originality of a truly independent nature."* — Marcus du Sautoy

| Subcompetency | Does AI Outperform? | Does AI Complement? | Can AI Catch Up (5 yrs)? |
|---|---|---|---|
| CRE1: Developing personal tastes, aesthetics, and style | NO | YES | MIXED |
| CRE2: Generating and seeking new ideas | YES | YES | YES (already there) |
| CRE3: Being comfortable with risks, uncertainty, and failure | YES | YES | YES (already there) |
| CRE4: Connecting, reorganizing, and refining ideas into a cohesive whole | MIXED | YES | YES |
| CRE5: Realizing ideas while recognizing constraints | NO | YES | MIXED |

AI's creativity (DeepDream, MuseNet, GANs) is rooted in pattern-based algorithms that can produce novel outputs at the imitation, variation, combination, and transformation levels of Nilsson's "Taxonomy of Creative Design." Original creation remains philosophically contested. Expert creative humans — particularly those raised in education systems that preserved wide imaginations — still hold deep societal value, especially when collaborating with powerful AI on the hardest global challenges.

**CCR key insight**: The largest proportion of human creativity is *incremental* — this is where AI already excels or will shortly. The emphasis for education must shift toward **Imagination**: the emotional and experiential depth that fuels truly original inspiration. AI's "confabulations," used strategically, can approximate radical creativity.

- **CRE1**: AI mimics but does not develop personal tastes; it can help humans discover styles and advance creative projects
- **CRE2**: AI may already outperform in speed and volume of idea generation; human oversight for quality remains essential
- **CRE3**: AI is inherently "fear-less" — emotionlessly comfortable with risk; complements human tendency toward loss aversion
- **CRE4**: AI excels at synthesizing/summarizing data; cohesive abstraction and conceptual integration still require human judgment and prompting
- **CRE5**: Realizing ideas in complex, socially nuanced, or physical environments remains a human domain

### Critical Thinking

| Subcompetency | Does AI Outperform? | Does AI Complement? | Can AI Catch Up (5 yrs)? |
|---|---|---|---|
| CRI1: Identifying, clarifying, and organizing information | YES | YES | YES (already there) |
| CRI2: Assessing validity and quality of information | NO | YES | YES |
| CRI3: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices | MIXED | YES | MIXED |
| CRI4: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making | MIXED | YES | MIXED |
| CRI5: Reflecting critically on one's own reasoning and assumptions | MIXED | YES | MIXED |

AI outperforms at pattern recognition, data analysis, strategic games (Chess, Go), and medical imaging. But its critical thinking remains limited to algorithms and training data; it lacks genuine reflective thought or contextual understanding.

**CCR key insight**: As AI generates seemingly plausible answers, knowing how to ask questions and evaluate answers matters more than ever. The emphasis must be on **Decision-Making** — humans must not abdicate decisions to AI.

- **CRI1**: AI excels at identifying, clarifying, organizing data; still benefits from human contextual clarification. Trust but verify.
- **CRI2**: AI can identify contradictions but struggles with validity assessment and sometimes confabulates. "Ill-formed questions lead to bland, false, or confabulated answers."
- **CRI3**: AI does better in well-defined closed systems; most real choices contain context-dependent nuance AI cannot fully handle
- **CRI4**: AI lacks "sound" reasoning — not logically bad, but missing the ethical and contextual dimensions of human judgment
- **CRI5**: True critical self-reflection is a metacognitive, introspective process; AI's nearest equivalent (GAN self-evaluation, reinforcement learning) is far from this

### Communication

|---|---|---|---|
| COM1: Asking questions and actively listening | MIXED | YES | MIXED |
| COM2: Sharing one's vision and inspiring others | NO | YES | NO |

| COM4: Communicating with fidelity across distinct modes and mediums | NO | YES | MIXED |

Chatbots increasingly mimic human communication (BERT, GPT series) via pattern recognition and data processing — without genuine understanding or intent.

**CCR key insight**: While AI excels at articulation and audience adaptation, the world's fracture calls for genuine **Dialogue** — back-and-forth human meaning-making that requires context, body language, and authentic intent.

- **COM1**: AI has limitless attention and generates questions instantly; genuine listening requires emotional cues and context AI lacks
- **COM2**: Personal vision and the capacity to authentically inspire others require empathy, creativity, and emotional resonance — deeply human
- **COM3**: AI excels here — language is LLMs' core domain
- **COM4**: Multimodal communication (text, speech, visual, body language) remains a challenge; GPT-4 and Gemini are making early steps
- **COM5**: AI can already adapt to audience demographics; fully capturing emotional and cultural nuance is the remaining challenge

## Relative Gains vs. Losses

The framework for evaluating AI's impact on human capabilities: **relative gain > relative loss = acceptable tradeoff**.

- Scripts caused memory loss — but relative gain of writing exceeds relative memory loss (Socrates was wrong)
- Calculators diminished mental arithmetic — but freed humans for higher-order reasoning
- Key delineation within the CCR framework: **Knowledge and Skills** are more easily automated (greater risk of capability atrophy); **Character and Meta-Learning** are more distinctly human (greater opportunity to cultivate)

**Individual level**: If an individual can only produce baseline-level work in an area, delegating to AI makes sense — freeing them for areas where human added value is highest. The most imaginative children should keep imagining. Intense localization (a doctor well-honed to a specific community's culture) remains hard for AI to replicate.

**Societal level**: Analog redundancy is a risk management strategy:
- **Brittle** technologies (driverless cars, nuclear plants, financial algorithms): require analog backup and human "fail-safes" 
- **Supple** technologies (bicycles, mechanical watches): simpler, more robust
- Society will benefit from deliberately training human redundancy capacity — archivists, semiconductor engineers, navigators

Drawbacks of AI follow the **n-curve** (the Golden Mean): reliance is fine; *over*-reliance atrophies critical reasoning. Moderation is key.

## AI as a Different Form of Intelligence

AI's non-anthropocentric capabilities:
- **Scalability, Speed, Memory**: handles vast information and never forgets
- **Adaptability**: optimizes algorithms to new data
- **Versatility**: expert across many fields simultaneously
- **Customizability**: designed for specific tasks
- **Multiplicity**: not a single entity — many different AI systems, as microprocessors in cars
- **Global Connectivity**: learns from every instance across all connected systems — a healthcare robot learns from every other healthcare robot's mistake
- **"Syncognitism"** (Azeem Azhar): AI as collective intelligence — many systems behaving intelligently in different ways create a broad substrate that feels more intelligent as a whole

**The AI Generation gap**: iGen/Gen-Z grew up with smartphones and have no concept of a world without them. An "AI Generation" will similarly assume AI as normal — creating a significant gap between pre-AI and post-AI humans. Pre-AI adults' skepticism is natural (early bugs, limitations) and may remain even as AI's outputs become indistinguishable. Post-AI humans may anthropomorphize more readily.

**Ethical inflection**: AI without human gut-reaction heuristics may make more consistent decisions within a defined moral framework — but will also make blind-spot mistakes in high-stakes situations. Society must accept that even 99%-accurate AI will fail on important decisions. The appropriate response is not zero-tolerance but calibrated trust, gradually increasing as AI is battle-tested.

Humans must remain in control and use AI's unique capabilities to amplify positive outcomes and mitigate negative ones.

# Chapter Seven: The Need for Personalization

> *"Education must be increasingly concerned about the fullest development of all children and youth, and it will be the responsibility of the schools to seek learning conditions which will enable each individual to reach the highest level of learning possible."* — Benjamin Bloom

---

## Why Personalization Has Always Been Important

Students are not homogenous. They bring varied backgrounds, abilities, and learning rhythms; traditional "one-size-fits-all" instruction fails to engage all students equally and can cause persistent underachievement. Benjamin Bloom's **Two-Sigma Problem** captures this with unusual precision: students tutored one-on-one using mastery learning outperformed group-instruction peers by **two standard deviations** — meaning a tutored student could outperform 98% of the traditionally taught cohort. The challenge was always cost and scale. AI is now uniquely positioned to address both.

When education resonates with a student's interests and aspirations, it also ignites intrinsic motivation, yielding deeper and more lasting understanding — another powerful argument for personalization beyond mere performance gains.

> ⚠️ **Note on terminology**: CCR uses "personalization" (more widespread) while acknowledging its negative connotations regarding the importance of social and collaborative learning. "Differentiation" would be the technically more precise term.

---

## Choices at Different Levels

Personalization is not an all-or-nothing proposition. Many layers can be exercised simultaneously, even as some disciplines remain mandatory:

| Level | Example of Choices |
|---|---|
| **Macro** | Track chosen (e.g. STEM, Humanities, Arts); discipline within tracks (economics vs. psychology) |
| **Meso** | Project type (e.g. swimming vs. flying robot) |
| **Micro** | Grouped pathways (e.g. whole-word vs. phonetic language acquisition) |
| **Nano** | Individualized pathways — "Flat" or Adaptive, without or with AI |

---

## Do Students Always Know Best?

Kirschner & van Merriënboer (2013) debunk three "urban legends" in education:

1. **Digital natives**: Students who grew up with technology are not automatically technologists. With AI, they risk becoming "**Digital nAIves**" unless taught digital literacy, computational thinking, and, for some, computer science.
2. **Learning styles**: Howard Gardner himself has been careful to clarify he proposed a scientific theory — not a prescription for schooling. Learning styles as pedagogical prescriptions have been widely debunked.
3. **Learners as self-educators**: Students do not always profit from maximum control. "Learners often choose what they prefer, but what they prefer is not always what is best for them." The challenge is providing the *right amount* of choice with appropriate scaffolding.

---

## Why AI Makes Personalization Even More Important

| Dimension | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| **Youth Expectations** | Netflix, Spotify, TikTok condition students to expect personalized content. "One-size-fits-all" feels outdated. Risk of "filter bubbles" if personalization goes unguided. |
| **Social & Emotional** | AI can analyze individual learning patterns and emotional responses to tailor experiences for social/emotional contexts — e.g. adjusting content delivery based on detected emotional state. |
| **Competencies** | As AI takes over tasks, human-centric competencies matter more. AI can simulate real-world challenges, track metacognitive progress, and provide adaptive feedback tuned to each student's strengths and weaknesses. |
| **Motivation** | Adaptive platforms identify struggle points and provide customized resources at the right difficulty level — consistent success and immediate personalized feedback fuel intrinsic motivation. |
| **Identity** | AI can recognize cultural, socioeconomic, and personal backgrounds to provide contextually relevant content, fostering identity validation and belonging. |
| **Agency** | AI-driven platforms offer students choices in how to approach topics and what assessments to tackle, empowering them as active participants rather than passive recipients. |
| **Purpose** | AI can curate resources, tasks, and experiences that directly align with a student's evolving goals — a student passionate about conservation receives tailored readings, field trips, and project ideas. |

---

## How AI Can Help Solve Mass Personalization

AI-powered platforms can analyze vast amounts of student data in real time, continuously adapting instructional content. A non-exhaustive list of current capabilities:

- **Data-Driven Insights**: Which teaching methods are most effective; which topics are most challenging; which students are at risk
- **Adaptive Learning**: Content adapts based on individual interactions — struggling students get more resources; advanced students get greater challenge
- **Individual Learning Paths**: AI identifies learning pace patterns and tailors accordingly
- **Personalized Resources**: Math problems framed around a student's passion for space exploration, etc.
- **Instant Feedback**: Immediate responses to student inputs — hints, explanations, or redirections
- **Smart Content Creation**: Simplified explanations for difficult concepts or advanced readings where students excel
- **Emotion Recognition**: Facial recognition to detect frustration/confusion and adjust teaching method
- **Predictive Analysis**: Identifies at-risk students before they fall behind
- **VR/Immersive Learning**: Tailored simulations of historical events or scientific phenomena
- **Lifelong Learning**: Adults can upskill throughout life; AI recommends paths based on current skills and future goals

Scalability is key: while human educators cannot provide personalized attention to every student in large classes, AI-driven platforms can scale easily. By automating repetitive tasks, AI also frees educators to focus on mentorship, critical thinking facilitation, and socio-emotional support.

## The Four Drivers: MIAP

The contemporary understanding of human development recognizes the fluid, plural nature of Motivation, Identity/Belonging, Agency/Growth Mindset, and Purpose/Passion. Individuals possess these in the plural — a person may simultaneously identify as parent, artist, and activist, each offering a different lens through which they see the world.

**These constructs are not competencies that can be taught as content** — they are personal positionings. However, CCR competencies and subcompetencies directly support their discovery, development, and maintenance. The following sections explore each Driver and its relationship to AI.

---

### Motivation (Extrinsic and Intrinsic)

> *"The best time to plant a tree was 20 years ago. The second-best time is now."* — Chinese Proverb

**What is motivation?** The driving force that instigates, directs, and sustains behavior toward particular goals. It is the interplay of intrinsic motivation (driven by internal rewards — personal satisfaction, values) and extrinsic motivation (external rewards — grades, money, recognition), along with emotional, cognitive, and subconscious elements.

| CCR Term | CCR Definition | Associated Terms |
|---|---|---|
| **Motivation** | Why you take action | Drive; incentive; self-determination; inspiration |

**Primary Motivators** (categorized by human needs):

- **Biological/Physiological**: Survival (food, water, shelter), health, pleasure
- **Safety & Security**: Physical safety, economic security, health security
- **Belongingness**: Relationships, community, love and affection
- **Recognition**: Self-esteem, achievement, respect from others
- **Intellectual Needs**: Knowledge/understanding, curiosity, creativity
- **Self-Actualization**: Fulfilling potential, personal growth, contributing to a larger purpose
- **Emotional Needs**: Expression, support, connection
- **Social/Cultural Influences**: Norms, cultural background, peer pressure
- **Moral/Ethical Values**: Moral code, religious/spiritual beliefs, prosocial values

In this evolutionary sense, all motivators trace back to humanity's original fear: **survival**. Humans have the capacity to take negative motivators (fear) and transform them into positive ones (aspiration).

**CCR Synthesis of Motivation Theories**: Combining Maslow, Vroom's Expectancy Theory, and Self-Determination Theory (SDT) with the CCR Framework:
- *Foundational stage*: Maslow's basic needs met → resilience supports safety → self-efficacy cultivated
- *Intermediate stage*: Social needs + collaboration → belonging + recognition → curiosity and character foster intrinsic motivation
- *Advanced stage*: Self-actualization + SDT intrinsic motivation → metacognition and growth mindset → learners take charge of their own learning

**Why motivation is crucial in the age of AI**: Human motivations shape AI's development and deployment. Automation can cause laziness and overconfidence (human brains are "lazy" by evolutionary design). Current extrinsic motivators (compulsory schooling, credentials, job links) remain powerful for now — but if they erode as AI becomes ubiquitous, intrinsic motivation may be insufficient to compensate unless deliberately cultivated.

**Can AI possess motivation?** According to GPT-4 itself: *"When we say an AI has a 'motivation,' what we're really referring to is its programmed objective or goal... The AI doesn't 'want' to achieve these objectives in the same way humans 'want' to achieve their goals; it simply processes information in a manner that aligns with its programmed objectives... AI doesn't have feelings, consciousness, or biological drives. It doesn't experience hunger, fatigue, joy, or frustration."* CCR: "The authors could not have said so any better themselves :-)"

**Key risk**: Anthropomorphizing AI (ascribing desires or motivations to machines) leads to ethical dilemmas and inappropriate decisions about AI's use, development, and regulation.

**Conclusion**: Motivation, identity, and belonging are intricately connected. Identity is a dynamic construct tied to "Who am I?" and "Where do I belong?" Behaviors aligned with one's authentic identity are more intrinsically motivated and more sustainable — leading the chapter into Identity.

---

### Identity (& Belonging)

> *"Know thyself."* — Oracle of Delphi

**What is identity?** An individual's understanding and internalization of their role, characteristics, values, and beliefs in the context of the broader social world — how individuals see themselves and how they believe others perceive them.

| CCR Term | CCR Definition | Associated Terms |
|---|---|---|
| **Identity** | Who you are in the world and in your relationships | Belonging; self-concept; personhood |

**Discovered vs. Created**: Two schools of thought: Identity is either *discovered* (passive — waiting for "true self" to emerge) or *created* (active — constructing it through choices and actions). CCR favors the **active view**: identity is created, allowing for multifaceted, evolving identities. This philosophical choice empowers individuals and validates the role of education.

**Multiple Identities**: It is common and healthy to possess multiple simultaneous identities (mother, scholar, activist, Asian American). "Code-switching" is a natural expression of this multiplicity — shifting which identity is foregrounded depending on context.

**Primary Facets** (shaping identity and belonging): Biology/genetics, family environment, society/culture, life experiences, social interactions, personal values, personal interests, education and socioeconomic status, gender and sexuality, relationships.

**What is Belonging?** An individual's sense of acceptance and inclusion within a group or community — a fundamental human need. "Belonging indexes" assess: Inclusivity, Diversity, Fair Treatment, Support & Community, Representation. The Valuegraphics Project's Belonging Index identifies three core indicators: membership in a group that matters; varied relationships; contributing to something larger than oneself.

**Why identity is crucial in the age of AI**: AI systems can inadvertently perpetuate stereotypes and biases from training data. Facial recognition technologies have misidentified individuals from certain racial groups. As AI curates digital experiences, individuals must actively understand and assert their identities to avoid being "boxed in by algorithms."

**Can AI possess identity?** AI already possesses *extrinsic* identity — shaped by (1) training data biases embedded in word embeddings, (2) programmed behavioral expressions (tone, style), and (3) user interface traits. META's user-created chatbots illustrate how AI identities are now proliferating. But GPT-4 acknowledges: *"AI doesn't possess identity in the way humans understand and experience it... They don't have consciousness, emotions, self-awareness, or subjective experiences."* Society can anthropomorphize AI (naming assistants, assigning pronouns), but this is externally constructed identity, not intrinsic.

**Conclusion**: The path from adolescence to adulthood is more unstable than previous generations. COVID-19 exacerbated this role ambiguity. Without strong identity or purpose, today's youth risk feeling helpless in a volatile future. Identity strongly influences agency — particularly self-efficacy — leading to the next Driver.

---

### Agency (& Growth Mindset)

> *"You have agency, and you are free to choose. But there is actually no free agency. Agency has its price. You have to pay the consequences of your choices."* — Dieter F. Uchtdorf

**What is agency?** An individual's capacity to act independently, make choices, and exert control over their actions and decisions. It emphasizes the proactive role individuals play in shaping their lives, rather than being passive recipients of external forces.

| CCR Term | CCR Definition | Associated Terms |
|---|---|---|
| **Agency** | Your capacity to take action | Autonomy; self-efficacy; empowerment; intentionality |

**Key distinctions**:
- *Agency vs. Autonomy*: Agency is influenced by both internal and external factors; autonomy emphasizes acting based on one's own values without external compulsion. Both involve individual choice, but autonomy stresses independence from external influence.
- *Agency vs. Executive Function*: Executive function provides the cognitive *how* (working memory, cognitive flexibility, inhibitory control) for goal-directed actions; agency is the *belief* that one can achieve it and the drive to do so.

**Self-Agency** (self-regulation) develops even before self-awareness — observing causal connections between one's actions and environmental changes. Emotional control is a prime example: individuals cannot control having an emotion, but can control how they express and act upon it.

**Comparison with Growth Mindset**: Growth mindset (Dweck) = belief that abilities can be developed through effort. The relationship is recursive: growth mindset fosters greater agency (believing you can grow makes you more proactive); exercising agency cultivates growth mindset (seeing results of proactive choices reinforces belief in growth). AI-powered tools using LLMs to provide automated coaching on Growth Mindset Supportive Language (GMSL) for teachers represent an early complementary application.

**Primary Facets of Agency**: Personal values, goals, self-efficacy, autonomy, metaemotion/EI, educational and socioeconomic background, cultural/social influences, physical and mental health. Structural factors (socioeconomic background, family, culture) significantly impact agentic possibility — factors often outside adolescents' control.

**Levels of Agency in Education** (CCR):

| Agency Layer | Example | Comment |
|---|---|---|
| Discipline | Environmental science | Tracking choices in high school |
| Branch | Conservation biology | Depends on availability |
| Subject/Topic | Climate change impact on a specific ecosystem | Easy to implement |
| Implementation | Restoration projects | Often underappreciated; naturally multidisciplinary |
| Purpose | Educational programs on conservation | Most impactful; often naturally multidisciplinary |

The degree of choice starts low (young students need to master basics) and gradually increases with age and competence.

**Why agency is crucial in the age of AI**: AI is performing tasks once exclusive to human cognition (diagnosing diseases, composing music, writing). Maintaining human agency ensures humans remain active decision-makers, not passive bystanders. As Fei-Fei Li argues: *"The future of AI is so profoundly impactful that the agency must remain within us... If we give up agency, it would be a freefall."* Humans must remain ultimately responsible for AI outputs.

**Can AI possess agency?** This is a gray area. AI does possess narrow forms: GANs compete independently; AutoGPT and similar autonomous agents handle tasks previously exclusive to humans; NVIDIA's Eureka uses GPT-4 and reinforcement learning to train robots to acquire new skills. But GPT-4 concedes: *"While AI can exhibit autonomous behaviors within the boundaries of their programming, they do not possess agency in the rich, conscious sense that humans do."* Current AI "functional agency" lacks intentionality or conscious understanding.

**Conclusion**: Agency is intrinsically linked to purpose. A clear sense of purpose directs agency toward fulfilling that purpose; high agency amplifies motivations. The risk: AI automating decision-making could impinge on human agency and diminish sense of purpose (Harari). Cultivating human agency and purpose is therefore essential — not optional.

---

### Purpose (incl. Passion)

> *"Education is not the filling of a bucket, but the lighting of a fire."* — W.B. Yeats

**What is purpose?** A central, self-organizing life aim that drives an individual's thoughts, actions, and behaviors — providing direction and meaning. It emerges from personal passions, strengths, values, and a desire to make a positive impact. Viktor Frankl: a meaningful life is anchored in the pursuit of purpose, even in adversity. Having clear purpose correlates with improved well-being, health, resilience, and longevity.

| CCR Term | CCR Definition | Associated Terms |
|---|---|---|
| **Purpose** | Your sense of significance | Passion; interests; intentions; ambition |

**Two types of purpose**:
1. **Self-interested**: Purpose that primarily benefits the self (e.g. an enjoyable activity)
2. **Self-transcendent**: Purpose rooted in benefiting others or working toward something larger than oneself — *more effective* at motivating people

The road to self-transcendent purpose is often paved by self-interest: curiosity about something personally interesting can spark a purpose that transcends the individual.

**Discovered vs. Created**: Coleman's "Hollywood version" (Neo in The Matrix, Rey in Star Wars) depicts purpose as passive destiny waiting to be found. CCR and research follow Coleman's other argument: **purpose is actively constructed** through choices and actions. The Ikigai model reinforces this. People who frame life as a "hero's journey" lead more meaningful lives. Purpose evolves across life stages.

**What is passion?** A strong inclination toward an activity individuals deeply value, find personally meaningful, and invest significant time and energy in. Passion is the *fuel* toward purpose's *why* — it provides the "vigor and enthusiasm for the how." Purpose without passion is direction without drive; passion without purpose is energy without direction.

**Major Facets**: Enjoyment, values alignment, self-efficacy and competence, autonomy, belonging, varied experiences, self-discovery, ambition and goal-setting, inspiration from mentors and role models.

**CCR Composite Theory of Purpose** (developmental layers):
- *Foundational (0–12)*: Safety, exploration, basic accomplishments → Curiosity, Communication, Courage. Pre-full consciousness.
- *Identity Formation (13–24)*: Exploration, seeking identity, societal role → Metacognition (MET2), Creativity, Curiosity, Ethics. Ask big questions; reflect on values.
- *Societal Interaction (25–50)*: Deep connections, contributions, legacy → Collaboration, Communication, Ethics; Resilience and Courage when worldview anomalies arise.
- *Reflective (50+)*: Life review, acceptance, imparting wisdom → Meta-Learning; "Did I live a meaningful life?"

**Why purpose is crucial in the age of AI**: As automation takes over routine tasks, individuals need meaning in activities machines cannot replicate. Human doctors' purpose lies not in data analysis (where AI already outperforms) but in *empathetic care* and *humanistic decision-making*. Clear purpose provides a compass for navigating uncertainty and disruption. Collectively, a sense of purpose guides AI development toward the greater good.

**Does AI have purpose?** Intrinsically, no. Current AI depends entirely on a human taskmaster. AI has *objectives*, not purpose — objectives are the "what" and "how"; purpose is the "why." GPT-4: *"AI doesn't have emotions, consciousness, or self-awareness, so while it can perform tasks aligned with a specified goal, it doesn't 'feel' or 'understand' purpose in a human sense."* AutoGPT-style agents can define objectives within parameters — but attributing purpose to their actions is not yet justified.

**Conclusion**: Motivation, Identity, Agency, and Purpose are the core of the human experience. Motivation drives action; identity informs agency; purpose gives direction to agency toward meaningful goals. In an AI-driven world, these constructs gain greater significance — and greater vulnerability. Actively cultivating them is essential to ensure technology augments rather than diminishes what makes us human.

|---|---|
| **CUR4**: Envisioning and prioritizing one's interests and passions | Visualizing passions as future goals acts as a motivational compass; research shows individuals with clear paths between present action and desired future remain more committed |
| **COU5**: Believing in one's agency and self-efficacy | High self-efficacy → engagement with challenging tasks → greater effort in adversity; viewing oneself as master of one's destiny reinforces goal-setting and persistence |
| **MET5**: Determining goals, plans, and reviewing progress | Explicit, challenging goals with feedback motivate more effectively than vague objectives (Locke & Latham's Goal Setting Theory); progress reviews recalibrate motivation continuously |

### Supporting Identity & Belonging

| Subcompetency | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **CRE1**: Developing personal tastes, aesthetics, and style | Exploration phase broadens exposure and informs personal identity; the desire to belong drives individuals to seek various group affiliations throughout life |
| **COU4**: Acknowledging one's strengths and weaknesses | Expressing facets of identity requires courage; honest self-assessment guides individuals toward roles where strengths flourish and helps navigate "the feeling of too many possible identities" |
| **MET2**: Reflecting on processes, learning, and identity | Metacognition enables "performativity" — adeptly modulating identities across contexts while maintaining internal coherence |

### Supporting Agency

| Subcompetency | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **RES1**: Persevering through challenges but seeking help when needed | Perseverance reinforces belief in capacity for change; knowing when to seek help demonstrates mature understanding of limitations and fosters collective intelligence |
| **COL1**: Taking and sharing responsibility | Assumes ownership (boosts confidence and agency); sharing promotes interdependence, diverse perspectives, and empowerment |
| **COU1**: Pursuing ambitious goals despite risks | Possession of agency does not guarantee use — it requires courage. Self-belief drives action; past achievements reinforce the belief one can influence outcomes |

### Supporting Purpose & Passion

| Subcompetency | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **CUR1**: Seeking to understand deeply | Deep curiosity-driven understanding helps individuals discern what aligns with their values and passions — a bridge to purpose |
| **RES5**: Motivating oneself via meaning or purpose | A self-transcendent purpose makes less enjoyable tasks bearable and effective; resilience sustains purpose through the inevitable unrewarding stretches |
| **MET5**: Determining goals, plans, and reviewing progress | Purposes evolve over a lifetime; metacognition recognizes shifting priorities and assesses permanence; planning and review equip individuals to adapt to evolving purposes |

## Chapter 7 Summary

The Two-Sigma Problem demonstrates that personalized instruction can vault students to the 98th percentile — AI now makes mass personalization feasible for the first time. But personalization is not unlimited student control: CCR's framework provides layered choices (Macro to Nano) with appropriate scaffolding, rejecting "digital native" myths and debunked learning style prescriptions.

The four Drivers — Motivation, Identity, Agency, and Purpose — represent increasingly important human attributes precisely *because* AI is becoming more powerful:

- **Motivation**: AI has programmed objectives, not motivation. Extrinsic motivators in education may erode as AI becomes ubiquitous; cultivating intrinsic motivation is urgent.
- **Identity**: AI already has extrinsic identity (training data biases, behavioral programming). Human identity must be actively created, not passively discovered, to avoid being "boxed in by algorithms."
- **Agency**: AI has narrow "functional agency" but lacks conscious intentionality. Human agency — the insistence that humans remain decision-makers — is the essential check on technological determinism.
- **Purpose**: AI has objectives, not purpose. Human purpose in an AI world becomes more vital, not less — guiding not only individual lives but the development of AI itself toward the greater good.

CCR competencies and subcompetencies provide practical levers for fostering all four Drivers in classroom settings.

## Modern Curriculum = Integrated Design

Chapter Eight focuses on **Design** — how all prior recommendations can be assembled cohesively. Delivery aspects are addressed only briefly (Prompts and Adaptive Learning/ITS). The formula:

> **Modern Curriculum** = Essential Content + Interdisciplinary Themes + Core Concepts + Competencies + Identity/Agency/Purpose + Pedagogical Considerations

---

## Redesigning Curricula and Courseware: A Real Case

A school in Ontario, Canada (transposable to any discipline) illustrates how all parameters intertwine:

| Step | What Happens |
|---|---|
| 1. Essential Content | Existing jurisdictional standards are modernized where possible (Essentiality; Produce/Interpret/Appreciate; Given/Asked/Can). Always contains enough leeway for teacher customization. |
| 2. Core Concepts | Extracted and mapped at discipline-level and branch-level to the standards (CCR has done this for Math, History, T&E, and higher-ed courses in Chemistry, Psychology, Sociology, Statistics). |
| 3. Interdisciplinary Themes | E.g. Grades 9–10: Information Literacy + Environmental Literacy; Grades 11–12: Global Literacy + Civic Literacy |
| 4. World Literature | Texts chosen to correspond to interdisciplinary themes (CCR database of 275 curated works from diverse cultures) |
| 5. Drivers of Personalization | Students choose a subset of texts to explore Identity/Agency/Purpose → generates motivation |
| 6. Modern Pedagogies | Modes of expression — digital communications, public speaking, debate, journalism, poetry, SDG projects |
| 7. Digital Humanities | Applied during teaching for expanded digital literacy: word frequency analysis, sentiment analysis, text-to-image, concept extraction, knowledge graphs, pattern recognition |
| 8. Competencies | Embedded based on CCR's research on which competency is best served by each discipline. For Native Language: Creativity, Critical Thinking, Communication, Metacognition, Ethics |

**Key insight**: With careful design, all parameters intertwine "like a rope" — each one adding solidity to the complete construct.

---

## Consequences for the Role of Teachers

The design complexity above is substantial — "what takes CCR a team of experts several months to design for a single course." The OECD Global Video Study of Teaching confirms teachers are already stretched at instruction itself.

**CCR's proposed shift**: From teachers being both content designer AND delivery agent → much greater focus on classroom delivery, offloaded from courseware design. Content creation should rely on well-resourced expert teams assisted by AI (as book publishers have historically done).

A modern pre-designed curriculum should be seen as an **"exoskeleton for teachers"** — a force multiplier, allowing them to focus on:

- Local adaptations and classroom strategies
- Personalized student learning (with varying degrees of AI assistance)
- Student social-emotional support
- Collaboration with other teachers and outside experts
- Working with parents and administrators
- Professional and personal growth

Professional development must be vastly increased to handle pedagogical complexity. AI-assistants to teachers for course design and lesson plan augmentation represent a first, likely transitory phase.

> ⚠️ The world needs 44 million more teachers (UNESCO). Half of OECD countries already face teacher shortages. CCR predicts growing acceptance of "nurse-equivalents" assisted by AI — not ideal, but economically and politically necessary in many settings.

## Prompts and Prompt Engineering

A **prompt** is a query sent to an LLM — effectively, coding without a programming language. Explicit, carefully constructed prompts are determinant in query success.

**Prompt Engineering** has emerged as a serious field (high salaries; prompt compilations sold on websites; some prompts are pages long). Key considerations:

- LLMs are partially stochastic — the same prompt can produce different answers even seconds apart
- AI can itself be better at prompt generation than humans, and can refine/optimize its own prompts
- Chain-of-thought prompting: a model prompted to explain itself solved math problems the same model without that prompt could not
- CCR's view: for a precise answer, LLMs will likely continue needing precise questions — "prompt design proficiency will still be required from teachers"

---

## From Adaptive Learning to Intelligent Tutoring Systems (ITS)

Adaptive learning offers two complementary benefits:
1. **Learning time reduction for the median student** — most students reach mastery faster
2. **Self-pacing** — faster students are not held back; struggling students are not abandoned

The degree to which ITS systems are teacher-driven vs. student-driven, and the optimal mix per discipline/age group, remains an open research question requiring numerous experiments.

## Chapter 8 Summary

AI holds significant promise in education's two critical, interdependent facets:

- **Design**: AI assists in crafting innovative, tailored, learning-sciences-based curricula and lesson plans
- **Delivery**: AI powers ITS platforms providing personalized learning tuned to individual strengths and weaknesses

The integration of all design parameters — content, concepts, themes, literature, drivers, pedagogies, digital humanities, competencies — is achievable but requires deliberate systems-level work that cannot be placed entirely on individual teachers. CCR's mantra: "explicitly deliberate, systematic, comprehensive, and demonstrable hard work."

## CCR's Scope and Mandate

Education is a large field. CCR did not attempt to address all policy dimensions. The book's purpose was to clarify the complexities induced by AI and foster thorough conversation leading to action. CCR's core argument: all these complexities **can** be designed into standards, curricula, courseware, and assessments — but require significantly enhanced professional development.

This is not magic. It is **explicitly deliberate, systematic, comprehensive, and demonstrable hard work** — CCR's mantra.

**Training approach**: CCR recommends a gradual release of responsibility — "you watch, I do; we co-design; I watch, you do." Start with "Significant Rework," then progress to "Deepest Redesign" once mastered.

---

## The Real Culprit: University Entrance Requirements

CCR's root-cause analysis identifies the Gordian knot freezing all educational change: **university entrance requirements**. Whether called Gao Kao, Baccalauréat, or SAT/GPA, these summative end-of-studies tests enshrine what gets taught — and they are highly resistant to improvement. CCR's efforts in coming years, in concert with the OECD, will target loosening this stranglehold.

The choice is not between acting and not acting. Not acting is increasingly more harmful.
