# Element Hypothesis — Required Intake Data (Ages 10–12)

**Purpose of this list:** Defines what a Guide must collect to draft an Element hypothesis for a child aged 10–12. Ordered by necessity. Grounded in Ken Robinson, *The Element* (2009) and *Finding Your Element* (2013).

**Core definition:** The Element = where natural **Aptitude** ("I get it") meets **Passion** ("I love it"). **Attitude** and **Opportunity** decide whether that intersection ever surfaces. All four are required signals — collapsing any one loses diagnostic resolution at this age.

---

## Tier 1 — Irreplaceable (no hypothesis without these)

| # | Data to Collect | Purpose | How it looks |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Aptitude signals** — specific activities the child grasps intuitively, learns faster than peers, or does with unusual ease | Aptitude is the non-negotiable half of the Element (*The Element*, Robinson 2009). Capture it *specifically* — not "good at art" but "draws machines and how they connect." Robinson: aptitudes are highly personal, not generic. | "She picked up the code logic in one session while the others needed three." / "He explains the rules of every game to adults — unprompted, accurately." / Parent: "Teachers always comment on how fast she reads a room." |
| 2 | **Passion signals** — what the child does voluntarily, returns to without prompting, would do without reward | Passion is the second irreplaceable half. A stated "like" is weak evidence; unprompted return is strong evidence (*Finding Your Element*, Robinson 2013, Ch. 4). | "He's been making YouTube videos about history on weekends for two years — nobody asked him to." / "She fills notebooks with character sketches on her own time." / "He researches football statistics for fun, not for any class." |
| 3 | **Signal mechanics: flow & ease** — does the child lose track of time, resist stopping, or describe the activity as effortless? | This separates a real Element from a parent-pleasing answer. Loss of time and effortless absorption are Robinson's primary markers that aptitude and passion are genuinely overlapping. | "We have to call her three times for dinner when she's drawing." / "He says math contests don't feel hard — they feel like a puzzle he wants to solve." / "She loses track of time completely when she's building things." |

---

## Tier 2 — Determines whether the Element can develop

| # | Data to Collect | Purpose | How it looks |
|---|---|---|---|
| 4 | **Attitude** — self-belief and response to difficulty (gives up vs. persists; "I'm bad at this" vs. "I can't do this *yet*") | Attitude decides if aptitude is ever pursued (*Finding Your Element*, Ch. 6; aligns with Dweck, *Mindset*, 2006). A fixed mindset suppresses Element expression even where aptitude exists. **Currently underweighted in your intake.** | Growth: "She got a low score and immediately asked what she did wrong so she could fix it." / Fixed: "He said 'I'm just not a music person' after one bad lesson and hasn't touched it since." / Watch for: a child who quits only in front of peers but practices alone. |
| 5 | **Opportunity / access** — what environments, tools, mentors, and domains the child has actually been exposed to | Robinson: a missing aptitude is often a missing *opportunity*, not a missing capacity ("Did I lack aptitude, or instruction?"). Without access data, absence of a signal is uninterpretable. **Currently underweighted in your intake.** | "She's never had access to a camera, so no video signal doesn't mean no aptitude." / "He grew up around his uncle's workshop — mechanical aptitude had years of exposure." / Flag: a child from a narrow environment with thin signals across the board. |

---

## Tier 3 — Sharpens and validates the hypothesis

| # | Data to Collect | Purpose | How it looks |
|---|---|---|---|
| 6 | **Domain breadth** — across which families does the child show signals? (mechanical, spatial, verbal, social, kinesthetic, etc.) | Robinson rejects single-axis intelligence; Element can sit in any domain. Breadth data prevents premature narrowing at age 10–12, when aptitude and passion often point to *different* domains. | Broad: "Strong signals in both storytelling and building — too early to narrow." / Narrow: "Only verbal signals so far — check if opportunity in other domains has been thin." / Watch for: aptitude in one domain, passion in another (common at this age). |
| 7 | **Tribe / social pull** — who the child seeks out, what groups energize vs. drain them | "Finding your tribe" is a core condition for Element growth (*The Element*, Ch. 4). Social context can reveal or mask a passion. | "She lights up around other kids who draw — stays late, talks fast, shares her sketchbook." / "He's quiet in most groups but completely alive in robotics club." / Flag: a child who hides an interest because none of their friends share it. |
| 8 | **Suppression signals** — activities the child has dropped, hidden, or been steered away from | Robinson lists family/cultural/age pressure as the main reason aptitudes go unrecognized. Captures Element candidates that adults have already filtered out. | "He used to write stories constantly — stopped in Grade 5 after a teacher marked him down for 'not being realistic'." / "She wanted to do coding but was told it's not for girls." / "His parents think art is not a real future, so he doesn't mention it anymore." |

---

## Tier 4 — Context that prevents misreads

| # | Data to Collect | Purpose | How it looks |
|---|---|---|---|
| 9 | **Adult projection risk** — what parents/teachers *want* or assume vs. what the child actually does | At 10–12 the child rarely self-identifies the Element; adults narrate it. This data isolates the child's signal from the adult's hope. | Red flag: parent describes only achievements ("she won the science fair") with no mention of what the child chooses freely. / Red flag: "We've always known he'd be an engineer" with no corroborating child behavior. / Cross-check: ask child directly what they do when no one is watching. |
| 10 | **Energy patterns** — when the child is most alert, engaged, or drained across a typical day | Energy maps onto genuine engagement; it cross-checks claimed passions against observed behavior. | "He's exhausted after school but immediately energetic when he sits down to code." / "She's flat during math tutoring but completely alive at drama rehearsal." / Watch for: high energy in an activity the family doesn't mention as a strength. |

---

## Guide Constraints
- **Do not draft a hypothesis if Tier 1 is incomplete.** Attitude and Opportunity gaps (Tier 2) must be flagged, not silently assumed.
- **Aptitude and Passion are separate signals.** Record them in separate fields; do not merge.
- At 10–12, identification stays **invisible to the child** — collect through real activity and observation, not self-labeling.

---

## MIAP Observation — Validation & Development Layer

**This is not intake data and not a score.** The four tiers above identify *where* the Element sits. MIAP (Charles Fadel, *Education for the Age of AI*, 2023) observes *whether that domain is taking root and will last*. Apply it **only after** a domain signal has appeared — never to find an Element, only to validate and grow one. MIAP drivers are fluid, plural, and pre-conscious at ages 10–12; record them as **presence / absence signals, not ratings**.

### How to use it
Once a candidate domain exists, the Guide watches for four observational signals around that specific domain:

| Driver | Observational question (domain-specific) | What it tells the hypothesis |
|---|---|---|
| **M — Motivation** | Does the child return to the activity *unprompted*? | Durability. Self-initiation separates a real Element from a passing spike. |
| **I — Identity** | Does the child describe themselves *through* it ("I'm the kid who builds things")? | Integration. The domain is becoming part of who they are. |
| **A — Agency** | Do they persist when it gets hard, or quit? | Unblocker. Low agency can mask real aptitude as "no signal" (also Dweck, 2006). |
| **P — Purpose** | Do they connect it to anything beyond themselves yet? *(faint at this age)* | Direction. Log lightly — purpose is emergent, not settled, before 12. |

### Rules
- **Observe, do not score.** A numeric MIAP profile at this age is false precision — drivers are positionings, not measurable traits (Fadel, 2023).
- **Diagnostic order, not developmental order.** Use M → I → A → P as a Guide *attention sequence* — Motivation is easiest to see, Purpose the slowest to form. It is not a claim about how the child develops.
- **MIAP validates and develops; it never invents.** Engineering Agency or Purpose around a domain the child has shown no aptitude for produces an artificial Element, not a discovered one.
- **Strong M + I hardens the hypothesis.** Aptitude + passion *plus* unprompted return *plus* self-identification = move from candidate to finding.
