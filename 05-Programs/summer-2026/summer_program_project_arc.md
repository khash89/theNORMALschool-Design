## Program Description
*What the program is, who it is for, and how it works*

[INTERNAL ONLY]

theNORMALschool Summer gives students aged 10–12 six days to discover what they love, build something that matters to them, and share it with a real audience on Saturday.

The program runs one cohort at a time, with up to 20 students. Every student arrives on Monday having already chosen a project. They spend the week planning, building, testing, and improving — with a Guide alongside them throughout.

### Projects
*What students build and how they choose*

Students choose from six pre-designed project briefs across three types: Storytelling, Entrepreneurship, and Web App Building. Most projects are team-based — three members, each with a defined role. Solo projects are available for students who prefer to work independently. Project selection happens before Monday, guided by each student's Element Hypothesis.

### The Element Hypothesis
*A working picture of who the child is, what they are meant to do*

Before the program begins, each student completes an Element Hypothesis — a working picture of what they are naturally pulled toward and where they come most alive. The hypothesis guides project selection. It is revisited and updated at the end of the week based on what the student discovered about themselves through the work.

### The Daily Routine
*What every day looks like*

Every day follows the same rhythm. The structure is predictable. The work inside it is personal.

- **8:30** — Unorganized fun: free movement, student-chosen games, music, and play. No agenda. No adult direction. Students arrive, move, and connect. Body and mood are lifted before the day begins.
- **9:00** — Daily results setting: each student commits to concrete deliverables for the day
- **9:30** — Timeless Competencies workshop: a 60-minute skill session tied to the current sprint phase
- **10:30** — Deep work: sustained project output, student-sequenced
- **12:00** — Nourishment hour: students cook and eat together
- **13:00** — Deep work: core afternoon output block with Guide check-ins
- **15:00** — End of day: peer feedback exchange and reflection against morning commitments

### The Guide's Role
*How adults support students without directing them*

Each Guide tracks student progress across four areas throughout the week: the Element (who the student is), Knowledge (what they are learning), Timeless Competencies (how they work and think), and Whole Child Development (how they are growing as a person). Guides do not direct the work. They observe, ask questions, and surface what the student cannot yet see about themselves.

### The Showcase
*How the week ends*

Saturday's showcase is a public event. Students present their finished work to parents, guests, and the community. Every student presents. Every project is real. The showcase is not a performance — it is the natural endpoint of a week of genuine work.

## Notes
*Design decisions, risks, and principles established during development*

[INTERNAL ONLY]

### Design Principles

**Workshop universality.** Workshop topics must serve all students simultaneously regardless of project type. A topic like "how to frame a driving question" works whether the student is building an app or writing a magazine. Project-specific workshops break the model.

**Pre-designed projects solve cold-start without removing ownership.** The brief sets the problem space. The student sets the angle, the stakes, and the personal meaning. Two students can pick the same brief and produce entirely different outputs.

**Knowledge acquisition is demand-driven, not scheduled.** Students pull a knowledge session when they hit a real blocker in their project work. Willingham (2009) is explicit: the brain encodes knowledge better when it is pulled by a genuine question the student is already carrying.

**Daily results setting only works if End of Day is accountable.** Students will write vague morning commitments to avoid accountability unless End of Day directly compares actual output against morning commitments. No softening.

**The showcase is not a performance.** It is the natural endpoint of a week of genuine work. Every student presents. Every project is real.

### Risk Flags

**Guide assessment load.** Tracking Element, Knowledge, Timeless Competencies, and Whole Child across up to 20 independent projects during sprint days requires a clear observation protocol. Without it, the Guide circulates without purpose. A protocol is required before launch.

**Nourishment hour social dynamics.** Ages 10–12 have complex social dynamics around food. A clear rotating role system — planner, buyer, cook, cleaner — must be established on Day 1 or the block becomes unmanageable by Wednesday.

**Age calibration.** The program is designed for ages 10–12 — a fundamentally different developmental stage from teenagers. Project complexity, sprint expectations, workshop facilitation style, and feedback protocols must all be calibrated specifically for this age group before the program briefs are finalized.

**Element Hypothesis is load-bearing on Day 1.** Project selection is guided by each student's Element Hypothesis. If a student arrives without a completed hypothesis, Day 1 project selection becomes arbitrary. The pre-program intake process must guarantee every student arrives with one.

### Structural Notes

**Robinson's Element requires Aptitude and Passion as separate signals.** Aptitude = natural facility (I Get It). Passion = deep love for the activity (I Love It). At ages 10–12 these frequently point to different domains. Collapsing them into a single signal loses diagnostic resolution at the most critical window.

**Cohort composition at full capacity.** Up to 20 students with team projects as primary format means a maximum of 6 full teams (18 students) plus up to 2 solo students. Guide-to-student ratio must be confirmed against this composition.

**Multiple week enrollment creates a curriculum opportunity.** Students enrolling across multiple weeks of the 6-week program can run successive projects. Each week's Element Hypothesis reflection feeds into the next week's project selection. This compounds learning in a way a single week cannot.
