### Target 1: Statistics and Data Literacy

**Why it matters for Zara:**
She makes quantitative claims in her work that are not yet rigorous.
She needs to know when a number is trustworthy and when it is not.
She also needs this for the documentary's data section (Hypothesis C).

**What we are developing:**
- Descriptive statistics: mean, median, variance, what each tells you and what it hides
- Sample bias: how to evaluate whether a dataset is representative
- Data visualisation: how to represent numbers honestly for a general audience
- Critical reading of statistics: how to interrogate a number someone else produced

**How it is taught:**
Not as a standalone module.
The AI tutor runs the knowledge acquisition in parallel with Month 9 of the documentary project.
Zara builds her displacement rate model at the same time as she works through the statistics sessions.
The concept and the application happen together.

**AI tutor brief for this module:**
- Hook: start with a real dataset that contains a hidden bias. Ask Zara to find it before explaining the concept.
- Use examples exclusively from her project domain: urban data, housing statistics, population movement.
- Do not run abstract exercises. Every concept must connect to something she is currently building.
- Flag to guide: if Zara's session engagement drops below her baseline for two consecutive sessions, this may be confirming a non-signal in Numbers & Patterns. Document and report.

**Checkpoint:**
End of Month 9 — Zara presents her displacement rate model to the guide and the NGO partner.
Success indicator: she can explain her methodology, identify one limitation of her data, and defend her visualisation choices.

### Target 2: Media Economics and Distribution

**Why it matters for Zara:**
She produces work intended to reach and affect specific audiences.
She does not yet understand how publication decisions are made, who funds media organisations, or how distribution shapes who actually sees the work.
This limits her ability to design work that lands.

**What we are developing:**
- How editorial decisions get made in different publication contexts: journalism, documentary, advocacy
- Funding models: who pays for what, and how that shapes what gets made
- Distribution: how reach is built, what platforms do to stories, how audiences find work
- The gap between what gets made and what gets seen

**How it is taught:**
Three structured sessions with a working media practitioner.
Sessions are conversations, not lectures.
Zara prepares a question for each session in advance.

**Session structure:**

| Session | Focus | Zara's preparation |
|---|---|---|
| Session 1 (July 2026) | How this practitioner makes editorial decisions — what gets commissioned, what does not, and why | Zara brings one example of a story she thinks should have been told but was not. She explains why she thinks it was not told. |
| Session 2 (July 2026) | Funding and independence — where the money comes from and how it does and does not affect the work | Zara brings one example of a piece of media where she suspects the funding source shaped the story. She explains her reasoning. |
| Session 3 (August 2026) | Distribution — how this practitioner gets their work to the people it is meant for | Zara brings her current project and asks: how would you distribute this? Who is the actual audience, and how do you reach them? |

**Practitioner brief:**
We are not training Zara for a career. We are helping her understand the system she wants to work inside.
Be honest about constraints, compromises, and failures — not just the ideals.
She responds well to specificity. Abstract principles land less well than real examples from your own work.

**Checkpoint:**
After Session 3 — Zara produces a one-page distribution plan for her documentary.
Not a finished plan. A thinking document that shows she now understands the decisions involved.

### Priority 3: Judgement

**Current status:** Developing
**What growth looks like:** Zara makes good decisions when the ethical dimensions are genuinely unclear — not just when there is a right answer.

**The specific gap:**
Zara has not yet encountered a project moment where the ethical decision involves genuine competing goods — where reasonable people would disagree and no answer is clean.

**How we are building it:**
The documentary creates at least two of these moments naturally:
- One interview subject has asked to remain anonymous. The story is stronger if the person is named. What does Zara do?
- The NGO partner has asked her to emphasise a finding that supports their policy position. The finding is real — but it is not the most important finding in her data. What does she include?

The guide does not resolve these for her.
The guide names them as Judgement development moments and asks: what are the competing goods here, who is affected by each choice, and what do you decide?
The decision is Zara's. The guide's job is to make the structure of the decision visible before she makes it.

**Milestone:**
By Cycle 2 close: Zara has navigated at least one genuine competing-goods decision in her project and can explain — in her reflection journal — what she decided and why. Not just what she decided.

### Priority 4: Self-Mastery

**Current status:** Developing (honest gap)
**What growth looks like:** Zara maintains effort on knowledge acquisition that does not immediately feel relevant — not just on work she has already decided matters.

**The gap:**
Zara gives up on things she believes do not matter.
She has named this herself.
The gap is not motivation — she has abundant motivation.
The gap is in her ability to temporarily subordinate her own judgement about relevance in order to build something that will matter later.

**How we are building it:**
1. The statistics module is the primary test. It does not feel relevant to Zara yet. It will become relevant in Month 9. She has to get through the early sessions on trust.
2. Before the module begins, the guide names the purpose explicitly: *"This will not feel connected to your work yet. It will in Month 9. Here is exactly how."* Make the future relevance visible in advance.
3. At the end of Month 9, after the data section is built: return to the question. *"Was the statistics module worth doing?"* Let her answer it herself.

**Milestone:**
Zara completes the statistics module without a guide-initiated motivation intervention.
Completion is the milestone — not enjoyment, not enthusiasm.

## Section 4 — Whole Child Focus Areas

*How Zara is being supported as a whole person this cycle*

---

### Physical: Nourishment Hour Pattern

**The pattern:**
During high-pressure project periods, Zara skips the nourishment hour to continue working.

**Why this matters:**
This is not a health crisis. It is a habit that will compound.
Zara needs to understand — from evidence, not prescription — that sustained output requires deliberate recovery.

**The approach:**
Do not make a rule. Have a conversation.
Guide to raise this directly in the next one-to-one session: *"I've noticed you skip the nourishment hour when the project is pressured. Tell me about that."*
Listen. Then share the evidence: what she has observed about her own energy and focus in the afternoons on days she skips vs. days she does not.
Ask her to run a two-week experiment and track it herself.
The goal is self-observation, not compliance.

**Timeline:** Conversation in May 2026. Two-week experiment June 2026. Debrief in June 2026.

---

### Mental: Looping Under Pressure

**The pattern:**
When a project is not progressing, Zara sometimes loops — re-analyses the same considerations without moving to action.

**Why this matters:**
Circular thinking under pressure is a cognitive pattern she will encounter throughout her life.
Learning to name it and interrupt it now is a high-value investment.

**The approach:**
The guide has already begun naming this directly.
This is working — when the pattern is named, Zara moves.
The goal for this cycle is to help her name it herself, without waiting for the guide.

**Specific tool:**
Give Zara a phrase she can use internally when she notices the loop: *"What is the next smallest action?"*
This is not a technique — it is a bridge from circular thought to forward movement.
Review in the monthly one-to-one: is she using it? Is it working?

**Timeline:** Ongoing through Cycle 2.

---

### Emotional: Feedback Recovery Support

**The pattern:**
Zara takes critical feedback on her work personally in the first instance, then recovers and engages with it.
The recovery gap has shortened from days (Stage 1) to hours (current).

**Why this still needs attention:**
The gap will be tested more severely in this cycle.
The hard-deadline journalism assignment and the public screening both involve external audiences who do not know Zara and have no investment in managing her feelings.
This is appropriate. It is also harder.

**The approach:**
1. **Brief every external reviewer** before they work with Zara: feedback should be specific, evidence-based, and direct. Not softened. Not general. See the Student Hypothesis for the feedback format that works for her.
2. **Build in a 48-hour gap** between any significant external feedback session and the debrief with the guide. Do not process it immediately.
3. **Name the pattern with Zara directly** before the journalism assignment begins: *"You will get feedback from someone who does not know you. The feedback will be about the work, not about you. Your first response will probably feel personal. Give it 48 hours before you act on it."*

**Milestone:**
By end of Cycle 2: Zara receives external feedback from the public screening audience and can process it in a reflection entry without the guide's immediate support.

## Pathway Revision Triggers

*What would cause this pathway to be updated before the November 2026 review*

This pathway is updated immediately — not at the scheduled review — if any of the following occur:

| Trigger | What it means | Response |
|---|---|---|
| Zara disengages from the documentary project for more than two weeks | Signal durability question needs reassessment | Emergency hypothesis review. Do not reduce scope first — understand the reason first. |
| A new strong activation signal emerges in an unexpected domain | The hypothesis may need reorientation | Document carefully. Do not act immediately. Raise in next one-to-one and observe for two more weeks before updating the hypothesis. |
| Zara's mental or physical wellbeing deteriorates significantly | The pathway load may need adjustment | Whole Child takes priority. Adjust scope in consultation with Zara. Document what changed and why. |
| The peer friction with [peer name] escalates to a point that affects either student's daily functioning | Collaboration development plan needs revision | Separate the joint project if necessary. Do not abandon the working relationship work — just adjust the method. |
| Zara independently articulates a change in her Element direction | She may be learning something the hypothesis has not caught yet | Listen carefully. This is high-value data. Revise after two weeks of further observation, not immediately. |
| The NGO partner relationship breaks down | The primary project loses its real-world grounding | Identify an alternative partner within two weeks. The project scope holds — the audience changes. |
