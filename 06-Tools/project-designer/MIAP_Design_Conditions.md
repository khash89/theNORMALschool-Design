# MIAP Design Conditions
## How Guides Strengthen Student Development Through Project Design

**[INTERNAL ONLY]**
*For Guide use only. Not for distribution to students or families.*

---

## What This Document Is For
*A reference for Guides working with students across all stages*

This document tells you what to watch for and what to do. It covers four development drivers every student needs: Motivation, Identity, Agency, and Purpose (MIAP). When one driver is weak or absent, student development stalls. This document gives you the diagnostic signals and specific conditions to address each one.

Three things to hold before reading further.

**First:** drivers do not develop in sequence. A student builds all four drivers at the same time — through the same experiences. Think of them like the four legs of a chair. One weak leg makes the whole thing unstable.

**Second:** drivers are domain-specific. A student can have strong Agency in design and weak Agency in writing. "Low Agency" is not a student trait. It is a domain-specific signal.

**Third:** your job is to create conditions. You cannot manufacture a student's natural aptitude. Conditions create access to what is already there. They do not create what is not there.

---

## How to Diagnose Which Driver Is Weakest
*Reading behavior, not self-report*

Do not ask students to diagnose their own drivers. Students cannot accurately see what is missing. Read behavior instead.

| Driver | Absent Signal |
|---|---|
| **Motivation** | Compliant but passive. Does exactly what is asked, nothing more. Describes all work as "fine." Flat energy regardless of activity. |
| **Identity** | Cannot describe themselves as a learner in their own words. Self-description shifts based on who they are with. Defines self by negation: "I am bad at math." |
| **Agency** | Asks "is this right?" before attempting solvable problems. Stops completely at the first obstacle without trying. Outsources decisions to the Guide even when they have enough information to decide. |
| **Purpose** | Cannot say why their work matters beyond "it was interesting." Work quality rises and falls with personal interest. Never mentions others when reflecting on what they produced. |

## Part 1 — Motivation
*How Guides help students find genuine reasons to engage*

A student with low Motivation is not lazy. Their engagement is tied to external rewards: grades, approval, completing tasks to avoid consequences. When those rewards disappear, so does the effort.

Think of Motivation like fuel. A car with an empty tank will not move no matter how capable the engine is. The conditions below refuel it.

**Root causes.** Low Motivation comes from one of three places (Deci & Ryan, Self-Determination Theory, 1985):

- No real choice in the work
- No visible evidence of progress
- No connection to real people or real stakes

### Conditions to Design

| Condition | What the Guide Does |
|---|---|
| **Choice injection** | Give the student one genuine choice inside the project. Topic, format, audience, or approach. Not a choice between three options you pre-selected. A real choice that changes direction. |
| **Early win design** | Build a milestone the student can complete successfully in week one. One early success changes their relationship to the entire project. |
| **Relatedness connection** | Connect the student to one real person affected by the project output. A community member, a practitioner, or a peer who needs the solution. |
| **Pressure removal** | Temporarily shift from "produce this deliverable" to "explore this domain for two weeks." Performance pressure kills intrinsic motivation. Remove it when Motivation is absent. |

### Example

> **Tariq, 13.**
> Signal detected: During a site visit, Tariq spent 40 minutes asking unprompted structural questions. He missed the bus.
> Prior observation: Flat energy across all projects. Completes work to minimum requirements only.
> Decision: Guide designs the next project with one genuine choice of format. Builds a first milestone around structural analysis. Connects Tariq to a local engineer.
> Outcome: Tariq initiates a project extension without being asked.

## Part 2 — Identity
*How Guides help students build a stable picture of who they are*

A student with weak Identity has no consistent sense of what they are good at or what matters to them. They define themselves by negation. Their self-description shifts depending on who they are with.

Think of Identity like a compass. Without it, the student moves in whatever direction the environment pushes. With it, they navigate.

**Root causes.** Weak Identity comes from three places:

- Not enough domain exposure to know who they are (Marcia, identity moratorium, 1966)
- An identity adopted from parents or peers without personal exploration
- No accumulated record of what they have done and who that makes them

### Conditions to Design

| Condition | What the Guide Does |
|---|---|
| **Structured reflection** | At every project midpoint, ask: "What did you notice about yourself when you did this?" Not: "How did the project go?" The question is about the student, not the work. |
| **Role experimentation** | Rotate team roles across projects: leader, researcher, maker, presenter, critic. The role that consistently feels most natural is an Identity signal. |
| **Portfolio review conversation** | Show the student their own work from three months ago. Ask: "What does this tell you about yourself?" Identity builds partly from looking backward at accumulated evidence. |
| **Naming back** | Name a specific behavioral observation. "I notice that when you hit a structural problem, you go quiet in a way that looks like concentration, not avoidance." Do not evaluate. Observe. |
| **Practitioner introduction** | Introduce the student to someone further along the same apparent path. Identity builds partly by seeing yourself reflected in another person. |

### Example

> **Yemi, 14.**
> Signal detected: Yemi fills notebook margins with floor plans during an unrelated project. Guide observes this across three separate weeks.
> Prior observation: Describes herself as "bad at school." Self-description shifts daily.
> Decision: Guide names the observation: "I notice that whenever there is a spatial problem, you slow down and go deep." Reviews portfolio with Yemi from eight weeks prior.
> Outcome: Yemi begins describing herself as "someone who thinks in three dimensions." Self-description stabilizes.

## Part 3 — Agency
*How Guides help students direct their own development*

A student with weak Agency waits. They wait for permission. They wait for answers. They wait for someone to solve the obstacle in front of them. This is not a character flaw. It is often the result of years of schooling where waiting was the correct strategy.

Think of Agency like a muscle. It atrophies without use. The conditions below rebuild it through repeated exercise.

**Root causes.** Weak Agency usually comes from one of four places:

- Learned helplessness: past experience taught them that their actions do not change outcomes (Seligman)
- Fixed mindset: belief that ability is fixed makes effort feel pointless (Dweck, 2016)
- Challenge miscalibration: tasks too hard produce frustration; tasks too easy require no agency
- Guide rescuing: someone solves problems before the student has to try

### Conditions to Design

| Condition | What the Guide Does |
|---|---|
| **ZPD calibration** | Set tasks at the edge of the student's capability — not above it. Vygotsky: work below this zone produces boredom. Work above it produces frustration. Both prevent Agency from developing. |
| **Guide response protocol** | Never answer a solvable question directly. Redirect: "Where could you find that?" or "What have you already tried?" Hold the discomfort. This is the hardest condition to maintain. |
| **Revision culture** | Build mandatory critique and revision cycles into every project. Returning to fix work is the expected norm, not a sign of failure. |
| **Single complete ownership** | Give the student one small project they own entirely. No group. No Guide involvement in decisions. Even mediocre output from a fully self-directed project builds Agency. |
| **Decision celebration** | Praise the decision, not the outcome. "You chose to try that approach even when you were unsure." Not: "That was correct." |

### Example

> **Diego, 12.**
> Signal detected: Diego has enough information to proceed but defers to the Guide on every decision. Asks "is this right?" before every attempt.
> Prior observation: Stops completely at the first obstacle without trying. No revision attempts without prompting.
> Decision: Guide adopts the response protocol. Stops answering solvable questions. Assigns Diego one small project he owns entirely.
> Outcome: After two weeks of discomfort, Diego begins attempting solutions before asking. Revision rate increases without prompting.

## Part 4 — Purpose
*How Guides help students connect their work to something beyond themselves*

A student without Purpose produces good work in domains they find interesting and inconsistent work in everything else. They have no stable "why." When a project becomes difficult or unglamorous, they disengage.

Think of Purpose like a rope anchored to something outside yourself. When the path gets hard, you pull the rope. Students without it have nothing to pull.

**Root causes.** Absent Purpose comes from two places:

- All projects have been abstract or self-referential. No encounter with real human stakes.
- Passion exists but remains self-contained: "I love this" without "this matters because."

### Conditions to Design

| Condition | What the Guide Does |
|---|---|
| **Real-stakes deliverable** | The project output must affect a real person outside the school. Not simulated. Not hypothetical. |
| **Site visit to the problem** | Take the student to where the problem lives. Not a classroom discussion about it. Direct contact with the real context. |
| **Purpose reflection prompt** | At every project end, ask: "Who is better off because of your work? How do you know?" Not: "What did you learn?" |
| **Passion-to-purpose bridge** | If passion exists but Purpose is absent, ask one question: "Who suffers when this is done badly?" This converts personal passion into social purpose. |
| **Purpose-modeling mentor** | Introduce a practitioner who does this work because of why it matters, not only because they are skilled at it. Purpose is modeled before it is activated. |

### Example

> **Tariq, end of year one.**
> Signal detected: Strong Motivation and Agency in spatial design. Cannot say why it matters. Describes work only as "fun."
> Prior observation: No reference to others when reflecting. Work quality drops on difficult phases of the same project.
> Decision: Guide arranges a site visit to a low-income housing block with poor spatial design. Asks afterward: "Who suffers when spaces are designed badly?"
> Outcome: Tariq writes in his reflection: "Someone decided this was good enough for these people." Purpose activates. Work quality on difficult project phases stabilizes.

## High-Leverage Conditions
*Four interventions that strengthen multiple drivers at once*

Since drivers develop together, some conditions create cross-driver effects. Design these first. They have the highest return per effort.

| Condition | Drivers Strengthened |
|---|---|
| **Real-stakes project with a real audience** | Motivation (connection to real people) + Agency (real consequences) + Purpose (real impact) |
| **Guide naming back** | Identity (student sees themselves through a reliable mirror) + Motivation (being genuinely seen by another person) |
| **Revision culture built into every project** | Agency (effort is the mechanism of growth, not evidence of weakness) + Motivation (visible progress signal) |
| **Practitioner mentorship** | Identity (seeing yourself reflected further along the same path) + Purpose (hearing a practitioner's why) + Motivation (connection to a real field) |

## The Authenticity Boundary
*What Guides can and cannot do*

MIAP conditions create access to the student's natural aptitude and passion (what Ken Robinson calls the Element). They cannot create the Element itself. The distinction rests on one component MIAP cannot touch: natural aptitude.

**What conditions do:** Remove the obstacles between the student and the discovery of who they already are.

**What conditions cannot do:** Manufacture aptitude that is not present. Sustain a student long-term in a domain where natural facility is absent.

**The design rule:** Observe across all domains during Wide Exposure. Only target specific conditions after aptitude signals have appeared naturally and repeatedly.

| Safe Use of Conditions | Corrupted Use |
|---|---|
| Wide Motivation conditions across all domains | Designing Motivation conditions toward a domain you have already decided on |
| Naming back what you observed | Interpreting ambiguous signals to confirm a preference you already hold |
| Agency-building through any real project | Designing Agency conditions only in your preferred domain |
| Purpose connection after aptitude signal is confirmed | Using Purpose to sustain a student in a domain with no aptitude signal |

A Guide who targets conditions before aptitude signals have appeared is not helping the student find their Element. They are building enthusiasm toward a conclusion they have already reached.
